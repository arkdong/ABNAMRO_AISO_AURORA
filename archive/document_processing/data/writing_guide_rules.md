# ABN AMRO Writing Guide 2026 V1.1 — Translator Checklist

A focused subset of the 48-page Writing Guide, tailored for Dutch→English
translation. Edit this file to refine translator behaviour. The OpenAI system
prompt loads it verbatim; keep it stable so prompt caching activates.

## Spelling — British English (mandatory)

ABN AMRO uses British English. American spelling is wrong unless the text
explicitly targets a US audience.

- **-our** not **-or**: colour, behaviour, favour, neighbour, labour
- **-re** not **-er**: centre, theatre, calibre, metre, fibre
- **-ise / -isation** not **-ize / -ization**: organise, analyse, criticise, recognise, organisation
- **-yse** not **-yze**: analyse, paralyse
- **-ae- / -oe-** kept: anaesthesia, archaeology, encyclopaedia, foetus, manoeuvre
- Final **e** kept before **-ing**: ageing (not aging)
- Single-`l` verbs double in past/present participle: travelling, travelled, travellers, modelling, cancelled
- Verb **practise**, noun **practice**; verb **license**, noun **licence**
- **pretence**, **defence**, **offence**
- **-ogue** not **-og**: analogue, dialogue, catalogue
- Collective nouns are plural: "the team are", "the family are"

## Tone & style

- **Forward-thinking, engaged, smart** — never corporate, salesy or stuffy.
- **Plain B1 language**: everyday words a non-specialist understands. If a banking term must stay, briefly explain it.
- **Active voice**: "We will call you", not "You will be called". Always say who is doing what.
- **Short sentences**: aim 10-20 words; vary the length. Break long Dutch subclauses into separate English sentences.
- **One topic per paragraph**; lead with the key point.
- **No figurative language**: avoid sayings, proverbs, idioms — non-native readers won't follow.
- **No greenwashing or unfounded claims**: ESG and sustainability claims must stay accurate, specific and substantiated. Don't soften or strengthen the source's claims.
- **Avoid weakening modal verbs** (may, can, would like to, shall) and vague hedges (some, mostly, try, likely, etc., a number of, among other things) unless legally required. Be specific.
- **Don't nominalise verbs**: "We will process your data" not "Processing of your data will take place".
- **Avoid relative clauses** — start a new sentence instead.
- **No double negatives**.
- **Avoid Dutch-style Q&A constructions** ("Need help? Then contact us.") — use natural English flow.
- **Aim for consistency**: same term throughout; pronouns can vary it without confusion.
- **Personal pronouns**: use **we**/**us** for the bank, **you** for the reader. Never **one**. Refer to ABN AMRO as **it**, never **her**.

## Numbers

- **Words 0–9**, plus "one thousand", "(one) hundred thousand", "million", "billion", "trillion" up to multiples of nine ("three hundred", "ten thousand").
- **Digits** for: decimals (2.25), percentages (8%), units of measure (8 m, 3 km), ranges (9-10%, 13,000-15,000), tables, ages ("his 9th birthday"), page numbers, prominent identifiers ("5-digit code"), precise announcements ("31 degrees", "50 miles an hour").
- **Decimal point** with a leading zero when |x| < 1: 0.023.
- **Comma thousands separator**: 1,000 / 10,000 / 1,000,000.
- **No space before %**: 8.75%. Tables only — running text uses "per cent".
- **Never start a sentence with a digit** — rephrase or spell out.
- **Numbers are separate words** in plurals: "23 billion", not "23-billion".
- **Don't use fractions for amounts/percentages** — use decimals. Written-out fractions are hyphenated: one-third, three-quarters.

## Dates

- Format: **`21 September 2017`** (day, month, year, no commas, non-breaking spaces between day-month and month-year).
- **Don't abbreviate month names** in body text. Tables/charts may use abbreviations like `21-09-2017`.
- Year ranges: `2010-2017`. With "from", you must use "to": `from 1995 to 2000`. Never `from 1995-2000`.
- Decades: **1990s**, **1800s** — no apostrophe.
- Months and days of the week always start with a capital.
- Translate the source's `19/01/2026 • 8 minuten lezen` lead-in as `19/01/2026 • 8 minutes read` (keep the bullet, the slash format, and the figure).

## Currencies

- **Euro symbol** in front of amounts with a non-breaking space: `€ 100`. Round amounts have no decimals unless a table mixes round and tenths.
- **Where the symbol can't render**, write `100 euros` after the figure.
- **Tables and financial reporting**: ISO codes (EUR, USD, GBP), even in running text in financial reports.
- The word **euro** is lower-case (except sentence start). Composites are separate words: "10 euro note", "euro rate", "euro cent".
- Refer to the euro as **it**, not "the single currency she".

## Capital letters

- **Proper nouns**: capitalise only significant words; "of", "the" stay lower-case (the University of Amsterdam).
- **Department names** capitalised (Group Compliance, Press Office); the word "department" itself is lower-case ("the Credit Risk department").
- **Headings**: only the first word + any proper nouns ("How to apply for a mortgage").
- **Months, days of the week** capitalised.
- **Cardinal directions** lower-case ("from the west") — but recognised regions take capitals (Middle East, Eastern Europe, North Africa, the West Bank).
- **Lower-case always**: internet, intranet, online, email, website (unless sentence-initial).
- **Job titles** capitalised when official (Director, Chief Financial Officer, Head of Operations); lower-case when generic ("the editor", "the project manager").
- **Don't capitalise to emphasise** a word — readers find it shouty.

## Abbreviations

- Use only when a long term recurs and slows the reader. Spell out on first use: "independent software vendor (ISV)".
- **Familiar abbreviations** don't need spelling out: US, EU, IMF, AI, NGO, ESG, AFM, EUR, USD, GBP.
- **Capitalised**: USD, EUR, PDF, AI, NGO. **Lower-case**: kg, km, mph (units of measure).
- **e.g.** and **i.e.** — informal writing only, lower-case. Otherwise spell out "for example", "that is".
- **"&"** only inside official names. Otherwise write "and".
- **Mr / Mrs / Ms / Miss** — no full stops in British English.
- Don't use "etc." — be specific.

## Compound words

- English usually keeps them separate: **bank account**, **team leader**, **working day**, **IT department** (not "IT-department"). When in doubt, write separately.
- Adjectival compounds before a noun may hyphenate: "end-of-year review", "well-read book". After the noun, no hyphen: "the book was well read".
- Hyphenate after consecutive vowels: "anti-inflationary".
- Hyphenate prefixes before capitalised words: "anti-American", "non-native".
- Re-/pre- usually no hyphen — except before another **e**: "re-entry", "pre-empt" (but "reopen", "preconceive").
- English does not use the omission hyphen for compounds: write **"internal and external communications"**, never "in- and external communications".

## Punctuation

- **Apostrophes** for possessives only ("ABN AMRO's profits", "a year's data"). Never for plurals, dates or abbreviations: "two photos", "the 1990s", "three MPs".
- **Commas** sparingly. Use them for list separators, after intro phrases, and when expansive (non-restrictive) information is added. Inside quotation marks when quoting.
- **Full stops**: one space after. None after Mr/Mrs/Ms, at the end of a heading, or at the end of a hyperlink.
- **Quotation marks**: double for quoting a person; single for a quote within a quote, on-screen text, seminar/conference titles. Place the full stop inside the closing quote when the quotation is a full sentence.
- **Semicolons**: rarely. When in doubt, full stop.
- **Square brackets**: only to insert clarifying words inside a quoted passage.
- **Avoid exclamation marks** — they read as exaggerated.
- **Brackets sparingly** — prefer a new sentence. Don't use brackets as Dutch-style "(international) operations" shorthand for "and/or".

## Inclusive writing

- **Avoid he/she/his/her** for generic people. Rewrite using:
  - definite article: "the bank employee should make sure the name badge is visible"
  - direct address: "as a bank employee, you should…"
  - plural: "bank employees should make sure their name badges are visible"
  - singular **they/them/their** — accepted English usage.
- **Gender-neutral job titles**: businessperson (not businesswoman), salesperson, sales representative, chair (not chairman), spokesperson, firefighter, police officer.
- **People-first language**: "person with a hearing disability" (not "the deaf"); "person with a vision disability" (not "the blind"); "people with an immigrant background" (not "foreigners").
- **Avoid gendered labels** when irrelevant: "mother" not "working mother"; "director" not "female director"; "manager" not "manager with an immigrant background".
- **Make implied comparisons explicit**: "women earn less **than men**" (not just "women earn less"); "people with an immigrant background work harder **than people born in the Netherlands**".

## Hyperlinks

- Anchor text must describe the destination — never "click here", "read more", "go to the next page". (Exception: a **standalone** call-to-action like "Read more." at the end of an article is fine.)
- Strip the `http://www` prefix in displayed URLs (e.g., `abnamro.com`).
- Https URLs need `www.` shown if you write the full URL.
- Don't put a full stop immediately after a URL — embed the URL mid-sentence.

## Brand & proper nouns

- Always **ABN AMRO** (with a non-breaking space). Never `ABN/AMRO`, `ABN_AMRO`, `ABN-AMRO`, `ABN AMRO bank`.
- Combine with "we"/"us" to soften: "ABN AMRO has many years' experience. We…".
- ABN AMRO is **it**, not "her".
- Don't translate division names: "ABN AMRO Hypotheken Groep" stays Dutch.
- "the bank" is fine in legal text but avoid in client copy — could refer to any bank.
- Sponsorship/project names take capitals: "ABN AMRO World Tennis Tournament", "ABN AMRO Art Prize".
- Don't capitalise product names in possessive form: "ABN AMRO's student account", not "ABN AMRO Student Account".
- People's names, ticker symbols, URLs, foreign-language proper nouns, and quotations from external sources stay verbatim.

## Markdown structure (translation-specific)

When translating an article body, **preserve every Markdown construct exactly**:

- Hero image at the top (`![](https://…)`) — do not move, drop, or alter the URL.
- Headings (`#`, `##`, `###`) — translate the heading text, keep the level.
- Bullet (`-`) and numbered lists — keep punctuation rules (lower-case fragments without full stops; full sentences capitalised with full stops).
- Hyperlinks `[text](url)` — translate the anchor text, keep the URL byte-for-byte.
- Inline images, iframes, raw HTML, code fences — preserve verbatim.
- Blockquotes (`>`) — preserve marker, translate content.
- Bold/italic emphasis — preserve.
- Date lead-in `19/01/2026 • 8 minuten lezen` → `19/01/2026 • 8 minutes read` (just translate the trailing words).
- The standalone "Analyse" / sector-link line at the top — translate the label ("Analysis"), keep the URL.
- Author name lines (just the name on its own line) — keep verbatim; do not translate or annotate.

## What NOT to translate

- Proper nouns and people's names (Han Mesters, Amad Khan, Henk Hofstede, …)
- Brand names (ABN AMRO, ING, Rabobank, Bain & Company, MIT, …)
- Ticker codes, ISO codes, abbreviations already in English
- URLs and email addresses
- Dutch division names embedded in proper nouns ("ABN AMRO Hypotheken Groep")
- Quotations from external Dutch sources unless the surrounding text translates them — if they do, follow the source's lead

## Output discipline

- **Don't invent facts.** If a phrase has multiple plausible renderings, prefer the one closest to the source meaning.
- **Don't add commentary**, footnotes or apologies. Output only the translated content.
- **Match length within reason**: don't pad, don't truncate.
- If a sentence resists clean BrE translation, prefer accuracy + clarity over literalness.
