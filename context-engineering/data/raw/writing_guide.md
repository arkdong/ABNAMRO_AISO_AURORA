Once you’ve defined the target audience and objective of your text, it’s time to develop 
your story. Only provide the information readers need in order to take the required 
action. This can include things like arguments, information that answers potential 
questions, or a practical explanation. 
Strike the right balance between what you can tell readers and what your readers

message. A good text should usually answer at least the following questions: who,

3.3 Choosing the right words
Avoid formal language, use everyday words
Be careful about using jargon
Plain language uses everyday words that ‘everyone’ knows. Abstract, formal and 
difficult words aren’t good for your text. They make it vague, distant, unfriendly and 
even annoying. What’s more, formal texts often come across as old-fashioned. So use 
commonly used words wherever possible. If you can’t avoid using a difficult, abstract 
word, explain it and give an example.
We have a tendency to use terminology that is familiar to us, but may be hard for 
others to understand. Always ask yourself whether there is an easier word to use than 
professional jargon. And if you do have to use a difficult word, explain what it means.
If you’re writing for colleagues, other people in your line of work, or business relations 
who will definitely understand professional jargon, using jargon is not a problem. 
Giving too much explanation to these readers may come across as patronising.
Remember that business language is not the same as formal language, and everyday 
language is not the same as slang. You can write a business-like text without it 
sounding too formal. 
‘The CEO could not call the secretary because she was not present at the 
time.’
Who was not present? The CEO or her secretary?

4. Accuracy
Standards for spelling and vocabulary
At ABN AMRO, we always strive to write correct and consistent texts. We want 
readers to take us seriously and we don’t want mistakes to distract them from 
content. In this chapter, we present three topics that will help you write your 
text according to ABN AMRO’s standards.

Accuracy
Abbreviations
Abbreviations are not the best way to save space because they make a text harder to 
understand. Avoid abbreviating unnecessarily.
If a long term is frequently used in your text, it may make your text awkward to read. In 
that case, abbreviating the term may be the best solution. To abbreviate a term, write 
out the words in full when the term is first mentioned, followed by the abbreviation 
in brackets; for example: ‘independent software vendor (ISV)’. After that, use only 
the abbreviation, but try not to repeat it too often. If an abbreviation or acronym is 
so familiar that it is used more often than the full form (for example: ‘US’, ‘EU’, ‘IMF’), 
you do not need to give the full form at all. If an organisation has formalised an 
abbreviation of its name, use that spelling (for example: iDEAL).

6. Inclusive writing
Reach everyone with your text
At ABN AMRO, we want to help people move forward with action-focused, 
personal and accessible texts. We don’t want to exclude anyone, and 
language plays a key role. All it takes is one poorly chosen word or inadequate 
explanation to give a large target group the feeling the text isn’t meant for 
them. But when you succeed in writing without unconscious bias or blind 
spots, every reader will feel valued, seen and taken seriously.

Not everyone in our society finds reading easy. To make sure your message also gets 
across to readers who struggle, it’s important to pay attention to things like jargon, 
length of sentences, structure and style. Check chapter 3, ‘Wording your message’ for
