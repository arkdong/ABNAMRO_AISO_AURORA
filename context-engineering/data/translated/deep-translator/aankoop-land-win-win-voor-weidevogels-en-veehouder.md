# Purchase of land is a win-win for meadow birds and livestock farmers

[Entrepreneurial story](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/ondernemershalen/index.html)

[Agricultural](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/agrarisch/index.html)

12/17/2025 • 4 minutes reading

[Jan de Ruyter](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/authors/jan-de-ruyter/index.html)

Dairy farmer Floris Slingerland always had an eye for and appreciation for nature and meadow birds, but unfortunately they were not on his land until 2014. That changed when he purchased land that year that already had a deferred mowing ANLb package on it. With this he also 'bought' the meadow birds and that passion has since been greatly fueled. ANLb stands for Agricultural Nature and Landscape Management, a subsidy scheme that financially supports farmers and other landowners to take measures for nature management on their land.

> “It shouldn't just be about the money.”

Floris Slingerland

Dairy farmer

Floris milks 115 dairy cows and associated young cattle in partnership with his wife. With the 47 hectares owned and 3 hectares leased, you can speak of intensive land use. The livestock farmer indicates that there is a constant search for the balance between successful pasture management and ensuring sufficient roughage. This quest gives him great satisfaction. “It shouldn't just be about the money.” He now also has an ANLb package for ecological ditch management and is active with the package for deferred mowing. “We also do 'last minute' management: we look at where the meadow birds are and then decide to opt for delayed mowing,” says the livestock farmer. The neighbor at the back is also a dairy farmer with many meadow birds and he manages to maintain the population of lapwings, godwits, redshanks, shovelers and a few oystercatchers.

## Land provides space for meadow birds

The livestock farmer in Hoogblokland continues to struggle with his land. After the purchase in 2014, a unique opportunity arose in 2021 to expand the house plot by 16 hectares. Floris: “The farmer who stopped had his young cattle walking there and that was already a kind of extensive grazing where the meadow birds did well.” So in that respect too it was a nice addition that fits well with the company. “The house plot also already had a depression that forms a kind of natural marsh and attracts many meadow birds,” says Floris. This is how he literally got the meadow birds 'at home'. Last year, the livestock farmer lost 12 hectares due to construction activities by Rijkswaterstaat and part of the leased land that was sold. “I grew corn on it, and that attracted a lot of lapwings,” says Floris. But he indicates that the corn yields on the ground, clay on peat, were not great either. Now he buys corn.

## Ditches instead of puddles

At the company in the green heart, there are many natural ditches on the land that usually contain water, fed from the ditch water level, says Floris. “You always see the chicks wandering around there.” Even though he saw the attraction that the water exerts on the birds, he still stopped puddle feeding. He says that a puddle marsh gives his grassland too much of a 'thump', causing him to miss yields, which is why he has stopped. According to him, the presence of ditches and ditches keeps the land sufficiently attractive for birds. He is considering whether he can deepen or mill the ditches a little more to enhance the puddle effect.

> “When I walk into the country and the birds attack you, that is the most beautiful thing there is!”

Floris Slingerland

Dairy farmer

## Find nests yourself

He is involved in meadow bird protection together with Collective Alblasserwaard-Vijfheerenlanden. “I call the collective to tell them they can come with the drone. I will then be happy to look for the nests myself,” says the enthusiastic livestock farmer. “When I walk into the country and the birds attack you, that is the most beautiful thing there is!” The 'air force' of the birds knows how to defend itself very well against air predators, Floris sees. “And fortunately there are no foxes here and we have little trouble with other ground predators.” He sees this as one of the success factors for the meadow bird population in his area. What does worry him are the increasingly tightened CO₂ targets from the dairy sector. Delayed mowing reduces the quality of the grass, which has a negative impact on the CO₂ calculation because it can be milked less efficiently. “Fortunately, I can still use the grass for the young cattle,” he says matter-of-factly.
