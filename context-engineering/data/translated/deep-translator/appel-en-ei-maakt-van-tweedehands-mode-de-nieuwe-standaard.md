# Appel & Ei makes second-hand fashion the new standard

[Entrepreneurial story](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/ondernemershalen/index.html)

[Retail](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/retail/index.html)

21/10/2025 • 5 minutes reading

[Henk Hofstede](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/authors/henk-hofstede/index.html)

Appel & Ei puts second-hand fashion in a new light with a fresh look and a range that looks like new. “That is our strength,” says director Martin Pot. The store chain, nominated for the ABN AMRO Sustainable Retail Award 2025-2026, strives for growth and scalability to make second-hand fashion the new standard.

“Is this second-hand clothing?” is written in sleek white letters on the store window of [Appel & Ei](https://appelenei.nl/) in Gouda. It is precisely this sense of wonder that the retail chain wants to evoke. On the racks you will hardly find second-hand clothing, brought in by consumers, that can hardly be distinguished from new. Only the most beautiful and current pieces pass the strict inspection. The contributor receives 40 percent of the sales price of a sold item. Is a piece of clothing still in the store after six weeks? Then it goes back to the owner or to circular textile collector Drop & Loop.

## New fashion or second hand?

According to general manager Martin Pot, this puts Appel & Ei in a unique position. "We sell second-hand clothing, but act as new fashion. Our range is of good quality, changes every day and is based on the seasons. We do not sell vintage, but clothing from about two fashion seasons ago. When you walk into our store, it feels like you are in a new fashion store. Only we don't have that sweater in a different size."

> “When you walk into our shop, it feels like you are in a new fashion store.”

Martin Pot

Director Appel & Ei

With this concept, Appel & Ei has steadily grown into a national chain in recent years. Founder Arda Palm opened the first store in Leiden in 2001, driven by the mission to make people happy with beautiful second-hand clothing. The success of the second store in Doetinchem made it clear that there was growth potential. The only question was: in what form? The choice fell on a franchise formula. Appel & Ei now has 22 locations, including 18 franchise stores and four own stores.

## From 22 to 50 stores

After almost 25 years of steady growth, Appel & Ei is now at a crossroads. The years 2025 and 2026 are important, when the retail chain wants to lay the foundation for further growth and scalability. The ambitions are clear: from 22 to 50 stores in 2030.

Pot is certain: second-hand is the future. “I believe that a third of clothing turnover will soon be second-hand. Our mission is therefore simple: make second-hand fashion the new standard.”

> “Our mission is simple: make second-hand fashion the new standard.”

Martin Pot

Director Appel & Ei

## From local to central

The biggest challenge? Uniformity in the sorting and pricing of second-hand clothing. *“*Selecting exactly the right clothing requires a sense of fashion and the market,” Pot explains. "You have to know which fashion is still current and be strict about quality. That is our strength, but monitoring this uniformity is also a challenge. It requires instinctive sense and fashion and price knowledge from our franchisees and store employees. At the moment, all stores still do the selection themselves."

That is why Appel & Ei will focus on centralization and economies of scale in the coming years. “We will soon start centralizing everything in one warehouse at a central location,” says Pot. “Consumers bring their clothes to the stores, we pick everything up there, sort and price the items in one central location and then distribute them again to the stores.”

That sounds simpler than it is. Pot: "We work on consignment with our contributors. If an item is not sold, it often has to be returned to the owner. That makes it complex. Take the cash register system. We now have a basic system, but it is not sufficient for the rollout we have in mind. Many suppliers of cash register systems drop out as soon as they hear how our model works. You have to integrate everything: customer data, the consignment agreements and the settlement of that 40 percent."

> “We will soon start centralizing everything in one warehouse at a central location.”

Martin Pot

Director Appel & Ei

## Sort smarter with AI

According to Pot, technology is also essential to scale up. That is why Appel & Ei is working with partners on an AI tool that can recognize and classify clothing by brand, type and value. Pot: “That helps us to make selection and pricing more consistent centrally and to guarantee quality.”

In addition, the coordination of supply and demand is an important point of attention. “We can be offered more clothing than we can sell,” Pot explains. "But how do you get the right item, at the right time, in the right store? That's what it's all about. That's why our stores only accept fall clothing in the fall. We try to convey that well to our contributors."

But what if it also includes beautiful summer pieces? “We don't accept them now, because our stores are too small to store them,” Pot answers. “If we start working centrally, I see an opportunity to accept these items and put them back in stores at the right time.”

## Franchise or own stores?

How does the role of franchise fit within Appel & Ei's growth ambitions? Pot: "We have mainly grown through franchisees in recent years, but have now put a stop to new applications. The focus is on strengthening the foundation. Only when the central processes are in place will we consider in what form we will continue to grow."

## Not 100 percent sustainable

Although second-hand is obviously sustainable, Pot emphasizes that Appel & Ei does not profile itself as a sustainable retailer. *“*Sustainability makes sense to us, but you also have to be honest: we are not 100 percent sustainable. Because our operation also emits C0₂. Being successful in second-hand means that sustainability must be in balance with quality and commerce. Only then will you have a future. I truly believe that second-hand will acquire a definitive and significant place in the retail landscape. But if you want to survive, all these things have to be in order.”

## Proud of the nomination

This approach earned the chain a nomination for the ABN AMRO Sustainable Retailer of the Year 2025-2026. “We could hardly believe it,” says Pot. “We are relatively small, but our concept stands out and is appreciated. That makes us proud and gives us energy to continue growing.”
