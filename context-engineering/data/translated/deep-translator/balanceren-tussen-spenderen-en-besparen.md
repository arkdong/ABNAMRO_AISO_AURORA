# Balancing between spending and saving

[Retail](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/retail/index.html)

05/09/2025 • 4 minutes reading

[Henk Hofstede](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/authors/henk-hofstede/index.html)

[Ward van der Stee](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/authors/ward-van-der-stee/index.html)

Today's consumer is no longer predictable. Where there used to be clearer boundaries between bargain hunters and luxury lovers, in 2025 we will see the emergence of a consumer who switches effortlessly between these worlds. Sometimes he specifically searches for the lowest price, but a day later he chooses an A-brand or designer product with just as much conviction.

This 'hybrid consumer' is not consistent - he is smart, strategic and aware of the situation and looks for a balance between price, quality, brand and convenience. In this report, we use recent research to explore the preferences of this hybrid consumer, what drives him in different situations and in various product categories, and how factors such as age and income influence this behavior. Based on the research data, we try to make the profile of this consumer tangible so that retailers can respond to this.

## Loyalty is under pressure

It is important for retailers to take these insights into purchasing behavior into account when developing promotions, range and customer approach. Loyalty is under pressure due to the hybrid consumer. The hybrid consumer is not necessarily someone from a higher income group with many options, but someone who uses his or her budget strategically. This strategic commitment may differ per product category and life phase. The research was conducted in consultation with Q&A Retail among more than 2,100 respondents, divided into five age categories: ranging from 18 to 26 years to 70 to 82 years. A distinction is made between five product categories:   
 Supermarket products, Clothing and shoes, Cosmetics and beauty, Living and interior and Electronics.

The research results show that 51 percent of the households that wanted to share this information have a gross annual income between 42,000 and 84,000 euros. That seems sufficient to be able to make choices, but not generous enough to always opt for luxury. In addition, 32 percent have an average income of 42,000 euros or less and approximately 17 percent have more than twice the average income. Almost one in ten households have a state pension, a pension, a fixed income or no fixed income and are divided into the three income categories according to income. It is striking that almost one in five respondents does not want to share the amount of their income, a possible sign that purchasing power and financial status are still sensitive issues.

## Price and quality are most important

Price and quality are the aspects that consumers find most important when purchasing a product. Women are slightly more likely to be price-conscious, while men are more likely to opt for quality or brand in some categories. But other aspects also play a role in a consumer's choice per product category. The retailer must also pay attention to this. The hybrid consumer is price-conscious, but does not always go for the lowest price or discount.

Consumers have a pragmatic and goal-oriented approach to shopping. For example, 40 percent say they are willing to pay more for some products and specific features, but not for others. In all product groups examined, it appears that the balance between price and quality clearly dominates for a consumer. Consumers are also flexible and context-driven in their choices: they alternate cheap brands or private labels with expensive and well-known brands from the same category, depending on what they find important at that moment. Switching between cheap private labels and expensive well-known brands is done by half of consumers.

Take a family with an average income as an example. They choose to do their weekly shopping at a discount store to save money, but for special occasions this family chooses premium products at the supermarket or luxury specialty store. This change occurs in almost all product groups. This flexibility characterizes the hybrid consumer: they switch when a more attractive purchase presents itself.

Of the examined types of stores and online shops that the retail landscape offers, namely discounters, mid-range, more expensive/luxury and second-hand stores, it is the mid-range stores that are visited most often by respondents. This also reflects the desire for good value for money. The relevance of discounters is much lower in all product categories.

The research also shows that consumers who have a limited budget are most likely to opt for the lowest price. Offers and private labels remain good options to save money. You can also save money by purchasing another similar product. A large majority say they have never purchased a so-called 'dupe product', a legal but very similar variant. Consumers who do buy these products are relatively young. Of those respondents, 85 say they have never purchased purely counterfeit products. Here too, it is the young people who sometimes do this.

Price and quality remain the most important for the hybrid consumer, while retailers can at the same time respond well to specific preferences within a certain product category. Ensuring trust through brand experience, service and clear conditions are building blocks for this. Make hybrid purchasing behavior easier by properly connecting offline and online channels. Actively using customer reviews and influencers strengthens credibility.
