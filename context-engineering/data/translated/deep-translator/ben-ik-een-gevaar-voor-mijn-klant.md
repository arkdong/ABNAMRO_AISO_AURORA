# Am I a danger to my customer?

[Column](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/column.html)

[Food](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/food/index.html)

05/06/2025 • 3 minutes reading

[Rob Morren](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/authors/rob-morren/index.html)

In today's digital world, cybersecurity is not a luxury, but a necessity. Three in ten large companies were damaged by cybercrime last year. And there are plenty of examples of how this can affect our food supply: from empty shelves of cheese in the supermarket to malfunctioning beer kettles.

> “Supply reliability is an important competency for food manufacturers.”

Rob Morren

Sector Banker Food

## A crucial sector

Food manufacturers belong to a crucial sector for the Netherlands and must therefore prepare for the new NIS2 legislation. These regulations oblige large companies to have their cybersecurity in order and to proactively communicate about possible shortcomings. Companies that do not directly fall under these regulations due to their more limited size are also affected. Namely when their customers, such as supermarkets, demand that cyber resilience is in order to, for example, exclude the risk of delayed delivery. Ensuring supply reliability is an important competency for food manufacturers in times of geopolitical tensions and climate change.

## Food safety puts food manufacturers ahead

The food sector faces the tough challenge of properly securing production processes and data in the chain. And that spans many links of suppliers. Currently, the flows of goods between many food companies are already tightly regulated. Mutual protocols and data systems quickly provide insight into incidents that could affect food safety. So there is already some kind of structure in place to arrange cybersecurity over this. However, NIS2 goes a lot further because it also requires control over the risks that service providers and chain partners pose: from the website builder to the company that maintains machines and installs software, everything must be mapped out. After all, accidents can happen throughout the entire ecosystem surrounding a company and spread like wildfire.

## Culture and commerce

The NIS2 obligation is an important starting point for identifying risks and taking measures. How far companies should go in this regard is primarily common sense. My colleague cybersecurity expert Richard Verbrugge puts this nicely: “Ask yourself: 'Am I a potential danger to my customers?'” In other words: if my company is hacked, will my customers also suffer? This question must be central to the approach to cybersecurity within a company.

And that also touches on an organizational and cultural aspect. Cybersecurity is not just something for the IT department, but affects all employees. From the administrative employee who opens a dangerous attachment without thinking, to the technical service who installs new equipment without changing the default password.

Properly arranging cybersecurity remains a human job and requires thorough planning, testing and creating backup scenarios. Is it just doom and gloom? No. Properly arranging cybersecurity must also appeal to colleagues from the commercial sector. Because what could be better than emphasizing your own delivery reliability during a commercial negotiation? This is of vital importance for the supermarket and makes the endless discussion about the lowest price a little less sharp.
