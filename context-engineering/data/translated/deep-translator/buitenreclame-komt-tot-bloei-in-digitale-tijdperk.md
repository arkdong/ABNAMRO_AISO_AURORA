# Outdoor advertising is flourishing in the digital age

[Analysis](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/analyses/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

12/18/2025 • 7 minutes reading

[Mario Bersem](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/authors/mario-bersem/index.html)

Digital outdoor advertising is making remarkable progress. New screens are popping up in shopping centers, bus shelters and train stations. Analog outdoor advertising also remains popular due to mass reach. Turnover from outdoor advertising will grow by a total of 5 percent in 2025 and 6 percent in 2026, ABN AMRO expects. Until 2030, outdoor advertising turnover is expected to grow by an average of 5 percent per year and will overtake radio as an advertising channel.

The Netherlands has 83,000 objects for outdoor advertising, such as bus shelters, highway masts, transformer houses, scaffolding cloths, blank walls, billboards, information columns and lampposts. Even buses, trams and ferries are used as advertising platforms. The number of objects is growing due to the addition of new digital screens. At the same time, the yield per object increases due to the digitization of existing objects.

The most common format is the bus shelter format (1185 mm x 1750 mm, approximately 2 m²), accounting for 65 percent of all advertising media, found in bus shelters and information kiosks. The A0 format (841 mm x 1189 mm) occupies 32 percent and can be found on triangular signs and electrical boxes. Larger objects are rare and vary in size; only 1300 objects are larger than the bus shelter size. These are located at locations with a large reach, such as the LED facade of the Ziggodome in Amsterdam (23 meters high and 400 meters long) or the NEO screen at Hoog Catharijne in Utrecht (193 m²).

## Digital outdoor advertising is growing explosively and offers new possibilities

In three years, digital outdoor advertising turnover has more than doubled to 172 million euros in 2024, according to the Digital Ad Spend 2024 report from VIA/Deloitte. In 2025, turnover will grow by 4 percent and reach approximately 179 million euros, ABN AMRO expects. That is 56 percent of the total outdoor advertising turnover in 2025, which is expected to amount to 317 million euros. This is striking, given that only 17 percent of all advertising objects are digital; the remaining 83 percent is analog. Digital screens generate more turnover than analog ones because they are larger on average, are located in the busiest places and generate more attention.

Digital screens attract more attention thanks to moving images and changing advertisements. They are also larger and are located in busy places in large cities and along busy roads, where many passers-by ensure a high range. Advertising objects at these locations were the first to be digitized to recoup the high investment costs. Analog objects are digitized when the range is large enough to justify the costs. The new BRO Next research is the new standard for measuring reach.

The majority of new digital outdoor advertising screens are located indoors, in store windows. As long as the screens are visible from the street or public space, they count towards the outdoor advertising stock. This phenomenon is part of the [rise of retail media](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/artikelen-lucratie-bverdienste-voor-winkelketens.html), whereby large grocers and other retailers focus on advertisements as a lucrative additional income. For example, HEMA has almost 330 digital screens with various campaigns that generate money in the evening, when the stores are closed. Global, a major outdoor advertising operator, handles advertising sales for HEMA.

For advertisers, digital screens offer more flexibility. Campaigns can be tailored to circumstances, such as advertising ice cream on hot days. Advertisers can also better reach specific target groups, such as young people who see advertisements for matcha tea at a train station on their way to school. About 30 percent of digital advertising space is placed automatically using algorithms that take into account factors such as time of day, location and weather conditions. This percentage is increasing and indicates a more efficient and flexible use of digital advertising space.

## Digital advertising dominates media landscape

Digital advertisements, especially those via web pages, social media, streaming services and the aforementioned digital screens, dominate the Dutch media landscape and accounted for 4.1 billion euros in 2024 – almost three quarters of all advertising expenditure. Large tech companies, with a market share of 80 percent (3.5 billion euros), dominate the digital advertising market. The digital transformation of the media is not yet complete and the share of digital advertising is still increasing.

While traditional mass media such as television, radio and newspapers are losing reach, outdoor advertising - also called 'out of home' - is gaining in importance. Johan Smit, director of PMA, a trade association for media agencies, explains: “Out of home is actually the only remaining mass medium and is therefore taking over the leading role from linear TV.”

Digital outdoor advertising differs from other digital media, such as online advertisements, because it is usually not experienced as disruptive. Ad blockers can be used online and some people may experience ad fatigue. “Outdoor advertising is not disruptive on the one hand and cannot be missed on the other,” says Sita Bakker, director of OUTREACH, the trade association for outdoor advertising operators. “It does not interrupt people during their activities, which contributes to a positive experience,” Bakker adds.

## Analog outdoor advertising also remains strong in the digital age

Analog outdoor advertising remains relevant and is even growing, with an expected turnover increase of 6 percent in 2025. This means that the turnover from analog outdoor advertising will amount to approximately 138 million euros, ABN AMRO expects. Although digitalization slightly reduces the number of analog objects, traditional forms such as paper posters and static billboards remain popular with advertisers due to their mass reach. New measurement methods from the BRO Next research also play a role in this (re)valuation, because they allow advertisers to estimate their reach more and more accurately. As the “only remaining mass medium”, outdoor advertising offers an alternative to advertising channels such as television that are seen as more expensive.

Outdoor advertising remains unaffected by the online competition affecting other media. When moving, for example to school or work, people come into contact with outdoor advertising. Bus shelters, highway masts, billboards and A0 frames reach 80 percent of the Dutch population every week. By comparison: linear television reaches 80 percent of the Dutch population in one month.

About 5 percent of all advertising expenditures are spent on outdoor advertising. This share varies between advertisers: McDonald's spends as much as 20 percent of its budget on outdoor advertising and WPP Media, the largest media agency, spends around 10 percent on outdoor advertising on behalf of advertisers. These examples further demonstrate the growth potential of outdoor advertising, which is also evident from experience in other countries. In a positive scenario, in which current trends continue strongly, the share of outdoor advertising could increase to 7.5 percent and turnover increase to 600 million euros in 2030. In the base scenario, however, the share of outdoor advertising remains stable at around the current 5 percent.

## Municipalities determine outdoor advertising policy

Outdoor advertising is aimed at passers-by, on foot or by bicycle, in the car or on public transport. Municipalities apply strict rules about where advertising is allowed and how, i.e. in what format and, for example, the brightness of digital screens. Some municipalities levy a municipal advertising tax. Because municipalities themselves are responsible for the design of public spaces, the rules vary widely: the Netherlands has 342 municipalities, each of which has its own policy regarding outdoor advertising. For example, the municipality of The Hague has banned 'fossil advertising'. The Hague prohibits advertising for air travel, petrol cars and fossil energy contracts through a general local ordinance (APV). A tender is underway in Haarlem where advertising for meat and fish is not permitted.

Municipalities grant multi-year concessions for outdoor advertising through public tenders. In return, they receive a financial contribution, often in combination with material compensation such as street furniture or media space for their own campaigns and charities. JCDecaux is an example of an operator that invests in bus shelters and maintains them in exchange for advertising. It is striking that at JCDecaux more employees are active in technical services than in advertising sales. A [PWC study](https://www.outsmart.org.uk/resources/ooh-supporting-uk-society) in the United Kingdom shows that 46 percent of advertising revenue from outdoor advertising goes back to municipalities. Applied to the Dutch market, this amounts to approximately 146 million euros in 2025.

Outdoor advertising is largely dependent on municipal policy and concessions, which vary widely between the 342 Dutch municipalities. While some municipalities impose specific restrictions, such as the ban on fossil advertising in The Hague, there is no national guideline. This leads to variation in rules and uncertainty for operators. Central rules on tobacco, alcohol and gambling advertising show that a uniform approach can offer advantages. Without such guidelines, individual municipalities may impose stricter restrictions, posing a risk to the outdoor advertising industry.

The Dutch outdoor advertising market is relatively fragmented with 52 operators compared to neighboring countries such as Belgium and Germany, where only two major players are active in addition to a few specialists with their own niche. The acquisition of the Dutch part of Clear Channel by Bauer Media Group in April 2025 is an example of the consolidation that will continue. Internationally, the Netherlands still lags behind countries such as the United States and China, especially in the field of digital outdoor advertising and retail media. Based on international trends and the current market structure, ABN AMRO expects further consolidation and digital growth.

## Rise of the bus shelter

In 1964, Jean Claude Decaux introduced his first bus shelters in Lyon. In exchange for financing, installation and maintenance, his company, JCDecaux, received the exploitation rights for advertising in this 'abribus' (French for bus shelter). This model of public-private partnership spread worldwide, and the term 'bus shelter' became synonymous with a commonly used advertising size: 1185 mm x 1750 mm, approximately 2m². Bus shelter advertising represents 65 percent of all outdoor advertising in the Netherlands and can be found in bus shelters throughout the country, stations, shopping centers and along busy streets.
