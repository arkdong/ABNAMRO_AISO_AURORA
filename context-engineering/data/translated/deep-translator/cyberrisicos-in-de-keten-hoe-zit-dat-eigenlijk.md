ABN AMRO Editorial

The NIS2 Cybersecurity Act is coming and goes further than you think. The new European NIS2 directive is a fundamental shift in how organizations should approach risks. Not only digital risks such as hacking or data leaks, but also risks that could endanger the continuity of an essential or important entity. This is called the 'all hazards approach'. This means: IT, OT, IoT and physical risks in digital environments.

We're going to unravel it here. In understandable language. With clear examples. So that as an organization, as a supplier or as a CISO, you know what to do.

## IT risks - Information Technology

The classics, think of:

- [Ransomware](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/cybersecurity/wat-is-ransomware.html): systems are encrypted, the operation is paralyzed.
- Data leaks: customer data or medical records are stolen or leaked.
- Software that is not maintained: updates are not performed and older software is very vulnerable.
- Insecure API connections: for example in companies that use EDI (Electronic Data Interchange) to digitally exchange orders, invoices or stock information. These are often not IT companies, but ordinary logistics service providers, raw material suppliers or suppliers.

## OT risks - Operational Technology

Operational technology controls our machines, factories and infrastructures. Consider:

- Production lines that are digitally controlled: if someone gains access to the controls, production can come to a standstill or go wrong.
- Energy or water management: attacks on these systems can disrupt entire regions.
- Smart machines in the food industry: a hacked packaging line can endanger food safety.

## IoT risks - Internet of Things

Security cameras, sensors or smart thermostats connected to the internet: if these are unsafe, they can provide an entry point for cyber criminals.

Consider:

- Smart cooling systems in the food chain or sensors in containers. Often difficult to patch or update ŌĆō and therefore a weak spot.

## Physical risks in digital environments

This is the most underestimated category. But it is stated in black and white in the European NIS2 text: physical access to systems is also part of the risk inventory.

Examples:

- A USB stick is found in the company parking lot. An employee picks it up, puts it in a system ŌĆō and bam: malware inside.
- Suppliers who come to site: mechanics, technicians, maintenance teams. If they gain access to places where IT, OT or IoT systems are located, they pose a risk. Think of machines in a production hall. They all have a USB port for maintenance. It is often not secured. An unauthorized person can insert a USB stick there and your system is corrupted. There are examples of Russian hackers paying ŌĆ£locals with accessŌĆØ for this.
- Poor access control: if anyone can just access a server cabinet or industrial controller, there is no cyber security.

## Own built applications and apps

There are SME companies that create their own software. That is very common nowadays. For example, an installation company with its own maintenance software that they also sell to customers. With the advent of AI, anyone can build their own app. This happens so often that the IT department itself has little or no insight into it. Builders often have no idea about security. A whole new area of ŌĆŗŌĆŗinsecurity opens up here.

## Risks don't stop at the walls of your company

Software systems and access often extend far beyond the walls of your own company. The NIS2 guideline requires a broad view. You must identify risks, not only based on company type, but based on what a company does within your chain. As the National Cyber ŌĆŗŌĆŗSecurity Center (NCSC) also states in measure 7 about chain dependency: 'Identify risks in the chain and take measures that suit your chain'. It takes some getting used to for CISOs, lawyers and companies that already have ISO27001.

The NIS2 guideline is a wake-up call. Unfortunately, you cannot cover everything with policy and documentation. The reality is more complex: suppliers have access, systems are interconnected, physical and digital worlds are intertwined. You can no longer deny these risks.┬ĀCertainly not in sectors with essential NIS2 entities such as hospitals, water companies, energy supply or vital infrastructure. Everything has to keep running there.

## NIS2 = all hazards

NIS2's '[all hazards](https://samendigitaalveilig.nl/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/All-hazards-examples.jpg)' approach makes sense. And necessary. Not every risk is the standard hacker you expect. Sometimes it is a forgotten USB stick, a subcontractor with outdated software or a refrigerated truck with a poorly secured IoT sensor.

If you want to be demonstrably safe, you must:

- Understand where you are vulnerable ŌĆō digitally, operationally and physically.
- Knowing who your partners and suppliers are ŌĆō and how safely they work.
- Manage the risks, appropriate for your chain, industry and role.

NIS2 is all-hazards and that is not a threat, but an opportunity to really make your entire chain safer. Inform direct suppliers that they also have to get started. Most efforts are primarily aimed at securing one's own company. Give them a heads up in advance to implement the NIS2 Cybersecurity Act.
