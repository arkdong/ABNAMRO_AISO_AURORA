# The regional accountant: the silent director of the takeover

The total number of mergers and acquisitions in small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) doubled between 2007 and 2024. This is shown in the graph below, where SMEs include companies with a maximum of fifty employees.

It is notable that the sharp decline in gross domestic product (GDP) of approximately 3.8 percent during the 2009 credit crisis did not lead to a decrease in the number of transactions during those years. In fact, a rapid recovery followed that crisis, after which the mergers and acquisitions market again remained relatively stable during the euro crisis of 2011-2013. However, this was different during the corona crisis: the overheated market suffered a serious blow, but is now recovering. This shows that acquirers usually work from a long-term vision and do not always care much about temporary crises.

There have been relatively large differences in the number of takeovers per sector. The energy sector and the catering industry stand out, with an annual average growth of 9.9 and 5.5 percent respectively, the so-called CAGR. By comparison, the annual average GDP growth rate over that period was 1.27 percent.

## Drivers for consolidation in SMEs

SME entrepreneurs may have various reasons for consolidating, which means: taking over, merging or selling the company. Scale plays an important role in a world in which compliance and cybersecurity are becoming increasingly important. It is becoming more difficult, especially for SMEs, to meet today's demands. Another key reason for consolidation is access to employees. The structural shortage of employees already imposes restrictions on further growth for many companies. An acquisition could then offer a solution.

> “A business takeover offers entrepreneurs an opportunity to grow or make their company future-proof.”

A third important reason for making acquisitions is access to competencies that a company does not have in-house. The rapid rise of artificial intelligence (AI) is a good example of this. Specialized AI companies are springing up like mushrooms and it is important to have expertise in-house, especially in knowledge-intensive sectors such as consultancy. A fourth, classic driver for consolidation is to quickly gain a foothold within a particular sector or abroad. Finally, an acquisition is often the only way to achieve growth. A number of large companies specialize in this and grow by 'stringing beads': a frequent stream of takeovers, often of SMEs.

## The accountant and the role of the bank in mergers and acquisitions

An acquisition is a rare and unique event and the moment that many entrepreneurs look forward to, and at the same time keep them awake at night. And in the wings of this complex transaction, especially for financing up to around 1 million euros, the regional accountant plays an invaluable role. Not in the leading role, but as the personal and reliable advisor and director of his customer who makes the connection between buyer and bank.

Why is the accountant so indispensable in this? Because he or she knows the story. The bank reads the figures, but the accountant knows the soul behind them. The accountant knows why the profit margin fell briefly in 2022, for example due to an investment in a new machine, knows the loyalty of the most important customer and understands the growth opportunities that are still untapped in the books. This is no longer accounting, this is translation.

Of course, the bank is also crucial, namely in financing takeovers. However, the bank does not finance history, but the future. And it is the accountant's job to make that future story as credible and watertight as possible, using the figures as a foundation. How does he do that?

First, preparation is everything. Long before the first banker sits down at the table, the accountant must prepare the books for takeover and have answers to all possible questions: have the repairs to the building been fully written off, are the facilities adequate and how stable are the customers with long-term contracts? This 'financial due diligence light' is essential so as not to lose time and not encounter unexpected surprises. It enables the entrepreneur to negotiate with confidence and transparency.

Secondly: drawing up the business case. A bank does not lend a million euros based on yesterday's profit and loss account. She wants to see a detailed forecast. How is the loan repaid? What cost savings or growth opportunities does the buyer see? The accountant is the perfect party to examine this forecast, substantiate it with historical data and make it credible. He knows the margins, cycles and risks of the sector. He transforms an optimistic plan into a solid, substantiated investment.

Third: the accountant as a matchmaker with the bank. The regional accountant knows not only his client, but often also the local bank managers and their appetite for risk. He knows which bank is sensitive to a strong cash flow, and which attaches more value to collateral. The accountant can bring the right parties together and ensure communication runs smoothly. He speaks both the language of the entrepreneur and the language of the financier, and can thus prevent misunderstandings.

Finally: the accountant as a watchdog. During negotiations, the accountant is the cool, analytical party who ensures that the entrepreneur's emotions do not get away with him. He monitors the financial health of the deal by determining, among other things, whether the repayment obligations are realistic even in bad years and by assessing whether there are sufficient free cash flows to continue investing.

For a regional accountant, this role is an opportunity to definitively prove his value as a strategic partner. It is a service that goes far beyond discussing the annual figures. It involves thinking along at the highest level, with the company's survival as a priority. It is living up to the trust.
