# The two faces of Agentic AI: weapon and shield

[Analysis](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/analyses/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

11/17/2025 • 11 minutes reading

[Amad Khan](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/authors/amad-khan/index.html)

Criminals are making greater use of artificial intelligence to carry out lightning-fast attacks. Companies simultaneously use the same technology to defend themselves. The urgency to act is growing, but many organizations do not know where to start within this complex arms race.

The world of cybersecurity is at a tipping point. Agentic AI – artificial intelligence in which systems operate autonomously and make decisions without human intervention – is fundamentally changing how both defenders and attackers operate. Unlike generative AI, which relies on predefined instructions, these “agents” can independently initiate, execute and improve actions based on what they learn.

Recent research from Palo Alto Networks, an American cybersecurity company, shows how dramatic this shift is. Researchers managed to simulate a full ransomware attack in just 25 minutes using Agentic AI – a hundred times faster than traditional methods. “The scenario they outline is disturbingly realistic,” says Matthijs Mons, cybersecurity specialist at ABN AMRO. “Criminals will develop agents specifically tailored for targeted attacks.”

## From science fiction to reality

The process that Palo Alto Networks describes illustrates the power of Agentic AI. Agents begin gathering public information about their targets through social media and corporate databases. Based on current data, they continuously adjust their strategy and try to gain access to networks through personalized phishing attacks, messages on LinkedIn or fake invitations to video conferences. Once inside, they analyze patterns to identify the most valuable systems and choose attack techniques that seamlessly match the vulnerabilities of their target. “For many people, this scenario sounds fictional, as if everything happens automatically,” says Mons. "But with traditional AI, at least an initial action had to be performed by a human. With Agentic AI, things now happen completely autonomously, without anyone being involved. And that applies to both sides."

The other side is the protective measures that organizations implement. Hundreds of cybersecurity specialists work at large financial institutions to protect vital infrastructure. There is a major staff shortage in this sector, and Agentic AI offers solutions here. “In the event of an incident, a security expert often has to look up information in different systems,” Mons explains. “That can take hours. An agent for security tasks can independently examine data from system reports and paint a complete picture based on one command, while an analyst has to follow an entire step-by-step plan.”

> “At Agentic AI, things now happen completely autonomously, without anyone being involved.”

Matthijs Mons

ABN AMRO

## Simulate an attack

The most promising applications can be found in simulating attacks on one's own organization, so-called 'red team' actions. Traditionally, security teams spend days or even weeks on this: mapping systems, finding access routes and testing different routes.

By learning from previous simulated attacks, Agentic AI can penetrate much faster and map out which attack sequence is most effective – exactly what criminals will do. There are already companies that offer this as a service. They use simulations for their customers and learn from both the attacking 'red teams' and the defensive 'blue teams'. This combined approach, also called 'purple AI', enables organizations to learn from both perspectives and continuously improve their security. It is important to note that Agentic AI uses existing methods. It executes attacks faster than ever, but it doesn't invent new methods. To date, Agentic AI has mainly been a leap in efficiency.  “The major cybersecurity companies are investing heavily in AI,” says Mons. "But criminals are at it just the same. The romantic image of hackers as lonely boys in basements is completely outdated. Cyber ​​attacks are carried out by professional organizations with specific roles that focus on gaining access, building malware, installing malware, stealing data, negotiations, etc. They even have HR departments and call centers, with a solid business case based on the benefits they generate from stolen data."

## The paradox of adoption

The use of Agentic AI within organizations – for both security and regular business processes – also creates new vulnerabilities. For example, hackers can try to manipulate the AI ​​agents that already have access to systems.

This leads to a striking paradox, revealed by a survey by SailPoint (an American technology company focused on identity and access management) of 353 cybersecurity professionals worldwide. While 96 percent of respondents view agents as an increasing security threat, a whopping 98 percent of organizations plan to expand their use of Agentic AI in the coming year. The research shows that 82 percent of companies already use agents, with more than half reporting that they access sensitive data on a daily basis. Despite this, only 44 percent have policies in place to protect these agents from external threats. Perhaps most alarming, nearly one in four companies reported that agents can be manipulated into giving away access credentials.

“The use of this technology is a risk in itself,” warn Hans van Haarlem and Steven Ottenhoff of MMOX, a Dutch cybersecurity consultancy. They point out a crucial point that many entrepreneurs overlook. "In the past, attacking large companies took an enormous amount of time and resources. That threshold has now been drastically lowered. Criminals can now work much more efficiently, making medium-sized and smaller organizations attractive targets."

> “Organize a digital fire drill regularly, so that everyone is prepared and knows what his or her role is in a crisis.”

Hans van Haarlem

MMOX

## Volume and quality increase

The lower attack threshold is immediately visible in practice, with attackers expanding their working area. “We receive tons of phishing emails to analyze,” says Van Haarlem. "What is striking is that both the volume and the quality are increasing. Spear phishing - targeted attacks on specific people - is being carried out much better. We regularly see that new employees receive text messages from 'our' CEO in no time with requests to transfer money or to contact us. On their first working day. It is almost inevitable that this will be done automatically in the background."

The number of cyber attacks has not yet exploded, and there is an explanation for this. “Agentic AI systems are still super expensive,” explains ABN AMRO's cybersecurity specialist. "They require a lot of computing power and training models costs money. Criminals therefore continue to focus on relatively easy targets. Hacking a large bank can take years, but a small accounting firm can be cracked within a week with a significant loot. Generative AI has drastically increased the number of phishing emails. That is cheap and easy. Full Agentic AI attacks, in which systems independently screen social media and carry out spear phishing, are certainly coming, but are not yet massive."

## Internal risks to security

In addition to external threats, agents, which are used for both security and other business processes, pose specific security risks within organizations. “If such an agent is poorly set up or is insufficiently monitored, situations can arise that you do not want,” Mons warns. "Suppose an agent detects that you have clicked on a phishing email. The normal procedure is to isolate that computer from the network so that it cannot cause any damage. But if that agent accidentally isolates all computers in your network instead of one, no one can work anymore."

There is another more fundamental problem. Agentic AI can act on the basis of data and previous experiences without human control, but it can also 'hallucinate': draw wrong conclusions or perform unexpected actions. “You don't know exactly what it has done,” Mons explains. “It learns and acts on its own. If things go wrong, you have to find out what happened and what you need to repair. That is why it is crucial to build in good control mechanisms and keep options open that allow people to intervene themselves.”

The experts at MMOX qualify this image. "Agentic AI does not experience stress or workload that makes someone sloppy. It can do something wrong with good intentions because the underlying model is inadequate, but the margin of error is often smaller than with humans."

## What can companies do?

The perspective for action for organizations is clear but comprehensive. “Make sure you are better protected than the neighbor,” says Mons. "On a street, the most poorly secured house is broken into. This basic principle also applies in the digital world. It is not about absolute security, but about relative resilience." Concrete measures start with fundamental matters: good equipment security and two-factor authentication. But the latter is not waterproof. “There are scenarios available in which this double check can still be circumvented. It is a constant cat-and-mouse game between security makers and malicious parties.”

Van Haarlem therefore advocates a strategic approach that goes beyond just technology. “AI does not change the methodology of cybercriminals, it only changes the efficiency and therefore the scope,” he says. “We are supporters of the NIST framework: a widely recognized set of guidelines that helps organizations manage and reduce cybersecurity risks. Invest in employees and technology, in network segmentation and strong data analysis.” Impact mitigation measures are a crucial but underestimated element. “Instead of just focusing on keeping hackers out, you should ensure that they cannot do any damage once they get in,” Van Haarlem and Ottenhoff emphasize. "Segment your network so that an attacker cannot move freely throughout your systems. Try to build a high wall, but realize that if someone climbs over it, there will have to be even more barriers inside."

> “The threshold for cyber attacks is lower than ever. Companies that do not act now run major risks.”

Steven Ottenhoff

MMOX

## The human factor

The role of employees remains complex. While some see people as the weakest link, Mons from ABN AMRO thinks differently. "We view people as the last resort, the strongest link. Technology may have failed, but an alert employee can still stop an attack. That is why we promote safe behavior throughout the organization." Van Haarlem and Ottenhoff discuss this aspect in more detail. “Changing human behavior is extremely difficult, especially if the digital skills of the current population lag behind and employees are not intrinsically motivated to work safely.”

The experts at MMOX distinguish three essential factors. First, organizations must establish clear guidelines for secure behavior and provide easy-to-use security tools. They warn: “Many tools die in beauty because adoption lags.” Secondly, the urgency of cyber risks must be continuously emphasized. "This is not something for a single training course, but must be institutionalized. Discuss it monthly or quarterly during meetings, repetition is essential." Third, organizations must understand the work environment. "Identify which systems you have. Listen carefully to your employees. If they download software or extensions themselves because they are missing something in the official systems, you must take this seriously. By understanding their needs and meeting them, you prevent them from looking for unsafe solutions. Make the topic a topic of discussion."

## Resilience instead of perfection

The central message from all experts is clear: there is no such thing as perfect security. “The question is not if you will be hacked, but when,” say the experts at MMOX. “The costs of attacks have become so low that every company is a potential target. We therefore advise customers: be prepared. Develop resilience.” In concrete terms, this means: ensure backups that actually work, develop an incident response system - a structured step-by-step plan to respond quickly and effectively to a cyber incident - and regularly practice with scenarios. “Simulate that you have been hacked,” they advise. "What are you going to do? How are you communicating? What if your data becomes public? Regularly organize a digital fire drill, so that everyone is prepared and knows what their role is in a crisis."

The challenges for small and medium-sized enterprises are different than for large organizations. “The blue team agents and advanced systems that are now available require so much investment that they are not relevant to most entrepreneurs.” Entrepreneurs often don't know where to start. That is why we focus on helping to lay a solid foundation: getting basic measures in order so that you are better protected than your neighbor.”

## Opportunities and threats

Despite all the risks, Agentic AI also offers substantial opportunities. Cybersecurity specialist Mons from ABN AMRO underlines this. “Rescue team actions that previously took weeks can now be done in days. Analyzes that took hours are done in minutes. With Agentic AI we can test ourselves better, find vulnerabilities faster and respond to incidents more effectively," he says. But both parties also emphasize the need for caution. Ottenhoff: "Agentic AI is not necessarily transparent. Agentic systems can do something wrong with good intentions. Sometimes a simple, predictable system is a better choice than complex AI.”

## The urgency is growing

In the future, cyber attacks will occur faster, on a larger scale and more under the radar. This requires security systems that continuously adapt. Old systems that rely on fixed checks and slow updates are falling short. A new approach is needed that continuously monitors for vulnerabilities in the cloud and other digital systems, and where simulating realistic threats helps identify and remediate weaknesses. Within organizations, all Agentic AI systems – whether used for security or other business processes – should not be viewed as regular system components, but as separate entities with their own security needs. This requires a strategy that identifies, monitors and controls all agents. This allows companies to not only comply with regulations, but also strengthen their overall security position.

At the same time, agents deployed by malicious parties pose a growing external threat. The rise of Agentic AI does not introduce completely new attack methods, but makes existing techniques faster and more effective. The speed and adaptability of these systems pose a major challenge. Companies must therefore proactively innovate and anticipate agent-driven attacks.

“It is very difficult to get cybersecurity from 'important but not urgent' to 'urgent',” Ottenhoff notes. "But with the rise of Agentic AI, that urgency is real. The industry behind cybercrime has matured, with solid business cases. The threshold for attack is lower than ever. Companies that do not act now run major risks."

The two faces of Agentic AI – as a weapon and as a shield – force organizations to reconsider. The technology offers unprecedented opportunities for efficient security and scaling cybersecurity teams. But that same power also lies in the hands of criminals who operate in an increasingly professional manner. The challenge is clear: companies must invest now in fundamental security, engage and prepare their employees, and realize that absolute security does not exist. Resilience and preparation are the new motto in an era where the question is not if, but when you will be attacked.
