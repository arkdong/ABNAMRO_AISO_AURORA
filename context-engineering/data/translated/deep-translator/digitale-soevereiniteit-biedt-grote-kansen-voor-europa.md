# Digital sovereignty offers great opportunities for Europe

[Interview](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/interviews/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

02/12/2025 • 8 minutes reading

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/authors/julia-krauwer/index.html)

Is Europe being crushed between the US and China in technology? Are we losing our digital control and our role in the tech economy? Far from that, predicts ABN AMRO sector specialist Julia Krauwer. She sees plenty of opportunities in a Europe that is digitally awakening. 'A lot of good things are already happening, but the internal barriers must be removed. And we must say our ambitions out loud!'

When it came to technology, Europe has always followed big brother America. Software, servers, cloud storage: all our data and systems were trusted, but completely dependent. Initially that was fine, until geopolitical relations drastically changed and the rise of AI put almost all sections of our business community and society in question. 'The rapid breakthrough of AI is transforming business models. This affects every company and certainly the companies in tech, media and telecom,' says Julia Krauwer, sector specialist in that area at ABN AMRO. 'The question is not whether you change, but whether you as an entrepreneur can keep up with the pace.'

## Dependence makes you vulnerable

The European tech sector has strong growth figures, but also major uncertainties. Behind digital progress lies an enormous dependency, which is little visible to end users. Almost all servers, chips and software that power companies, industries and even societies come from the US or Asia. 'If that supply stops, it will affect our entire digital economy. The war in Ukraine, the recent developments with Nexperia and the ongoing tensions between China and Taiwan have accelerated that awareness.'

This dependence is reflected in the figures from Synergy Research. Although the European cloud market grew five times larger between 2017 and 2022, the combined market share of European providers nevertheless fell: from 27 to just 13%. Cause: Amazon, Microsoft and Google grew much faster. They have also invested billions in data centers on European soil in recent years. “So while we ourselves are growing, the outside world is growing even more,” Krauwer summarizes the urgency.

> “The future lies with companies that deliver technology that is difficult to reproduce and that integrate smartly into other people's ecosystems.”

Julia Krauwer

Sector Banker Technology, Media & Telecom

## AI as a crowbar and a threat

According to Krauwer, artificial intelligence is the most important driving force and the biggest disruptor of the moment. “AI changes everything: from the way we develop software to the way we advertise and sell products.”

She outlines a double movement: on the one hand, AI creates an explosion in productivity. IT service providers help customers with automation, prediction and customization, which opens up new markets. On the other hand, artificial intelligence threatens to undermine parts of the software industry itself. 'Software companies are in a crazy dilemma: AI can make your product smarter, but it can also make it redundant. The winners have a strong core, unique data or deep domain knowledge. Companies that base their value on unique algorithms or datasets are better protected against generative AI that can easily copy standard functions.

According to Krauwer, a new distinction is emerging, where it is not just about scale, but especially whether you offer a very specific and ingenious solution. 'Small tech companies can also scale up very quickly; just look at the Swedish Lovable. But anyone who only provides a nice interface runs the risk that an AI agent will take over tomorrow. The future lies with companies that deliver technology that is difficult to reproduce and that can be smartly integrated into other people's ecosystems.'

## From risk to opportunity

While dependence on foreign players used to be mainly considered a risk, Krauwer now also sees an opportunity. 'Due to the changing relationship between America and Europe, we in Europe are strongly aware of the importance of cloud sovereignty. As a European company, you want to know more than ever where your data is, who can access it and who operates the main switch. Companies that demonstrably consciously deal with privacy and transparency, with data sovereignty and thus with the continuity of their services, have an important USP: your data is safely stored in Europe, under European laws and regulations, and therefore less dependent on geopolitical unrest.'

## European Google Maps alternative chooses its own course

Amsterdam-based Magic Lane shows that digital sovereignty and commercial success can go together with their white-label Location Intelligence Technology: technology with which other platforms can easily integrate navigation or shared mobility solutions, from electric bicycles to shared cars, and with smart functionalities such as offline navigation and live traffic data. CEO Raymond Alves: 'And the technology guarantees privacy: unlike American providers, users are not tracked; all data remains anonymous.' The fact that this approach is paying off is also evident from the figures: in the spring, the number of users of the consumer app increased by 250% in six weeks. “This shows that privacy and performance do not have to be mutually exclusive.”

## European sovereignty and privacy in practice

This creates opportunities for European entrepreneurs who implement these local values well: privacy-friendly, transparent and sustainable. “Being compliant turns out to be quite innovative,” Krauwer quotes a quote from Saskia Lensink, product manager of GPT-NL. 'Companies that know how to translate European values ​​into technology with scale can make a real difference. 'Look at Deeploy, a Utrecht-based platform for AI monitoring, AI governance and AI compliance, which helps financial companies and large healthcare institutions, among others, to monitor the responsible application of AI in their organizations. Or Mistral AI, the French platform for artificial intelligence, which has the potential to become a serious challenger for the major AI developers.'

> “We are great at R&D in the Netherlands and Europe, but we find it exciting to say that we are going to conquer the world.”

Julia Krauwer

Sector Banker Technology, Media & Telecom

## Culture and capital

There are plenty of great initiatives, but Europe is still far from being there. Krauwer: 'We are great at R&D in the Netherlands and Europe, but we find it exciting to say that we are going to conquer the world.' That modesty does not work in our favor. Certainly not when it comes to raising sufficient capital, which is simply indispensable to bring good ideas to fruition. Tech companies in Europe have significantly more difficulty finding major investments than their counterparts in America. According to the State of European Tech 2024 report, the cumulative difference has now risen to $375 billion. “In the US, start-ups are twice as likely to raise more than $15 million,” says Krauwer. 'That's a huge difference. And ultimately, capital determines whether you can scale up.' Fortunately, Krauwer also sees the beginning of expansion there, although cautiously. “Investors seek each other out more often,” she notes. 'Brussels is also working on a so-called 28th regime to simplify investing and scaling up across borders. With this, Europe is developing a single European framework for corporate law, bankruptcy law, labor law and tax law, to put an end to the current fragmentation of 27 countries, each with their own rules.'

## Cyber resilience as a maturity test

In addition to economic uncertainty, the security risk is growing. Tech companies are a popular target for hackers and espionage groups, especially since the digital infrastructure has become part of geopolitical power. “As a tech company, you have to assume that you will be hacked at some point,” says Krauwer. “The question is how you deal with it.” According to her, cyber resilience is part of strategic maturity. 'It is not only about measures to keep attackers out of your systems, but also about communication and recovery capacity if you are unexpectedly hacked. Being resilient means that you can get up again quickly. Companies that anchor safety and transparency in their brand gain trust from customers and investors.'

## Fronts of growth

Krauwer sees how European tech companies can grow on several fronts, provided they show courage. 'We should not only want to be less dependent on Big Tech, we should above all want to become stronger ourselves.' The first layer of growth lies in AI integration: companies that cleverly integrate artificial intelligence into their products or processes. 'You see software companies that smartly integrate AI into their products. And thereby develop specialist and preferably very intelligent, domain-specific applications that are preferably highly scalable, but not easy to imitate by others.'

A second growth layer is the technology infrastructure, from chips to data centers. Krauwer: 'The demand for computing power is growing faster than the supply of data centers, while energy and grid capacity are also setting limits. That need has already led to great innovations. Such as Axelera AI, a Dutch chip start-up that develops energy-efficient and powerful AI processing units. Or Asperitas, which has developed a cooling system for data centers in which servers are immersed in a coolant, making them more compact, energy efficient and more sustainable.'

## Building blocks for developers

With Magic Lane's modular architecture, developers can now build a solution even faster with the support of AI. 'After all, we do not supply one app, but separate building blocks that can be used by developers. In addition, our software development kit can even be called by AI development tools. We consciously position ourselves at the back of an application," says Alves. 'That is where the strength lies: difficult to replicate technology, built on European values. With this we show that this type of technology - just like Signal and Protonmail, which have an alternative to a messenger and email application respectively - is not idealism, but a realistic business model.'

> “We offer technology that is difficult to replicate, built on European values.”

Raymond Alves

CEO Magic Lane

The third layer of growth lies in the focus on European values. Providers of innovative products that are not only smart but also privacy-friendly, respect copyrights, are energy efficient, run on local servers and work without bias, gain trust within and outside Europe. Krauwer mentions the Dutch Ab Ovo as an example. 'They have integrated AI into both the development process and into their actual software, and also focus on developing software that works more flexibly and sustainably. That is exactly the step that more companies need to take.'

## Talent and ecosystems

In addition, the success of the sector depends on people. “Talent moves to places where ambition is palpable,” says Krauwer. 'Universities, start-ups, investors: you need concentration. We need to create more breeding grounds where science and business meet. Only then can you retain the talent that is now moving abroad.' She points to, among others, TU Delft and TU Eindhoven, which are now helping to scale up dozens of spin-offs. 'These are hopeful signals. But it must be faster, more ambitious and more international – with more courage and daring. In Silicon Valley, failure is mainly seen as a learning opportunity; here it seems mainly a shame. If you dare to say your big ambitions out loud, you will achieve them sooner.'

> “In Silicon Valley, failure is mainly seen as a learning opportunity; in the Netherlands it still seems mainly a shame.”

Julia Krauwer

Sector Banker Technology, Media & Telecom

## More equal

All things considered, Krauwer expects the European Big Tech and AI ecosystems to grow strongly in the coming years. Not with the primary goal of completely breaking away from American tech, but to become more equal. 'We are building plan B by strengthening our own alternatives. Those who dare to invest in that independence will build the next generation of European tech. You should not assume that what worked five years ago will still be good enough for the next five years. The entrepreneurs who understand this are the winners of tomorrow. Anyone who dares to link their own values to innovation and scale can now become a global player.'
