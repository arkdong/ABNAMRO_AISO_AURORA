# Sustainability without blah blah: the down-to-earth way of Dille & Kamille

[Entrepreneurial story](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/ondernemershalen/index.html)

[Retail](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/retail/index.html)

23/10/2025 • 6 minutes reading

[Henk Hofstede](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/authors/henk-hofstede/index.html)

Dille & Kamille has been nominated for the ABN AMRO Sustainable Retailer of the Year Award for the second time. Yet CEO Hans Geels does not call the company sustainable. “If you sell new stuff, you are by definition not one. However, we do everything we can to be the next best thing. Concrete, close and without blah blah.”

The first Dille & Kamille store opened in 1974 as a reaction to the throw-away society. Plastic and batteries were not included, but timeless products made from natural materials were. More than 50 years later, the company has 54 stores in the Netherlands, Belgium, France and Germany and the philosophy is still the same.

Yet Geels does not think the store chain is sustainable by definition. "The honest story is: if you sell new things, you are not sustainable. You always use energy to make and move them and that leads to a negative CO₂ footprint. Moreover, there is already enough stuff in the world. If you really want to do something good for the earth, you would have to close the shops. But that is not realistic."

## The next best thing

What is realistic then? “Being the next best thing,” says Geels. "People simply want to buy new things. We want them to choose products that are timeless, long-lasting and made from materials that the earth can produce again: wood, glass, earthenware, cotton. As a retailer, we perform above average in the field of sustainable entrepreneurship with our 'less is more' approach, I think. But it remains a step ahead of sustainability. Truly sustainable would be if you sold second-hand or started a loan service."

> “The honest story is: if you sell new stuff, you are not sustainable.”

Hans Geels

CEO, Dille & Kamille

## No blah, but specific

The down-to-earth view translates into a practical approach. In the coming years, Dille & Kamille will focus on two spearheads throughout the chain: achieving a living wage and further reducing CO₂ emissions. “We try to make it very flat,” Geels explains. “This is how we make sustainability concrete. Otherwise it remains blah blah.”

For example, Dille & Kamille calculated the total negative CO₂ emissions of the chain and linked it to an amount per kiloton. Geels: "By determining the price of our emissions, we know what our emissions are and what we take from the earth. We want to give back as much as we take from the planet. That is why we donate to social and sustainable projects. That is around 10% of our profit. Ultimately, we want to be able to calculate the CO₂ emissions per product. Then the consumer can choose whether they want to compensate. Voluntarily, of course. That is the next step."

> “We want to give back as much as we take from the planet.”

Hans Geels

CEO, Dille & Kamille

Dille & Kamille also looks at where improvements are possible in the chain. "In the coming years, we will check with all our suppliers whether they pay a living wage. There are 360 ​​of them, so that will take some time. We are also looking at CO₂ reduction. If a supplier cannot tackle this itself, we are prepared to give a loan."

## Sustainability is more than green

For Dille & Kamille, sustainability means more than green. “Sustainability is also social,” says Geels. "In Nepal, for example, women who are at a distance from the labor market have been working on our felt products for years. The wool comes from New Zealand, so in terms of transport it is not very sustainable. But the project has enormous social value for these women. That also weighs heavily on us."

## Don't communicate, just do

The retailer hardly communicates about these sustainable steps. According to Geels, it is not a USP. “Consumers are turning away when it comes to sustainability,” he explains. "They find it complicated or associate it with expensive. Customers come for the atmosphere of our stores, the modern nostalgia, and the high-quality products. If they then discover afterwards that their purchase is also sustainable, it makes them happy. But it is mainly a 'satisfyer' afterwards, not an entrance. That is why we do not do it for the consumer, but for ourselves. Based on our philosophy."

For example, at Dille & Kamille you will find 100% recycled bath textiles and kitchen towels, made from so-called 'pre-consumer waste'. “That is cutting waste that remains after the production of other cotton items,” Geels explains. "We would prefer to work with used cotton, but these fibers are too weak. You have to add plastic to make it stronger and we don't want that. That is why we choose cotton from cutting waste." A quality mark is not sacred to Geels. “This is one step better than GOTS certified organic cotton. However, you do not get a quality mark for it.”

## A paper reality

How does Geels see the future of retail? "We still have a long way to go. People want to buy things, but unfortunately you can't just change that. Changing an economic model is one of the most complicated transitions that exist. And as long as retail prices are so low and new products follow each other in quick succession, the temptation to always buy something new remains great and the mountain of waste grows."

According to Geels, the bureaucratic side of the transition does not help either. "Take the CSRD legislation, in my opinion this creates a paper reality. Companies are busier reporting than improving. You help the accountants and consultants, but not the planet. Sustainability must remain practical. Make it concrete and close to people."

## Taking rigorous steps

What does the market need for a more sustainable offering? “Guts and a government that does something,” says Geels. "If you want to change something, you have to work more rigorously. You need courage for that. It is not easy, we experience that every day. Retail is not a sustainable game. For sustainability you have to do all kinds of things that are currently not economically rewarded."

> “I think you should ban products that are demonstrably bad. For example microplastics, stop that.”

Hans Geels

CEO, Dille & Kamille

Geels therefore believes that the government should reward sustainability with lower taxes instead of punishing it. "Now it is exactly the other way around. Isn't it strange that sustainable groceries or products that are made fairly are often more expensive than non-sustainable ones?" Stricter legislation also helps, according to Geels. “I think you should ban products that are demonstrably bad. For example microplastics, stop that.”

## Don't grow for the sake of growing

That same down-to-earth attitude is also reflected in the way Dille & Kamille grows. The company now has 54 stores and expects 56 locations by the end of this year. The largest growth comes from Belgium and Germany. "We finance everything ourselves, without outside capital. We don't grow for the sake of growing. We only open new stores if we can and slow down if we have to," says Geels.

Geels' greatest wish for the future? "That Dille & Kamille will still be relevant in 50 years' time. Perhaps in a different form. We are a strong brand with a clear philosophy, you can also do other things with it. We have built a culture based on the values ​​of our company. What you feel in our stores, you also feel in the office. That is what I am most proud of after 12 years."
