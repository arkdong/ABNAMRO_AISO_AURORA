# Equal pay gap remains persistent, but progress is possible

Research by ABN AMRO and Impact Institute shows that the equal pay gap is still a persistent problem. Unequal pay for the same work between men and women still occurs regularly. European legislation to tackle this problem is underway. Companies can reduce the equal pay gap by creating equal opportunities that contribute to equal pay.

The report Hidden price tag of social impact Dutch business from ABN AMRO and Impact Institute shows that the equal pay gap is a persistent problem. The equal pay gap is the difference in pay between men and women for the same work. The costs for the equal pay gap from the Dutch business community amount to more than 16.9 billion euros, according to this research by ABN AMRO and Impact Institute. The equal pay gap is quantified based on the true pricing method. The report discusses the equal pay gap method in more detail.

Companies that have an equal pay gap run risks. Unequal pay for the same work can undermine employee confidence in the company. At the same time, companies that actively address the equal pay gap and are transparent about it can give employees confidence, resulting in higher satisfaction, loyalty and productivity. In addition, a company that practices equal pay strengthens its reputation and reduces the risk of financial losses and legal consequences. Customers, employees and investors appreciate companies that are transparent and fair in their compensation policies, which increases the attractiveness of the company. In addition, from 2027 onwards, companies must report on the equal pay gap according to European Legislation. If the equal pay gap is greater than five percent, they must reduce it.

The equal pay gap is the difference in pay between men and women for the same work. This is also called the corrected wage gap. The unadjusted pay gap looks at the difference in the average pay between men and women, without taking into account whether it concerns the same work. This study corrects for important composition effects, including age, education level and working hours (full-time versus part-time). In addition, adjustments have been made for the male-female ratio in the sector. Thus, the effects of a difference in wages weigh more heavily in sectors with more women than men.

The figure concerns the direct impact in the Netherlands and the indirect impact in the value chain (suppliers and customers). In contrast to, for example, child labor and forced labor, where the negative social impact mainly takes place in the chain, the equal pay gap occurs to a relatively large extent directly at the Dutch companies themselves. Nearly 30 percent of the costs of the equal pay gap can be directly traced to the Dutch business community itself, which amounts to approximately 5 billion euros.

A number of sectors stand out when it comes to their contribution to the equal pay gap. The sectors Business Services, Industry, Healthcare and Government & Education stand out (see Figure 1). The calculation method for the equal pay gap takes into account the added value that a sector has on the Dutch economy. For example, business services make a relatively large contribution to the economy and therefore have a relatively larger share in the equal pay gap.

## The equal pay gap is caused by an accumulation of factors

[the National Salary Survey](https://www.intermediair.nl/onderzoek/nationaal-salary-onderzoek) (NSO) even shows that the equal pay gap in the Netherlands has grown in the period from 2021 to 2023, from 5 percent to 7.4 percent less pay for women for the same work. This concerns the corrected wage gap.  Furthermore, the research shows that more men received a salary increase than women during this period. Men also generally use a company car three times as much, profit sharing and relocation compensation twice as much, and expense reimbursement and overtime compensation one and a half times as often. Moreover, men receive a bonus more often than women. These secondary employment conditions contribute to inequality between men and women in the workplace.

The gender composition of a sector also appears to influence the average equal pay gap in a sector. If men are in the majority, favorable working conditions are easier to achieve for men than for women, according to research by economists' journal [ESB](https://esb.nu/meer-vrouwen-in-beroep-dempt-de-lonen/).

The research shows an equal pay gap in all sectors, but this is strongest in male-dominated professions (see Figure 2). Furthermore, ESB concludes that a higher share of women in an occupation leads to a lower average wage. The [World Health Organization] report(https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/9789240052895) comes to the same finding for the Health sector, where they see that working in this female-dominated sector dampens the level of wages.

This provides an explanation for the highest scoring sectors in terms of the equal pay gap. In male-dominated sectors, such as Industry, the equal pay gap is relatively large because there is a relatively large gap in wages between men and women. However, in female-dominated industries, such as Education and Healthcare, the overall impact of the equal pay gap is also large; this can be explained by the large share of women in the sector.

Another explanation for the equal pay gap that is often mentioned is that women negotiate less or not at all about their salaries. Yet this picture is more nuanced. For example, [Harvard research](https://gap.hks.harvard.edu/social-incentives-gender-differences-propensity-initiate-negotiations-sometimes-it-does-hurt-ask) shows that women who do negotiate are socially punished for doing so. The negative association that arises when starting a negotiation is 5.5 times greater for female candidates than for male candidates. The negotiators viewed the women as 'demanding' and 'not friendly'. This association was not observed among the male candidates. Deep-rooted prejudices therefore already play a role at the start of the negotiation process.

Men in the Netherlands also often start [higher](https://nos.nl/artikelen/2492582-intermediair-nyenrode-salary gap- Tussen-mannen-en-vrouwen-groeit) in the same salary scale than women. This disadvantage can widen the equal pay gap in the course of a further career. In addition, women are more likely than men to have a work break due to, for example, having children or providing informal care. This can hinder them from salary increases and promotions.

## European legislation on the equal pay gap is approaching

The European Union will introduce the '[Gender Pay Directive](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/EN/legal-content/summary/equal-pay-for-equal-work-or-work-of-equal-value-between-men-and-women-rules-on-pay-transparency.html)' legislation in 2026. The legislation is expected to come into effect in the Netherlands in 2027 under the Wage Transparency Act. Companies and institutions with more than 250 employees must then publicly report annually (https://www.consilium.europa.eu/nl/policies/pay-transparency/) on the equal pay gap; organizations with more than 100 employees must do this once every three years. If the equal pay gap is greater than 5 percent, the employer is obliged to reduce it. If companies do not take action, they risk a fine.

In addition, the legislation shifts the burden of proof from the employee to the employer, who also has easier access to information about wage differences. The law also establishes provisions on compensation for victims of wage discrimination, such as payment of back wages.

## Equal pay starts with equal opportunities

In the Netherlands, almost 30 percent of the equal pay gap can be closed directly by Dutch companies themselves, our research shows. To achieve equal pay for the same work between men and women, equal opportunities are necessary. To reduce the equal pay gap, companies can take a number of steps.

## Measuring is knowing

In order to close the equal pay gap, it is first important for an entrepreneur to identify how big the problem is and which teams it affects. It is necessary to investigate which factors underlie the equal pay gap, such as the inflow, throughflow and outflow of women. Work stoppages, secondary employment conditions and gender relations can also increase inequality of opportunity and must therefore be included in the research.

## Equalize odds

Closing the equal pay gap requires a level playing field between men and women. Prejudices, such as the [stricter](https://gap.hks.harvard.edu/social-incentives-gender-differences-propensity-initiate-negotiations-sometimes-it-does-hurt-ask) assessment of women in salary negotiations, contribute to the equal pay gap. It is essential that awareness is created through unconscious bias training. Another aspect of bias can be addressed by making the interview process more inclusive. In a vacancy text, using too many requirements or using masculine words such as 'targets' can cause women to drop out more quickly, even though they are suitable for the position. An inclusive application process contributes to the hiring of women, which in turn helps achieve a gender balance.

In addition, it is necessary to make work interruptions count as little as possible, for example by reducing the difference with men. Nicole Böttger, Product Owner Diversity and Inclusion at ABN AMRO, emphasizes the added value of this: "Facilitating a relatively long parental leave for men ensures that not only women but also men have a work break when having a child. This promotes equal treatment of men and women when expanding their family."

## Monitor the equal pay gap

To complete the circle, it is important that a company continues to monitor the equal pay gap. By introducing time-bound controls, the equal pay gap remains a relevant topic. The (annual) reports can then be checked externally to guarantee their reliability. [Transparency](https://www.consilium.europa.eu/nl/infographics/pay-transparency/) and discussion contributes to improvements in the field of the equal pay gap.
