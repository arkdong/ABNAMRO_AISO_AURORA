Only ten percent of Dutch agricultural entrepreneurs are younger than 40 years old and of the group older than 50 years, more than half have no successor. This poses a risk to the continuity of food production. That is why the European Commission (EC) presented plans in October this year to promote business succession by young farmers.

The EC sees an important role for this group to guarantee food security and quality of life in rural areas. In this forecast we look at the expected production in the Netherlands. Volumes in agriculture are strongly driven by the amount of area cultivated and the size of the livestock. Weather conditions, plant and animal diseases, concentrates and crop protection products also play an important role. In 2025, these conditions were mainly positive compared to 2024, which promoted production. The financial results at company level varied greatly per sector (prices, including milk prices, now differ compared to the analysis in September.

For the entire agricultural sector and fisheries, ABN AMRO expects a volume increase of 3 percent in 2025. Harvests in arable farming were significantly higher than in 2024. In addition, milk production increased sharply compared to 2024 after an initial decline. For 2026 and 2027, -2.0 percent and -0.5 percent volume decline, respectively, is expected for the entire sector, mainly driven by a further decline in livestock. In 2025, this trend will only be visible to a limited extent. The arable area actually increased by three percent.

## Young farmers needed for future food production and rural vitality

In October this year, the European Union presented a strategy for generational renewal in agriculture with the aim of doubling the share of young farmers in the EU to 24 percent by 2040. Young farmers play a crucial role for food security in the EU and for vibrant rural areas. The EU also recognizes the heavy pressure on the sector: an aging workforce, a shrinking rural population and economic and environmental challenges. Agriculture in the Netherlands is also aging rapidly.

The approximately 49,000 companies active in agriculture vary in size from very small to very large. Most agricultural companies are partnerships such as a general partnership (VOF) or partnership. Especially the larger companies in greenhouse vegetables are private companies. Only 10 percent of farmers are younger than 40 years old, while 60 percent are over 55. The share of young farmers is increasing at larger companies.

Research by Ipsos I&O on behalf of ABN AMRO shows that when agricultural entrepreneurs have plans to succeed the company, in 90 percent of cases the intended successor is a family member. Only 4 percent of respondents indicate that they want to close their business.

## High value combined with low profitability

Transmission within the family can be an emotional and complex process. Letting go of the company by the transferring generation is often a stumbling block. This is further complicated by land prices, which increase the value of the company and therefore the financial challenge.

High land prices pose an additional challenge for dairy farms and arable farms. Dairy farms must be land-based from 2032. In short, this means that companies sell manure on their own land or in the surrounding area. However, land is scarce and land prices are sky high. At the same time, the earning capacity of dairy farms is highly dependent on the number of animals they keep.

The sector's lack of future prospects and stagnation in permitting also create a lot of uncertainty. The location of the company determines development opportunities. This makes the future challenging for successors. Clear frameworks and standards within target management are necessary so that young farmers gain perspective. In addition, supporting policy is indispensable to achieve these goals, especially to prevent accelerated scale-up.

## Tax schemes and subsidies are indispensable for succession

In the Netherlands, rent, leasehold and experimental forms of ownership are increasingly being used, such as in initiatives such as Aardpeer and Land van Ons. In this way, access to land for dairy farmers is stimulated. In addition, advice on tax, legislation and financing remains indispensable during transfers. For example, various schemes are frequently used in agriculture, such as the Business Succession Scheme (BOR), Establishment Aid for Young Farmers and the Transfer Scheme (DSR). These schemes make succession accessible to young entrepreneurs, but they all also have specific conditions and rules. Finally, good communication in the family is invaluable. Talking to each other in a timely manner, including family members who do not follow up, will prevent surprises if transfer becomes an issue.
