# Europe's software market looking for scale and niches

[Analysis](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/analyses/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

25/09/2025 • 8 minutes reading

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/authors/julia-krauwer/index.html)

The European technology sector is struggling to scale potential winners due to a lack of growth capital. Yet the continent has a wide range of innovative players. In the Netherlands, for example, almost two-thirds of investments in young software companies go to parties that apply artificial intelligence.

In 2024, young European technology companies will collectively raise around $54 billion in venture capital. Almost half of this, more than 23 billion, flowed to Software-as-a-Service (SaaS). This makes SaaS the largest category, larger than digital marketplaces or companies that develop physical technology. For investors, the combination of stable subscriptions and scalability is particularly attractive.

Abundant capital flowed into technology during the coronavirus pandemic, helped by low interest rates and an investor search for scalable business models. SaaS benefited from this above average. However, from 2022 onwards, inflation took hold and central banks raised interest rates, making capital more expensive and correcting valuations. That made investors more cautious, especially with mega deals and loss-making growth companies. Meanwhile, the volumes of venture capital investments appear to be stabilizing. SaaS remains the focus of European tech financing, although within this category capital is increasingly going to companies that have AI woven into their core.

## The scalability gap

Within Europe, the United Kingdom attracts the most SaaS investments with more than $77 billion in ten years – more than a third of the European total. France and Germany follow with 35 billion dollars (16 percent) and 33 billion (15 percent) respectively. The Netherlands is in stable fourth place with 9.9 billion, accounting for a share of 4.5 percent of all European SaaS investments between 2015 and 2025.

Unfortunately, Europe faces a persistent problem: the lack of capital for tech companies to grow into internationally competitive companies. American startups are twice as likely to raise a round of more than $15 million than their European counterparts, according to the [State of European Tech report](https://www.stateofeuropeantech.com/chapters/investment-levels) from 2024. The share of young companies that manage to progress to the scale-up phase is therefore much smaller in Europe. According to the researchers, the cumulative deficit now stands at $375 billion – the total amount that young tech companies would have raised over the past decade if access to larger investments were as easy as in the United States (US). In the US, a large part of the money even goes to rounds above 100 million dollars, with a dominant role for mega deals above 250 million. Although there are not so many fewer SaaS deals being closed in Europe – last year there were 4,616, versus 5,040 in the US – the focus remains on smaller rounds.

## Broadly applicable SaaS niches

Venture capital in Europe mainly flows into a number of recognizable SaaS niches. The largest category is 'enterprise software' – solutions that make internal processes of organizations more efficient, from HR and planning systems to workflow and ERP tools. For example, the German unicorn Personio managed to further strengthen its position in HR software, and its compatriot N8N specializes in process automation.

In addition, there are other solutions that can be applied in almost every sector. Marketing and communication software remains popular, including marketing platform Brevo as an example. Skeepers helps brands to cleverly weave customer reviews and other user-generated content into campaigns. Cybersecurity also benefits from broad applicability, combined with an overall sense of urgency as cyber attacks become increasingly sophisticated. For example, the French Sekoia.io raised growth capital in 2024 for its AI platform that detects threats in real-time.

## SaaS for specific industries

SaaS companies also attract a lot of financing in the field of FinTech. For example, the British Volt.io offers a platform that allows companies to also accept payments from international customers, and Qover helps integrate insurance into its service offering across sectors - from mobility to e-commerce. On the other hand, there are parties that operate more specifically at the core of financial service providers, such as Backbase, which offers a customer platform for financial institutions as a replacement for their old IT systems.

It is an example of one of the many 'vertical' SaaS services, designed for a specific sector. In healthcare, Doctolib enters the broader European market with a platform that facilitates appointment management, consultations and patient communication, and Unmind raised growth funding for a platform that helps company employees improve their mental health. In hospitality, the Dutch Mews raised new capital to roll out its platform internationally, and in construction and real estate, PlanRadar previously did this with its project management software.

## AI-SaaS as a new engine

Within the broader landscape, AI-driven SaaS is taking an increasingly important place. In Europe, a third of software deals now fall into this category, accounting for almost half of the investment volume. In the Netherlands that is even two-thirds. In the US, development is going even faster; There, AI-SaaS consumes almost 80 percent of all SaaS funding.

Nevertheless, Europe is producing a lot of innovative, 'AI-first' SaaS companies that specialize in all kinds of niches. The Netherlands even has an above-average number of AI-SaaS deals and funding. For example, Cradle developed an AI model that can design new proteins and sell them to laboratories as a SaaS service. Compatriot Fourthline focuses on identity fraud and compliance in the financial sector with AI, and FRISS does the same for insurance risks. TestGorilla makes selection processes smarter and less sensitive to bias, and Sympower uses AI to keep electricity grids in balance.

The German Accure offers a platform for AI analysis of battery data in, among others, the mobility and industrial sector, in order to improve the safety and lifespan of batteries and accumulators. The British Synthesia automates the production of corporate films and training videos with generative AI and raised $180 million at the beginning of 2025 at a valuation of more than $2 billion. And from Sweden, Lovable is experiencing enormous growth with a platform that uses AI to accelerate software development and make it more accessible. The company was founded in 2023 and reached a valuation of almost $2 billion in just a few years.

## Major AI developers are setting the tone

A large part of the investments is concentrated among a few large AI developers, who set the tone in the market with mega deals. Their AI systems are offered in two ways: as a building block for software developers to build AI into their own product, and in the form of chat apps such as ChatGPT (OpenAI) and Claude (Anthropic) for end users. Anyone who wants access to the most powerful functions pays a monthly subscription. This makes the revenue model essentially the same as SaaS: software that is offered via the cloud and for a periodic fee.

In the US, the mega deals in question resulted in enormous capital injections. OpenAI raised $40 billion at a $300 billion valuation in March this year, followed by an additional round in the summer. Anthropic secured another $13 billion in September 2025, and xAI closed a $6 billion round in late 2024. Europe has fewer such outliers, but here too the AI-SaaS segment is growing rapidly. The French Mistral AI raised 1.7 billion euros in a round in 2025 - of which 1.3 billion came from ASML - doubling its valuation to almost 12 billion. The German Aleph Alpha received half a billion in previous rounds.

## Success factors

Amid the growth momentum of these major players, it is important for SaaS companies to position themselves sharply. An important characteristic of promising providers is that they focus on critical business processes – parts of an organization that are simply crucial. For example, Fourthline automates the mandatory identification of customers in the financial sector. Banks must carry out this process to comply with the law, and switching to another provider entails risks. FRISS does the same for insurers, who have a great interest in recognizing fraudulent claims. Doctolib supports doctors and patients with appointments and communication; an essential, but often suboptimal digitalized process.

In addition, opportunities lie in niches that large but generic AI models cannot handle. For example, Cradle, which focuses entirely on generative AI for protein design, is trained on a huge database of protein structures and properties, and combines deep knowledge of biology with technical capabilities. Sympower developed an AI solution that balances electricity grids and connects both large consumers and energy producers. Collaborating with parties in energy-intensive industries such as agriculture, cement production and data centers requires strong relationships and a lot of domain knowledge that cannot be quickly copied.

Finally, it is striking that successful SaaS companies often form a connecting layer between multiple parties. For example, N8N's automation platform allows different software packages to 'talk' to each other - from CRM systems and email tools to databases and cloud services. In this way, users can automate complex work processes, across different applications. Qover offers a connecting layer in the insurance world; Through APIs, the company makes it possible to directly integrate insurance products into, for example, mobility apps or e-commerce platforms. In both cases it concerns a software platform that enables others to enrich or optimize their own services.

## The way forward

The European SaaS market is full of innovation, but the path to global dominance is anything but self-evident. The continent faces a structural scalability gap and a more cautious investment culture. However, encouraged by a geopolitical need to also become more technologically self-sufficient, it is taking several steps in the right direction. On September 23, 2025, Minister Karremans presented a list of [measures](https://www.rijksoverheid.nl/actueel/nieuws/2025/09/23/meer-groei-nederlandse-startups-en-scale-ups-door-meer-capital-talent-en-projecten-techleap) to support “the tech champions of tomorrow”, with an emphasis on research-intensive key technologies such as cybersecurity, quantum computing and artificial intelligence. For example, the outgoing cabinet is investing 200 million euros in a second fund within the European Tech Champions Initiative (ETCI), and Invest-NL is exploring ways to bring more pension capital to venture capital funds.

In any case, the current generation of SaaS companies shows that there are plenty of opportunities. A focus on business-critical processes, AI applications in specific niches, and building a connecting layer are strategies with which local players can position themselves strongly, even amid the growth violence of American parties. In any case, Mistral's success proves that even capital-intensive models can also gain traction here, provided there is sufficient willingness to invest. Now it is important to make these incidental successes structural.
