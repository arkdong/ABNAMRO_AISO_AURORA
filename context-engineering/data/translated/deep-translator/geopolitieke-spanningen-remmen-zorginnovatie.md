# Geopolitical tensions inhibit healthcare innovation

Healthcare providers must innovate to keep healthcare accessible. They are dependent on digital applications, medical devices, aids and medicines. US tariffs threaten the availability of such resources and slow down innovation.

The healthcare sector is currently mainly struggling with personnel shortages, but in the longer term it may also be affected by import tariffs from the United States (US). In the short term, the sector will hardly experience any disruption because almost all income comes from providing care in the Netherlands. The sector actually benefits from more money that the government makes available. As a result, the healthcare sector will grow by 3.5 percent this year and by 2 percent in 2026. The fall of the government makes growth expectations for the coming years uncertain. The next government can adjust the healthcare budget and, for example, eliminate the halving of the deductible.

Long-term care providers, especially in elderly care, will likely face cutbacks next year, meaning rates will not rise with inflation. In addition, regional hospitals are seeing their offering continue to decline because they are no longer allowed to perform certain procedures that they rarely perform. Healthcare providers in primary care such as general practitioners, psychologists and community nurses are seeing their workload increase: the demand for care is increasing while the number of healthcare providers hardly increases.

## Innovation needed for accessible care

Healthcare providers are therefore faced with the challenge of caring for more people and that makes innovation necessary. This can partly be done by organizing care in a different way, whereby care providers make use of people's independent capacity and only provide care when it is really necessary. On the other hand, care can also be provided more efficiently with the help of digital communication tools, tools that promote self-reliance, medical devices that facilitate operations and effective medication.

The Netherlands is dependent on foreign countries for such resources, including the US, which mainly supplies medical equipment, digital systems and medicines. For example, the healthcare sector in the Netherlands imported 18 percent of their goods from the US in 2023 and the pharmaceutical industry almost 13 percent, according to figures from the Central Bureau of Statistics (CBS). A trade war drives up healthcare providers' costs and could lead to shortages. In addition, the uncertainty inhibits investments by companies working on medical innovation.

## Trump threatens to lower pharmaceutical prices

The geopolitical tensions mainly affect manufacturers and traders of medical devices and medicines. For the time being, medicines are exempt from US import duties, but President Trump announced on May 12 that pharmaceutical companies must reduce the sales prices of their products in the US. The US pays two to four times as much for certain medicines as Australia and France, for example. If pharmaceutical companies do not lower their prices to the level of other developed countries within thirty days, Trump threatens to take measures.

If Trump succeeds in lowering drug prices in the US, this could affect the Dutch medicine market in several ways. On the one hand, the availability of medicines in the Netherlands can improve because the price difference between the US and the Netherlands is becoming smaller. Currently, medicine shortages regularly arise because suppliers prefer countries that pay more, such as other European countries and the US. On the other hand, pharmaceutical companies are probably trying to raise prices in countries outside the US, including the Netherlands, to compensate for the price drop in the US. On balance, it is likely that pharmaceutical companies will ultimately have to give up. This means that they can invest less in the development of new medicines and that Dutch pharmaceutical companies will also achieve less turnover and profit.

## Staff shortage remains the biggest challenge

Healthcare providers are hardly affected and are mainly busy attracting healthcare providers. In the last quarter of 2024, almost 60,000 vacancies from healthcare providers are open, just slightly more than at the end of 2023. Most vacancies are available from providers of disability care, mental health care and nursing, care and home care (VVT).

Healthcare providers are facing a shortage of staff because the demand for care is increasing, while more and more healthcare providers are retiring and more healthcare providers are sick at home. At 7.3 percent, absenteeism due to illness is almost as high as the 7.4 percent at the end of last year, according to figures from Statistics Netherlands.

Recently resigned Minister Fleur Agema of Health, Welfare and Sport worked with industry parties and municipalities on the supplementary care and welfare agreement (AZWA) with the aim of reducing the staff shortage. This agreement is delayed due to the fall of the cabinet and may no longer be signed by all parties involved. The question is whether it will alleviate the staff shortage at all. Sharp choices and a broad policy on health and prevention are necessary to keep healthcare accessible in the future.
