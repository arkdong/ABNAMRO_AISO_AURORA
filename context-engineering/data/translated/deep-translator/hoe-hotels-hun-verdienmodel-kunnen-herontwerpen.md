# How hotels can redesign their revenue model

[Column](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/column.html)

[Leisure](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/leisure/index.html)

27/08/2025 • 4 minutes reading

[Stef Driessen](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/authors/stef-driessen/index.html)

The classic hotel revenue model is creaking under rising costs. That's scary. But there is also hope: those who use data and space more intelligently can now build a stronger revenue model.

From 2026, the VAT rate on overnight stays will increase from 9 to 21 percent. This instantly makes the Netherlands one of the most expensive holiday destinations in Europe. Research by Significant APE, conducted on behalf of the [Ministry of Finance](https://www.eerstekamer.nl/overig/20250703/impactanalyse_btw_hoogg_logies/meta), predicts a decline in tourist overnight stays by 30 percent. It is expected that 26 to 30 percent of Dutch people will move to surrounding countries and 5 percent will stay at home entirely. This increases inequality of opportunity and puts considerable pressure on the level of facilities in tourist regions - especially outside the high season.

The government is counting on extra tax revenue, but [experts](https://decisio.nl/impact-btw-hoogtering-op-de-hotelsector/) warn that this advantage will quickly evaporate as soon as volumes decline. After all, fewer guests also mean less turnover for shops, restaurants and public transport. This article is therefore a wake-up call for hoteliers. Those who don't change anything will feel the blow hard. Those who dare to innovate can convert unused meters into new sources of income.

## An inspiring example

On the roof of the A'DAM Tower in Amsterdam is the Over the Edge attraction, where visitors literally float over the edge. In 2024, the few square meters on which the swing is located attracted 615,000 visitors (source: [Ginder/Pretwerk](https://pretwerk.nl/recreatie-actueel/top-50-dagrecreatie-over-2024-is-weer-verschenen/93505/)). With an entrance fee of 24.50 euros per person, this generated more than 15 million euros in turnover. This illustrates what technology company Mews – active in more than twelve thousand hotels worldwide – is referring to with revenue per square meter ([RevPAM](https://www.mews.com/en/blog/must-follow-revenue-management-metrics-hospitality)). The idea: every square meter must yield a return. Sometimes an unmanned lobby shop is more profitable than room service after midnight.

Research by Spring Partners and Mews (2025) shows that hotels waste billions in unused square meters. The [day rental of rooms](https://www.hospitality-management.nl/hotels-moeten-een-korting-van-minimaal-30-procent-aanbieden) alone, rented between two stays, represents a turnover potential of 86 billion euros, of which only 14 billion is currently being utilized. Accessible spa facilities for day guests could add another 35 billion euros. Event and pop-up spaces, such as meeting locations, drinks lounges or roof terraces, account for an additional 17 billion euros.

According to the researchers, many hotels only utilize 10 to 30 percent of this non-room-related potential. This means that up to 70 percent of space for growth remains unused, often literally under and on your own roof.

In this context, think like an airline selling the same seat multiple times a day. Hotels can also apply this principle by offering their inventory per hour, per seat or per service. Meeting rooms, restaurant chairs, saunas, parking spaces and bicycles can generate income several times a day. Subscription models for spa, fitness or coworking also ensure a predictable, loyal revenue stream.

> “Rising costs? Map profit potential per room and discover where the most value can be achieved for hotel entrepreneurs.”

## From hotel to hybrid concept

Hospitality living – a mix of hotels, serviced apartments, co-living and senior housing – is gaining ground worldwide. In some cities, the number of co-living beds is even growing faster than the number of hotel rooms. A striking example is The Social Hub, which rents rooms to international students for ten months a year and receives tourists in the summer. This way, occupancy remains high all year round and the hotel spreads its risks across multiple target groups. Hotels that go this route will benefit from longer stays, lower costs for online booking platforms and a more stable revenue stream.

In these times of increasing costs, hotel entrepreneurs would do well to map out all square meters, draw up a mini profit and loss account per room and test where the most potential lies. Creative possibilities present themselves, such as converting an empty lobby corner into a 24-hour unmanned mini market, renting out parking spaces to external parties during the week or using them outside peak hours for pop-up shops or events. More effort, but just as lucrative, requires setting up co-working memberships for quiet lobby spaces or day rentals of rooms between overnight stays.

New revenue models require clear agreements. Particularly for day rentals, strict house rules and check-in procedures are essential to prevent abuse, such as [illegal prostitution](https://www.hospitality-management.nl/no-room-for-sex-trafficking-approach-sexual-uitexploiting-in-hotels). Transparency and careful controls prevent abuse. The future of profitability does not lie in the room rate, but in the meters that currently remain unused. It's time to calculate differently.
