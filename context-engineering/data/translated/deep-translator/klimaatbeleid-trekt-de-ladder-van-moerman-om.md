# Climate policy reverses Moerman's ladder

[Column](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/column.html)

[Agricultural](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/agrarisch/index.html)

19/09/2025 • 4 minutes reading

[Pierre Berntsen](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/autoriteit/pierre-berntsen/index.html)

Food waste takes many forms and prevention is the highest form of value retention. In this column I focus on the 'Moerman ladder'. Anyone who uses the ladder properly reduces waste and uses raw materials as circularly as possible. The ladder deserves attention because financial values ​​do not always correspond with circular or social values.

The [Moerman's ladder](https://www.milgro.eu/blog/moerman) is widely accepted scientifically, it shows the most and least desirable ways to deal with food. Prevention is at the top while composting and incineration are the least desirable. The model provides a compass for companies and policymakers.

## Moerman's ladder has 7 steps and looks like this:

1. Prevention (most desirable)
2. Human consumption
3. Animal nutrition
4. Raw materials for industry
5. Fermentation
6. Composting
7. Burning (least desirable)

Food waste naturally has more dilemmas. Take the plastic packaging to keep a cucumber longer, or chemical products to preserve the harvest. Both limit waste, but may also have undesirable effects. I will not consider these dilemmas here.

The ladder not only shows the ecological value but also the economic value. The higher on the ladder, the greater the value. For example, bread costs more than animal feed. However, government policy can break this hierarchy, for example with the European climate agenda.

## Green gas displaces animal feed

According to the ladder, unavoidable residual or by-products from the food industry must first serve as animal feed. The animal's manure then supplies green gas and then the residual product (digestate) provides fertilizer for the field.

Through climate policy, companies are mandatorily or voluntarily seeking to reduce their CO₂ emissions. They also look at the use of their own residual flows, such as the fermentation of their own pressed pulp by a sugar factory or spent grain by a brewer. On top of this is the increasing demand for green gas as a result of the blending obligation.

As a result, residual flows are increasingly in danger of disappearing into the digester. According to the ladder, this is undesirable and limits the availability of animal feed. If the demand for animal proteins remains the same, more agricultural land will be needed for feed. Another consequence is that the Dutch market for organic fertilizer is further flooded with digestate from residual flows. That is undesirable.

## Upcycling as the ultimate step

It is a good idea to use residual flows for human consumption. In this way, old bread can serve as a raw material for fresh bread. The ultimate step is the upward step, the so-called upcycling. For example, Duynie, a daughter of Cosun, extracts protein from spent grains through biorefinery for use in human food. Avebe no longer discharges potato proteins, but processes them in high-quality applications.

Plants also have value. At the end of their productive life, removed tomato plants still provide fiber for clothing or insulation. In this way, they replace fossil or chemical raw materials in construction, packaging, textiles, dyes and furniture.

## RENURE fertilizers need broad acceptance

The ladder focuses mainly on food, but we should not forget the bottom. It requires nutrition to stay healthy and productive. Organic fertilizers are preferred over chemical fertilizers. After all, nitrogen fertilizers are made with fossil energy and other minerals are mined in mines. That is why broad acceptance of RENURE fertilizers is important. These fertilizers, rich in nitrogen, are extracted from animal manure or from air scrubbers that clean stable air. They use minerals that are already on the farm and thus close the nitrogen cycle better.

## Increasing tension calls for sensible policy

The ladder does not comment on land use. It is clear that land use for human food takes priority over animal feed, fiber and energy. In practice, the market and economic reality determine the direction. Biogenic carbon is gaining more and more value in a society that wants to use less fossil raw materials for food, fiber and energy. But that production requires cultivation area, while agricultural land in the Netherlands is under pressure as a result of nature objectives, housing construction and infrastructure. Elsewhere, land degradation and climate change are causing area shrinkage.

In short, the tension between 'feed, food, fiber and fuel' will increase further in the coming years. To limit unwanted side effects, a clear vision and sensible policy are necessary.

\*REcovered Nitrogen from manURE: nitrogen-containing fertilizers extracted from animal manure, or digestate for which animal manure has been used.
