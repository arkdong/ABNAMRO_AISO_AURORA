In this 'Stand of' we publish our liquidity expectations for the agricultural sectors. Liquidity, the difference between income and expenditure, is reflected in the development of the current account and is an important indicator of the financial development of the agricultural company.

In addition, it is an indication to determine whether an agricultural entrepreneur can meet his financial obligations in the short term. Liquidity is emphatically different from the income of the farmer or gardener.

## Yields in arable farming are under pressure

In the agricultural sector, yields will develop favorably in the first half of 2025, with the exception of arable farming, where prices of chip potatoes fell after a good start due to oversupply and stagnant sales. In food horticulture, on the other hand, prices between the different products fluctuate widely, but due to higher production per hectare, yields are on average good. Floriculture also continues to grow, mainly driven by price increases; increase in supply is not expected until later this year.

> “Yield prices, especially in animal sectors, are higher than average due to market tightness”

Melanie Murk

Sector economist Agricultural and Food

In many animal sectors, yields are very high due to tight market conditions. This applies in particular to poultry farming, in both the laying and meat sectors. Calf farming and dairy farming also benefit from higher prices due to shortages in supply. The yields for fattening pig companies are under some pressure due to international competition. In addition, it is expected that Chinese import duties and the opening of the European market for American pork could have a disruptive effect. In fisheries, fish volumes are sufficient to meet demand and prices are in line with 2024.

## Costs remain high but are stabilizing somewhat

After years of sky-high inflation, costs remain as high as ever. Nevertheless, entrepreneurs experience some relief because a number of important items stabilize or decrease slightly, as shown in the graph below. For example, after a small peak in early 2025, the prospects for the gas price are favorable for the long term based on the TTF future prices. On average, feed costs have fallen slightly compared to 2024, due to a downward trend in raw material prices, such as wheat and soy. In addition, farmers were able to spread more liquid manure in the spring, the placement capacity was expanded and exports to Germany and France increased by 25 percent, which has moderated manure sales prices somewhat in 2025. The deposit rate of the European Central Bank (ECB) is 2 percent (June 2025) lower than last year, which generally leads to lower financing costs for new short-term loans and loans with variable rates.

On the other hand, labor costs remain high, causing more and more entrepreneurs to look for alternatives such as automation or moving production. Fertilizer prices have actually risen further in 2025, partly because the European Parliament announced import duties on fertilizer from Russia and Belarus in January.

## Predominantly positive liquidity development

Given the favorable developments in revenues and stabilizing costs, we anticipate positive liquidity developments for the animal sectors for the remainder of 2025 and early 2026. Good demand and a shrinking livestock population are particularly decisive in this regard. Poultry farmers stand out, they strengthen their liquidity position the most.

The vegetable sectors are faced with volatile prices. Floriculture is improving and food horticulture maintains its current liquidity position. Arable farming, especially companies that depend on the free market, is having a difficult year due to sales problems for potatoes. ABN AMRO assumes a deterioration in the liquidity position of arable farms.
