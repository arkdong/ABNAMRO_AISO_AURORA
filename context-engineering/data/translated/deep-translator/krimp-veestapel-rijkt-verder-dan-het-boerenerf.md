# Livestock decline extends beyond the farm

[Report](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/reports/index.html)

[Agricultural](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/agrarisch/index.html)

23/06/2025 • 6 minutes reading

[Jelmer Schreurs](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/authors/jelmer-schreurs/index.html)

More than 1,500 farmers are currently doing a lot of calculations and weighing up their interests. After voluntary registration, they have received a proposal from the government in the context of the National Termination Scheme for Livestock Farming Locations (Lbv(-plus)).

Farmers must decide within a few months of receiving the proposal whether they will actually end their livestock farming. A tough decision that involves business economics, social and emotional aspects.

Since January 1, 2025, the Minister of Agriculture, Fisheries, Food Security and Nature (LVVN) has also significantly increased the skimming of animal and phosphate rights. The aim of this is not to exceed the manure production ceilings and to prevent a generic discount on animal and phosphate rights. As a result of these measures, the livestock population will decline significantly in 2025 and 2026. The government focuses strongly on careful implementation in combination with a decrease in nitrogen emissions. At the same time, companies in related industries are scratching their heads: how do they deal with the decline in Dutch livestock farming? Can overcapacity in the dairy factory or slaughterhouse be prevented? ABN AMRO investigates the impact of the decline in livestock on other sectors in two analyses.

## Government measures

To visualize the impact of a smaller livestock population on other economic sectors, it is important to know how sharply the livestock population is declining. In this analysis we focus on the four major animal sectors: dairy, veal and pig farming and poultry. We look at the impact of three government measures aimed at these sectors:

1. The National Termination Regulation for Livestock Farming Locations (Lbv);
2. The National Termination Scheme for Livestock Farming Locations 'plus' (Lbv-plus), a variant for peak loads;
3. Removal of phosphate and animal rights as included in the Fertilizers Act.

The Lbv and the Lbv-plus offer farmers who want to stop voluntarily compensation for production rights and the loss of value of production capacity. Implementation of the measure will take place in 2025, extending to 2026.

The most recent information shows that 1,578 companies have registered. The question remains how many farmers will actually accept the "wildly attractive scheme". The evaluation of comparable schemes shows that in the past only 55 percent of the companies that registered actually participated. Uncertainty about future government policy and the lack of a successor can be incentives to participate, although the amount of compensation and market conditions play the largest role. When selling prices are good, a farmer's willingness to participate decreases. In the Explanatory Memorandum to the Fertilizers Act (2024), the Ministry of LVVN assumes an expected participation of 65 percent.

In addition to the termination scheme, animal and phosphate rights will be skimmed off in trade as of January 1, 2025. Trade as a result of inheritance, within the family or business mergers are exempt from skimming. The measure is limited to dairy cattle (30 percent skimming of phosphate rights), pig farming (22 percent skimming of animal rights) and poultry (chickens & turkeys; 13 percent skimming of animal rights). There is no system of phosphate or animal rights for other animal species - such as calves, ducks and goats - so skimming is not possible here. Once again we use estimates as included in the Explanatory Memorandum to the Fertilizers Act.

Skimming animal and phosphate rights is a temporary measure that is in force to ensure that the maximum manure production ceiling is not exceeded. As soon as manure production falls below the standards, the government intends to scale back the skimming. Because the effect of the LBV(-plus) comes into effect simultaneously, we assume that skimming will be phased out from 2028 onwards. A minimum skimming of 10 percent applies to phosphate rights (dairy cows), but there is no minimum skimming for chickens and pigs.

## Livestock herd is expected to decline between 12 and 15 percent towards 2030

ABN AMRO expects a decline in the livestock population of between 12 and 15 percent until 2030. Considering the start of the nitrogen crisis in 2019, this is even a reduction of 23 percent. Figure 1 shows the estimate of the relative development of the livestock population per animal species over time. We expect the strongest decline in pig farming (16 percent) and poultry (13 percent). We assume a decline of 8 percent and 11 percent respectively for dairy farming and veal farming. In appendix 1 we discuss the applied methodology and assumptions in more detail.

The decline in the livestock population will be strongest in 2025 and 2026. We expect a slight decline in 2027 and 2028 due to increased skimming of animal and phosphate rights. For the following years we assume a stable number of pigs and chickens. The number of dairy cows continues to decline slightly because a 10 percent skimming off on trade in phosphate rights continues to apply. However, new policies could cause a further decline in livestock numbers.

## Compound feed, food industry and veterinarians affected by decline in livestock population

Not only farmers are affected by the termination and skimming scheme. Suppliers to livestock farms - such as contractors, veterinarians, compound feed companies, farm consultants, machine and stable builders - are faced with lower demand. The food industry is experiencing a lower supply of dairy, meat and eggs. To visualize these dependencies, economists use so-called Input/Output analysis, see Appendix 1.

This quantitative analysis provides insight into how the output of one sector serves as an input for livestock farming, and how changes in supply or demand in livestock farming affect other sectors further down the chain. On this basis, ABN AMRO estimates that the structural decline in gross domestic product (GDP) could amount to 1.5 billion euros per year, with 13,300 jobs at risk. If we put this into perspective, the impact seems limited: this is equal to 8 percent of the agricultural sector and only 0.15 percent of the entire Dutch economy. However, the damage can be great for individual companies and employees. Especially in areas where there are relatively many stoppers.

To know which sectors are affected by the decline in livestock farming, it is important to know which sectors provide inputs for livestock farming. Figure 2 shows this relative ratio. In particular, companies in the agricultural (-275 million euros) and compound feed sector (-200 million euros) will be confronted with a drop in demand. But the energy sector, machine builders and veterinarians are also faced with lost income that can amount to 60, 20 and 15 million euros respectively.

Further down the chain, the food industry is faced with a decline in the supply of dairy, meat and eggs. Companies can respond to changes in the Dutch livestock sector and thus limit economic damage. For example, consider purchasing from suppliers across the border or specializing in premium products. However, the sharp decline comes quite abruptly, which limits adjustment options. An in-depth analysis will follow in the summer of 2025 on the impact on the food industry - such as the creation of overcapacity - and the options that companies have to anticipate this.

## Redistribution involves winners and losers

At the same time, a smaller livestock herd also provides opportunities for the economy. Because there are too many variables, we have not calculated the positive effects, but we explain this qualitatively. First of all; the stalled permit granting process may possibly be smoothened, provided nature is given sufficient space to recover and nitrogen emissions fall below the set standards. In this case, space is created for new economic activities elsewhere in the economy. However, it may still take several years before we reach this point.

The freed up space on the energy grid and supply of labor can also be used elsewhere in the economy, which can limit damage. Finally, agricultural land – mainly grassland – will become available for extensification of remaining livestock farms or for arable farming. Suppose half of the released agricultural land is converted to arable farming, which would be roughly 10,000 hectares. This is equivalent to an increase of 1.7 percent of the current arable area in the Netherlands.

The calculated negative effects on our prosperity can be largely compensated by the new economic activities. However, this does not mean that no economic damage is suffered in livestock farming related sectors; After all, rebuilding the economy always produces both winners and losers.
