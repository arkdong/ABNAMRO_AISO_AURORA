# Logistics in turbulent times requires agile entrepreneurs

[Interview](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/interviews/index.html)

[Transport & Logistics](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/logistiek/index.html)

07/10/2025 • 8 minutes reading

[Bart Banning](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/authors/bart-banning/index.html)

Trade conflicts, cost increases, truck taxes and strict regulations are putting pressure on the transport sector. Volumes fluctuate, logistics chains change and construction stops and CO2 standards slow down economic activity. Yet there is also growth, sees sector expert Bart Banning from ABN AMRO. 'Those who look ahead and have scenarios ready can make a difference now.

'The strength of a good logistics company is that it has logistics knowledge, a strong network and the flexibility to adapt. Anyone who combines the three can grow even in these challenging times.' Bart Banning, Sector Banker Transport & Logistics at ABN AMRO, says it bluntly. He speaks from almost four decades of banking experience, the last fifteen years of which as a sector specialist. In this role he follows all developments in transport and logistics: from road transport and inland shipping to rail and logistics services. He speaks to entrepreneurs in the sector every day and provides companies with guidance in making their strategic choices.

## Logistics is a major sector

Transport and logistics is a major sector in the Netherlands. According to data from the Top Sector Logistics, the sector directly accounts for 8 to 9% of GDP and indirectly even for 12 to 14%, if you also include logistics functions within industry, trade and e-commerce. Banning sees the market changing faster than ever this year. Due to unrest in the world and geopolitical shifts, but also due to digitalization, sustainability and economies of scale. 'The world around us is changing rapidly and logistics is at the center of it due to their function in the chain. It requires entrepreneurs to look ahead, have scenarios ready and be able to switch immediately if circumstances change.'

## Waves in goods flows

President Trump's recent trade war illustrates how disruptive international politics can be. The increase in import tariffs – some up to 145%, later partly reversed – put global commodity flows and chains on edge. Companies anticipated by massively stockpiling strategic supplies, even before the tariffs came into effect. This phenomenon, called front loading, caused significant peaks in transport volumes. 'During those peaks, ports had to deal with congestion and terminals were overflowing. Air freight companies actually had extra work because shipments had to urgently cross the ocean,' says Banning. This was followed by off-peak periods when capacity was suddenly available. 'Forwarders offer added value by offering alternative routes or modalities. But road transporters, with their high fixed costs, have a more difficult time.' It must be said: the impact of this unrest differs per segment. Retail logistics, especially for supermarkets, remains fairly stable. Construction logistics responds strongly to permitting policies and housing production, while industrial flows, vehicles and luxury goods are particularly sensitive to trade restrictions and fluctuations in export demand.

> “The question is whether companies can fully pass on all cost-increasing factors to the market.”

Bart Banning

Sector Banker Transport & Logistics

## Cost increases on several fronts

At the same time, costs are rising structurally. The new collective labor agreement has not yet been finalized, but will have an increasing effect on costs. The truck tax will start from July 2026. Followed in 2027 by the increase in fuel excise duties and the introduction of ETS-2, which will also put a price tag on CO₂ emissions in the transport sector. Banning: 'The question is whether companies can fully pass on all these cost-increasing factors to the market.' This makes contract negotiations with clients even more important; focuses on long-term contracts aimed at increasing productivity.'

> “We want to be a leader in the transport of building materials.”

Arjen Tulner

Co-owner Van der Werff Logistics

## Sharp focus on strategy

According to Banning, the top of the market distinguishes itself through sharp strategic choices. 'Companies that do well have a clear vision and a detailed plan. They not only look at the quality of today's operations, but also at where their customers want to go in two or five years. And they make choices: which market segments do we serve, which do we not and how do we ensure that we remain distinctive for customers?' Building adaptive capacity plays a key role in this. This not only means being flexible in capacity, but also in adapting services and networks. Banning: 'Logistics in turbulent times requires agile entrepreneurs. To determine your own strategy, you need to know what your customer's strategy is. If a client is going to move its production or tap into new markets, you want to be ready for this as a logistics partner.'

## Steady growth in a shrinking market

“We want to be a leader in the transport of building materials,” says Arjen Tulner, co-owner of Van der Werff Logistics. Van der Werff is the market leader in deliveries with truck-mounted forklifts in the Benelux, and is a big name in the transport of large and unusual loads such as building materials and construction elements. Although the construction transport market fell by 12% in 2023, Van der Werff maintained its turnover well and continued to invest. The strategy is based on four pillars: good employment practices, sustainability, digitalization and economies of scale. 'I see it as a Monopoly board: you shouldn't sit in one corner, have a good spread in your customer portfolio and be present everywhere. Then you can achieve efficiency and a high load factor. It is also extremely important to be an attractive employer, we do everything we can to achieve this, because your employees are of course the driving force behind your company.'

> “In five years, the number of medium to large Dutch logistics companies will be a lot smaller.”

Bart Banning

Sector Banker Transport & Logistics

## Consolidation is shaking up the logistics landscape

The pressure on margins and the need for large investments are accelerating economies of scale in the sector. Mergers, acquisitions and international entrants have created a significantly changing playing field in recent years. 'In five years' time, the number of medium to large Dutch logistics companies will be a lot smaller. At the same time, a flexible layer of smaller companies will always be needed. You have to cherish them, because without them the sector will get stuck during peaks in demand.' Consolidation is also visible in inland shipping. Cooperatives can more easily invest in cleaner ships and digital systems, while smaller players look for partnerships to remain competitive.

> “Digitalization plays a key role for the future. Data helps with our load factor and productivity.”

Arjen Tulner

Co-owner Van der Werff Logistics

## Value of digitalization and AI

According to Banning, digitalization will help the sector considerably. 'The computing and solving power of AI can solve the route planning puzzle much faster and better than humans. That saves time, fuel and empty kilometers.' That potential is enormous, calculated McKinsey: companies that apply AI on a scale in their logistics processes can realize up to 15% cost savings. AI-driven route optimization can reduce transportation costs by 10 to 30%, while application in demand forecasting can improve forecast accuracy by 20 to even 50%. Supermarket deliveries can also be realized much more optimally. Thanks to AI planning, delivery vans for supermarket deliveries can drive up to 18% more efficiently, resulting in lower costs and fewer CO₂ emissions, according to recent research by Erasmus University Rotterdam and TKI Dinalog.

> “Innovating and working smarter is the only way to get more out of your existing capacity.”

Bart Banning

Sector Banker Transport & Logistics

## Increased efficiency through the use of drones and robots

There are also significant gains to be made in warehouses: automation with AI can reduce operational costs by 20 to 40%. Drones and robots also help with inventory and order picking. Banning: 'Innovating and working smarter is the only way to get more out of your existing capacity. Technology can also take a lot of work off your hands in customer communication, from automatic updates to predictive analyses.' Yet according to Banning, technology is not a goal in itself. 'As an entrepreneur you must carefully choose what you want to automate and why. Otherwise you run the risk of spending a lot of money without it contributing to your strategic goals.'

## Sustainability is a strategic theme

Making transport and logistics more sustainable is irreversible. Electric trucks, hydrogen, biofuels and cleaner inland vessels are increasingly becoming the norm. Municipalities are introducing zero-emission zones for city logistics, and clients are placing higher demands on CO₂ reduction. 'The pace varies per company, but the direction is clear. Those who invest now will be stronger later. You see that customers are also willing to choose demonstrably sustainable partners, even if the price is slightly higher.'

## It's about sustainability, digitalization and productivity

Van der Werff has received a third Lean & Green Star for sustainable working. 'Sustainability is self-evident to us. From electric trucks and forklifts to public charging plazas and solar panels. But also by planning smarter to save kilometers and CO₂ emissions. Digitalization plays a key role for the future. 'Measuring is knowing. Data helps with our load factor and productivity. We want to grow, but a healthy load factor, satisfied employees and a well-rounded customer network are more important to us than absolute volume.' Van der Werff counts on ABN AMRO for financing and sparring. ABN AMRO really understands our sector. They think along in scenarios, and that gives confidence. But ultimately we make our own choices. The bank can advise, we as entrepreneurs determine the course.'

## Opportunities in defense and infrastructure

Despite all the challenges, Banning also sees opportunities in the current dynamics. European investments in defense create new logistics flows, in which the Netherlands – and specifically the port of Rotterdam – plays a key role. That port has been designated by Defense for military transhipment and has now reserved special quay capacity for NATO transports, including facilities for ammunition and heavy equipment, Nieuwsblad Transport previously reported. “Dutch companies can respond to this, provided they can act quickly and have the right contacts,” says Banning. These new defense flows – from vehicles to construction materials for military bases – require precision, security and flexibility, qualities in which Dutch logistics has traditionally been strong.

> “Technology is one thing, but you also need people who can work with it.”

Bart Banning

Sector Banker Transport & Logistics

## Longer term: realism and vision

For the longer term, Banning takes a sober look at technological trends. Autonomous freight transport is already technically possible, but legislation and infrastructure are slowing down the rollout. Parcel delivery via drones is used here and there, but mainly for niche applications such as emergency medical deliveries. According to Banning, large-scale use in urban parcel delivery is not realistic for the time being. And the return of pneumatic tube in the form of the Hyperloop? 'An inspiring idea, but certainly not a practical solution for the next twenty years.' Where Banning does expect major steps is in the combination of automation and social innovation. 'Technology is one thing, but you also need people who can work with it. This means investing in training, in collaboration between companies, and in attracting talent who may not yet be thinking about logistics as a career option. Finally, he has an appeal for entrepreneurs. 'Logistics is all about movement. Anyone who stands still will be overtaken. But anyone who cleverly combines knowledge, network and flexibility can also make progress in uncertain times.'

After a difficult year, in which international trade declined due to a rapid reduction of excess inventories, the transport sector has started a cautious recovery. At the beginning of 2024, freight volumes increased in both container shipping and aviation.
