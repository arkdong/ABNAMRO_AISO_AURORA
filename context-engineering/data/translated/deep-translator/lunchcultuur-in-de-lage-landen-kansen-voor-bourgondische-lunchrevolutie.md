## Flanders leads, the Netherlands surprises

At 67.94 euros per person, Deinze is at the top of the rankings, followed by Knokke-Heist (59.70 euros) and Oudenaarde (58.67 euros). Coastal tourism, regional products and a pronounced “sit-and-enjoy” mentality push the amounts up. Of the first fifteen positions, fourteen are in Belgium, thirteen of them in Flanders. Yet the Burgundian spirit is blowing over: Zwolle (50.24 euros), Delft (36.71 euros) and Amersfoort (33.29 euros) show that a historic setting plus knowledge economy can also nourish a generous lunch culture in the Netherlands. The figures in this report are based on anonymized cash register data from more than 6,500 catering establishments that work with the Lightspeed platform in the period from April 2024 to March 2025.

## Millennials drive lunch sales

Figures from the FoodService Institute Netherlands (FSIN) show that younger generations in particular are driving this development. For example, 37 percent of Dutch people between the ages of 25 and 40 eat lunch outside the home at least weekly, significantly more often than older cohorts. Since 2018, average lunch expenditure in the Netherlands has increased by 57 percent, even after adjustment for inflation. Strikingly, the generational difference in spending remains relatively small—between 16 and 18 euros per lunch—underscoring the need for flexible, modular menus with light, plant-based options.

## Lunch moment as economic leverage

The lunch moment also takes on more importance from an economic point of view. According to Statistics Netherlands (CBS), more than 22 percent of Dutch companies will have revised their import or export markets in 2025, with Belgium often mentioned as an alternative closer to home. Since 2020, two-way trade in goods and services has grown by 55 percent to €195 billion, partly driven by geopolitical uncertainty. At the same time, the number of Belgian overnight guests in the Netherlands has doubled since 2010 to 2.6 million; Estimates point to more than 3 million by 2030. Behavioral research shows that joint lunches are also functional: business partners who order the same dish are more likely to reach an agreement, and teams that regularly have lunch together appear to be demonstrably more creative and productive.
