The Dutch greenhouse vegetable sector is among the world's best. Greenhouse vegetables are also performing well within the Dutch agricultural sector; the income of the Dutch greenhouse vegetable grower exceeds the agricultural average by a factor of 3. ## The greenhouse vegetable sector is among the world's best

Greenhouse vegetable cultivation is part of greenhouse horticulture, which consists of growing vegetables, fruit, flowers and plants in greenhouses. Dutch greenhouse vegetable cultivation covers approximately half of the area of ​​total Dutch greenhouse horticulture and is known for its high efficiency, rapid adaptability and innovative cultivation methods, including the application of protected, conditioned cultivation, substrate cultivation and advanced cultivation techniques such as geothermal heat and LED lighting. These factors ensure high yields per hectare with minimal use of resources, which contributes to the Netherlands' position as one of the strongest vegetable producers in Europe.

The Dutch greenhouse vegetable sector - which mainly grows tomatoes, peppers, cucumbers and eggplant - has developed into a high-tech top sector in the last decade. The Netherlands and Canada produce more than twice as many kilograms per square meter per hectare than number three, Mexico. The constant wave of innovation and the rapid implementation of technologies by entrepreneurs has created a high-performing sector to which the entire ecosystem of science, greenhouse builders, technology suppliers, breeders and data specialists has contributed.

Before then, business was considerably slower. In fact, in 2011 the sector faced major challenges. The credit crisis, the pressure on prices due to the rise of international competition from Spain and Morocco and the opaque sales market caused financial problems for Dutch growers. In 2011, five in ten growers were unable to meet their financial obligations and 65 percent were financially unhealthy. In response, the sector came together and asked consultant McKinsey for advice. A problem analysis revealed continuous pressure on prices and margins, lack of cooperation and efficiency in the chain, increasing international competitive pressure in a saturated market, high interchangeability of anonymous products and an underdeveloped market position. The recommendations – including the formation of the Federation Fruit Vegetable Organization – led to a positive change in the revenue model and market position of the Dutch greenhouse vegetable sector.

Since 2014, the Dutch greenhouse vegetable sector has been able to capitalize on the greatly improved efficiency into good financial results for the grower, see Figure 2. The income of the Dutch greenhouse vegetable grower exceeds the agricultural average by a factor of 3 over a period of ten years. This leads to the question of what the drivers are that have allowed the greenhouse vegetable ecosystem to flourish so much. We answer that question in the attached report.

In a subsequent analysis we will address the question: What can other agricultural subsectors learn from the insights from the greenhouse vegetable sector?
