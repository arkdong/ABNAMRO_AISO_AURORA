# Dutch industry sees orders drop, cuts jobs

[Analysis](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/analyses/index.html)

[Industry](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/industrie/index.html)

02/06/2025 • 3 minutes reading

[Albert Jan Swart](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/authors/albert-jan-swart/index.html)

The Nevi Purchasing Managers Index fell further in May, from 49.2 to 49.0. Despite the uncertainty surrounding international trade and a further decline in the number of new orders, Dutch industry increased production for the third month in a row, albeit slightly.

The overall index fell partly due to a further decline in employment. It fell for the tenth month in a row. And while at first mainly temporary workers and employees with a temporary contract were sent away, there are now also layoffs, partly due to reorganisations.

## Slightly more optimistic than in April

Entrepreneurs were slightly more optimistic in May than in April, according to the future production index, which reflects expectations for the next twelve months. A possible explanation is that entrepreneurs are relieved about the provisional trade agreement that the United States (US) and China concluded in Geneva on May 12.

The decrease in the number of new orders is also not too bad, given the great uncertainty. Order portfolios are shrinking, but only slightly. Although the US has lowered tariffs, American importers still have to pay hefty duties, for example 10 percent on most European products, 25 percent on cars and car parts, steel and aluminum. In addition, there is a risk that levies will soon increase again. President Trump is now threatening 50 percent tariffs on European products, but has paused discussions with the European Union (EU) until July 9. It is also possible that trade tensions between the US and China will escalate again.

## Possible assembly in the US

The Dutch machine industry, among others, exports relatively large amounts to the US. In recent years, approximately 15 to 20 percent of the machines for the food industry produced in the Netherlands were destined for the US, according to figures from Statistics Netherlands (CBS). Several Dutch producers indicate in discussions with ABN AMRO that ongoing projects are still continuing, but that the number of new orders is declining. Presumably, buyers are postponing their purchasing decisions. Some Dutch manufacturers who are already active in the US with a small sales office or distribution center are also considering assembling there to avoid import duties, although it will not be easy to recruit the technically trained employees required for this.

Although world trade has been disrupted, especially due to the rapidly escalating trade war between the US and China in April, supplies to Dutch industry are still functioning well. Delivery times even decreased in May, according to the Nevi survey. This may be because there is less demand from the US, which means that the European market can be supplied more quickly.

Trade tensions between the US and Europe could ease after the NATO summit in The Hague at the end of June. The European NATO member states are expected to make commitments about additional defense spending, money that may partly be spent on American weapon systems. That could reduce the EU's trade surplus with the US, which Trump denounces.

## Read more in the industrial sector

The industry is a heavyweight in the Dutch economy. Export is an important driver for this, but at the same time creates uncertainty. There are also themes that have a positive influence on growth, such as cooperation in the chain, innovation and sustainability.
