# Topping remains stuck in promise; only 7 percent of potential is utilised

Topping, the realization of homes on top of existing buildings, seems to be a smart, fast and sustainable construction solution. But a new analysis shows that housing associations are expected to realize only 4,805 homes through topping up until 2030, or only 7.2 percent of the estimated potential of 67,000 homes.

## Why topping still falters

Despite the technical and spatial advantages, topping appears to be difficult to get off the ground in practice. An important bottleneck lies in policy and regulations. Many municipalities still apply strict parking standards, which are difficult to fit into existing buildings. At the same time, permit processes are often slow, partly due to a lack of municipal capacity and experience with this specific form of development.

In addition, there is a lack of sufficient scale and standardization. Conceptual top solutions can reduce costs and shorten construction time, but they often lack sufficient volume to realize these benefits. Corporations hesitate to start multiple projects at the same time, partly due to unfamiliarity with the legal, technical and social implications.

> “Standardization of concepts helps to reduce costs and speed up procedures, but that is not enough.”

Leontien de Waal

Sector Banker Construction

## Organize the process smarter

According to Leontien de Waal, Construction sector banker at ABN AMRO, it is important to organize the process smarter in addition to technical solutions to give a boost to topping up. “Standardization of concepts helps to reduce costs and speed up procedures, but that is not enough.” It is precisely the process side, involving residents, creating support, and streamlining the implementation route, that is crucial. Developing builders, maintenance parties and concept providers can effectively relieve housing associations in this regard.

> “Topping up is in many cases competitive with regular new construction. But without process innovation and administrative courage, the potential remains untapped.”

Claire van Staaij

Sector Banker Real Estate

Claire van Staaij, Real Estate sector banker, also emphasizes that the combination of sustainability and housing addition is financially and ecologically attractive. “The environmental performance is convincing, and in many cases topping up is competitive with regular new construction. But without process innovation and administrative courage, the potential remains untapped.”

## Four practical interventions

In the report 'Topping: from promise to practice', we formulate a number of concrete recommendations:

1. Accelerate permitting, for example via faster BOPA routes
2. Unambiguous approval approaches for common top models
3. Stimulate scale, with financial incentives such as subsidies for cluster development or lower fees
4. Innovative financing, such as co-exploitation or links to healthcare and quality of life budgets (Wmo)

## Start with urban opportunities

Topping up is not a luxury option but a no-regret strategy in shaping the total housing construction task. Especially in urban areas, it offers fast, sustainable and land-saving housing construction. It also provides increased quality of life, means to make existing buildings more sustainable, and an upgrade of existing facilities and public space. But then topping should no longer be organized as an exception, but as a structural choice in the construction arsenal.
