# Personnel remains an important key to growth

Increased labor costs, limited growth in labor productivity and a shortage of personnel are putting a brake on growth in the food industry. Automation and robotization offer part of the solution. Retention of staff and the influx of young people form the other part.

Collective labor agreements in the food industry have risen sharply in recent years, as this 'Stand of Food' also shows. Because labor costs in the sector amount to approximately 17 percent of the cost of sales, entrepreneurs immediately feel an increase in wages. The July Business Survey of the Netherlands from the Central Bureau of Statistics (CBS) also shows that 34 percent of companies in the food sector see the labor shortage as the most important factor hindering further growth.

An earlier analysis by ABN AMRO also shows that labor productivity, the production per employee, of food companies has not increased in the last decade. Higher labor costs are not offset by an increase in productivity. Growth of the sector in the last decade therefore mainly resulted from the increase in the number of (cheap) labor hours employed. In addition, the available labor has become limited and more expensive.

Although the tightness on the labor market is decreasing slightly, the unemployment rate is still low at 4.0 percent in September. In addition, a significant number of political parties are considering taking measures for a more selective and targeted labor migration policy. These could make hiring migrant workers in certain sectors more difficult, expensive or even impossible.

Automation and robotization therefore remain important, but structurally the demand for labor in the food industry remains high. This is due to the aging population, the nature of the work and production processes that are difficult to automate.

## An aging workforce and little rejuvenation

The workforce in the food industry has been aging significantly since 2014. At 23.9 percent, the number of elderly people in the sector is one percentage point higher than the national average, while its increase over the years also shows that the workforce is further aging. It is striking that the share of young people is only 13.4 percent, while the national average is 16.9 percent.

## The importance of staff well-being

In order to recruit staff more effectively, retain them longer and increase labor productivity, employers would do well to focus on improving the well-being of their employees, according to previous ABN AMRO research. In addition, an understanding of what practically trained young people want is needed to promote the influx of personnel into the production environment. The remainder of this report will therefore look in more detail at the well-being of staff and what practically trained young people find important.

## Well-being in the food sector is slightly behind other sectors

The food industry caught up last year in the field of staff well-being. Yet the sector still has a long way to go. Employers should especially improve the health, personal development and work-life balance of staff.

Satisfied staff leads to a lower outflow of employees, resulting in lower costs for recruitment and training of staff, as well as retention of knowledge and expertise in the organization. While well-being deteriorated in most sectors last year, the food sector showed a small improvement. This is mainly due to significant progress in personnel safety. Last year the sector had fewer safety incidents than in 2023. If the sector also shows such an improvement in other aspects of well-being, it can catch up with other sectors.

Because growth in the food industry is still highly dependent on personnel, the key to growth lies largely in the well-being of employees. High well-being makes the organization attractive to new people, while existing employees are less likely to leave the organization. Moreover, employees with higher well-being are sick less often and are more productive. Food manufacturers who invest in personnel are rewarded with more turnover and higher returns.
