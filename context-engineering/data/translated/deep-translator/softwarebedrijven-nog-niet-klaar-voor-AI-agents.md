# Software companies not yet ready for AI agents

[Report](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/reports/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

08/07/2025 • 15 minutes reading

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/authors/julia-krauwer/index.html)

While four out of five software companies expect AI agents to noticeably change the software landscape, nearly a third of enterprises are not yet technically ready to embed the technology. Half are in the experimental phase, and a quarter are not yet doing anything with agentic AI.

A large majority of software companies expect that the software landscape will noticeably change due to 'AI agents' - smart programs that can independently perform complex tasks. Nearly half of those surveyed even expect drastic or fundamental changes in the way software is designed, used or controlled, according to the survey by ABN AMRO and Panelwizard among 204 Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) companies. SaaS is software that is purchased in a subscription form and is offered as a cloud service.

## AI agents are transforming the software market

The development of artificial intelligence (AI) is moving from generating text, software code and images within an app to independently completing complete work processes. Examples are AI agents that manage inventories, send quotes or prepare and implement a complete social media strategy. Through agentic AI, SaaS companies can make their product more attractive, for example by automating tasks or by introducing an AI agent as a point of contact for the user.

## The AI agent is not a new concept

The term 'intelligent agent' was introduced in the 1980s to describe systems that can perceive, reason and act independently. However, advances in technology have made AI agents much more powerful in recent years. Through 'Large Language Models' (LLMs), the engine of ChatGPT, among other things, agents have the necessary knowledge about the world, which ensures smooth communication. With the rise of so-called 'reasoning models', language models that can reason step by step, agents can also devise strategies themselves and determine the associated intermediate steps. By linking to external applications, AI agents can actually perform actions, such as launching an online advertising campaign or contacting a customer or supplier.

Aside from the opportunities to improve existing SaaS or create new, AI-first SaaS, the rise of agents could also impact the market on a deeper level. According to Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella, this means the end of software in its current form. According to the CEO, in the foreseeable future people will no longer work with separate software packages, but with AI agents that independently draw from various data sources and carry out the necessary actions based on them. To automatically prepare a customer quotation, the AI ​​agent retrieves information from the customer database, combines it with current product or price information, and, if necessary, adds financial conditions based on previous customer behavior. After checking – or completely autonomously – the quotation is automatically sent by e-mail. The user no longer has to switch between systems themselves, because the thinking and control work is done by application-independent agents, according to Nadella.

AI agents can also create completely new applications. For example, AI assistant Claude from tech company Anthropic creates immediately usable apps based on a few user instructions. With specific software such as Lovable, Windsurf and Cursor, people without programming knowledge can also develop software, apps and websites; the AI ​​agent interprets the user's query and converts it into code – in some tools the result is even immediately visible. Although creating good, safe and reliable software is a profession in itself, this trend of 'vibe coding' could mean that more and more companies choose to develop tools themselves instead of purchasing them in the form of SaaS.

## An AI agent as a point of contact

The influence of agents will extend across multiple levels. For example, 75 percent of respondents assume that the buttons and menus we now see on our screens will at least partly make way for an AI agent as a point of contact. This takes tasks from the user, executes them in the background and provides feedback on the outcome. Many people are now familiar with such a form of interaction from smart AI assistants such as ChatGPT, Gemini and Claude.

Samir Selimi, founder of software company PurchAIse, has also put forward an AI agent on his platform. The assistant, called PAI, can handle a wide range of supplier management tasks, such as validating data, placing orders and renegotiating contracts. Selimi sees the chat with PAI as a smart addition to the traditional user interface, which he believes will still remain valuable. “We're used to things being represented visually when we interact with a computer,” he says. “You don't need forty screens or sixty tabs, but you do need something like a cockpit where you as a user can keep an overview.”

It is expected that communication in natural language - or ordinary spoken and written language as people use it themselves - will play an important role within software. “You see a shift in the way users interact with software,” says Jelle Visser of ValueBlue, an enterprise architecture platform. ValueBlue makes software that allows companies to schematically record, analyze and improve their IT landscape. “If a business analyst wants to understand what exactly such an architecture plate tells him, it is nice if he can also ask that question on our platform.” For themselves, 38 percent of software companies currently see opportunities for an AI agent as a point of contact. Visser himself feels there is no room to wait quietly. "As a software company, you have to reinvent your user interface. If we don't do it, our competitor will."

## Communicating brand identity through agents

A fifth of the companies surveyed are afraid that this will no longer make their user experience distinctive. What is currently done via mouse clicks and menu structures can at least partly be replaced by typing or speaking a single sentence. This could put pressure on an important element of distinctiveness of software companies – design, ease of use and visual branding.

A third of respondents see the user experience as their most important source of distinctiveness for the future. Even if the interaction with software becomes more 'conversational', there are still plenty of opportunities to give the AI ​​agent a clear brand identity. Ever since the early emergence of chatbots on company websites, experts have been looking at ways to give these digital assistants a personality that fits the brand - for example with a suitable avatar, name and way of communicating.

## A different dashboard for each user

More than a quarter of SaaS companies see benefit in ad hoc generated screens. “Then you tell an agent: the window must be green, with corners and no roundings. And suddenly there is a screen, adapted to your preferences,” illustrates Franklin Evers of software company Usere light-heartedly. The company wants to keep its 'front-end', the part that the user sees, flexible in any case. Co-founder Pim Schipper: "Every employee benefits from a different type of information. Through agentic AI, everyone can get a tailor-made dashboard."

Visser from ValueBlue also recognizes the tension. “The people who use our software all have different roles and different maturity levels. But it is a lot of work to program a separate version of the software for everyone.” According to him, agentic AI can offer a solution in this regard. “By providing an agent with the necessary context about a role, it can determine what screens and functions an individual user needs.”

Dashboards generated on the spot, or even entire applications and games created from scratch, are not a hypothetical scenario. For example, researchers developed an AI model with which the shooting game Doom can be generated ad hoc. The player encounters enemies and navigates through all kinds of spaces without the need for any software code; there is only an AI model, trained on large amounts of footage from the game. Google made a prototype in which their latest Gemini model creates a user interface, always based on the events in the previous screen - so no menu was fixed in advance.

## Software as a silent force in the background

Of the SaaS companies surveyed, 16 percent fear that their software will fade into the background – a concern that is not completely out of the blue as consumers and companies increasingly turn to independent AI assistants. ChatGPT and Claude now support so-called 'actions' with which they independently perform tasks in other applications, such as sending emails or adjusting CRM data. The established order in the tech world is also increasingly positioning themselves as coordinating platform players. Microsoft lets companies build their own AI agents with Copilot Studio, which can call not only Microsoft apps but also external software. Salesforce developed a similar infrastructure with Agentforce.

One in five software companies is considering connecting to such AI agent platforms. A quarter do not focus on specific platforms, but want to make their own software 'agent-ready' in a broader sense. These entrepreneurs make functions available in a modular manner, provide clear documentation, and design their software in such a way that instructions from external agents - whether or not in natural language - can easily be translated into concrete actions within the application. “As a software company, you have to ensure that LLMs can talk to your back end,” summarizes Dennis De Reus of Stellar. He is the co-founder of Stellar, a platform that allows companies to develop their own customer service agents.

Nearly a third of companies focus more broadly on improving data access, linking options and standardized communication with other systems ('interoperability'). Among them is Last Mile Solutions, a software platform for electric transport service providers – such as charging station operators – that offers technology for payments, fraud detection, and preventing overload of the electricity grid, among other things. In order to offer these services, a lot of data is needed, such as information about the amount of green energy generated at a certain time and how full individual car batteries are.

“That data can be used by other parties to develop AI models and specialized agents,” says marketing director Ben Ridgway. “We therefore offer a 'data backbone' with which we enable AI innovations, such as smart charging and predictive maintenance.” Ridgway of Last Mile Solutions, which serves as a central hub for e-mobility software, fully agrees. "Our back-end is extremely complex. There is so much data that we manage and must make available correctly. It is crucial for us to demonstrate time and time again that we are a reliable partner."

## Develop applications à la carte

AI agents do not necessarily operate within the boundaries of an application, but can also switch between separate databases and tools to perform tasks independently. For example, an agent who retrieves historical sales data from system A, predicts future sales figures based on an algorithm from system B and displays the corresponding graph in a dashboard of system C. It is not so much about the total package, but the way in which individual pieces of software can be accessed by AI systems.

Even before the emergence of AI agents, many software companies were breaking up their systems into small, reusable modules and making them available via so-called APIs or SDKs. Many AI models are also offered this way, so that developers can, for example, embed a smart chatbot on their own website. “Creating software is increasingly a matter of combining all kinds of components,” says Visser of ValueBlue. “Entering the software market is therefore easier than ever.”

This also applies to AI agents, which can draw 'à la carte' from data and functions from different suppliers. However, only a quarter of SaaS companies appear to have the preconditions in place for this. They have the necessary digital connections in the form of APIs – short for 'application programming interfaces' – or are sometimes even explicitly designed to work together with AI agents and agent platforms.

If software companies make individual elements of their systems available on a large scale, it can become quite difficult for external agents to always determine which function they purchase from which party. Sebastiaan den Boer, Director AI and Data Science at digital agency Youwe: "If you always have to let an AI agent choose between twenty tools and fifteen databases, you also have to record the required knowledge in a prompt. That can quickly become a very complex instruction."

## Existing software under attack by AI agents

Some companies (14 percent) see the risk that specific functions in their software will become redundant as externally developed AI agents perform tasks in their own way, without using existing software modules. An example is an agent that divides customers into segments itself, instead of calling a built-in filter function in existing software. This fear exists only to a limited extent among companies with an annual turnover of more than 25 million euros (7 percent), while a quarter of smaller companies fear this.

Another 9 percent fear that their carefully constructed logic or AI models will no longer be used, for example when agents developed elsewhere can make decisions based on their own reasoning skills. For example, a recruitment agent could rate applicants using a proprietary scoring method, completely ignoring the software's internal matching model. This previously purchased HR software then only functions as a data source, or is even bypassed entirely.

The Giant of Stellar is less afraid of such a scenario. He can imagine that software users will increasingly interact with AI agents, but is convinced that the underlying systems will often remain relevant. "For example, an agent cannot store data haphazardly. You need a system that ensures that this happens according to certain structures and rules. The same applies to customer identification; you need modules that allow you to determine someone's identity with certainty, and then rules that determine what data a particular customer is or is not allowed to view." According to Franklin Evers, co-founder of Usere, this is exactly how software companies distinguish themselves. “It is precisely on that smart intermediate layer that your intellectual property is located, and you must protect it,” says Evers, who is developing an AI-driven marketing solution with partner Pim Schipper.

## Smartly automate tasks

The companies with fundamental concerns about their business model are in the minority. Software companies are sensing massive opportunities to improve their own product with AI agents. Two-thirds of respondents see value in agents who perform tasks on behalf of the user, such as retrieving information, or go through processes that previously took place based on strict steps and rules. Six in ten companies would like to use AI agents to support their own customers.

For example, PurchAIse uses agentic AI for supplier management, including collecting and providing market insights. For example, an automatically detected price drop in ingredients can be a reason to renegotiate a contract, for which AI agent PAI then also sends an email to the supplier. Usere focuses primarily on marketing and relieves SME companies of all the worries by, among other things, automating the creation and placement of social media posts.

There is concern among respondents about the possible unpredictability of AI agents. Nearly half of software companies see a lack of transparency and control as a real risk. AI agents do not work according to fixed rules, but based on probability; they choose the most likely next step and its implementation, but not necessarily the right one.

“It is difficult to set up processes well and predictably with agentic AI,” says Rogier Hosman, co-founder of digital agency Youwe, which develops digital solutions for companies. Hosman knows from experience what is needed for a successful implementation. “Do I have sufficient control over the technology, or will half of my SaaS product soon collapse? What risk do I want to take?” Although concrete rules can also be given here to direct the agent, it is a fundamentally different way of working than with traditional software.

## Technically, a lot still needs to be done

Although software companies identify numerous opportunities in the field of agentic AI, the question is whether they can take the necessary steps in the short term. Nearly thirty percent of respondents are unsure whether they are technically ready for AI agents; their software architecture or organization is not yet sufficiently equipped for the application of and integration with agents. It is striking that the smallest companies, with an annual turnover of less than 2 million euros, see this risk much less - possibly because their IT landscape is less complex and therefore more flexible.

It is also striking that a large proportion of software companies (53 percent) are still in the experimental phase with AI agents. These companies are exploring the technology, but have not yet structurally embedded it in their product. More than a quarter have no concrete plans in the field of agentic AI at all. The maturity in the field of agentic AI appears to be highly dependent on company size; There is hardly any large company (turnover greater than 25 million euros) that does not yet use the technology, while within micro companies (turnover less than 2 million euros) this applies to 40 percent. Vertical software companies that focus on specific sectors are also less advanced on average than software companies with a broader orientation.

This limited maturity is worrying, because a new generation of technology companies is storming the market. In 2024, according to data from Dealroom, around $3.2 billion was invested globally in technology start-ups that have embedded agentic AI into their services. It is an exponential growth compared to the previous years, which recorded 727 million and 1.3 billion in investments respectively.

Moreover, not all customers of SaaS companies are ready for large-scale application of AI agents, says Selimi of PurchAIse. “Technology can do everything in theory, but in practice companies find it difficult to adapt. It is often the people and processes that fail.”

## Distinctiveness of the future

Amid this technological transformation, software companies are clinging to the relationships they have built with their customers. More than half – and within large companies even two-thirds – expect to retain the most distinctive character here in the long term, for example due to a deep understanding of the customer context or cooperation in the implementation and further development of the software. Schipper van Usere is on the same page and even thinks there is room for improvement. “You have to invest in the relationship with your customer. Many software companies have neglected to really listen to their customers for a long time.”

For the time being, AI often lacks context that is essential in specialist sectors. That is why a significant number of software companies expect to be able to fall back on domain knowledge in the future, such as relevant legislation and regulations or industry-specific processes. “Ultimately you need domain expertise to really add value,” says Hosman of Youwe. Selimi from PurchAIse agrees. “One of the ways to distinguish yourself as a software company is to know your end market and the associated processes inside and out.”

This awareness is logically stronger among vertical software companies (47 percent), which already focus on specific niches, than among horizontal players (32 percent). Also among large companies, a higher percentage (55 percent) expects to be able to remain distinctive through deep domain knowledge than SMEs and micro companies, where the percentage is approximately one third.

## Investing at the front or back

The 'soft' elements of customer relationship and domain knowledge are followed by concrete matters, directly visible in the software: the functional coverage or 'feature set' of the software, and the user experience - both mentioned by a third of respondents as points of differentiation in the long term.

Conversely, it is the technical matters in the background – data, integration power, and algorithms – that are lowest in the order. According to Schipper and Evers from Usere, software companies would do well to continue investing under the hood. Evers: “You must keep the core of your software very robust. Other parties can then talk to it via an API. They must have the feeling that they can build on your technology and trust it.” Ridgway from Last Mile Solutions, which acts as a spider in the web in the field of e-mobility software, fully agrees. "Our back-end is extremely complex. There is so much data that we manage and must make available in the right way. It is important for us to always demonstrate that we are a reliable partner in this."

## Adaptability is essential

SaaS companies agree: the software market will change significantly in the coming years. This way, they will be able to optimize their software themselves with AI agents, as a point of contact or in the background. In the meantime, the well-known chat assistants are acquiring an increasingly prominent position. Now that they are becoming the daily starting point for more and more users and are increasingly integrated with all kinds of tools, there is also a growing chance that existing software companies will become less visible in one way or another.

The emergence of platforms that allow users to build agents or software themselves may also mean that activities that were previously supported by software packages are now handled by individually manufactured AI agents or self-built software. Some software companies see merit in an offensive strategy in which they themselves fulfill a platform role, or rely on the unique added value of (elements of) their software.

A black-and-white scenario in which SaaS is completely wiped out by AI agents developed elsewhere is unlikely to happen. However, the new reality does require the necessary adaptability from software companies; links with external tools and parties are more important than ever, and making the much-needed automation yourself is essential to stay relevant.
