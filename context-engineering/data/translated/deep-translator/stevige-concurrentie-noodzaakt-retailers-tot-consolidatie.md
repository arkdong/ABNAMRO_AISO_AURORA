# Stiff competition forces retailers to consolidate

[Sector forecast](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/sectorprognoses/index.html)

[Retail](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/retail/index.html)

04/12/2025 • 5 minutes reading

[Gerarda Westerhuis](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/authors/gerarda-westerhuis/index.html)

While consumer confidence is improving, pressure on retailers remains high due to rising costs, online competition and a rapidly changing customer journey. At the same time, the sector is aging rapidly: many entrepreneurs want to stop within ten years, but have not yet arranged a successor.

For 2026 and 2027, ABN AMRO expects consumption to grow again by 1 percent and 1.2 percent respectively. The recovery in domestic demand is supported by rising purchasing power and improved consumer confidence. In November, confidence increased from -27 to -21, the largest increase in more than four years. Sentiment surrounding larger purchases is also improving slightly. However, spending behavior remains cautious, as evidenced by the high savings rate; consumers choose to save rather than consume. Price perception plays a role in this: groceries such as meat, coffee and chocolate have risen sharply in price, which continues to give the feeling that consumption in general is expensive. Our sector estimates for broad retail – retail trade, wholesale non-food and car trade and repair – have been adjusted upwards from 0.5 to 1 percent volume growth for 2026, followed by 1.5 percent in 2027.

## Physical stores under pressure

Against this backdrop, structural challenges for retailers remain persistent. Staff shortages and rising costs are putting pressure on continuity, while competitive pressure is increasing due to the dominance of online platforms such as Bol, Coolblue and Amazon and ultra-cheap platforms such as Shein and Temu. The structural price pressure they cause makes it difficult for physical stores to remain distinctive and profitable. Online shopping continues to grow and is increasingly developing into a hybrid form in which physical stores are used for advice, immediate product availability, more efficient returns handling and creating a brand experience. The customer journey therefore becomes more complex and requires investments in technology, data analysis and integration of the online channel.

To remain competitive within this dynamic, economies of scale and innovation are necessary. Retailers must invest in digitalization and artificial intelligence to work more efficiently and gain better insight into customer behavior. This investment need mainly puts pressure on smaller companies, which are increasingly opting to join franchise formulas or be taken over by larger chains.

## Electrification requires automotive innovation

Similar issues arise in the car trade and repairs, such as staff shortages and high costs. In addition, the business model is shifting. Aftersales is the major driver of the sector, but that will change in the long term due to a more electric fleet. The transition to electrification requires different skills from technicians and investments in technology. The capital intensity is also high due to investments in buildings, equipment and stock. Just as in retail, economies of scale are important for greater investment power and to remain interesting for employees; many small and medium-sized car dealers and garages are being taken over by dealer holding companies.

## Silent wave of succession?

Against this background, aging is another structural theme: currently, almost one in ten entrepreneurs in the retail sector (in companies with at least two employees) are 65 years or older, according to data from Statistics Netherlands (CBS). That amounts to almost 9,000 entrepreneurs; for car trade and repair this is more than 12 percent and approximately 2,500 entrepreneurs. Our survey conducted by Ipsos I&O on behalf of ABN AMRO shows that almost 65 percent of retailers want to close their business within ten years. Of this group, more than a quarter have not yet started succession plans and a significant proportion (17 percent) are working on succession, but have not yet found anyone.

The picture is diverse within the different types of succession, as shown by the Ipsos survey. Transmission within the family is still common. Of the entrepreneurs who want to stop within ten years, 17 percent say they want to transfer the business within the family. Transfer to third parties is also a relevant option. For example, more than 7 percent indicate that they want to transfer the company to an employee. Transfer to, for example, the manager can guarantee continuity of the company, which is important for maintaining customer relationships. This percentage is relatively high compared to the other sectors. Sales to chains or franchisors are common in the retail sector due to the need for economies of scale; 13 percent indicate that they are considering a strategic acquisition. For example, men's fashion store OFM is growing not only by opening new branches but also through takeovers of existing stores. This often concerns entrepreneurs aged around 60 who have no successor, [according to CEO Arthur Feenstra](https://www.deondernemer.nl/actueel/arthur-feenstra-ofm-herenmode-groei-ai-familiebedrijf-marketing~9091ea4?referrer=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.google.com%2F).

Also interesting is the relatively high score of entrepreneurs who say they want to close their business (15 percent). Reasons for this may be that there is a lack of follow-up or the earning prospects are insufficient. This latter problem is especially prevalent in non-food retail, where margins are thin and costs are high. Younger generations are less likely to choose traditional retail careers and are more likely to turn their attention to digital business models. The high investment requirement for these generations constitutes an additional barrier, making it difficult to obtain sufficient financial resources.

## Opportunities for successful concepts

At the same time, large successful retailers are opting for private equity to accelerate growth and gain expertise. An example is MyJewellery, which sold a majority stake to private equity company Freshstream. This investment brought not only capital, but also international knowledge, strategic guidance and experienced directors to the company. This illustrates how external investors can play a role in professionalization, expansion and modernization.

## Investing remains essential

Investing in digitalization, omnichannel strategy and data analysis is essential for retail entrepreneurs to remain relevant in a market in which technology is increasingly making a difference. In any case, it is wise to develop a succession plan in a timely manner, given demographic developments and the declining number of internal successors. Finally, distinctiveness remains a key to success. Smaller retailers can profile themselves through specialization, local anchoring and good service, so that they remain attractive to customers and can maintain their position in a market characterized by consolidation.
