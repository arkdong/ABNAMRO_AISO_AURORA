# Tech entrepreneurs navigate between ambition and limitation

[Analysis](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/analyses/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

04/06/2025 • 3 minutes reading

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/authors/julia-krauwer/index.html)

The European business climate for tech companies has been under discussion for years. In their rapid growth, young technology companies often encounter complex regulations, fragmented markets and limited access to capital. However, legislators promise improvement, and entrepreneurs see the advantages of the relatively stable Europe.

Europe is known for its strong regulations, but this rarely works in favor of fast-growing technology companies. Startups looking to scale up face 27 national tax systems, labor laws and insolvency rules. Each country has its own interpretation of profit tax, shareholder structures and dismissal law - something that hinders rapid international growth. According to the [State of European Tech report](https://www.investeurope.eu/media/g3yobbcs/soet2024_report.pdf), 47 percent of founders and investors see regulation and policymaking as a major barrier to growth.

## Limited access to growth capital

Another frequently mentioned frustration is the limited access to growth capital. Since 2015, European growth-stage startups have raised around $300 billion, but that could have been double that if they could close large rounds as easily as in the US, according to the report's authors. European companies do get through the first financing rounds, but fall back on investments of 15 million dollars or more: only 4.1 percent reach that threshold, compared to 8.3 percent in the US.

One of the causes is the lack of deep capital buffers. European pension funds, for example, invest only 0.01 percent of their assets in global venture capital, more than three times less than their American counterparts. As a result, it remains difficult for European startups to scale up into the kind of tech companies that support digital autonomy.

## Europe isn't that bad after all

Yet the narrative that Europe is unfriendly to tech entrepreneurs is making way for a new story. In the State of European Tech 2024, a large majority of European founders indicated that they would relocate their company in Europe if they could make that choice again. According to Maarten Cleeren of Techleap, the current geopolitical situation can even act as a flywheel: “America is turning inward, the market is destabilizing. This creates space for companies in Europe.”

The European Commission now wants to put an end to the patchwork of legislation and regulations with the '28th regime'. This allows a company to register under a set of rules that is valid in all Member States. The plans are part of the broader European Innovation Act. This should make it easier for startups to raise growth capital, also across the border. Investors currently encounter different tax rules, regulators and legal structures per country. Europe wants to make this simpler, so that funds and investors can operate more smoothly in multiple countries.

## Smart reward, better financing

Reforms should also make it more attractive for startups to reward staff with stock options. Since 2019, eleven countries have relaxed their national regulations, including Germany, France, Spain and the Netherlands. The European Commission is now investigating whether an overarching system is possible, as part of broader efforts to improve the business climate.

At the same time, political pressure is growing for pension funds to play a more active role. They now invest an average of only 0.007 percent of their assets in venture capital. In theory they can invest larger amounts, but strict rules and internal guidelines discourage this. European institutions such as the Commission, the Investment Bank and the Investment Fund see pension funds as an important link in increasing the amount of growth capital in Europe, and are investigating ways to boost this - for example by spreading risk through EU funds or adjusted capital requirements.

The conditions for a stronger ecosystem for startups and scale-ups are slowly starting to fall into place. It is not only Brussels that is moving, entrepreneurs themselves also see the benefits of Europe. This is essential, because technological sovereignty starts with retaining our own innovative power.
