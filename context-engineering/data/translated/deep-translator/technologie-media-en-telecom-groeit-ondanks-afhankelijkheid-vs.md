# Technology, Media and Telecom growing despite US dependence

[Sector forecast](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/sectorprognoses/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

19/06/2025 • 5 minutes reading

[Mario Bersem](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/authors/mario-bersem/index.html)

Despite the trade issues and a strong dependence on the United States (US), ABN AMRO expects the Technology Media and Telecom (TMT) sector to show healthy growth of 4.5 percent in 2025 and 4 percent in 2026. Digitalization, cloud migrations and AI innovations are the driving forces behind the expected growth.

Entrepreneurs in the TMT sector are surprisingly even more positive than a year ago about their export turnover, as shown by the latest Economic Survey of the Netherlands (COEN) for the second quarter of the Central Bureau of Statistics (CBS). For gross domestic product (GDP), expected growth is 1.1 percent in 2025 and 1.2 percent in 2026.

Because American import duties have a major impact on world trade and the Dutch economy, the TMT sector is also affected. For example, via the cyclically sensitive media, including advertising agencies, which generally follow macroeconomic developments; ICT wholesale also suffers from trade barriers. But because the American import duties currently only apply to goods, the direct effect on important software and IT services remains limited.

If the trade battle escalates further and also affects services, the TMT sector could be hit particularly hard, due to its strong interconnection with American technology and its dependence on American software, cloud services and applications based on artificial intelligence (AI). The European Union (EU), with the so-called Anti-Coercion Instrument (ACI), has a legal framework in place to take countermeasures aimed at American services that the EU imports. Import duties are an important part of this Brussels plan, but on top of this, restrictions on market access or stricter competition rules for American big tech could also be imposed.

## The growth pearl of the Dutch economy

The Dutch TMT sector includes companies active in information technology (IT), such as software developers, IT service providers, hardware manufacturers and wholesalers; companies active in the production and distribution of media, including publishers, and marketing and communications agencies; and companies active in telecom.

There are many innovative and fast-growing companies in the sector, such as Amsterdam-based WeTransfer, the cloud service provider aimed at creative professionals that started as a simple file sharer. The sector is the growth jewel of the Dutch economy, with an average growth of 6 percent per year over the past thirty years, while GDP grew by an average of only 2 percent. The sector represents approximately 5.4 percent of the Netherlands' GDP and employs approximately 410,000 people.

## TMT focused on foreign countries and exports to the US

The TMT sector earns relatively much abroad. The total added value of the sector, output minus intermediate consumption, amounts to 45.9 billion euros, equal to 5.4 percent of GDP based on figures for 2024. More than half of the total added value of the sector is earned through exports abroad, approximately 23.7 billion euros.

Of the total export earnings of the TMT sector, approximately 10.5 percent or 2.5 billion euros comes from the US, ABN AMRO estimates based on data from Statistics Netherlands. The importance of the US as a sales market is therefore almost twice as great for the TMT sector as for the Dutch economy as a whole. About 20,000 people working in the sector are involved in exports to the US.

Exports also include certain American activities on Dutch soil. The TMT sector has proportionately the most American multinationals of all industries and this is visible in export statistics. There is a vibrant international service trade within multinationals. For example, Dutch data center subsidiaries export services to the American parent company, which are included in Dutch exports to the US.

In their quest to expand computing power and network capacity, American tech companies have their eye on the Netherlands as a strategic location for their servers and data centers. For example, Microsoft built a 'hyperscaler' in Middenmeer, with 300MW of power capacity; Amazon mainly rents capacity for its servers in so-called 'colocation data centers'.

## TMT sector and business community are highly dependent on big tech

On balance, the Netherlands – and also Europe – has a trade deficit in telecommunications, computer and information services, because it imports more of these services than it exports. Almost all Dutch companies and institutions use software and cloud services from the US, mainly from Microsoft, Google and Amazon, Salesforce and Oracle. This dependence applies in particular to tech companies involved in software applications or IT services; they often build their business models on an American foundation. Because the AI ​​revolution is also dominated by American companies, that dependence will remain for the time being.

The trade deficit shows the dependence of Dutch and European companies and institutions on the US. Import duties, restrictions on market access or stricter competition rules for American big tech could hit the US hard if the trade war escalates further. At the same time, such measures also affect almost all European companies and institutions that are faced with rising costs or have to look for European alternatives.

To break the dominance of big tech, Europe is committed to strengthening its own technological foundations through initiatives such as the European Chips Act, the InvestAI initiative and the Dutch ECOFED project. These initiatives are aimed at increasing technological self-reliance in Europe.

Despite rising economic and political tensions with the US, consumers are expected to continue to have a strong preference for American films and music. The import dependence of the US on the film, TV and radio industries shows this. Nearly 40 percent of all services imported by the industry come from America, which is the highest import dependence of all industries.
