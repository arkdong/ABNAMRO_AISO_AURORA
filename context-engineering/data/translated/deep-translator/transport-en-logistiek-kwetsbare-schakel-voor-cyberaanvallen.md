# Transport and logistics are a vulnerable link for cyber attacks

The transport and logistics sector forms the backbone of the Dutch economy and is therefore also a vulnerable link for cyber attacks. An attack on a specific transport company can disrupt the entire chain. In light of increasing digitalization, the sector is increasingly vulnerable to cyber attacks.

Advanced technologies such as AI can strengthen defense mechanisms, but at the same time pose a risk. It is therefore crucial that transport and logistics companies strengthen their digital resilience to ensure the continuity of their services.

Technological innovations such as IoT (Internet of Things) solutions and robotics have improved logistics efficiency and led to growth and higher productivity. At the same time, with these new technologies, the landscape has become more threatening and complex. Transportation systems fall under critical infrastructure and are therefore often targeted by both politically and financially motivated cyber threats.

## Recent attacks in the transportation and logistics sector

- In 2024, [Schneider Logistics and AB Texel](https://www.nt.nl/wegtransport/2024/02/29/hackers-slaan-toe-bij-ab-texel-geen-impact-op-dienstdiensten/) were attacked by the hacker group Cactus. The group used ransomware, software that captures and retains data until the victim pays a ransom. After the attack, a 'recovery operation' was initiated, according to an AB Texel spokesperson. The police and the Dutch Data Protection Authority were also informed.
- [Klarenbeek Transport](https://www.ccinfo.nl/menu-nieuws-trends/cyberattacks-weekoverzichten/2163910_slachtofferanalyse-en-trends-van-week-46-2024) discovered on November 15, 2024 that it had been the target of an attack by the Blacksuit group. Cybercriminals have stolen critical data and encrypted important files, threatening business operations and customer data.
- On Thursday, November 21, 2024, the logistics systems of Jumbo and Hema, which run on the software of [Blue Yonder](https://www.computable.nl/2024/11/29/ransomware-bij-softwaresupplier-treft-logistieke-systemen-hema-en-jumbo/#:~:text=Ransomware%20bij%20softwaresupplier%20tref t%20logistics%20systems%20Hema%20and%20Jumbo,-29%20November%202024&text=Hema%20and%20Jumbo%20kampen%20with,automated%20logistics%20processes%20in%20distribution centers.), attacked with ransomware. The backup systems have taken the hit and the software has been temporarily disabled.
- Our neighboring countries are also confronted with cybercrime. For example, in 2024 [Belgian port websites](https://www.nt.nl/havens/2024/10/08/websites-belgische-havens-en-gemeenten-getroffen-door-cyberattacks/) were hit by a DDoS attack, making them temporarily inaccessible. The websites were previously attacked by a pro-Russian hacker group called NoName 057.
- According to the Czech Transport Minister Kupka, the European railway network is exposed to cyber threats from Russia, writes the [Financial Times](https://www.ft.com/content/f8207823-f5e1-4caf-934d-67c648f807bf). The consequences of these cyber threats can be fatal. So far, Russian threats have not resulted in serious disruptions to the rail network.

## Cybersecurity in the sector

Research by ABN AMRO from May 2025 shows that 80 percent of transport entrepreneurs state that cybercrime is a risk within their organization. 16 percent indicate that they have suffered financial damage due to cyber incidents. Phishing emails, invoice fraud and malware are particularly common attack methods.

[Read also: Cyber resilience of entrepreneurs lags behind reality](/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/alle-sectoren/cyberweerbaarheid-ondernemers-blijft-achter-bij-reueel.html)

The same research from ABN AMRO shows that companies are continuing their investments in the security of their digital environment. 58 percent of participants indicate that they have spent approximately the same on cybersecurity as in 2023. A minority of participants (36 percent) indicate that they have invested more in cybersecurity than in 2023. The main reason for this is the increase in the cyber threat and the need to comply with legislation, such as the European cybersecurity directive NIS2 and the GDPR, which sets requirements for data protection.

Entrepreneurs see challenges in employee discipline (53 percent T&L versus 37 percent of the total of all Dutch business sectors) and the rapid emergence of new threats. An example of a rapidly emerging threat is a cyber attack using AI. The majority of transport entrepreneurs (60 percent) consider innovation in the field of AI to be a threat to their organization. At the same time, 55 percent of companies see AI as an opportunity to strengthen cybersecurity.

The transport sector has a realistic self-image when it comes to cyber risks. About half of transport companies (47 percent) indicate that cybercrime poses a major risk to their organization. This percentage is higher than in other sectors, where on average 32 percent of companies experience this risk.

## NIS2: new European legislation must improve cyber resilience

The NIS2, the successor to the Network and Information Security Directive (NIS), came into force in the European Union in October 2024. This guideline focuses on critical organizations and sectors where failure of their services can cause social and economic disruption. In these cases, the law aims to improve the cybersecurity of key services in Member States. The Netherlands has not managed to transpose this directive into national law within the set period. National legislation, consisting of the Cybersecurity Act (Cbw/NIS2) and the Resilience of Critical Entities Act (Wwke/CER), is in preparation. These laws are expected to come into effect in the third quarter.

[Read also: Cybersecurity Act: prepare yourself for stricter rules](/nl/zakelijk/insights/cybersecurity/veiligheid/cybersecuritywet-prepare-je-voor-op-stricter-rules.html)

The recent research by ABN AMRO indicates that 56 percent of transport companies are not familiar with the NIS2 obligations. At the same time, 36 percent indicate that they can fully comply with the obligations of NIS2. Not all transport companies are therefore ready for the NIS2 obligations. However, it is wise to make preparations now, because companies that are not NIS2 compliant run the risk of their clients dropping out.

## When does a transport organization fall under the Cybersecurity Act?

\*The following [transport sectors](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/NL/TXT/HTML/?uri=CELEX:32022L2555#d1e32-143-1) are subject to the NIS2 regulations:

- Aviation: this concerns airlines, airports and air traffic control services.
- Rail transport: this includes railway undertakings and railway infrastructure managers.
- Road transport: this includes transport companies and road traffic system operators.
- Water transport: concerns both maritime and inland shipping companies, as well as port authorities.
- Logistics and postal services: companies that offer postal and parcel delivery services.
