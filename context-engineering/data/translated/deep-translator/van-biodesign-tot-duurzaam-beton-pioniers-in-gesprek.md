# From Biodesign to Sustainable concrete: pioneers in conversation

[Interview](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/interviews/index.html)

[Construction](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/bouw/index.html)

08/12/2025 • 5 minutes reading

[Leontien de Waal](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/authors/leontien-de-waal/index.html)

Emma van der Leest and Niki Loonen won an ABN AMRO Sustainable 50 award during the Built Environment Climate Summit on November 5. Emma for founding BlueCity Lab and her role as a maker and connector in the field of biodesign. Niki for his efforts at construction group TBI, where he is working on the transition to sustainable concrete. At first glance two different worlds, but their work has more in common than you might think.

## A natural jacket

For Emma it all started with a jacket that she saw hanging in the Boijmans van Beuningen museum. “It looked like a leather jacket, but it was made with bacteria and yeasts in a fermentation process,” she says. The idea that you can create raw materials in barrels of liquid immediately fascinated her. So after studying product design at the art academy, she decided to specialize in biodesign. In 2015 she founded BlueCity Lab, located in the former Tropicana swimming pool in Rotterdam. There she brings scientists, entrepreneurs and designers together to work on circular materials and processes. “For example, we develop sustainable building materials from residual flows.”

> “To make cement, you have to heat limestone to 1,400 degrees. This releases a lot of CO2: almost one kilo per kilo of cement.”

Niki Loonen

Sustainable concrete advisor at TBI

## Melting glacier as a warning

The moment Niki realized that something really had to be done was eight years ago. While on holiday with friends he walked along a glacier at an altitude of 3,740 metres. 'It was 8 degrees there, while it should be freezing, even in summer. That glacier melted like crazy. Then I realized: this is not right.' Niki was working as a consultant at an engineering firm at the time, and decided to use his knowledge to make concrete more sustainable. According to Niki, the big problem lies with cement production. 'To make cement, you have to heat limestone to 1,400 degrees. This releases a lot of CO2: almost one kilo per kilo of cement.' Since his switch to construction group TBI, he has supported construction companies in the transition to more sustainable concrete. He also does committee work to change the rules for concrete.

## Pioneering and innovating as a connection

And so their worlds come together: Emma gets science from the lab, Niki puts that knowledge into practice. “A lot happens behind the doors of universities, but we need doers and makers who then get to work with that theory,” says Emma. Niki agrees. 'Exactly, scientific breakthroughs must be converted into solutions that work on a large scale. For example, I am now working on bringing bacterial technologies to industrial level.'

> “A lot happens behind the doors of universities, but we need doers and makers who then get to work with that theory.”

Emma van der Leest

Designer and founder at BlueCity Lab

A good example of such an innovation is self-healing concrete. “The company [Basilisk Self-Healing Concrete](https://basiliskconcrete.com/) uses bacteria to seal cracks in concrete,” Emma explains. 'That bacterium naturally produces limestone. If a hairline crack appears in concrete, the bacteria are activated and seal it within a few days.' Niki: 'We poured a basement wall with TBI two weeks ago with two types of self-healing concrete, one of which is bacterial. These types of innovations enormously extend the lifespan of buildings.'

> “I would like to teach the new generation of construction workers, constructors and concrete pourers how to work with new materials.”

Emma van der Leest

Designer and founder at BlueCity Lab

## Sustainable port and circular processes

The overall challenge in the construction sector is clear: the transition from primary to circular and biogenic materials, while emitting less CO2 – or even becoming CO2 negative. According to Niki, 75 percent residual material in concrete is technically possible, but 100 percent circularity is unattainable. 'We build more worldwide than we demolish.' Emma sees a bright future. 'A lot is happening in the construction sector in the field of sustainability. I hope to teach the new generation of construction workers, constructors and concrete pourers to work with new materials.'

Her big dream? Making the port of Rotterdam more sustainable by converting old, linear processes into circular processes. 'People sometimes call me naive, but I just know that it is possible.' Niki also has a clear goal in mind: sustainable concrete should be the standard by 2035, so that his expertise is no longer needed.

> “By 2035, sustainable concrete must be the standard, so that my expertise is no longer needed.”

Niki Loonen

Sustainable concrete advisor at TBI

## On the right track

The Sustainable 50 award certainly has an impact on both. “I thought it was an honor to be nominated and to be in a list with big names like TBI,” says Emma. 'I am currently working on a water-repellent coating for biomaterials using fungi. Since the award, I have received a lot of questions about this from construction companies.' For Niki, the recognition makes his work easier. 'If sustainable construction in the Netherlands says that you are on the right track, that makes discussions within the concrete industry a lot easier. You just get a little more to crumble into the milk.”

## About the ABN AMRO Sustainable 50

The ABN AMRO Sustainable 50 is the list of professionals who demonstrably contribute to a more sustainable built environment. They are the drivers, connectors, accelerators and innovators in the sector. Every year we put these people on stage to honor their work, commitment and vision and to inspire others to get moving. There are awards in three categories: maker, inventor and doer. The winners were announced on November 5, 2025 during the Climate Summit for the Built Environment.

## Read more in the construction sector

The Dutch construction sector has been one of the best performing sectors in recent years, despite several challenges facing the sector. Examples are the nitrogen dossier, the significant price increases for energy and building materials and also the personnel shortages. Driven by trends, innovations and legislation and regulations, the relevance of themes such as sustainability and environmental impact is growing and the sector is looking for a new balance.
