# From rooms to opportunities

[Analysis](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/analyses/index.html)

[Leisure](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/leisure/index.html)

16/12/2025 • 7 minutes reading

[Stef Driessen](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/authors/stef-driessen/index.html)

Wouter Geerts

The Dutch hotel industry is facing a tipping point. Not because guests stay away, but because the way value is created is fundamentally changing. The VAT increase increases pressure on profitability, while personnel costs, energy rates and maintenance costs are rising faster than turnover. The traditional reflex - increasing room rates - is wearing thin. Anyone who wants to remain relevant will have to reinvent their revenue model.

Yet that does not have to be a threat. It is precisely in times of pressure that innovation arises. The hotels that become future-proof are not necessarily the largest or the cheapest, but those that learn to read their space again: no longer in square meters of walls, but in square meters of possibilities.

## The value of a square meter

The classic hotel room is a paradox. It forms the core of the revenue model, but is empty most of the day. Research by Spring Partners on behalf of Mews shows that worldwide only a third of the available space in hotels is actively used at any time. The other meters cost money in energy, cleaning and interest, but do not yield anything at that time.

The challenge therefore does not lie in building more, but in making better use. An inspiring example is the Over the Edge swing on the roof of Hotel Sir Adam in Amsterdam: an investment of several dozen square meters that now generates millions of euros in turnover. The message is clear: value is not in surface area, but in experience per meter.

Other hotels show that it can also be done more subtly. Zoku Amsterdam designed compact lofts that serve as a bedroom, office and meeting space at the same time. There are freelancers during the day, long-stay guests in the evening. The Social Hub combines student housing, hotel rooms and coworking, allowing spaces to be used all year round. And just like The Social Hub, Hotel Casa Amsterdam rents rooms to tourists in the summer and to students in the winter — the same building, a double operation.

What the parties in these examples have in common is that they are moving away from the idea that a room is only for the night. They think in units of time instead of days, in use instead of possession.

## From RevPAR to RevPAM

This change in thinking also requires new ways of measuring. For years, Revenue per Available Room (RevPAR) was the holy grail for hotel performance. But if the room is no longer the only source of income, that standard becomes too narrow. More and more progressive hoteliers are therefore focusing on Revenue per Available Meter (RevPAM): the turnover divided by the total usable area of ​​the hotel.

> “We see a shift in the way hotels measure their performance. The room rate does not say everything. The future lies in turnover per square meter.”

Wouter Geerts

Head of Market Research at Mews

This forces hoteliers to look at their properties differently: not as a stock of rooms, but as a portfolio of spaces that can each have their own function and yield.” Other metrics, such as revenue per guest, are slowly emerging, but the real breakthrough is revenue per meter — because it provides insight into utilization, not just occupancy.

The technology to support the optimal use of surface area is now mature. Hotels today can already offer any type of space — from parking to a roof terrace — by the hour or by the day, and automatically adjust prices based on demand. It is a fundamental shift from accommodation to logistics: no longer selling nights, but optimally allocating time and space.

An inspiring example is [Hotel Paradiso](https://www.mews.com/en/customers/hotel-paradiso) in Paris. There, the roof terrace functions as an open-air cinema during the day and as a meeting place in the evening, while rooms can be rented out flexibly per day. Due to this smart use of space and direct online bookings, the 'Revenue per Available Room' is, according to internal analyses, more than 40 euros higher than for bookings via other channels — tangible proof that better use pays off.

Hotels that actively manage their spaces — including day rooms, coworking, events or parking spaces — not only realize higher turnover per square meter, but also a more stable cash flow. Perhaps even more important: they build a closer relationship with their guests and their neighborhood, making their business less dependent on seasonal peaks.

Please note: flexibility also has limits. [Day rental](https://www.hospitality-management.nl/hotels-moeten-een-korting-van-minimaal-30-procent-aanbieden) and short-term use of rooms can be valuable, but at the same time entail risks. Without proper control systems, this form of rental can be abused for illegal activities, such as prostitution or drug trafficking.

> “Hotels with day rentals must apply strict rules. This guarantees a balance between innovation, integrity and protects guests and the image.”

Stef Driessen

Sector Banker Leisure

## The new guest: local, temporary and connected

The shift from room to community is in line with changing guest behavior. Travelers are not only looking for comfort, but also flexibility and connection. They combine work and relaxation, and often opt for shorter, more intensive stays. At the same time, the local market is undergoing a revaluation.

More and more hotels are discovering that their own neighborhood is a valuable target group. A fitness room that is empty during the day can be opened to members of the neighborhood. A restaurant can double as a coworking café during quiet hours. A roof terrace can be rented for pop-ups or cultural events. Such initiatives attract repeat visitors and increase daytime occupancy rates, without having to pay expensive OTA commissions.

As Hotel Paradiso in Paris shows, multifunctional use of space can lead to a lively mix of guests: rooftop film screenings, day rooms and karaoke nights. Not more space, but smarter use. And that translates into more experience and a stronger brand.

According to McKinsey's [ConsumerWise Survey](https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/consumer-packaged-goods/our-insights/an-update-on-european-consumer-sentiment), despite economic uncertainty, Europeans want to continue spending on experiences such as travel and hospitality. The question is not whether people want to come, but where and when they do so. As a hotelier, if you offer guests new ways to use the space, you not only gain sales, but also loyalty.

## Less manual work, more hospitality

Technology helps accelerate transformation. The sector has been struggling with staff shortages for years. According to Eurostat, productivity per employee in European hotels has fallen by 8 percent since 2014. Many employees spend their time on repetitive tasks that can also be taken over by software.

Artificial intelligence offers a solution here. Modern PMS systems answer simple questions automatically, generate quotes in real time and make personalized suggestions to guests; from a day pass for the spa to a late check-out. This relieves the team and increases the experience. The great thing is that technology does not replace [human warmth](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/leisure/de-comeback-van-het-personale-reisspraak.html), but rather makes it possible. Automating routines frees up time for real contact. And that is exactly where hospitality retains its value.

The next step lies in the application of generative AI. That technology is beginning to change not only the way hotels operate, but also how guests plan and book trips. Perhaps the guest [of 2030](https://skift.com/2025/09/26/skift-global-forum-2025-4-insights-from-industry-decision-makers/?utm_source=ask_skift) will no longer search and book via an OTA, but via an AI assistant who books directly with the hotel.

## The financial logic of flexibility

The benefits of a diverse revenue model extend beyond turnover. Anyone who has multiple income streams — rooms, coworking, food and drinks, events, memberships — spreads risks and increases financeability. For banks and investors, a hotel that is used 365 days a year is more robust than a property that is dependent on seasonal peaks.

That flexibility does require discipline. Operational complexity is increasing, as is the need for clear segmentation and brand monitoring. A day guest expects a different experience than an overnight tourist. Governance and pricing strategy must be aligned accordingly. But the reward is great: if you use space wisely, you will get more return from the same bricks and make the organization more resilient to market disruptions.

## The hotelier of 2030

The VAT increase will hurt, especially in border regions where guests can easily move. But those who now have the courage to rethink their business can make a profit where others expect a loss.

The hotel of 2030 is no longer a collection of rooms, but a living organism of spaces and rhythms. Workplace during the day, restaurant in the evening, accommodation at night. A place where technology simplifies operations and where hospitality does not end at the reception, but starts with the question: how can this space add value for the guest, the neighborhood and the company today?

The future of hospitality does not lie in more square meters, but in better square meters. Not in more staff, but in [better used time](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/leisure/waarom-geen-enkel-Event-straks-meer-zonder-ai-agent-kan.html). Not in higher prices, but in higher utilization. The hotel room is not the product, the guest's time is the product. And there is enough time if we learn to deal with it better.
