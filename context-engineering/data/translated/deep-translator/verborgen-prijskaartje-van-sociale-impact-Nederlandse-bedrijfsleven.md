The hidden costs for Dutch companies as a result of not respecting human rights are estimated at almost 41 billion euros. This is evident from research by ABN AMRO in collaboration with Impact Institute. This negative social impact mainly occurs in the value chain, both with suppliers and customers, and to a lesser extent directly with the companies themselves. However, this does not absolve them from taking their responsibility. Our current economic model emphasizes profit maximization and economic growth. But the [limits of this growth](https://www.volkskrant.nl/volkskrant-magazine/sandra-phlippen-mijn-grote-zorg-is-dat-de-maatschappij-uit-elkaar-zal-viel~bc591a1f/?referrer=https://www.google.com/) are becoming increasingly clear, as can be seen in the exploitation of vulnerable groups and increasing inequalities.

Social impact is about the consequences of business activities on people: individuals, groups or society as a whole. Human rights violations, the focus of this report, are a form of negative social impact. When we think of human rights, people often think of war situations and distant foreign countries. But human rights can also be at stake closer to home. This concerns, for example, inadequate housing and the lack of a living wage for migrant workers, and unequal pay between men and women for the same work. Companies come into contact with people directly through their own activities and indirectly through the purchase of raw materials and the sale of products in the global value chains. All these people can be affected by the company's activities.

## 1. The hidden costs of not respecting human rights

This report identifies the hidden costs resulting from failure to respect human rights. These hidden costs are also called 'social costs'. This report adopts this terminology and means by social costs the hidden costs associated with a negative impact on human rights. The true pricing methodology is used to calculate these hidden costs. This methodology quantifies the social costs of, for example, underpayment, unsafe working conditions and child labor. These social costs are then translated into financial values ​​that ultimately lead to the total cost of a product or service. True pricing provides incentives to companies to absorb or reduce these costs and therefore does not necessarily lead to higher consumer prices (see Chapter 4 for the recommendations).

The influence of companies affects all universal human rights recognized in the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR). By looking at the social costs resulting from underpayment, child labor, forced labor, workplace safety and the equal pay gap, this report focuses in particular on the following human rights: freedom from discrimination and coercion, and equal pay for equal work.

Failure to comply with human rights in one specific area can lead to neglect in other areas. For example, underpayment can lead to other negative social consequences, such as child labor. The greater the degree of underpayment in a region, the greater the chance that children have to work and not go to school. Underpayment increases the likelihood of falling into poverty, leaving families struggling to meet their basic needs, such as food, education and health care.

## Purpose of the report

The report serves as a first step towards providing insight into the social costs for various sectors of Dutch business, including partners in the global value chain. The social costs of the entire value chain often fall on vulnerable groups and/or society as a whole. Providing insight into hidden costs helps companies reduce their impact on society. By calculating the actual price of products or services, companies become aware of the social costs that were previously invisible. This awareness then stimulates them to strive for cost reduction by producing more sustainably and improving working conditions. This not only has positive effects on people's well-being, but also strengthens the company's reputation and increases customer loyalty.

In the report we make recommendations on how companies can gain insight into social costs and reduce or prevent them by working with fair prices, making social costs transparent and taking responsibility. Companies that become involved in human rights abuses can suffer reputational, legal and financial damage. We not only want to warn companies about the negative consequences of doing nothing, but also encourage companies to be at the forefront of this theme. After all, consumers, employees and investors are becoming increasingly aware of social issues and prefer companies that act ethically.

In addition to moral responsibility, more and more legislation is in force that forces companies to identify and quantify social costs. The OECD due diligence is useful for the steps that companies can take to comply with this legislation, as we show in Chapter 4.

> “Those who already work on social responsibility will soon reap the benefits.”

Gerarda Westerhuis

Sector economist Retail and Leisure

## Guidelines and legislation on social impact

Companies have a responsibility to respect human rights according to the 2011 United Nations Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights (UNGPs). This means that they must identify, prevent, reduce and counter the negative social impact that their activities directly or indirectly cause on human rights. The principles are based on the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948) and, together with the OECD guidelines, form the standard for International Corporate Social Responsibility (ICSR). By following the OECD guidelines, a company contributes to the seventeen Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) of the United Nations. These goals form global ambitions for a sustainable world in 2030.

While the OECD Guidelines and the UNGPs are not binding on companies, the European Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) and Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD) are. The CSRD contains a legal obligation for a company to prepare a sustainability report and have it tested by an accountant. This report provides insight into how sustainable a company is in the areas of the environment, social impact and governance (ESG) and provides the basis for discussions with stakeholders about this. For example, companies must report on risks relating to labor and human rights, both in the company itself and in the value chain, provided it is material. The CSRD explicitly refers in several places to the above-mentioned OECD guidelines and UNGPs.

In addition, the CSDDD establishes a corporate due diligence obligation for large companies. Thanks to this directive, companies have a chain care obligation and are therefore forced to screen their production chain for environmental regulations and non-respect of human rights and, if applicable, to reduce, prevent and/or repair these. The law applies to companies with a turnover greater than 450 million euros and at least a thousand employees.

Recently, in February 2025, the European Commission proposed an Omnibus Regulation to bundle and simplify existing sustainability guidelines, such as the CSRD, CSDDD and the EU Taxonomy. In addition to the number of companies in the scope of CSRD, the proposal reduces the number of data and reporting requirements. The CSDDD includes changes such as a limited scope of assessment of only direct business partners, reduced frequency of assessments to once every 5 years and postponed compliance deadlines until 2028. The aim is to reduce the administrative burden of the legislations and ultimately increase the competitiveness of the EU. The proposals still need to be approved.

Furthermore, in April 2024, the European Parliament adopted [new rules](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/news/nl/press-room/20240419IPR20551/producten-die-met-dwang Labor-zijn-maken-worden-verboden-op-de-interne-markt) banning the sale, import and export of products made with forced labor within the EU. The legislation, a step toward fair trade, requires companies to eliminate forced labor from their chains before their products are reauthorized. After approval by the Council of Europe, the regulation will enter into force in three years.

## Complexity of the global value chain

The purchase and sale of goods and services by companies that pay their employees too little, employ children or reward men and women unequally has a negative impact on society worldwide. Companies may unintentionally contribute to or benefit from such violations if they do not implement adequate due diligence processes to assess and manage the impact of their activities on human rights. However, the complexity of the chains makes it difficult for companies to identify violations in the chain and to combat them. The rapid expansion of the global value chain since the 1990s in particular has largely contributed to this complexity.

About 70 percent of international trade concerns products and services associated with global value chains. This means that for the production of an end product, various services, raw materials and components first cross national borders, often several times. Once processed into finished products, they are shipped to consumers around the world. For example, a smartphone assembled in China might contain graphic design elements from the United States, computer code from France, silicon chips from Singapore, and precious metals from Bolivia. The example of the smartphone shows how parts from different countries come together in a final product, which is typical of the globalization of production processes.

As a result, exports from one country to another often involve complex interactions between various domestic and foreign suppliers. Even more than before, trade is determined by strategic decisions by companies to outsource activities to regions where the necessary skills and materials are available at the lowest possible costs. Our report shows that there is a link between seeking the lowest price and the downside of this in the form of disrespect for human rights.

> “Different trading conditions can cause confusion for farmers and cooperatives. For example, one farmer receives a premium, the other does not. So cooperation is needed.”

Milande Busquet

PHD researcher at Utrecht University on child labor in the global value chain

## 2. Social costs mainly in the chain

The hidden costs for Dutch companies as a result of not respecting human rights are estimated at almost 41 billion euros. These social costs have been calculated in collaboration with Impact Institute. Our research shows that approximately 76 percent of the social costs, or 31 billion euros, are the result of non-respect of human rights abroad by chain partners of Dutch companies. Although the lack of respect for human rights remains mainly hidden in foreign chains, this does not absolve Dutch companies of their responsibility to gain insight and take action.

In this study, the social costs relate to five indicators: child labor, forced labor, underpayment, equal pay gap and safety in the workplace. We examined the impact on these indicators for 65 sectors through trade with 140 countries and regions.

Of the 41 billion euros in social costs, more than 7 billion euros are accounted for by the Business Services sector and its value chain, and 6.5 billion euros by the Industry sector (see Figure 1). Together these two sectors cause almost a third of the total social costs. Although the impact intensity of Business Services is relatively low, the impact is greatest due to the high added value of the sector in the Dutch economy.

## Breakdown of the social impact

The majority of the negative impact, estimated at around 88 percent, occurs with suppliers (upstream) or customers (downstream) and therefore not directly with the company. It is about the impact that occurs with domestic and foreign chain partners. The chain of the Food sector, for example, includes suppliers from the agricultural sector at home and abroad (upstream) and customers such as restaurants and supermarkets (downstream). The largest negative social impact for the Food sector lies with suppliers, where food production is often dependent on labor. This concerns, for example, children who work on cocoa plantations in West Africa.

The social costs largely occur at foreign chain partners of Dutch companies, namely 76 percent of the total social costs, or 31 billion euros (see Figure 2). For the Industry and Transport & Logistics sectors this is even 92 and 93 percent respectively. The social impact of the Food sector also mainly takes place outside our national borders (94 percent). Only for the Healthcare and Government & Education sectors does the impact largely occur in the Netherlands, especially in the equal pay gap. Despite the fact that the greatest impact occurs with foreign chain partners, Dutch companies bear the responsibility, partly stimulated by the CSRD and CSDDD, to mitigate their negative social impact in the chain.

> “Being able to pay a living wage to garment workers is about pennies in the actual consumer price, and collaboration between brands that produce in the same factory is essential.”

Jesse Bloemendaal

Fair Wear's Policy Coordinator

As shown in Figure 3, the equal pay gap has the most direct impact; more than 28 percent of this impact takes place directly at companies in the Netherlands. Dutch companies can therefore take immediate action in their organization. Safety on the work floor (8 percent), forced labor (4 percent) and to a lesser extent underpayment (1 percent) also take place directly at the companies. The child labor indicator only occurs with foreign chain partners.

## Which indicators have the most negative social impact?

Although the lack of respect for human rights remains mainly hidden in foreign chains, this does not absolve Dutch companies of their responsibility to gain insight and take action. To comply with laws and regulations such as the CSRD and CSDDD, companies must identify, reduce, prevent and/or remediate negative social impacts on human rights. The extent to which a particular indicator is influential varies per sector, as can be seen in Figure 4. For example, for the Healthcare and Government & Education sectors, the greatest risk is the equal pay gap, while for the Food, Agri and Transport & Logistics sectors, underpayment poses the greatest human rights risk. Child labor is most common in the Food, Agri and Leisure sectors. Safety mainly plays a role in Construction, and to a slightly lesser extent in Industry, Trade and Transport & Logistics.

## 3. Deeper insight into social challenges: underpayment, child labor and equal pay gap

In the previous chapter it became clear that of the five indicators examined, underpayment, child labor and equal pay gap are the most common. In this chapter we delve deeper into these three indicators. Underpayment in the Netherlands mainly occurs through employment agencies, but also plays a role in the international value chain, such as in the clothing industry. Child labor mainly takes place in foreign value chains, with a quarter of cases occurring in Sub-Saharan Africa. Finally, the equal pay gap is a significant problem within Dutch companies themselves. We show why this gap persists so persistently.

## 4. Action required: four recommendations

Most of the negative social impact of Dutch companies takes place within the (international) value chain. Identifying and addressing these negative social impacts is a complex task that requires entrepreneurs to take greater action, collaboration and transparency. This chapter first emphasizes the importance of fair prices. The OECD guidelines for due diligence are then highlighted. It helps companies meet their legal obligations such as CSRD and CSDDD. Finally, we provide practical guidelines for recognizing, reducing and preventing the two important indicators from our research: underpayment and the equal pay gap.
