# Enticing sustainable behavior works better than harping on the facts

[Column](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/column.html)

[Retail](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/retail/index.html)

10/30/2025 • 4 minutes reading

[Henk Hofstede](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/authors/henk-hofstede/index.html)

Anyone who wants to persuade consumers to behave more sustainably should not harp on the facts, but should use smart persuasion. This is done by focusing on positive framing about convenience, savings, pleasure and status. I came to this conclusion after attending two events with sustainable retail and marketing as topics.

The reason for this column is my recent visits to the Sustainable Retail event of RetailTrends and Q&A Retail and a lecture on The Future of Marketing at the University of Amsterdam. It brought me to an uncomfortable realization: if we want to persuade people to behave more sustainably, seduction works better than pointing out serious facts.

The noticeable effects of climate change and declining biodiversity, such as increased flooding, extreme heat and dying coral reefs, are having a huge impact on our society. And yet, unfortunately, people act less on threat than on short-term gain, status, convenience and social confirmation. I clearly have to get used to this idea that humanity can apparently only be adjusted with a 'hedonic' message.

> “If we want to persuade people to behave more sustainably, seduction works better than pointing out serious facts.”

Henk Hofstede

Sector Banker Retail

## Sustainable Retail 2025

The gist of the Sustainable Retail event was clear: sustainability is no longer a choice for both established and young retailers, but a condition for remaining future-proof based on legislation and regulations and stable supply chains. At the same time, customers choose less based on facts and sustainability ideals, but mainly on convenience, service, quality and a good price. In the public debate, citizens like to present themselves with high moral values, and at the checkout they often opt for benefits and simplicity. Because the citizen apparently takes on a different form in his role as consumer, success in communicating about sustainability lies mainly in framing the message and less in sending doom facts and sustainability ideals.

> “This makes sustainable behavior normal, because the customer no longer has any other choice.”

Henk Hofstede

Sector Banker Retail

HEMA showed how it deals with this creatively: the five G's - cheap, cozy, easy, cool and good - where 'good' stands for the use of more sustainable materials, better quality and longer lifespan, do not make the sustainable choice more difficult, but more self-evident. Dille & Kamille and even discounter Action show that you can achieve a lot with so-called 'defaults': the most sustainable products become the standard in a certain segment. This makes sustainable behavior normal, because the customer no longer has any other choice.

## The Future of Marketing

That same week, I was a guest at the UvA during the interdisciplinary course The Future of Marketing, where students presented their visions and ideas about how to use marketing for a more sustainable society. In preparation for this lecture, I read an article on a topic that has occupied me for years: The evolutionary bases for sustainable behavior by Griskevicius, Cantú and Van Vugt (2012). It states that awareness, standards and education stimulate sustainable and environmentally friendly behavior, but that deep-rooted evolutionary tendencies actually work against this.

## Doom facts don't help enough

Griskevicius and colleagues thus provide an answer to the question of why seduction works better than stating the facts, especially when this is done in doomsday scenarios. Much of our behavior is apparently anchored in short-term thinking, self-interest and 'herd behavior'. In the distant past, responding to immediate risks meant survival, it paid to choose the short term, for immediate rewards and to do what the group did. In the context of today's climate and biodiversity challenges, these deeply ingrained behaviors work against us: CO₂ is invisible and the returns from reducing environmental impact are remote, while the gratification of 'just catching a plane', buying fast fashion or eating a cheap steak is immediate.

> “Much of our behavior is apparently anchored in short-term thinking, self-interest and 'herd behavior'.”

Henk Hofstede

Sector Banker Retail

But these instincts also offer opportunities. People have been modeling themselves on others and groups since prehistoric times. If sustainable behavior is visible and socially attractive, for example driving an electric car, then we follow suit. People compete on status: if the 'greenest' option is the most prestigious and distinctive, the standard will eventually shift. And framing helps respond to the desire for short-term gain. Insulating your home saves a certain amount of energy and money. Communicating about these concrete results works better than about difficult matters such as CO₂ emissions.

## Seduction, credibility and standardization are the basis

Communicating effectively to encourage more sustainable choices should not be confused with manipulation. A retailer that 'seductively packages' sustainability in convenience, price, prestige or 'fun' is not doing anything wrong as long as it is correct. The challenge for retail - and actually for companies in all sectors - is to make the sustainable choice so normal that it is no longer called 'the sustainable choice', but simply 'the best choice'. The sustainable choice is then 'satisfier' afterwards, as CEO Hans Geels of Dille & Kamille said. Personally, I have to get used to the fact that sometimes communication has to be 'different', even though the facts are so clear. The end justifies the means and the mission is still a success, but let's keep up the pace.
