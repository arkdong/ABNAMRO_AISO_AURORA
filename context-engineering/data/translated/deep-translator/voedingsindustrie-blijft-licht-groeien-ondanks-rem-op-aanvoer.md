# Food industry continues to grow slightly despite supply constraints

The food industry grew strongly in 2025 and will maintain this growth in 2026 and 2027. Pressure on margins will continue and consolidation will continue. Food companies are increasingly choosing between economies of scale and cost leadership on the one hand and specialization on the other. Obstacles such as grid congestion and staff shortages slow the growth of food companies.

In 2025, the food industry will account for almost a quarter of the total Dutch industrial production value. ABN AMRO expects a volume increase of 1.5 percent for the sector in 2025, driven by growth in domestic consumption and higher exports. In 2026 and 2027, the sector is expected to grow by 0.5 and 1 percent respectively.

In these forecasts we take a closer look at specific subsectors to identify underlying trends in the food industry. The following subsectors are covered in this analysis: Dairy processing and Meat processing, which together represent 29 percent of the total food turnover. We also look at Fish Processing and Trade, Bakery and Confectionery Production and Fruit and Vegetable Processing and Trade, which together represent 25 percent of the food industry. Finally, we will discuss Beverage production, which accounts for 9 percent.

To explain the context of the food sector, we first start with an analysis of relevant economic developments. This is followed by an in-depth look at developments in production and demand. Relevant challenges for the sector are then discussed. Finally, the developments per subsector are discussed in more detail.

## Food companies can benefit from economic development in Europe

The Dutch economy will grow by 1.7 percent in 2025. ABN AMRO also expects growth of 1.2 percent in 2026 and 1.4 percent in 2027. The global economy is characterized by volatility in supply chains and uncertainty due to geopolitical tensions, among other things. However, the trade agreement concluded in July 2025 between the United States (US) and the European Union (EU) reduces uncertainty for exporting companies and supply chains. In addition, at the time of writing there is no clarity about the composition and policy of a new cabinet.

Despite these uncertainties, the Dutch economy appears to be resilient in 2025. And according to forecasts by the Central Planning Bureau (CPB), the purchasing power of households will also increase in 2026, which in itself is favorable for food sales. Nevertheless, consumers are still in two minds: on the one hand, they think food has become very expensive, but on the other hand, they want to enjoy good food without any worries. Furthermore, structural obstacles ensure that the economic growth of the Netherlands in 2026 and 2027 remains below potential. Examples of this are grid congestion and staff shortages, something that directly affects food companies.

Inflation in the Netherlands will normalize in the coming years, although it will still remain above the European Central Bank's (ECB) target of 2 percent in 2026 and 2027 at 2.4 and 2.5 percent respectively. An important cause of the higher inflation in the Netherlands is the significant wage increase of 5 percent in 2025. ABN AMRO expects a wage increase of 4 percent in 2026.

Because Europe is the most important sales market for the Dutch food industry, food companies can benefit from economic growth in Europe. ABN AMRO expects the European economy to grow by 1.2 percent in 2026 and 1.4 percent in 2027, which is positive for food exports. Germany is the largest buyer of Dutch food products, followed by Belgium, the United Kingdom and France. These markets show varying pictures, with a predominantly positive effect on private consumption.

For example, AMRO expects an increasingly better German economy in the coming years, as a result of significant government investments. This also drives private consumption. The Organization for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) expects a slight decline in economic growth for the United Kingdom, but with an underlying increase in consumer spending. The Belgian economy is stabilizing, with a decline in consumption. In France, the economy is growing slightly with an increase in consumption.

An important note is that entrepreneurs indicate in the Business Survey of the Central Bureau of Statistics (CBS) that their competitive position abroad is deteriorating. This has to do, among other things, with the entrepreneurial climate, which scores just above average in the Netherlands, with important bottlenecks such as energy infrastructure, the tax climate, facilitating legislation and the predictability of government policy, according to research by the University of Amsterdam (UvA). Sharply increased costs, including energy costs and personnel costs, as a result of a tight labor market also play a role.

## Demand for food will also increase slightly next year

Production volumes in the food and beverage industry increased by 1.5 percent in 2025. Although food inflation stabilized to 3.5 percent in 2025, the prices of beef (+27 percent), coffee (+23 percent) and chocolate (+16 percent) rose sharply in November compared to a year earlier, which had a dampening effect on sales of those products.

Domestic demand for food will moderate slightly in 2026, as will exports, before picking up further in 2027. General inflation is expected to drop to 2.4 percent and the purchasing power of consumers will continue to increase in 2026. As a result, the volume of food sales in the supermarket and out-of-home channel will also develop slightly positively in the coming year. According to market research agency Circana, promotional pressure – spending on offers – in the supermarket channel will stabilize at approximately 27.5 percent in November 2025. The private label share is increasing again and will be almost 34 percent at the end of 2025.

Furthermore, it appears that the difference between A-brands and private labels is increasing. A-brand manufacturers are therefore under pressure. For example, Nestlé announced a reorganization earlier this year and the Dutch Heineken is also making cuts in the organization. Unilever already did this last year. Previous research by ABN AMRO shows that consumers are 'hybrid' and inconsistent, and are constantly looking for a balance between price, quality and convenience. One moment the consumer chooses the lowest price and the next moment price plays no role because the preference is for enjoyment. Research by EFMI Business School also shows that the share of bargain hunters among consumers is increasing. This group is not loyal and visits many different supermarkets in a week. In 2024, the group has grown to 43 percent, compared to 35 percent in 2018. The convenience generation more often chooses cheaper alternatives as an alternative to quick service and meal delivery.

## Structural bottlenecks also affect Dutch food companies

The CBS Business Survey (COEN) shows that entrepreneurs in the food industry expect profitability to remain virtually the same in October 2025 in the next three months. This is an improvement compared to the summer of 2025. However, food manufacturers still see a labor shortage (27 percent) as the main obstacle to growth. In addition, personnel costs remain high. Because an increase in personnel costs in the food industry is also expected in 2026, albeit more limited than the increase of 4.2 percent in 2025.

Producers in the beverage industry are still pessimistic: their profitability expectations for the next three months remain negative. One side note is that they expected the same thing last August, while in October the opinion about the past months was positive. Insufficient demand is still the main barrier for these producers.

Entrepreneurs in the food industry are also limited in expanding and making the production process more sustainable due to a lack of space, lengthy permit processes and waiting times for connection to the power grid. However, for many food companies, economies of scale, automation and sustainability are of great importance, because in addition to increased personnel costs, they also saw energy costs and raw material prices increase. This has increased pressure on margins. ABN AMRO therefore expects more consolidation in the food sector in the coming years, especially among medium-sized companies for which cost leadership is unattainable and specialization in, for example, a niche or production method is not part of the business model. A complicating factor is that in many companies there is a lack of succession, which may lead to discontinuity and thus the loss of employment, capital and knowledge.

## Legislation and chains are increasingly demanding more from the food industry

Although within the EU the emphasis on sustainability has shifted to competitiveness and simplification, many initiatives from, for example, supermarket organizations and legislation remain in force. For example, food companies are still encouraged to contribute within the supply chain to, among other things, the reduction of harmful emissions and food waste. Combating deforestation also remains on the agenda with the EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR), although it is being revised and simplified and its introduction has been postponed again. In addition, the higher requirements regarding packaging and packaging waste as a result of the Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation (PPWR) and regarding water quality as a result of the Water Framework Directive (KWR) remain in force.
