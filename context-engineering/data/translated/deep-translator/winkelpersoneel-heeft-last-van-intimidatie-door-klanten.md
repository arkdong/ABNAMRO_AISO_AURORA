The well-being of staff in the retail sector fell slightly in 2024 after a record high in 2023. Safety and health in particular deteriorated. For example, employees were threatened more often by customers and store staff felt exhausted more often. Employers will have to better protect their staff.

Because store staff were less satisfied, the retail sector was at the bottom of the list of sectors last year. The leisure sector had the lowest well-being score in previous years, but actually showed an improvement in 2024.

The key to growth of retail companies lies in the well-being of employees. Satisfied employees are less likely to leave the organization and committed employers are more likely to attract new staff. In addition, higher well-being contributes to higher productivity because satisfied employees are more productive and sick less often. Retailers who invest in staff are rewarded with more turnover and higher returns.

The fact that the well-being of store staff was slightly less good last year is mainly because the safety of the working environment decreased and the health of the staff deteriorated. There was also hardly any room for personal development compared to other sectors. On the other hand, the decline in well-being is being slowed by a welcome improvement in equal opportunities in the sector. And the balance between work and private life is relatively good among store employees. This score remained virtually the same last year.

## More employees intimidated again

It is worrying that staff safety in retail is deteriorating. During the first measurement in 2015, working in stores was often safer than the environment in which employees in other sectors find themselves. The sector now scores lower than the average of other sectors. This is mainly due to customer behavior. Last year, more retail employees reported being threatened or intimidated by customers.

Customers mainly bother store employees. Last year, about one in six employees reported being intimidated or threatened by customers. This is slightly higher in healthcare and more than twice as much as the average for all sectors. But this share is not so high in all sectors with customer contact. In the leisure sector, which also includes the catering industry, staff are twice as likely to experience intimidation.

The increase in insecurity in retail last year was stronger than the average increase in healthcare. The strong increase in supermarkets may have been caused by the introduction of deposits on cans that customers often find difficult to return due to overcrowded machines, or more use of the self-checkout where the check sometimes causes irritation. A shortage of staff in stores could also potentially harm service delivery. It should be clear that none of these reasons legitimize threats from customers.

Not only employers, but also society and the government must make efforts to reverse this development. For example, the government can use measures to focus on a national awareness campaign. In addition, employers can take measures themselves by communicating clearly to customers, training staff on resilience and making incidents in the workplace open to discussion. Supermarkets have also previously decided to have staff wear [no name tags](https://nos.nl/artikelen/2525009) more so that misconduct no longer continues outside the store.

## Staff feel empty more often

The unsafe atmosphere may also affect the health of store employees. For example, a larger proportion of employees reported last year that they suffered from complete exhaustion from work, morning fatigue and an empty feeling. This may be partly due to customer interactions, which are considered demanding by around one in five employees. Another cause may be that the work is repetitive and physically demanding. More staff indicate that they are affected by both factors, while the sector already scores below average. Retail also scores poorly on independence in carrying out work. Retailers can challenge employees with more responsibility. Employees may then feel empty less often.

Working conditions therefore take their toll on more employees. This can lead to an increase in absenteeism due to illness. To protect staff, retailers could invest in tools. Automating work reduces the physical and mental impact on employees. Moreover, more time is left for varying and more challenging work. We previously also wrote about [other benefits](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/sectorprognoses/retail/retailprognoses-investeer-in-productbaarheid.html) that automation brings, such as higher labor productivity and more room to meet customer expectations.

## Research accountability

Every year, ABN AMRO measures the well-being of staff in all sectors in the Netherlands. ABN AMRO's Sectoral Welfare Monitor is based on predominantly public sources, such as the Central Bureau of Statistics and the Netherlands Organization for Applied Scientific Research (TNO). Separate data from the National Working Conditions Survey (NEA) was also purchased from TNO to make the monitor more complete. Variables that required calculations were added manually.

For more information about the Sectoral Welfare Monitor, [see this publication, page 33 (PDF)](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/media/rapport-sectorale-welzijnsmonitor-augustus-2023_tcm16-205163.pdf) and further. The monitor covers seven main categories (work-life balance, health, personal development, economy, equal opportunities, safety and environment) with 87 underlying variables. Due to a lack of current figures, only five of the main categories (work-life balance, health, personal development, safety and equal opportunities) are included in this publication. These categories contain 71 of the 87 variables.
