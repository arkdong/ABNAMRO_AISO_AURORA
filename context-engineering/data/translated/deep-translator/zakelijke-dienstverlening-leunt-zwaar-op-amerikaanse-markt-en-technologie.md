# Business services rely heavily on the American market and technology

[Sector forecast](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/sectorprognoses/index.html)

[Business services](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/zakelijk-diensten/index.html)

19/06/2025 • 5 minutes reading

[Mario Bersem](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/authors/mario-bersem/index.html)

The United States is by far the most important trading partner for Dutch business service providers. It is precisely this interconnectedness that makes the sector sensitive to trade barriers and political friction across the Atlantic Ocean.

According to ABN AMRO estimates, the business services sector will grow by 1.5 percent in 2025 and 1.0 percent in 2026. This is comparable to the growth of gross domestic product (GDP) of 1.2 and 1.0 percent respectively. The sector's modest growth reflects the trade battle with the United States (US) and structural labor shortages. The fall of the Schoof cabinet is expected to have a limited effect on business services, but has not yet been included in these estimates.

Since April 2025, new US import duties have applied to goods from the European Union (EU). The direct consequences for service providers are limited because trade in services has so far remained unaffected by the trade conflict. But the impact through international trade and macroeconomic uncertainty is real, as evidenced by the recent decline in business investment and consumer spending.

The European Commission now has a legal framework, the Anti-Coercion Instrument (ACI), with which trade in services can also be involved in the trade conflict. Due to the large deficit in the trade balance for services between the EU and the US, the EU could hit back hard if the trade battle escalated further. In that scenario, American companies could suddenly face restrictions on market access, regulations or import duties. With the ACI, the EU threatens to hit the important American tech sector, among other things.

## The largest industry in the Netherlands

Business services are the largest industry in the Netherlands, measured in terms of employment and added value. The sector includes a wide range of companies, divided into three main groups: professional services such as accountants, lawyers and consultants; facility services such as cleaning, security and catering; and HR services such as temporary workers, secondments and recruitment and selection agencies.

Business services represented approximately 17 percent of GDP in 2024 and approximately 2 million people were employed in the sector. Business services are an 'asset light' sector: human capital determines earning capacity, while external financing remains relatively limited. The sector is sensitive to economic cycles; when the economy slows down, business services usually follow suit.

The Netherlands exported services worth 32.7 billion euros to the US in 2024, according to the 'United States Internationalization Monitor' of the Central Bureau of Statistics (CBS). For services, the US is the most important export destination after Germany. Business services participate strongly in international services trade. Almost half of the added value of the sector comes from exports abroad.

## The US is an economic engine, but poses a political risk

Business services traded internationally include legal services, accounting services and management consulting services. Large firms such as Deloitte, KPMG, EY, McKinsey and A&O Shearman operate in networks with large American clients - and assignments related to mergers, compliance, climate transition and strategic transformations. Specialized IT service providers, financial consultants and niche agencies are also strongly represented on the transatlantic axis.

Legal and management advice achieved the most export earnings to the US of all industries in the Netherlands in 2023, amounting to 4.5 billion euros in direct export earnings. Indirect export earnings worth around 2 billion euros are added via international value chains, mainly through contributions to Irish, German and Belgian exports to the US. By comparison, the Netherlands will earn around 2.3 billion euros in 2023 from the export of specialized machines, including chip machines.

The US economy is an economic engine for the business services sector. The important temporary employment sector also earns indirectly from exports to the US, for example, when temporary workers are deployed in industry or in wholesale trade. This economic dependence also poses a risk; the business services sector has an above-average amount to lose if the transatlantic trade relationship deteriorates. Moreover, the interconnectedness with the US applies not only on the output side with regard to customers and turnover, but also on the input side in terms of production factors.

## Risks of escalation of trade conflict

In terms of services imports, the US is the Netherlands' most important trading partner. In 2024, the Netherlands imported services worth 50.5 billion euros from the US, of which approximately 43 percent consisted of 'intellectual property fees', such as franchise fees and software licenses. The Netherlands, like the EU, has a deficit in the services trade balance. For the Netherlands, this amounted to 17.8 billion euros in 2024.

Almost every business service provider depends on American software, cloud storage and AI applications. Microsoft Office 365 is ubiquitous, as are cloud storage services Amazon Web Services or Google Cloud. AI tools such as Copilot, ChatGPT or Gemini are on the rise.

From the perspective of individual companies, the dependence on the US is easy to explain: American providers are technologically at the forefront, offer ease of use and their solutions are scalable. But the convenience and dependency also create vulnerability. Data transfer restrictions, changing regulations or sanctions from the US or EU can be harmful to companies that rely on US suppliers. In fact, Dutch companies have little control over their technological backbone as long as it is in American hands.

The larger, internationally active offices are particularly at risk from deteriorating relations with the US. This includes loss of contracts, licensing issues or additional compliance requirements. The interconnectedness of business services with the US is broad and deep-rooted: from customers to technology and from knowledge networks to market access. Historically this has provided opportunities for the sector, but current times require more attention to the risks.
