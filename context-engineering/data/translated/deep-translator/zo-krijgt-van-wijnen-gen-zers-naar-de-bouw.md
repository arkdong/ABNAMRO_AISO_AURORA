# This is how Van Wijnen gets Gen Zers to work in construction

[Entrepreneurial story](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/ondernemershalen/index.html)

[Construction](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/bouw/index.html)

13/05/2025 • 5 minutes reading

[Leontien de Waal](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/authors/leontien-de-waal/index.html)

Housing shortage, energy and materials transition, emission reduction and a shortage of construction sites: the construction sector faces a number of enormous challenges. On top of that, it is becoming increasingly difficult to find new colleagues. Specialist roles – such as calculator or carpenter – are particularly difficult to fill, as construction company Van Wijnen notices. Partly for this reason, they focus many of their efforts on the youngest on the labor market: Gen Z.

A group that requires a completely different message and approach than its predecessors. HR director Tahlvik Leung and campus recruiter Mattias Voshol explain how they manage to get that unique generation to the construction site.

## A look at Generation Z with practical training

This article is part of a study by ABN AMRO into the youngest generation in the labor market: Generation Z, born between 1997 and 2012. In our research we specifically focus on those with a practical education. What do they find important in their work and what are they looking for in an employer? The message that emerges from our report is clear. Practically educated Gen Zers especially want:

1. Clarity about salary and contract form
2. Knowing what concrete work they will do and how they can grow
3. That the basics are in order: good equipment and professional guidance

Curious about our full analysis? Read the complete report here ['What practically educated Gen Zers really want'](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/industrie/wat-praktisch-opgeleide-gen-zers-echt-willen.html)

## More gray than green

It is an immense and growing challenge for organizations, especially where many practically trained people work: an aging population. Knowledge and experience that has been carefully built up over decades is in danger of disappearing in a short time when older employees retire. “We also have a large group of colleagues who are retiring soon,” Mattias begins. 'A significant outflow, while it is difficult to find new talent. Especially for certain roles: for example, we have a major shortage of carpenters and calculators – they determine the cost of our projects. While these professionals are really and quickly needed to retain as much expertise as possible from older colleagues.'

## Impact makes the difference

“Yet we should not make the problem bigger than it is,” Tahlvik adds. 'We have hired about a thousand people in the last three years. There is scarcity, but you can really find people – as long as you have an attractive story. We can offer challenging work with social impact. There is a huge shortage of housing and we have a solution for that, especially in the affordable segment. That story resonates, especially with younger generations. We see this reflected in our workforce: 6 percent of our people on construction sites are Gen Z.”

> “Professionals on the construction site prefer an experienced colleague to take care of them.”

Tahlvik Leung

HR director at Van Wijnen

## Proud of craftsmanship

“Challenge, guidance and social impact,” Tahlvik sums up what the youngest colleagues find important. 'Professionals on the construction site prefer an experienced colleague to take care of them. That they are given plenty of space to learn and make mistakes. They also want to do good work and contribute to something they are proud of. Coming from Leeuwarden and helping to build the new Kooi Stadium in Cambuur - that is beautiful every time you drive past it.'

## Construction site and office: completely different worlds

During recruitment events, Mattias notices a clear difference between practically and theoretically trained job seekers. 'The latter group quickly asks about ambitions and sustainability goals - especially in the big cities. MBO students pay more attention to social aspects, such as sports activities and Friday afternoon drinks. You spend a lot of time together on the construction site, so it's nice if your colleagues are also your friends. That is why we offer many social activities in addition to work: parties and drinks, but also sports tournaments, for example.'

Tahlvik adds: 'We also see that difference in our people: in the office they find purpose and the bigger picture important, the professionals just want everything to be well organised. A nice team, nice work, good stuff and a nice reward. And finally, another interesting difference: in the office they believe that managers should be empathetic, while professionals just want to get along with their manager.'

> “Forget purpose: Gen Zers in construction just want great work and a nice team.”

Tahlvik Leung

HR director at Van Wijnen

## Less on the road, more at home

“We do notice that Generation Z is less willing to travel far to work,” says Tahlvik. 'That is really different from older generations - even when they were younger. Now the youngest colleagues want to spend as much time as possible at home, and therefore spend less time in the car or train. In addition, increased travel costs and traffic jams will play a role. The commuting distance has become a theme. Fortunately for us, it makes a difference that we have national coverage: this means there is always a location near our people.'

## Room for development

Van Wijnen has already learned a lot about the wishes and needs of Generation Z. 'But we still have to get to know them better. Discovering what their push and pull factors are', says Mattias. Tahlvik: 'Ultimately, they are indispensable to our company, so we must do everything we can to appeal to the youngest generation. Bringing them in is one thing, but they also have to be trained as professionals. This requires an investment from us, but Generations X and Y also contribute to this: they guide those younger colleagues on a daily basis. We therefore give everyone the space to develop and learn new things at their own pace. And in the meantime, we continue to emphasize internally and externally how wonderful our work is. Real craftsmanship, that deserves so much pride and appreciation.'

## Want to captivate, attract and retain practically trained Gen Zers? These are Van Wijnen's tips:

- Offer an appealing story, sufficient challenge and beautiful work.
- Ensure good employment and benefits conditions: from salary and work supplies to culture.
- Do not distinguish between internship allowances between practically and theoretically trained students.
- Don't look for specialist professionals, but focus on specific skills or personalities and train them yourself.
- Place more emphasis on a pleasant team atmosphere than on purpose and sustainability ambitions.
- Organize enough social activities outside of work.

## Read more in the construction sector

The Dutch construction sector has been one of the best performing sectors in recent years, despite several challenges facing the sector. Examples are the nitrogen dossier, the significant price increases for energy and building materials and also the personnel shortages. Driven by trends, innovations and legislation and regulations, the relevance of themes such as sustainability and environmental impact is growing and the sector is looking for a new balance.
