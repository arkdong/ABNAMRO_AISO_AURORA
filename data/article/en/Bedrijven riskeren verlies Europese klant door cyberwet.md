# Companies risk losing European customers due to cyber law

[Report](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/rapporten/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

![ABN AMRO](https://assets.abnamro.com/api/public/content/cyber-1738022429-landscape-16x9.jpg?t=h320)

12/06/2024 • 3 minutes read

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

The Netherlands is behind on implementing the European NIS2 directive, which will take effect in October 2024. Dutch companies cannot sit back. They may now face critical questions from customers in Germany and Belgium, where the legislative process is on schedule. NIS2 requires companies to make sure that the cyber security of all their suppliers and customers is in order.

NIS2 succeeds the earlier Network and Information Systems directive (NIS). Although it is a European law, countries are moving at different speeds. Germany and Belgium are almost ready for national implementation, but the Dutch government has indicated it will most likely miss the 17 October deadline. The consultation, where market participants can give feedback on the draft laws, has been postponed several times. This puts the Netherlands behind its largest trading partners; Germany is finalising the legal texts, and Belgium will soon present its legal texts for approval.

## No postponement

This does not mean a postponement for Dutch companies. It is unwise to delay the time-consuming preparations. Customers elsewhere in Europe will also require their Dutch suppliers to be NIS2-compliant. If they cannot guarantee this, these suppliers risk losing their European customers. Large sums are at stake. According to Statistics Netherlands ([CBS](https://opendata.cbs.nl/statline/#/CBS/nl/dataset/85428ned/line?dl=A21FA)), in 2023 the Netherlands exported around € 500 billion of goods within the European Union, of which € 241 billion went to Germany and Belgium.

Germany is the Netherlands’ most important export destination, with a total value of [€ 165.3 billion](https://opendata.cbs.nl/statline/#/CBS/nl/dataset/85428ned/line?dl=A21FA) in 2023. Goods exports to Belgium amounted to € 75.9 billion. These are mainly chemical products and industrial (semi-)manufactures, machinery, equipment and means of transport, and agricultural and food products.

## Able to demonstrate cyber resilience

Given Germany’s culture of formality and strict rule compliance, supply chains are likely to be scrutinised closely — regardless of whether the NIS2 laws are already in force in the Netherlands. To succeed in Germany, Dutch companies should not only accelerate their NIS2 preparations. They should also keep an open and effective communication line about their compliance efforts. “Make sure you can at least demonstrate, for example with a certificate, that you are investing in your organisation’s resilience,” says Daan Hoogendijk, programme director of Samen Digitaal Veilig.

CEO Maarten Roerink of cybersecurity service provider MMOX confirms this from his own experience. “We see that companies are looking for a stamp to show their partners that they are compliant. An ISO 27001 or Cyber Essentials certification helps with this. Some entrepreneurs also get certified to gain a competitive advantage.”

Because such a certification process is demanding, industry organisations, specialists and companies have developed a new quality mark specifically for small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs). The so-called [NIS2 Quality Mark](https://www.samendigitaalveilig.nl/nis2-quality-mark/) is available via the Samen Digitaal Veilig platform and comes in different levels. Smaller organisations can thus demonstrably boost their cyber resilience without having to commit to a heavy ISO certification by default.

## Measures to increase cyber security

Entrepreneurs should get their cyber risks in order. You can do this in three relatively simple steps. The first is to carry out a risk analysis. Map which matters are critical to keep the company operating and services running. The second step is to take measures, such as encouraging safe behaviour among employees, recording who owns specific data, and discussing cyber risks with partners and suppliers. Finally, it is essential to develop procedures to detect and handle cyber incidents.
