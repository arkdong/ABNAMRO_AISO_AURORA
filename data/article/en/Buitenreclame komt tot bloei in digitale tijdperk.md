# Out-of-home advertising flourishes in the digital era

[Analysis](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/analyses/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

18/12/2025 • 7 minutes read

[Mario Bersem](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/mario-bersem/index.html)

Digital out-of-home advertising is making remarkable headway. New screens are appearing in shopping centres, bus shelters and railway stations. Analogue out-of-home also remains popular thanks to mass reach. ABN AMRO expects total out-of-home revenues to grow by 5 per cent in 2025 and 6 per cent in 2026. Up to 2030, out-of-home revenues are expected to grow by an average of 5 per cent a year and overtake radio as an advertising channel.

The Netherlands has 83,000 out-of-home objects, such as bus shelters, motorway masts, transformer houses, scaffold wraps, blank walls, billboards, information pillars and lamp posts. Even buses, trams and ferries are used as advertising platforms. The number of objects is growing due to the addition of new digital screens. At the same time, revenue per object is rising as existing objects are digitised.

The most common size is the abri format (1,185 mm x 1,750 mm, around 2 m²), accounting for 65 per cent of all advertising carriers, found in bus shelters and information pillars. The A0 size (841 mm x 1,189 mm) accounts for 32 per cent and can be found on three-sided boards and electricity boxes. Larger objects are rare and vary in size; only 1,300 objects are larger than the abri format. These are in high-reach locations, such as the LED facade of the Ziggodome in Amsterdam (23 metres high and 400 metres long) or the NEO screen at Hoog Catharijne in Utrecht (193 m²).

## Digital out-of-home is booming and offers new possibilities

In three years, digital out-of-home revenue has more than doubled to 172 million euros in 2024, according to VIA/Deloitte’s Digital Ad Spend 2024 report. In 2025, revenue will grow by 4 per cent to around 179 million euros, ABN AMRO expects. That is 56 per cent of total out-of-home revenue in 2025, which is expected to be 317 million euros. This is striking, as only 17 per cent of all advertising objects are digital; the remaining 83 per cent are analogue. Digital screens generate more revenue than analogue ones because they are on average larger, in the busiest locations and attract more attention.

Digital screens attract more attention thanks to moving images and rotating ads. They are also larger and in busy spots in big cities and along busy roads, where many passers-by ensure high reach. Advertising objects in these locations were digitised first to recoup the high investment costs. Analogue objects are digitised when reach is large enough to justify the costs. The new BRO Next study is the new standard for measuring reach.

Most new digital out-of-home screens are indoors, in shop windows. As long as the screens are visible from the street or public space, they count towards the out-of-home inventory. This phenomenon is part of the [rise of retail media](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/advertenties-lucratieve-bijverdienste-voor-winkelketens.html), where grocers and other retailers are turning to advertising as a lucrative side business. For example, HEMA has almost 330 digital screens with a range of campaigns that generate money in the evenings when the shops are closed. Global, a large out-of-home operator, manages ad sales for HEMA.

For advertisers, digital screens offer more flexibility. Campaigns can be tailored to conditions, such as advertising ice cream on warm days. Advertisers can also better reach specific target groups, such as young people who see ads for matcha tea at a railway station on their way to school. Around 30 per cent of digital ad space is placed automatically using algorithms that take into account factors such as time of day, location and weather. This share is rising and points to more efficient and flexible use of digital ad space.

## Digital advertising dominates the media landscape

Digital ads, especially those via web pages, social media, streaming services and the digital screens mentioned, dominate the Dutch media landscape and accounted for 4.1 billion euros in 2024 — almost three-quarters of all ad spend. Big tech companies, with a market share of 80 per cent (3.5 billion euros), dominate the digital ad market. The digital transformation of the media is not yet complete, so the share of digital ads is still rising.

While traditional mass media such as television, radio and newspapers are losing reach, out-of-home — also known as ‘out of home’ — is gaining importance. Johan Smit, Director of PMA, the industry association for media agencies, explains: “Out of home is actually the only mass medium left and is thereby taking over the leading role of linear TV.”

Digital out-of-home stands out from other digital media, such as online ads, because it is usually not perceived as intrusive. Online, ad blockers can be used and some people experience ad fatigue. “Out-of-home is, on the one hand, not intrusive and, on the other, impossible to miss,” says Sita Bakker, Director of OUTREACH, the industry association for out-of-home operators. “It does not interrupt people during their activities, which contributes to a positive experience,” Bakker adds.

## Analogue out-of-home also remains strong in the digital era

Analogue out-of-home remains relevant and is even growing, with a projected 6 per cent rise in revenue in 2025. That puts analogue out-of-home revenue at around 138 million euros, ABN AMRO expects. Although digitisation slightly reduces the number of analogue objects, traditional forms such as paper posters and static billboards remain popular with advertisers because of mass reach. New measurement methods from the BRO Next study also play a role in this (re)appraisal, as advertisers can increasingly estimate their reach precisely. As the “only mass medium left”, out-of-home offers an alternative to channels such as television that are seen as more expensive.

Out-of-home remains untouched by the online competition affecting other media. When travelling, for example to school or work, people come into contact with out-of-home. Bus shelters, motorway masts, billboards and A0 frames reach 80 per cent of the Dutch population each week. By comparison, linear television reaches 80 per cent of the Dutch population in a month.

About 5 per cent of all ad spend goes to out-of-home. This share varies between advertisers: McDonald’s spends as much as 20 per cent of its budget on out-of-home, and WPP Media, the largest media agency, spends about 10 per cent on out-of-home on behalf of advertisers. These examples show further growth potential for out-of-home, which is also evident from experience in other countries. In a positive scenario, in which current trends continue strongly, the out-of-home share could rise to 7.5 per cent and revenues could reach 600 million euros in 2030. In the base case, however, the out-of-home share remains stable at around the current 5 per cent.

## Municipalities set out-of-home policy

Out-of-home targets passers-by, on foot or by bike, in cars or on public transport. Municipalities apply strict rules on where and how advertising is allowed, that is, in which size and, for example, the brightness of digital screens. Some municipalities levy a municipal advertising tax. Because municipalities are responsible for the design of public space, rules vary widely: the Netherlands has 342 municipalities, each with its own policy on out-of-home. For instance, the Municipality of The Hague has banned ‘fossil advertising’. Through a general municipal by-law (APV), The Hague bans ads for air travel, petrol cars and fossil energy contracts. In Haarlem, a tender is under way in which advertising for meat and fish is not permitted.

Municipalities award multi-year concessions for out-of-home through public tenders. In return, they receive a financial contribution, often combined with payments in kind such as street furniture or media space for their own campaigns and charities. JCDecaux is an example of an operator that invests in bus shelters and maintains them in return for advertising exploitation. Notably, JCDecaux has more staff working in the technical department than in ad sales. A [PWC study](https://www.outsmart.org.uk/resources/ooh-supporting-uk-society) in the United Kingdom shows that 46 per cent of out-of-home ad revenue flows back to municipalities. Applied to the Dutch market, this amounts to around 146 million euros in 2025.

Out-of-home largely depends on municipal policy and concessions, which vary widely between the 342 Dutch municipalities. While some municipalities impose specific restrictions, such as the fossil ad ban in The Hague, there is no national guideline. This leads to variation in rules and uncertainty for operators. Central rules on tobacco, alcohol and gambling ads show that a uniform approach can offer benefits. Without such guidelines, individual municipalities can impose stricter restrictions, which is a risk for the out-of-home sector.

The Dutch out-of-home market is relatively fragmented, with 52 operators, compared with neighbouring countries such as Belgium and Germany, where only two large players are active alongside a few specialists with their own niche. The acquisition of the Dutch arm of Clear Channel by Bauer Media Group in April 2025 is an example of consolidation that will continue. Internationally, the Netherlands still lags behind countries such as the United States and China, especially in digital out-of-home and retail media. Based on international trends and the current market structure, ABN AMRO expects further consolidation and digital growth.

## Rise of the abri

In 1964, Jean Claude Decaux introduced his first bus shelters in Lyon. In return for funding, installation and maintenance, his company, JCDecaux, obtained the rights to exploit advertising in these ‘abribus’ (French for bus shelter). This public-private partnership model spread worldwide, and the term ‘abri’ became synonymous with a widely used ad format: 1,185 mm x 1,750 mm, around 2 m². Advertising in abri format accounts for 65 per cent of all out-of-home in the Netherlands and can be found in bus shelters across the country, stations, shopping centres and along busy streets.
