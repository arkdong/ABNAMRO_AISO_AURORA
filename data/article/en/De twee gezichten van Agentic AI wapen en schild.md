# The two faces of Agentic AI: weapon and shield

[Analysis](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/analyses/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

17/11/2025 • 11 minutes read

[Amad Khan](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/amad-khan/index.html)

Criminals are using artificial intelligence more often to carry out lightning-fast attacks. At the same time, companies are deploying the same technology to defend themselves. The urgency to act is growing, but many organisations do not know where to start within this complex arms race.

The world of cyber security is at a tipping point. Agentic AI — artificial intelligence in which systems operate autonomously and make decisions without human intervention — is fundamentally changing how both defenders and attackers work. Unlike generative AI, which depends on pre-defined instructions, these “agents” can initiate, execute and improve actions on their own based on what they learn.

Recent research by Palo Alto Networks, a US cyber security company, shows how profound this shift is. Researchers managed to simulate a full ransomware attack in just 25 minutes using Agentic AI — a hundred times faster than traditional methods. “The scenario they outline is disturbingly realistic,” says Matthijs Mons, cyber security specialist at ABN AMRO. “Criminals will develop agents that are tailored to targeted attacks.”

## From science fiction to reality

The process described by Palo Alto Networks illustrates the power of Agentic AI. Agents start by gathering public information about their targets via social media and corporate databases. Based on current data, they continually adapt their strategy and try to gain access to networks through personalised phishing attacks, LinkedIn messages or fake invitations to video conferences. Once inside, they analyse patterns to identify the most valuable systems and choose attack techniques that align seamlessly with their target’s vulnerabilities. “For many people this scenario sounds fictional, as if everything happens by itself,” says Mons. “But with traditional AI a human still had to perform at least an initial action. With Agentic AI, things now happen fully autonomously, without anyone being involved. And that applies to both sides.”

The other side is the protective measures that organisations implement. At large financial institutions, hundreds of cyber security specialists work to protect vital infrastructure. The staff shortage in this sector is significant, and Agentic AI offers solutions here. “In an incident, a security expert often has to look up information in different systems,” Mons explains. “That can take hours. An agent for security tasks can, based on a single instruction, independently examine data from system reports and present a complete picture, while an analyst has to follow an entire step-by-step plan.”

> “With Agentic AI, things now happen fully autonomously, without anyone being involved.”

Matthijs Mons

ABN AMRO

## Simulating an attack

The most promising applications are in simulating attacks on one’s own organisation, so-called “red team” actions. Traditionally, security teams spend days or even weeks on this: mapping systems, finding access routes and testing different paths.

By learning from previous simulated attacks, Agentic AI can break in much faster and map which attack sequence is most effective — exactly what criminals will do as well. There are already companies that offer this as a service. They deploy simulations for their clients and learn from both the attacking “red teams” and the defending “blue teams”. This combined approach, also known as “purple AI”, enables organisations to learn from both perspectives and continuously improve their security. It is important to note that Agentic AI uses existing methods. It executes attacks faster than ever, but it does not devise new methods. So far, Agentic AI is mainly a leap in efficiency. “The major cyber security companies are investing heavily in AI,” says Mons. “But criminals are just as active. The romantic image of hackers as lonely boys in basements is completely outdated. Cyber attacks are carried out by professional organisations with specific roles focused on gaining access, building malware, installing malware, stealing data, negotiations, etc. They even have HR departments and call centres, with a solid business case based on the proceeds they generate from stolen data.”

## The paradox of adoption

The use of Agentic AI within organisations — for both security and regular business processes — also creates new vulnerabilities. Hackers can, for example, try to manipulate the AI agents that already have access to systems.

This leads to a striking paradox, revealed by a survey by SailPoint (a US technology company focused on identity and access management) among 353 cyber security professionals worldwide. Although 96 per cent of respondents consider agents to be a growing security threat, as many as 98 per cent of organisations plan to expand their use of Agentic AI in the coming year. The study shows that 82 per cent of companies already use agents, with more than half reporting that these have daily access to sensitive data. Nevertheless, only 44 per cent have implemented policies to protect these agents against external threats. Perhaps most alarming: nearly one in four companies reported that agents can be manipulated to reveal access credentials.

“The use of this technology is a risk in itself,” warn Hans van Haarlem and Steven Ottenhoff of MMOX, a Dutch cyber security consultancy. They point to a crucial point that many entrepreneurs overlook. “In the past, attacking large companies took enormous time and resources. That threshold has now been drastically lowered. Criminals can now work much more efficiently, which makes medium-sized and smaller organisations attractive targets as well.”

> “Hold a regular digital fire drill, so everyone is prepared and knows their role in a crisis.”

Hans van Haarlem

MMOX

## Volume and quality are increasing

The lower threshold for attacks is already visible in practice, with attackers expanding their scope. “We receive buckets full of phishing emails to analyse,” says Van Haarlem. “What stands out is that both the volume and the quality are increasing. Spear phishing — targeted attacks on specific individuals — is carried out much better. We regularly see new employees receive texts from ‘our’ CEO in no time, with requests to transfer money or get in touch. On their very first working day. It is hard to believe this is not automated in the background.”

The number of cyber attacks has not yet risen explosively, and there is a reason for that. “Agentic AI systems are still super expensive,” the ABN AMRO cyber security specialist explains. “They require a lot of computing power and training models costs money. Criminals therefore continue to focus on relatively easy targets. Hacking a large bank can take years, but a small accountancy firm can be cracked within a week with a substantial haul. Generative AI has caused the number of phishing emails to increase drastically. That is cheap and easy. Full Agentic AI attacks, in which systems independently screen social media and carry out spear phishing, are certainly coming, but are not yet widespread.”

## Internal security risks

In addition to external threats, agents deployed for both security and other business processes pose specific security risks within organisations. “If such an agent is poorly configured or insufficiently monitored, situations can arise that you do not want,” warns Mons. “Imagine: an agent detects that you clicked on a phishing email. The normal procedure is to isolate that computer from the network so it cannot cause damage. But if that agent accidentally isolates all the computers in your network instead of one, no one can work.”

There is a more fundamental problem. Agentic AI can act based on data and prior experience without human control, but it can also “hallucinate”: draw wrong conclusions or perform unexpected actions. “You then do not know exactly what it has done,” Mons explains. “It learns by itself and acts by itself. If things go wrong, you have to find out everything that happened and what you need to restore. That is why it is crucial to build in good control mechanisms and keep options open that make it possible for people to intervene themselves.”

The experts at MMOX add nuance. “Agentic AI has no stress or workload under which someone becomes sloppy. It can do something wrong with good intentions because the underlying model is inadequate, but the margin of error is often smaller than with people.”

## What can companies do?

The course of action for organisations is clear but wide-ranging. “Make sure you are better protected than the neighbour,” says Mons. “On a street, burglars break into the worst-protected house. That basic principle also applies in the digital world. It is not about absolute security, but about relative resilience.” Concrete measures start with fundamentals: good device security and two-factor authentication. But the latter is not watertight. “There are scenarios in which that double check can still be bypassed. It is a constant cat-and-mouse game between security makers and malicious parties.”

Van Haarlem therefore argues for a strategic approach that goes beyond technology alone. “AI does not change the methodology of cyber criminals, it only changes efficiency and thus scope,” he states. “We are proponents of the NIST framework: a widely recognised set of guidelines that helps organisations manage and reduce cyber security risks. Invest in employees and technology, in network segmentation and strong data analysis.” Impact-mitigating measures are a crucial but underestimated element. “Instead of focusing only on keeping hackers out, you must ensure they cannot cause damage once they are in,” Van Haarlem and Ottenhoff emphasise. “Segment your network so an attacker cannot move freely through your entire systems. Try to build a high wall, but realise that if someone climbs over it, there must be more barriers inside.”

> “The threshold for cyber attacks is lower than ever. Companies that do not act now face major risks.”

Steven Ottenhoff

MMOX

## The human factor

The role of employees remains complex. While some see people as the weakest link, ABN AMRO’s Mons thinks differently. “We consider people the last resort, the strongest link. Technology may have failed, but a vigilant employee can still stop an attack. That is why we promote safe behaviour across the entire organisation.” Van Haarlem and Ottenhoff go deeper into this aspect. “Changing human behaviour is extremely difficult, especially when the digital skills of the current population lag behind and employees are not intrinsically motivated to work safely.”

The experts at MMOX distinguish three essential factors. First, organisations must set clear guidelines for safe behaviour and provide user-friendly security tools. They warn: “Many tools wither on the vine because adoption lags.” Second, the urgency of cyber risks must be emphasised continuously. “This is not something for a single training session, but must be institutionalised. Discuss it monthly or quarterly during meetings, repetition is essential.” Third, organisations must understand the working environment. “Map which systems you have. Listen carefully to your employees. If they download software or extensions themselves because they are missing something in the official systems, you need to take that seriously. By understanding their needs and meeting them, you prevent them from seeking unsafe solutions. Make the topic open for discussion.”

## Resilience rather than perfection

The central message from all experts is clear: perfect security does not exist. “The question is not whether you will be hacked, but when,” say the experts at MMOX. “The costs of attacks have become so low that every company is a potential target. We therefore advise clients: be prepared. Develop resilience.” Concretely, that means: ensure backups that actually work, develop an incident response system — a structured step-by-step plan to respond quickly and effectively to a cyber incident — and practise regularly with scenarios. “Simulate that you have been hacked,” they advise. “What will you do? How will you communicate? What if your data are leaked? Hold a regular digital fire drill, so everyone is prepared and knows their role in a crisis.”

For small and medium-sized enterprises, the challenges are different from those of large organisations. “The blue team agents and advanced systems that are now available require such investment that they are not relevant for most entrepreneurs.” Entrepreneurs often do not know where to start. That is why we focus on helping lay a solid foundation: getting basic measures in order so you are better protected than the neighbour.”

## Opportunities and threats

Despite all the risks, Agentic AI also offers substantial opportunities. ABN AMRO cyber security specialist Mons underlines this. “Red team actions that used to take weeks can now take days. Analyses that took hours now take minutes. With Agentic AI, we can test ourselves better, find vulnerabilities faster and respond more effectively to incidents,” he says. But both parties also stress the need for caution. Ottenhoff: “Agentic AI is not necessarily transparent. Agentic systems can do something wrong with good intentions. Sometimes a simple, predictable system is a better choice than complex AI.”

## The urgency is growing

Cyber attacks will in future be faster, on a larger scale and more under the radar. This calls for security systems that continually adapt. Old systems that rely on fixed controls and slow updates fall short. A new approach is needed in which vulnerabilities in the cloud and other digital systems are continuously monitored, and in which simulating realistic threats helps identify and fix weak spots. Within organisations, all Agentic AI systems — whether used for security or other business processes — should not be regarded as ordinary system components, but as separate entities with their own security needs. This requires a strategy that identifies, monitors and controls all agents. In this way, companies can not only comply with regulation, but also strengthen their overall security posture.

At the same time, agents deployed by malicious actors form a growing external threat. The rise of Agentic AI does not introduce entirely new attack methods, but makes existing techniques faster and more effective. The speed and adaptability of these systems pose a major challenge. Companies must therefore innovate proactively and anticipate agent-driven attacks.

“It is very difficult to move cyber security from ‘important but not urgent’ to ‘urgent’,” Ottenhoff observes. “But with the rise of Agentic AI that urgency is real. The industry behind cyber crime has matured, with solid business cases. The threshold to attack is lower than ever. Companies that do not act now face major risks.”

The two faces of Agentic AI — as weapon and as shield — force organisations to rethink. The technology offers unprecedented opportunities for efficient security and scaling up cyber security teams. But that same power is also in the hands of criminals who are becoming more professional. The challenge is clear: companies must now invest in fundamental security, involve and prepare their employees, and realise that absolute security does not exist. Resilience and preparation are the new watchwords in an era where the question is not whether, but when you will be attacked.
