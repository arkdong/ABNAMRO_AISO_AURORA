# The virtual human as a commercial opportunity

[Analysis](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/analyses/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

![The virtual human as a commercial opportunity](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/media/de-virtuele-mens-als-commerciele-kans_1554013514_tcm16-207101.jpg)

25/09/2023 • 8 minutes read

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

From virtual replicas of celebrities to social media personalities generated from scratch – virtual humans are popping up everywhere. The public seem open to virtual brand ambassadors and the underlying technology is also becoming more widely available. This creates opportunities for brands, technology providers and forward-looking advertising agencies

Model Eva Herzigova has had an exact replica of herself created, from the tiny hairs on her face to her facial expressions. The model’s digital counterpart can appear in photo shoots and videos and even walk catwalk shows; Herzigova’s walk has been copied as well. If the real Herzigova no longer has to travel the world for every job, the model told [Vogue Business](https://www.voguebusiness.com/technology/brands-can-now-hire-supermodel-eva-herzigovas-digital-twin), she can spend more time with her children.

That makes Herzigova one of the first celebrities to actively market her replica. In this way she not only increases her productivity. It is also a step towards more control over her digital identity. In a world where artificial intelligence (AI) can easily create fake material of people, one remedy may be to pre-empt this by presenting the most convincing replica yourself.

## A scalable and personal message

Brands do not seem to shy away from such innovations either. Holidaymakers who want to persuade their friends to join them on a Virgin Voyages cruise can send their prospective travel partners a personalised invitation in which Jennifer Lopez paints them a picture of a dream holiday. Jen AI addresses them by name and knows the occasion and the group’s favourite activities. In a campaign by crisp brand Lay’s with Lionel Messi, the real version of the footballer appeared in commercials while a digital twin of the footballer was simultaneously available for personalised online video messages. In these so-called Messi Messages, a virtual Messi addresses the viewer by name, in ten possible languages.

Chocolate brand Cadbury created a win-win in India through an advertising campaign that applied ‘deepfake’ technology in an innovative way. Small business owners, from clothes shops to electronics stores, could have film star Shah Rukh Khan promote their own business in an online advert by the chocolate brand. Cadbury itself plays only a small role in the video, but strengthens its name in a sympathetic way by giving space to the local shopkeeper.

For brands, such campaigns are a way to tie a big name to them while also narrowing the distance between superstar and target group. It fits seamlessly with the present day, in which celebrities are closer than ever through social media; it is easy to follow their ups and downs and sometimes even watch them live. A scalable yet ‘personal’ message is a logical next step.

## The rise of virtual influencers

But however perfect and choreographed such videos are, the celebrity remains a human being with associated whims. That carries risks, as every brand that has ever associated itself with a famous person knows. In 2001, for example, Britney Spears was photographed by paparazzi with a can of Coca Cola, while at that time she had a costly sponsorship deal with Pepsi. Even now, any crack in the reputation of a carefully chosen ambassador is also a vulnerability for the brand. The solution: partner with a fully virtual ‘influencer’, without a compromising past or present.

Miquela is a frequently cited example. Her full name is Miquela Sousa and she was fully created by marketing agency Brud. Sousa’s identity has been built in detail, with her own background, personality and preferences. On video platform TikTok, where she has no fewer than 3.5 million followers, she posts about her daily life and answers questions. “Hormones,” Miquela replies when asked what she really doesn’t understand about people. She has created content with brands such as Samsung and Calvin Klein, and last year started a long-term partnership with the US retailer Pacsun.

## Branding opportunities

iIt turns out to be a lucrative business. US research shows that about [35 per cent](https://theinfluencermarketingfactory.com/virtual-influencers-survey-infographic/?utm_source=pr&utm_medium=link&utm_campaign=virtual_influencers_PR) of internet users have at some point bought a product or service after a virtual influencer drew attention to it. People also seem to enjoy interacting with these created individuals; research shows that internet users have [three times](https://hypeauditor.com/blog/the-top-instagram-virtual-influencers-in-2021/) as much engagement with posts by virtual influencers as with those by real people – think likes and comments.

A textbook success story is Lu, the much-loved ambassador of Brazilian retailer Magalu. She grew from virtual assistant in the webshop to authoritative [social media personality](https://www.ogilvy.com/work/lu-magalu). She even appears in music videos by real artists, and the outfits she wears there then invariably sell out. The label ‘influencer’ is therefore anything but empty in Lu’s case.

Brand-specific personalities are not new, but by transforming them into 3D models that can showcase their character and lifestyle across many channels, the possibilities for branding increase significantly. From social media to games and virtual reality worlds, they can make an appearance everywhere.

## Proceed with caution

Even amid the hype, caution is advised; many people are not waiting for this. When retailer Marks & Spencer introduced its virtual personality Mira at the end of 2022, the public were not enthusiastic. In the comments under her Instagram posts, people called Mira “creepy” and expressed incomprehension at M&S’s choice of a perfect appearance. If you do opt for a virtual brand ambassador, the criticism ran, then choose someone whose appearance is a better reflection of the brand’s customer base. No new posts from Mira have appeared since November.

Although it is an experimental – and often successful – way to connect mainly with Gen Z and younger, the virtual influencer will be a bridge too far for most smaller brands. It remains to be seen whether the advantages over regular influencer marketing currently outweigh the risks and investments. For now, the opportunities seem to be concentrated mainly among larger brands that are actively experimenting with their branding efforts and want to reach a younger target group.

Knowledge and expertise are needed to steer such initiatives in the right direction. That in turn creates opportunities for agencies that know the field. Advertising agency Ogilvy was the brain behind the successful Cadbury campaign in India and was also the party that managed to turn Lu into a true influencer. Recently, advertising agency Dentsu even created a specific service: Dentsu VI, where the two-letter abbreviation stands for ‘virtual identity’. The division creates completely new virtual personalities on behalf of brands, or adapts already developed models to the client’s wishes.

## AI-generated models

Smaller brands would nevertheless do well to read up further; technological developments are moving fast, and virtual humans can also add value behind the scenes of business operations. So says Sharief Mohamed, CFO and COO of Lalaland AI, which develops lifelike 3D models for fashion designers. Via a plug-in in a widely used programme, designers can have their three-dimensional clothing designs virtually worn by models with different ready-to-wear sizes and skin tones. “This allows them to see straight away whether a design also looks good on people with a size other than small,” says Mohamed. “And whether the chosen colour of the garment works with a particular skin tone.”

This also offers benefits on the purchasing side. Retailers can more easily form a picture of the individual garments in brands’ collections when they are worn by models. Due to the large number of designs, catalogues show most garments without a model, photographed against a white background. By using virtual models, buyers know better what they are bringing into their shops.

The models’ appearance can be fully adapted to the brand’s identity, from hairstyle to accessories. According to Mohamed, they are certainly no replacement for brands’ more creative campaign images. “Towards consumers you will still need real people,” he says. “Our added value at present is behind the scenes, in the design phase.”

## Major investments in technology companies

Lalaland is not the only company to have jumped on this wave of innovation. A growing number of technology companies offer services for creating virtual humans, whether replicas of existing individuals or fully invented figures. Recently, Synthesia, the technology company behind the Messi Messages, reached unicorn status after raising 90 million dollars from investors. Yoom, which creates 3D versions of people in a special studio with cameras on all sides, has raised 50 million dollars in funding since its inception. It is an interesting proposition for, among others, artists who want to have their performances recorded in three dimensions and brands that want to bring their ambassadors into people’s homes via augmented reality.

Hyperreal is another player facilitating the creation of virtual humans and showing highly convincing results. For example, their website features a video clip of a performance by rapper Biggie Smalls, who died in 1997. Only, it is not a real concert but a [lifelike reconstruction](https://www.technologyreview.com/2022/12/16/1065393/biggie-smalls-metaverse/) of the artist. Ownership and control from the real world are explicitly part of Hyperreal’s proposition; the rapper’s heirs have full say over the use of the underlying 3D model. In doing so, the company is preparing for a world in which everyone can have their own virtual replica and in which digital identities also need protection against theft and misuse.

## Technology is becoming more accessible

Due to their level of detail and versatility, replicas like those of Biggie Smalls and Eva Herzigova are time-consuming and costly to make. Even so, a host of technologies are available that also open doors for people and brands with less deep pockets. This allows them to experiment, in a low-threshold way, with the technological possibilities and limitations. Gaming giant Epic Games, for example, offers a free web application, MetaHumans, with which users can create virtual humans. It is even possible to import your own 3D scans – a functionality available in many mobile apps – and refine them on the platform into a convincing digital twin.

Rotterdam-based social media agency RauwCC also started experimenting with the available building blocks. Images of their creation Esther Olofsson used to be made by projecting her virtual face onto photos of a real model, but the influencer is now entirely ‘[AI-based](https://www.emerce.nl/nieuws/esther-olofsson-evolueert-tot-volledig-aifotomodel)’. New photos of Olofsson can now be created via a simple textual instruction – also called a ‘prompt’. Stable Diffusion, the underlying AI model, was trained on a 3D version of the influencer for this purpose. Video footage of Olofsson also appears regularly, with photos animated afterwards using Movio and a synthetic voice added with Replica Studios’ software.

In the near future, it is unlikely to be technology that is the biggest challenge, but the ability of brands and their agencies to forge unique partnerships with existing celebrities – real or created – and shape new personalities that resonate with their audiences. In a world where technological possibilities blur the line between real and fake, brands must have a clear vision of their own core and of creative ways to put that across to their target group.
