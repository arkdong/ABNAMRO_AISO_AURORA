# Deregulation is not the end of RegTech

[Analysis](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/analyses/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

18/03/2025 • 6 minutes read

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

Stricter regulation gave innovative start-ups that help companies comply with legislation a growth spurt, but with the global call for deregulation the market seems under pressure. The RegTech sector must reinvent itself to stay relevant.

In recent years, start-ups and scale-ups have focused on developing smart technology that helps companies comply with laws and regulations. This is also called ‘regulatory technology’, often shortened to RegTech.

The need for such technology grew, among other things, when the European Union (EU) introduced the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), a directive to safeguard citizens’ data privacy, and developed the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD), which mandates sustainability reporting. Demand also increased in the financial sector, partly due to guidelines that require financial institutions to carefully screen suspicious transactions and customers.

## Deregulation as a political and economic reality

A political shift is now under way. In both the United States and the EU, the call for deregulation and for reducing administrative burdens is growing, driven in part by geopolitical competition and economic uncertainty. President Trump is pushing for a drastic slimming down of regulation, with the principle that for every new federal law, ten existing rules must be scrapped. His goal is to reduce regulatory pressure and stimulate economic growth. Especially in the area of ESG compliance, companies’ obligations are expected to decrease sharply, and financial institutions are expected to experience less regulatory pressure as well.

On our side of the ocean, the European Commission proposed the Omnibus reform package in February 2025, which is estimated to reduce the administrative burden of sustainability reporting by more than € 6 billion. This reform package is intended to make compliance simpler and less costly for companies.

As a result, an estimated 80 per cent of companies, contrary to earlier proposals, will no longer have to comply with CSRD’s reporting requirement. Although some will continue to report out of intrinsic motivation, this development is a hard blow for RegTech companies specialised in ESG reporting.

## Investor confidence

RegTech is a niche, as shown by the relatively modest sums that have flowed to start-ups and scale-ups in this space. Capital flows peaked in 2021, with a global investment volume of more than 1 billion dollars. A similar peak was also visible in the broader tech sector, driven by low interest rates and the digitisation push in the wake of the pandemic. But while tech investment fell sharply across the board in 2022, the RegTech niche saw only a minimal correction that year — possibly a sign of confidence that laws and regulations would remain a driving force behind the market. Although a correction followed the year after, investment has since shown a cautious recovery.

The RegTech sector includes a wide range of services that help companies integrate laws and regulations into their operations efficiently. Some providers develop software that scans business processes and systems for compliance with all relevant laws and regulations in a given sector. Others take specific regulation as a starting point and offer tools that make it easier for companies to meet the associated obligations. Examples include software that identifies violations of human rights and environmental standards in supply chains, and systems that scan financial transactions for suspicious patterns.

## RegTech between growth and uncertainty

The rise of RegTech is closely linked to the financial sector. After the 2008 financial crisis, governments worldwide introduced stricter regulation to better oversee financial institutions, which led to strong demand for technological compliance solutions. Solutions for Know Your Customer (KYC), transaction monitoring and anti-money laundering regulation (AML) initially dominated the market. The advent of the GDPR in 2018 and the broader focus on data privacy and sustainability led RegTech to expand into other sectors, attracting new investment flows.

Laws and regulations are often a catalyst for innovation and new business opportunities. “Europe is known for its tendency to regulate, and that creates an interesting market for RegTech,” says Sophie Heijenberg, investor at No Such Ventures. For example, in 2022, the year after the CSRD was first announced, 1.8 billion dollars was promptly invested in sustainability measurement software, while a year earlier this amount was only 0.5 billion dollars. After this peak, however, a decline followed that has continued since. Although this type of software does not always fall directly under RegTech, it does show how regulation can set a market in motion. The question remains whether this niche will recover now that the political wind has turned, or whether investor interest will decline further.

## From reporting to risk management

Dependence on laws and regulations places RegTech companies in a dynamic playing field. That makes some players vulnerable. “Tools that only serve to generate reports will struggle,” says Heijenberg. “But broader platforms that focus on risk management in the areas of sustainability, privacy and cybersecurity remain relevant. Legislation may be slimmed down, but the underlying risks do not disappear and can come very close. Just look at the floods in Limburg.”

The Austrian scale-up Prewave develops software that helps manage those underlying risks. Under the banner of “supply chain superintelligence”, the company uses public data to identify risks in the supply chain, such as natural disasters, cyber incidents and financial stress. Although the platform simultaneously helps its users comply with laws and regulations using this data, co-founder Harald Nitschinger places his product in a broader perspective. “As laws and regulations become more complex, companies should not simply tick boxes, but rather use compliance to become stronger and more resilient,” he said last year when Prewave secured € 63 million in investment.

## Sector-specific compliance as a growth market

It can also pay to focus on specific niches, as proven by the Dutch-British Naq, which developed a compliance platform for tech companies active in the medical sector. There are strict requirements for the use of medical devices, and privacy legislation is extra relevant due to the large amount of sensitive personal data being processed. The medical sector is also designated as ‘critical’ under the NIS2 cyber law, which brings with it all kinds of stricter cybersecurity requirements.

“MedTech companies must be compliant from day one if they want to sell their services to, for example, hospitals,” explains Heijenberg of No Such Ventures, one of the investors behind Naq. “If, as a start-up, you can take out a software subscription instead of paying tens of thousands of euros to a consultant, that really solves a pain.”

## Keeping a finger on the pulse

Even in a context of deregulation, laws change at a rapid pace; they shift, are relaxed or tightened in response to economic and political developments. For companies active in multiple jurisdictions or within complex global supply chains, it is important to keep a constant finger on the pulse. New compliance requirements can emerge suddenly, while existing obligations can change or lapse.

Platforms such as Regology respond to this by giving companies insight into legal changes and translating them directly into practical action points. This enables organisations to implement changes in good time, without having to keep track of legal developments themselves continuously. To keep up, it is important for RegTech companies to embed such intelligence in their services.

## Agility and strategic risk management

Regulation can set a market in motion, as seen in the explosion of investment in sustainability measurement software following the CSRD. But laws come and go, and anyone who sees RegTech purely as a reaction to new obligations is building on quicksand. The essence of regulation remains: managing risks, ensuring transparency and keeping markets fair and safe. RegTech companies that focus on this, and that can adapt quickly to changing risks and requirements, create lasting value — regardless of whether regulation is tightened or slimmed down.
