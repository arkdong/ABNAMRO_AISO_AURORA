# Sustainability is becoming a pressing necessity for software companies

[Analysis](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/analyses/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

23/12/2020 • 6 minutes read

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

As our society becomes ever more digital, the influence of software companies grows. They shape how we keep in touch with friends, what information we consume and how we travel. Because of that prominent role, software companies are facing closer scrutiny from both citizens and government.

Social media companies are being examined for how they can influence election results. Tech companies regularly make the news for privacy violations. And although software companies are generally—and rightly—praised for making things easier, there are many pitfalls: from algorithms that discriminate unjustly to energy-guzzling streaming services. Entrepreneurs in the digital world are therefore not in a boundless playground where only commercial opportunities lead the way. They constantly perform a challenging balancing act shaped by regulation and public opinion. They also have their own challenges, such as attracting staff in a tight labour market. That makes sustainability not only a noble aim for software companies, but also essential to safeguard their continuity and legitimacy. Data protection and AI ethics, diversity and energy efficiency are three key themes here.

## Data protection and AI ethics

In a digital society where large volumes of data are produced, processed and exchanged every day, privacy is unfortunately not a given. That is one reason why the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) was introduced in 2018. However, the GDPR does not offer a ready-made checklist for companies. It keeps evolving. New technological developments and expanding case law are shaping the GDPR further. For example, a recent [court case](https://fd.nl/ondernemen/1351533/baanbrekende-zaak-over-algoritmes-uber-waarschuwing-voor-andere-bedrijven) against taxi company Uber will clarify what ‘transparency’ means in the context of algorithmic decision-making. Software companies would therefore be wise to follow new cases on data processing closely.

Today, using data goes hand in hand with deploying Artificial Intelligence (AI), a set of technologies that mimic (elements of) human intelligence and use large volumes of data to do so. AI can unfortunately lead to unintended discrimination, for example when used in recruitment software. The European Commission therefore doubts whether current GDPR legislation suffices for effective enforcement and is exploring specific regulation on artificial intelligence. In 2021, the Commission will present a proposal, including specific attention to preventing discrimination. Until then, software companies can already assess themselves against the [Assessment List for Trustworthy Artificial Intelligence](https://futurium.ec.europa.eu/en/european-ai-alliance/pages/altai-assessment-list-trustworthy-artificial-intelligence).

Although the legal framework on data protection and AI ethics is still developing, software companies can already prepare for the relevant themes. That matters not only to avoid fines now and in future, but also because software users are making their voices heard. Citizens are assertive about their digital rights and do not shy away from pushing back. Recently, De Consumentenbond and the Data Privacy Stichting called on people to join a mass claim against Facebook. People in the Netherlands can also vote each year for the Big Brother Award from the Bits of Freedom foundation. The prize goes to the ‘privacy offender of the year’, which always draws media attention. Possible fines and public shaming are not the only incentives to invest in users’ privacy. In a survey by IT network company Cisco, as many as 74 per cent of companies reported a significant positive effect on [customer loyalty and trust](https://www.cisco.com/c/dam/en/us/products/collateral/security/2020-data-privacy-cybersecurity-series-jan-2020.pdf).

## Diversity

Where attention to privacy can lead to more loyal customers, a diverse workforce can lead to better processes and products. That is no different in software development. [Research](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/326079170_Diversity_in_software_engineering) shows that diverse teams of software developers collaborate more efficiently and show more creativity. Thinking from multiple perspectives can also help digital solutions fit the needs of a broader audience. Amazon even states that ‘[inclusive design](https://www.wherewomenwork.com/Career/1730/Amazon-solutions-architect-Suzie-Miller)’, which starts from the needs of people with disabilities, produces better products for everyone.

There is an even more pressing argument for diversity: the ICT sector has struggled for years with hard-to-fill vacancies. Even during corona times, the vacancy rate is high: about 43 vacancies per thousand jobs, compared with 24 for all sectors [on average](https://www.uwv.nl/overuwv/Images/uwv-duiding-arbeidsmarktontwikkelingen-oktober-2020.pdf). One solution to this shortage is to engage groups that are traditionally under-represented in ICT. The share of female software developers in the Netherlands is particularly low, at just 8.4 per cent of the total number of software developers ([source](https://insights.stackoverflow.com/survey/2020#demographics)).

Although this imbalance is partly due to the still modest number of women choosing a technical education, software companies also bear responsibility. A non-inclusive corporate culture is one of the main [reasons](https://www.accenture.com/_acnmedia/PDF-134/Accenture-A4-GWC-Report-Final1.pdf#zoom=50) why women leave tech companies. This masculine culture—also known as ‘brogrammer culture’—also makes it harder to hire women at all, because it seeps into recruitment and selection. For example, women may feel less addressed by a vacancy, or ultimately lose out to a male candidate. Software companies that actively attract and retain people beyond the usual pool improve their chances of success in a labour market plagued by shortages.

## Energy consumption

It is only a matter of time before software companies are drawn into the current debate about data centres and their energy use. For now, this mainly concerns the tech giants who are placing their ‘hyperscalers’ in the Dutch polder and the municipalities that grant permits. Software companies are also key players in this issue. Developers of digital services often buy the cloud services that run in these data centres. For instance, Netflix largely relies on Amazon’s cloud, and Zoom uses cloud services from Amazon, Microsoft and Oracle. Software companies can influence data centres’ energy consumption—indirectly—by choosing a cloud provider based on the extent to which the data centre uses renewable energy and on the facility’s energy efficiency.

Developers can also build their software to require less memory, data storage or data traffic. That gives them a direct influence on data centres’ energy use. For example, cleaning up and restructuring code (‘software refactoring’) can make a programme substantially more energy-efficient. Unfortunately, energy-efficient software is still largely uncharted territory; programmers’ knowledge in this area is [limited](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/308789636_What_do_programmers_know_about_the_energy_consumption_of_software), and measuring the energy consumption of different software components is complex. So it takes a clear intent during development to limit a product’s footprint.

This applies in particular to software that uses artificial intelligence. Training the underlying models uses [a lot of energy](https://medium.com/pcmag-access/ai-could-save-the-world-if-it-doesnt-ruin-the-environment-first-c8cd7e0de76c), and energy use rises exponentially for the last few percentage points of extra ‘accuracy’. Developers of facial recognition software could ask themselves whether those few extra correctly recognised faces outweigh the extra energy used to improve the model. The European Commission is exploring ways to encourage energy-efficient AI development. An ‘eco-label’ for software is one possible [avenue](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7225509/) being explored in academia.

Software companies operate in a challenging playing field. They face persistent labour market shortages, and both citizens and government are setting ever more demands on digital solutions. For these companies, themes such as data protection and AI ethics, diversity and energy efficiency are not empty phrases or attempts at ‘greenwashing’. They are a pressing necessity. It helps that investments here can lead to digital products that are better for people, nature and society.
