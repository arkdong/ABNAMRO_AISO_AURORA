# Europe is still a long way from its own chip industry

[Analysis](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/analyses/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

10/06/2025 • 4 minutes read

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

Without semiconductors, the digital economy stops. This is exactly where Europe proves vulnerable. Production is largely outsourced to Asian foundries, and the design of advanced chips also mostly sits outside Europe. The European Chips Act was meant to turn the tide, but it has run into a complex reality.

In the chip sector, Europe relies heavily on foreign production capacity. Only six per cent of global chip production takes place in Europe and the Middle East. Asia accounts for more than 80 per cent of production. The United States supplies eight per cent of all chips, but it has the strongest grip on design, patents and software. Europe lacks that control. Most chips made here are relatively simple: for cars, household appliances and industrial applications. Advanced chips — needed for smartphones, AI or military technology — mainly come from Taiwan and South Korea.

## Large dependencies despite strong local players

ASML is Europe’s trump card. The Veldhoven-based company is the only one in the world that supplies machines for extreme ultraviolet lithography (EUV), the technology used to make the most advanced chips. But ASML supplies the world, not just Europe.

Germany has strong positions in power electronics with Infineon and Bosch; chips that switch and regulate electrical energy efficiently, for example in cars and solar panels. Players such as Dutch NXP and Franco‑Swiss STMicroelectronics focus on embedded chips. These are microchips built into devices or vehicles to perform one specific task, such as controlling a motor or reading a sensor. For the most advanced chips, Europe has no fabs and depends mainly on Taiwan and South Korea.

The Draghi report from 2024 calls the lack of production capacity a strategic risk. Taiwan, home to the world’s largest chipmaker TSMC, is considered by China to be a breakaway province and has lived for years under the threat of military annexation. In a crisis, chip supplies could stop abruptly. Even in segments where Europe is traditionally strong, such as car chips, vulnerability turns out to be high. During the coronavirus pandemic, car factories came to a standstill due to a shortage of simple chips. Large European chip companies are largely dependent on foundries in Asia. Car brands are relatively small customers there, which put them lower on the priority list when demand returned after the first lockdowns.

## Chips as a priority in Brussels

With the European Chips Act, Europe took a first step in 2023 to reduce its dependence on semiconductors. With € 43 billion in public and private investment, Europe aims to double its market share in chip production to 20 per cent by 2030. Europe is trying to catch up in advanced chips, while also building further on its existing strength in simpler chips.

There are plenty of initiatives in that second category. Local champion STMicroelectronics is expanding production, including by building a new factory in Italy. A factory by Taiwan’s TSMC is coming to Dresden in partnership with European players Bosch, Infineon and NXP. Production of advanced chips, by contrast, has yet to get off the ground. The planned megafab by GlobalFoundries and STMicroelectronics in France has been halted, and construction of Intel’s advanced factory in Germany has been pushed back to 2029.

## Chip Act 2.0

Because the Chips Act has not delivered enough overall, the European Commission is considering a ‘Chips Act 2.0’. The chip industry sees value in a programme that supports the entire chain, from design and materials to machines and fabs. The European Parliament is calling for investment in AI chips, but the industry itself argues for continued support for older chip technology as well, so that chips for cars, machinery and power grids can also come from Europe. The new programme will be presented before the summer.

The road to strategic autonomy in chips is tough. The supply chain branches worldwide and is highly capital‑intensive. New factories cost tens of billions and take years to become operational. The revision of the Chips Act signals the ambition for a serious change of course. Europe does not have to do everything itself — which is impossible anyway — but it does need enough capacity not to stand by helplessly when geopolitics cause shocks.
