# Internet not only faster, but also more energy-efficient

[Headlines & Insights](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/headlines-insights/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

05/01/2021 • 4 minutes read

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

Two-thirds of Dutch households can now access ultra-fast ‘gigabit internet’, reports industry association NLconnect. This means the central government’s target for 2023 has already been met.

The high coverage of this fast, fixed broadband network can be explained by two efforts from the telecoms sector: the ongoing roll-out of fibre to the home and higher internet speeds over existing TV cables.

## Insight

Both strategies not only give consumers the chance to enjoy ultra-fast internet, but also improve energy consumption. The energy efficiency of communications networks is topical because data traffic has been growing exponentially for years. To limit CO2 emissions, it is important that the energy used for data traffic does not rise at the same pace.

Most of the fixed broadband network in the Netherlands now consists of fibre. This is good news, because fibre is known as the most energy-efficient way to exchange data. In most cases, however, the last leg of internet traffic, between the local exchange or street cabinet and the home, still runs over the ‘old-fashioned’ connection. In KPN’s existing network this is the copper telephone line, and in VodafoneZiggo’s network it is the TV cable. These parties are powerful; together they currently account for eighty to ninety per cent of fixed internet connections for households.1

In terms of speed, the copper network has reached its limit. That is why KPN has worked this year to fibre up this last stretch for more and more households. Such a connection is called ‘Fibre to the Home’ (FttH). FttH delivers ultra-fast internet, which is not possible with the existing copper network. VodafoneZiggo has a different strategy. The internet provider continues to use the last piece of TV cable and performs an upgrade on it according to the ‘gigabit without digging’ principle: it restricts itself to upgrades in local exchanges and overhead cables. These upgrades can achieve download speeds comparable to full fibre.2

Both strategies not only mean faster internet, but also improve energy consumption. For KPN, the gain lies in the fact that a full fibre network requires less network equipment. According to recent research by cable manufacturer Prysmian, this translates into energy consumption about 7.5 times lower than the old copper network.3 The fibre network also uses this energy more efficiently; where one kilowatt hour over a copper telephone line can carry a maximum of 4 terabytes of data, a Fibre to the Home network can transfer up to 56 terabytes with the same amount of energy.

The upgrade of the TV cable network by VodafoneZiggo also offers a sustainability benefit. However, this benefit seems to sit primarily in the equipment in people’s homes4: the modem. The new modem installed at customers with gigabit internet can automatically enter sleep mode when there is no internet traffic. In standby, the device uses less energy. Assuming eight hours of internet use per day — a figure that is probably higher now — researchers at the University of Melbourne estimate that using sleep mode can result in 40 per cent lower energy consumption.5

At present, such settings are not yet possible for FttH equipment in the home. That is a pity, because this is the most energy-hungry component in the entire chain. This applies to all types of networks, but in the case of Fibre to the Home the imbalance is striking: the router and ‘converter’ (the device that converts the optical signal from the fibre-optic cable into an electrical signal) are responsible for about 95 per cent of total energy consumption by FttH.6 This percentage is high in part because the fibre network itself is so efficient — a positive sign. At the same time, it means the sustainability journey is not over even with full fibre networks. We should therefore focus on developing energy-efficient routers and additional network equipment.

The specific strategies of both KPN and VodafoneZiggo seem to stem from the (im)possibilities of the existing network. Within those constraints, both parties have chosen the most logical route to gigabit internet, a route that also reduces CO2 emissions. Let us hope energy efficiency stays high on the agenda as these ultra-fast networks continue to develop.
