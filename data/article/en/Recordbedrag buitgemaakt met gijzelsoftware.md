# Record amount extorted with ransomware

[Report](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/rapporten/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

28/05/2024 • 3 minutes read

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

Where phishing and seemingly legitimate logins often mark the start of an attack, other tools come into play once the victim has been drawn in more deeply. Installing malware is a prominent strategy used by cybercriminals to achieve their ultimate goal.

This malicious software can be used to steal data, disrupt services, or spy. Nearly one quarter (23 per cent) of respondents faced this in 2023, according to a survey by ABN AMRO and research agency MWM2 among 895 organisations. Large companies were the most frequent victims at 32 per cent, followed by small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) and sole traders at 19 and 16 per cent respectively. The most common type of malware is ‘[ransomware](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/cybersecurity/wat-is-ransomware.html)’, which blocks systems and encrypts data until the affected party pays a ransom. It ranks in the top three most feared cyberattacks among both large companies and the sole-trader community. In SMEs, ransomware does not make the top three, but malware in a broader sense is cited.

## Record amount of ransom paid

This [ransomware](https://www.wired.com/story/ransomware-payments-2023-breaks-record/) is being deployed on a larger scale than ever; 4,399 ransomware attacks were recorded worldwide in 2023. Victims paid a record total of 1.1 billion dollars – almost double 2022, when this form of cybercrime briefly seemed to be in decline. The relatively modest 567 million dollars in 2022 now appears to have been an exception in an otherwise clear trend.

Although the rise in attacks and ransom collected is large, a smaller share of victims are actually paying. While between 70 and 80 per cent paid in 2019 and 2020, this fell to 29 per cent in the fourth quarter of 2023. The amount paid in 2023 can largely be explained by cybercriminals focusing on companies that cannot afford any disruption to their systems and that are also able to stump up the ransom. Three-quarters of the total amount came from payments of more than one million dollars. Arwi van der Sluijs, Managing Director of cybersecurity service provider NFIR: “Smaller companies are less willing to pay; they simply do not think it is worth it. They accept that this means losing certain data.”

At the end of last year, Tunstall Healthcare also fell victim to [ransomware](https://www.vpngids.nl/nieuws/tunstall-hackers-hadden-toegang-tot-data/). The company specialises in solutions that enable remote care and produces, among other things, wearable emergency buttons for older people. They use the system to get help quickly after, for example, a fall. But due to the hack, their alerts no longer reached the control room. With help from an external cybersecurity company, the system was restored within a few days. In the meantime, clients were advised to carry a mobile phone at all times.

## Measures to reduce risks

Entrepreneurs should protect themselves against cyberattacks. You can do this in three relatively simple steps. First, carry out a risk analysis to map out what is critical to keep the business running and maintain service quality. Second, take measures such as promoting safe behaviour among employees, defining who owns specific data, and discussing cyber risks with partners and suppliers. Finally, set up procedures to detect and handle cyber incidents.
