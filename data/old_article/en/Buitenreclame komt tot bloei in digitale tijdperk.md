Digital outdoor advertising is making a remarkable advance. New screens are appearing in shopping centres, bus shelters and railway stations. Analogue outdoor advertising also remains popular because of its mass reach. ABN AMRO expects outdoor advertising revenue to grow by 5 per cent in total in 2025 and 6 per cent in 2026. Up to 2030, outdoor advertising revenue is expected to grow by an average of 5 per cent a year and overtake radio as an advertising channel.

The Netherlands has 83,000 outdoor advertising assets, including bus shelters, motorway masts, transformer boxes, scaffold wraps, blank walls, billboards, information pillars and lampposts. Even buses, trams and ferries are used as advertising platforms. The number of assets is growing with the addition of new digital screens. At the same time, revenue per asset is rising through the digitalisation of existing assets.

The most common format is the abri format (1185 mm x 1750 mm, around 2m2), accounting for 65 per cent of all advertising displays and found in bus shelters and information pillars. The A0 format (841 mm x 1189 mm) accounts for 32 per cent and is found on triangular boards and electricity boxes. Larger assets are rare and vary in size; only 1,300 are larger than the abri format. These are located in high-reach locations, such as the LED facade of the Ziggodome in Amsterdam (23 metres high and 400 metres long) or the NEO screen at Hoog Catharijne in Utrecht (193 m2).

## Digital outdoor advertising is growing explosively and offers new opportunities

In three years, digital outdoor advertising revenue has more than doubled to EUR172 million in 2024, according to the Digital Ad Spend 2024 report by VIA/Deloitte. In 2025, revenue will grow by 4 per cent to around EUR179 million, ABN AMRO expects. That is 56 per cent of total outdoor advertising revenue in 2025, which is expected to reach EUR317 million. This is striking because only 17 per cent of all advertising assets are digital; the remaining 83 per cent are analogue. Digital screens generate more revenue than analogue ones because they are larger on average, located in the busiest places and attract more attention.

Digital screens attract more attention thanks to moving images and rotating adverts. They are also larger and positioned in busy places in major cities and along heavily travelled roads, where large numbers of passers-by create strong reach. Advertising assets in these locations were the first to be digitised in order to recoup the high investment costs. Analogue assets are digitised when their reach is large enough to justify the expense. The new BRO Next study is becoming the new standard for measuring reach.

Most of the new digital outdoor advertising screens are located indoors, in shop windows. As long as the screens are visible from the street or public space, they count towards outdoor advertising inventory. This phenomenon is part of the [rise of retail media](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/advertenties-lucratieve-bijverdienste-voor-winkelketens.html), in which supermarket chains and other retailers are turning to advertising as a lucrative side business. HEMA, for example, has nearly 330 digital screens running a range of campaigns that generate income in the evenings when the shops are closed. Global, a major outdoor advertising operator, handles ad sales for HEMA.

For advertisers, digital screens offer greater flexibility. Campaigns can be tailored to circumstances, such as adverts for ice cream on warm days. They also allow advertisers to reach specific target groups more effectively, such as young people seeing an advert for matcha tea at a railway station on their way to school. Around 30 per cent of digital advertising space is placed automatically using algorithms that take factors such as time, location and weather conditions into account. That percentage is increasing, pointing to a more efficient and more flexible use of digital advertising space.

## Digital advertising dominates the media landscape

Digital advertising, especially via web pages, social media, streaming services and the digital screens mentioned above, dominates the Dutch media landscape and accounted for EUR4.1 billion in 2024, nearly three quarters of all advertising spend. Large tech companies, with a market share of 80 per cent (EUR3.5 billion), dominate the digital advertising market. The media's digital transformation is not yet complete, and the share of digital advertising is therefore still rising.

While traditional mass media such as television, radio and newspapers are losing reach, outdoor advertising, also known as out of home, is becoming more important. Johan Smit, director of PMA, the trade association for media agencies, explains: "Out of home is effectively the only mass medium left and is therefore taking over the leading role from linear TV."

Digital outdoor advertising differs from other digital media, such as online adverts, because it is generally not experienced as intrusive. Online, ad blockers can be used and some people experience advertising fatigue. "Outdoor advertising is not intrusive on the one hand and impossible to miss on the other," says Sita Bakker, director of OUTREACH, the trade association for outdoor advertising operators. "It does not interrupt people during their activities, which contributes to a positive experience," Bakker adds.

## Analogue outdoor advertising also remains strong in the digital age

Analogue outdoor advertising remains relevant and is even growing, with revenue expected to rise by 6 per cent in 2025. That would bring analogue outdoor advertising revenue to around EUR138 million, according to ABN AMRO. Although digitalisation is slightly reducing the number of analogue assets, traditional formats such as paper posters and static billboards remain popular with advertisers because of their mass reach. New measurement methods from the BRO Next study are also playing a role in this revaluation, allowing advertisers to estimate their reach ever more accurately. As the "only mass medium left", outdoor advertising offers an alternative to advertising channels such as television, which are seen as more expensive.

Outdoor advertising remains untouched by the online competition affecting other media. When people are on the move, for instance travelling to school or work, they come into contact with outdoor advertising. Bus shelters, motorway masts, billboards and A0 frames reach 80 per cent of the Dutch population every week. By comparison, linear television reaches 80 per cent of the Dutch population in a month.

Around 5 per cent of all advertising spend goes to outdoor advertising. That share varies by advertiser: McDonald's allocates as much as 20 per cent of its budget to outdoor advertising, while WPP Media, the largest media agency, spends around 10 per cent on outdoor advertising on behalf of advertisers. These examples point to further growth potential for outdoor advertising, as experience in other countries also shows. In a positive scenario, where current trends continue strongly, outdoor advertising's share could rise to 7.5 per cent and revenue could reach EUR600 million by 2030. In the base scenario, however, outdoor advertising's share remains stable at around the current 5 per cent.

## Municipalities shape outdoor advertising policy

Outdoor advertising targets passers-by who are walking or cycling, driving or travelling by public transport. Municipalities apply strict rules on where advertising is allowed and how it may appear, meaning the permitted formats and, for example, the brightness of digital screens. Some municipalities levy a local advertising tax. Because municipalities are themselves responsible for the design of public space, the rules vary widely: the Netherlands has 342 municipalities, each with its own outdoor advertising policy. The municipality of The Hague, for instance, has banned "fossil advertising". Through a local by-law (APV), The Hague prohibits advertising for air travel, petrol cars and fossil energy contracts. In Haarlem, a tender is under way in which advertising for meat and fish is not allowed.

Through public tenders, municipalities award multi-year concessions for outdoor advertising. In return, they receive a financial payment, often combined with in-kind compensation such as street furniture or media space for their own campaigns and charitable causes. JCDecaux is one example of an operator that invests in bus shelters and maintains them in exchange for advertising rights. Notably, JCDecaux has more employees working in technical services than in advertising sales. A [PwC study](https://www.outsmart.org.uk/resources/ooh-supporting-uk-society) in the United Kingdom shows that 46 per cent of outdoor advertising revenue flows back to municipalities. Applied to the Dutch market, that amounts to around EUR146 million in 2025.

Outdoor advertising is heavily dependent on municipal policy and concessions, which vary widely across the Netherlands' 342 municipalities. While some municipalities impose specific restrictions, such as The Hague's ban on fossil advertising, there is no national guideline. This leads to variation in rules and uncertainty for operators. Central rules on tobacco, alcohol and gambling advertising show that a uniform approach can bring advantages. Without such guidelines, individual municipalities can impose stricter restrictions, creating a risk for the outdoor advertising sector.

The Dutch outdoor advertising market, with 52 operators, is relatively fragmented compared with neighbouring countries such as Belgium and Germany, where only two large players are active alongside a few specialists in their own niche. Bauer Media Group's acquisition of the Dutch arm of Clear Channel in April 2025 is an example of the consolidation that is expected to continue. Internationally, the Netherlands still trails countries such as the United States and China, especially in digital outdoor advertising and retail media. Based on international trends and the current market structure, ABN AMRO expects further consolidation and digital growth.

## The rise of the abri

In 1964, Jean Claude Decaux introduced his first bus shelters in Lyon. In exchange for financing, placing and maintaining them, his company, JCDecaux, received the advertising rights for these "abribus" shelters (French for bus shelter). This public-private partnership model spread worldwide, and the term "abri" became synonymous with a widely used advertising format: 1185 mm x 1750 mm, around 2m2. Advertising in the abri format represents 65 per cent of all outdoor advertising in the Netherlands and can be found in bus shelters across the country, stations, shopping centres and along busy streets.
