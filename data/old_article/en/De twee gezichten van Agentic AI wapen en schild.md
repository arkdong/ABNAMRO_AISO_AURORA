Criminals are increasingly using artificial intelligence to launch lightning-fast attacks. At the same time, companies are deploying the same technology to defend themselves. The urgency to act is growing, but in this complex arms race many organisations do not know where to begin.

The world of cybersecurity is at a turning point. Agentic AI, artificial intelligence in which systems operate autonomously and make decisions without human intervention, is fundamentally changing how both defenders and attackers work. Unlike generative AI, which depends on predefined instructions, these "agents" can independently initiate, carry out and improve actions based on what they learn.

Recent research by Palo Alto Networks, an American cybersecurity company, shows just how profound this shift is. Researchers succeeded in simulating a full ransomware attack using Agentic AI in just 25 minutes, a hundred times faster than traditional methods. "The scenario they describe is disturbingly realistic," says Matthijs Mons, cybersecurity specialist at ABN AMRO. "Criminals will develop agents specifically tailored to targeted attacks."

## From science fiction to reality

The process described by Palo Alto Networks illustrates the power of Agentic AI. Agents begin by collecting public information about their targets via social media and company databases. Based on current data, they continuously adapt their strategy and attempt to gain access to networks through personalised phishing attacks, LinkedIn messages or fake invitations to video conferences. Once inside, they analyse patterns to identify the most valuable systems and choose attack techniques that match their target's vulnerabilities perfectly. "To many people, this sounds fictional, as if everything happens by itself," says Mons. "But with traditional AI there still had to be at least an initial action by a human. With Agentic AI, things now happen fully autonomously, without anyone being involved. And that applies to both sides."

The other side is the protective measures that organisations are implementing. Large financial institutions employ hundreds of cybersecurity specialists to protect vital infrastructure. Staff shortages in this sector are severe, and Agentic AI offers solutions here too. "When an incident happens, a security expert often has to pull information from several different systems," Mons explains. "That can take hours. An agent for security tasks can independently investigate data from system reports on the basis of a single instruction and build a complete picture, whereas an analyst has to work through an entire step-by-step process."

## Simulating an attack

The most promising applications can be found in simulating attacks on an organisation's own environment, so-called red-team actions. Traditionally, security teams spend days or even weeks on this: mapping systems, finding access routes and testing different paths.

By learning from earlier simulated attacks, Agentic AI can infiltrate far more quickly and determine which attack sequence is most effective, exactly what criminals will do as well. There are already companies offering this as a service. They run simulations for their clients and learn from both attacking red teams and defending blue teams. This combined approach, also called purple AI, allows organisations to learn from both perspectives and continuously improve their security. It is important to note that Agentic AI builds on existing methods. It carries out attacks faster than ever, but it does not invent entirely new ones. So far, Agentic AI is above all a leap in efficiency. "The big cybersecurity companies are investing heavily in AI," says Mons. "But criminals are doing the same. The romantic image of hackers as lonely boys in basements is completely outdated. Cyberattacks are carried out by professional organisations with specialised roles focused on gaining access, building malware, installing malware, stealing data, negotiating and so on. They even have HR departments and call centres, supported by a solid business case built on the proceeds they generate from stolen data."

## The paradox of adoption

The use of Agentic AI inside organisations, both for security and for ordinary business processes, also creates new vulnerabilities. Hackers can, for example, try to manipulate AI agents that already have access to systems.

This leads to a striking paradox, revealed by a survey from SailPoint, an American technology company focused on identity and access management, among 353 cybersecurity professionals worldwide. Although 96 per cent of respondents regard agents as a growing security threat, no fewer than 98 per cent of organisations plan to expand their use of Agentic AI in the coming year. The survey shows that 82 per cent of companies are already using agents, with more than half reporting that these agents have daily access to sensitive data. Even so, only 44 per cent have introduced policies to protect these agents from external threats. Perhaps most alarming of all, nearly one in four companies reported that agents can be manipulated into disclosing access credentials.

"Using this technology is already a risk in itself," warn Hans van Haarlem and Steven Ottenhoff of MMOX, a Dutch cybersecurity consultancy. They point to a crucial issue that many entrepreneurs overlook. "In the past, attacking large companies took enormous amounts of time and resources. That threshold has now dropped dramatically. Criminals can work far more efficiently, which also makes medium-sized and smaller organisations attractive targets."

## Volume and quality are increasing

The lower barrier to attack is already visible in practice, as attackers widen their field of operations. "We receive piles of phishing emails to analyse," Van Haarlem says. "What stands out is that both the volume and the quality are increasing. Spearphishing, targeted attacks on specific individuals, is being carried out much better. We regularly see new employees receiving text messages almost immediately from 'our' CEO asking them to transfer money or get in touch. On their very first day at work. It is hard to imagine that this is not being automated in the background."

The number of cyberattacks has not yet risen explosively, and there is a reason for that. "Agentic AI systems are still incredibly expensive," explains ABN AMRO's cybersecurity specialist. "They require a lot of computing power and training models costs money. Criminals therefore continue to focus on relatively easy targets. Hacking a large bank can take years, but a small accountancy firm can be cracked within a week with a substantial payoff. Generative AI has caused the number of phishing emails to rise sharply. That is cheap and easy. Full Agentic AI attacks, where systems independently scan social media and conduct spearphishing, are certainly coming, but they are not yet widespread."

## Internal security risks

Besides external threats, agents used for security and for other business processes also bring specific internal security risks into organisations. "If such an agent is configured badly or not monitored properly, situations can arise that you really do not want," warns Mons. "Imagine an agent detects that you have clicked on a phishing email. The normal procedure is to isolate that computer from the network so it cannot cause damage. But if that agent accidentally isolates every computer in your network instead of just one, nobody can work any more."

There is an even more fundamental problem. Agentic AI can act on the basis of data and past experience without human control, but it can also hallucinate: draw incorrect conclusions or perform unexpected actions. "You do not then know exactly what it has done," Mons explains. "It learns by itself and acts by itself. If things go wrong, you have to work out what happened and what needs to be repaired. That is why it is crucial to build in strong control mechanisms and keep options open that allow people to intervene themselves."

The experts at MMOX add nuance to this picture. "Agentic AI does not suffer from stress or workload that makes someone careless. It can do the wrong thing with good intentions because the underlying model is inadequate, but the margin of error is often smaller than with people."

## What can companies do?

The practical course of action for organisations is clear, but wide-ranging. "Make sure you are better secured than your neighbour," says Mons. "On a street, the house with the weakest security gets burgled. The same basic principle applies in the digital world. It is not about absolute security, but about relative resilience." Concrete measures start with the basics: properly securing devices and using two-factor authentication. But even that is not watertight. "There are scenarios in which that second layer of control can still be bypassed. It is a constant cat-and-mouse game between security makers and malicious actors."

Van Haarlem therefore argues for a strategic approach that goes beyond technology alone. "AI changes nothing about the methodology of cybercriminals; it only changes the efficiency and therefore the scope," he says. "We are advocates of the NIST framework, a widely recognised set of guidelines that helps organisations manage and reduce cybersecurity risk. Invest in people as well as technology, in network segmentation as well as strong data analysis." Measures that mitigate impact are a crucial but underestimated element. "Instead of focusing only on keeping hackers out, you should ensure they cannot do damage once they are inside," Van Haarlem and Ottenhoff emphasise. "Segment your network so an attacker cannot move freely through all your systems. Try to build a high wall, but realise that if someone climbs over it, there still need to be more barriers on the inside."

## The human factor

The role of employees remains complex. While some see people as the weakest link, Mons at ABN AMRO takes a different view. "We see people as the last resort, the strongest link. Technology may have failed, but an alert employee can still stop an attack. That is why we promote safe behaviour across the entire organisation." Van Haarlem and Ottenhoff go deeper into this point. "Changing human behaviour is extremely difficult, especially when the digital skills of the current population lag behind and employees are not intrinsically motivated to work safely."

The experts at MMOX distinguish three essential factors. First, organisations need to establish clear guidelines for safe behaviour and provide user-friendly security tools. They warn: "Many tools die a beautiful death because adoption falls behind." Second, the urgency of cyber risk must be reinforced continuously. "This is not something for a single training session; it has to be institutionalised. Discuss it monthly or quarterly in meetings, repetition is essential." Third, organisations need to understand the working environment. "Map the systems you have. Listen carefully to your employees. If they download software or extensions themselves because something is missing from the official systems, you need to take that seriously. By understanding their needs and meeting them, you prevent them from seeking unsafe solutions. Make the topic discussable."

## Resilience instead of perfection

The central message from all experts is clear: perfect security does not exist. "The question is not if you will be hacked, but when," say the experts at MMOX. "The cost of attacks has become so low that every company is a potential target. Our advice to clients is therefore: be prepared. Build resilience." In concrete terms, that means making sure you have backups that actually work, developing an incident-response system, a structured step-by-step plan for responding quickly and effectively to a cyber incident, and practising scenarios regularly. "Simulate that you have been hacked," they advise. "What are you going to do? How will you communicate? What if your data ends up on the street? Run regular digital fire drills so everyone is prepared and knows their role in a crisis."

For small and medium-sized enterprises, the challenges are different from those faced by large organisations. "The blue-team agents and advanced systems available today require so much investment that they are not relevant for most entrepreneurs." Entrepreneurs often do not know where to begin. "That is why we focus on helping to lay a solid foundation: getting the basic measures right so you are better secured than your neighbour."

## Opportunities and threats

Despite all the risks, Agentic AI also offers substantial opportunities. ABN AMRO cybersecurity specialist Mons underlines this point. "Red-team actions that used to take weeks can now be done in days. Analyses that took hours now take minutes. With Agentic AI, we can test ourselves better, find vulnerabilities faster and respond more effectively to incidents," he says. But both parties also stress the need for caution. Ottenhoff adds: "Agentic AI is not necessarily transparent. Agentic systems can do the wrong thing with good intentions. Sometimes a simple, predictable system is a better choice than complex AI."

## The urgency is growing

Cyberattacks will become faster, larger in scale and more invisible in the future. That requires security systems that adapt continuously. Old systems that rely on fixed checks and slow updates are no longer sufficient. A new approach is needed, one that continuously monitors for vulnerabilities in the cloud and other digital systems, and where simulating realistic threats helps identify and remedy weak spots. Within organisations, all Agentic AI systems, whether used for security or for other business processes, should not be treated as ordinary system components but as separate entities with their own security needs. This requires a strategy that identifies, monitors and controls all agents. In this way, companies can not only comply with regulation but also strengthen their overall security position.

At the same time, agents deployed by malicious actors represent a growing external threat. The rise of Agentic AI does not introduce completely new attack methods, but it does make existing techniques faster and more effective. The speed and adaptability of these systems pose a major challenge. Companies therefore need to innovate proactively and anticipate agent-driven attacks.

"It is very difficult to move cybersecurity from 'important but not urgent' to 'urgent'," Ottenhoff observes. "But with the rise of Agentic AI, that urgency is now real. The industry behind cybercrime has matured, with solid business cases. The barrier to attack is lower than ever. Companies that do not act now face major risks."

The two faces of Agentic AI, as both weapon and shield, force organisations to rethink their approach. The technology offers unprecedented opportunities for efficient security and for scaling cybersecurity teams. But that same power is also in the hands of criminals who are operating ever more professionally. The challenge is clear: companies must invest now in foundational security, involve and prepare their employees, and recognise that absolute security does not exist. Resilience and preparedness are the new watchwords in an era in which the question is not if, but when, you will be attacked.
