The TMT sector is growing strongly once again. Growth weakened in 2023 and 2024 because of rising interest rates, and employment in the sector even fell. In 2025, growth recovered to 4.5 per cent and ABN AMRO expects growth rates of 5 per cent and 4 per cent in 2026 and 2027 respectively. Digitalisation, cloud migrations and investment in artificial intelligence (AI) are key drivers of this growth.

The TMT sector consists of IT companies, media companies and telecom companies. The sector has a long history of growth, particularly because of the strong use of information technology (IT). IT companies therefore make an important contribution to productivity growth in the Dutch economy. The digital transformation is clearly visible in the TMT sector's growth figures: an average of 6 per cent a year over the past thirty years, while annual growth in gross domestic product (GDP) remained stuck at 2 per cent. In 2025, 400,000 people work in the TMT sector, which accounts for around 7 per cent of total Dutch output.

## Entrepreneurs are optimistic

Entrepreneurs in the TMT sector are optimistic, according to business confidence data from the Netherlands Business Survey (COEN) by Statistics Netherlands (CBS). For the "information and communication" sector, the business sentiment indicator is positive in the fourth quarter of 2025, while business confidence is negative in all other sectors. Optimism is also visible in the mergers and acquisitions market, which is picking up again after two years of decline. The international trade war is focused mainly on goods. Trade restrictions therefore have only limited and indirect effects on the TMT sector, which primarily exports services.

## Sharp rise in AI investment

The United States is experiencing an unprecedented investment wave, with roughly USD400 billion in AI investment in 2025. Tech companies have all announced record levels of investment, including standout examples such as Amazon (USD100 billion), Microsoft (USD80 billion) and Alphabet (USD75 billion). These investments are focused mainly on chips, data centres and power supply. The breakneck pace of AI development stands in sharp contrast to the rest of the US economy, which is moving at [two speeds](https://www.abnamro.com/research/nl/onze-research/visie-op-de-vs-2026-de-ai-haves-en-have-nots): while AI and related sectors are flourishing, most other sectors are stagnating or shrinking. AI investment is now responsible for half of GDP growth.

Investment in artificial intelligence is also growing strongly in the Netherlands. According to data from Dealroom, AI investment has increased fivefold in five years. The sector is home to many innovative and fast-growing companies, such as DataSnipper, an Amsterdam software company and unicorn that uses AI to support accountants in preparing annual accounts and auditors in performing audits. Even so, AI investment in the Netherlands still trails countries such as Sweden and Switzerland.

The promise of AI is immense. A large share of knowledge work may be transformed or even replaced, as is already visible in fields such as marketing, communications and translation. According to the World Economic Forum's Future of Jobs Report 2025, by 2030 only one third of all work will still be carried out mainly by people, a sharp fall from the current 47 per cent. Even so, there is uncertainty about the returns on these vast AI investments, and high valuations on stock markets are prompting questions about a possible "bubble".

## Business succession in the TMT sector

A survey of 519 entrepreneurs, carried out by Ipsos I&O on behalf of ABN AMRO, shows that entrepreneurs who want to step back often still have no succession plan in place. Entrepreneurs in the TMT sector are no exception. Our sample for "information and communication" contains only 32 entrepreneurs, which is too small to be representative or to support detailed conclusions.

The survey clearly shows that younger entrepreneurs are more likely to look to an external party as their intended successor. A takeover by an external party is often seen as an opportunity to use the released capital to start a new company. This so-called serial entrepreneurship is common in sectors with a high share of young entrepreneurs, such as e-commerce, software development and IT services.

CBS data shows that the share of entrepreneurs under the age of thirty rose from 9 per cent in 2010 to 16 per cent in 2024. That may be linked to the rise of IT services and software development, as entrepreneurs in these emerging fields are younger than average.
