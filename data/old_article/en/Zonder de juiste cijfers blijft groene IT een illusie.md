Companies that use computing power and data storage in the cloud and want to make their IT more sustainable need to know where gains can be achieved. For the time being, however, cloud providers do not share all the figures on energy use and CO2 emissions, and they each do so in a different way.

As a result, IT teams do not know clearly where the greatest sustainability gains can be made, nor can they measure the impact of optimisations. For now, there is also a lack of legal obligations and binding standards determining what information cloud companies must make available to their own clients.

## Limiting negative environmental effects

Across the world, companies are moving their IT activities to the cloud, and both the development and use of artificial intelligence (AI) are driving demand for data-centre capacity. According to an [estimate](https://www.iea.org/reports/energy-and-ai/energy-supply-for-ai) by the International Energy Agency, global electricity consumption by data centres in 2024 amounted to around 460 terawatt hours (TWh), equivalent to the annual consumption of countries such as Sweden or Argentina. By 2030, the agency expects that to more than double to 1,000 TWh. Around 40 per cent of that power will then come from renewable sources, but coal- and gas-fired power stations will still be needed for nearly half of it. The CO <sub>2</sub> footprint of digitalisation therefore remains substantial for the time being.

Data centres have become increasingly efficient in recent years. That has much to do with economies of scale; a large data centre can spread the energy used to cool servers, a relatively large share of power consumption, across a large number of customers. Progress has also been made with new, more energy-efficient cooling techniques and software for managing the internal climate. In practice, however, users of AI and cloud services, who determine demand for data-centre capacity, do not have sufficient tools to reduce the harmful environmental effects of their IT efforts.

## Measuring is knowing

"Greening IT always starts with the right information," says Wiebren van der Zee, specialist in green IT at ABN AMRO. "Think of data on energy and water use, emissions and the use of raw materials. In our IT organisation, we collect these data for our employees' workplaces, so laptops and screens, but also for our own data centres and the cloud services we purchase."

## Guidance for sustainable digitalisation

Together with various organisations from the National Coalition for Sustainable Digitalisation (NCDD), ABN AMRO developed the [Sustainable IT Impact Assessment](https://eur03.safelinks.protection.outlook.com/?url=https%3A%2F%2Fcoalitieduurzamedigitalisering.nl%2Fsustainable-it-impact-assessment-siia%2F&data=05%7C02%7Cjulia.krauwer%40nl.abnamro.com%7C9948116aa7d649b3c17508de1082ebd8%7C3a15904d3fd94256a753beb05cdf0c6d%7C0%7C0%7C638966351662129336%7CUnknown%7CTWFpbGZsb3d8eyJFbXB0eU1hcGkiOnRydWUsIlYiOiIwLjAuMDAwMCIsIlAiOiJXaW4zMiIsIkFOIjoiTWFpbCIsIldUIjoyfQ%3D%3D%7C0%7C%7C%7C&sdata=Yz6QXd%2FJKDj3vTjpdic9%2FGPln8C8x2QH6hBRajvDbgc%3D&reserved=0) (SIIA). This tool offers practical guidance and extensive background information for getting started with greener IT. The advice is based in part on the concrete experiences of companies and public authorities.

For several years now, the major cloud providers have been trying to meet this need for information through CO2 dashboards that allow their customers to monitor the emissions from their digital activities. Willem Jan Groenewoud, CEO of software company Ab Ovo, welcomes this wave: "It is good that they are providing more transparency than a few years ago. Measuring is knowing." The company is strongly committed to making the software it develops for its clients' mission-critical processes more sustainable. "We assume a scenario in which energy may end up being rationed, so we design software that uses as little energy as possible."

The cloud world is far more transparent than it was a few years ago, but there is still a great deal to criticise in the information being offered. Not all the necessary data are provided, and providers differ greatly in their measurement methods and the level of detail they supply.

## A paper reality

The issue starts with the way cloud companies report their own organisation-wide emissions. According to an analysis by British newspaper The Guardian, the own data centres of the biggest tech companies emitted almost [eight times](https://www.theguardian.com/technology/2024/sep/15/data-center-gas-emissions-tech) more than was reported in total. That analysis covered data from 2020 through to 2022.

The difference can be explained by the measurement method. It is often market based. That means providers calculate their CO2 emissions on the basis of purchased green-energy certificates or long-term purchase contracts for energy from, for example, a wind farm. Such constructions, however, say nothing about the actual electricity mix at the location of the data centre. "It is a paper reality," says Van der Zee of ABN AMRO. "Green energy is administratively dragged all around the world."

This means a cloud provider can buy guarantees of origin for a hydropower station in Norway and allocate them on paper to a server park in Ireland, even though that park partly runs on electricity from coal-fired power stations. On paper, the footprint is then zero, but in reality CO2 emissions are definitely being produced through the use of fossil energy.

## Average power mix

Under the European sustainability law CSRD, the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive in full, market-based reporting alone is no longer enough. On the one hand, organisations must be encouraged to purchase green energy and it is right that they provide insight into these efforts; on the other hand, the market-based measurement method paints too rosy a picture of the actual emissions from electricity generation. That is why the CSRD also requires companies to report on a location-based basis. Here, the average power mix in the region where the company or data centre buys its electricity is the starting point.

Both methods also appear in the emissions dashboards of cloud companies. The location-based figures, however, are not presented equally prominently in every case. At Amazon, the market-based emissions data are put front and centre, and users have to click through to gain insight into the location-based figures. Microsoft does not show location-based emissions in its dashboard at all.

## Location-based reporting

Location-based emissions are calculated by multiplying electricity consumption in kilowatt hours (kWh) by the local emissions factor. This shows how much CO <sub>2</sub> equivalent is released on average when one kWh of electricity is produced. In regions with wind turbines or hydropower stations, the emissions factor is lower than in regions where electricity is generated mainly in coal- or gas-fired power stations. The location-based measurement method is an average and does not require insight into fluctuations in the energy mix per day or per hour.

## Suppliers' willingness

In the world of emissions dashboards, there are no binding standards, and in practice buyers of cloud services depend on the willingness of their cloud providers to open up information. As a result, the level of detail varies greatly. Most provide monthly figures, but the speed of updates differs. Some providers do this constantly, while others lag months behind. There is also variation in granularity; sometimes emissions are shown only per organisation or region, while in other cases they can also be monitored down to the level of individual services or projects.

Not all dashboards include scope 3 emissions in their calculations either. While scopes 1 and 2 here relate to the use of fossil energy and fossil-generated electricity to keep the data centres running, scope 3 concerns the more indirect emissions. These are caused, for example, by the production and disposal of IT equipment, and the construction of data centres and offices. Incidentally, the cloud providers that do report at scope 3 level do not do so consistently either; where one limits itself to hardware production, another, for example, also includes running the company canteen.

Boavizta, an initiative of dozens of European IT companies and consultancy organisations, has developed a uniform way of calculating the climate impact of digital infrastructure. At present, each cloud provider calculates in its own way, creating an incomplete picture and making figures hard to compare across suppliers. Boavizta, for example, makes data available on the environmental effects of producing and transporting servers, which companies can use for a more thorough calculation of the ecological footprint of their IT activities. Resilio also provides such data, combined with advice, but as a commercial service.

## Insight for development teams

The cloud dashboards are primarily aimed at insight and reporting. A positive point is that some providers also give concrete advice on how companies can reduce their emissions. Microsoft and Google, for example, suggest scaling back unused capacity.

Even so, it is difficult for organisations with a rich IT landscape to gain insight into emissions at application level. Software usually consists of several components spread across projects, regions or services. As a result, the direct effects of changes made by development teams, lighter code or a more efficient architecture, can easily remain out of sight. "That is also why we have developed our own AI-driven tooling for this," says Groenewoud of software company Ab Ovo.

## Using less energy

According to Max Schulze, founder of the Sustainable Digital Infrastructure Alliance (SDIA), focusing on emissions is actually not enough. "We should be optimising for lower energy use, for fewer data centres. We live on a continent with limited space and resources," he says.

The other experts also recognise that green IT starts with insight into energy use. "The emissions of a data centre are not a pure measurement instrument," says Groenewoud of Ab Ovo. "At the functional level, we first map the electricity used in kilowatt hours. Only then do we look at the location of the data centre and the energy mix at that place."

None of the cloud providers, however, gives insight into the number of kWh their customers consume. Schulze of the SDIA finds that illogical, because you should always start with the efficiency of your product. "If you buy a car, you want to know how many kilometres you can drive on a litre of fuel to get a picture of the vehicle's efficiency. Only then do you translate that into the number of grams of CO <sub>2</sub> equivalent per kilometre."

## Alternative solutions

Various parties are stepping into the gap created by cloud providers' limited information provision. One example is Cloud Carbon Footprint (CCF), an open-source tool that reads actual cloud-service usage and converts it into both kilowatt hours (kWh) and CO <sub>2</sub> emissions. These data are also made available by the hour, allowing IT teams, for example, to see when applications are used the most and which systems continue running unnecessarily at night.

Although it can be a good starting point for organisations seeking additional insight, open-source services such as CCF do not come with any guarantee that they will be updated in time and regularly. Commercial parties do provide that guarantee, and several paid services have therefore been launched in recent years, including Greenpixie.

## Data from the source

All services, from the cloud providers' own dashboards to externally developed solutions, are based on assumptions about the energy use of the servers and the digital services running on them. That is why project NADIKI, an initiative of SDIA together with the University of Stuttgart and data-centre operator Kolo, goes all the way back to the source: the data centre. It collects live measurement data via sensors and links it to the current CO <sub>2</sub> intensity of the power grid. "We have sensors everywhere, from the power supply of the full data centre down to rack and server level," Schulze explains. This information is used to collect energy-use and emissions data for specific workloads.

Schulze says: "This is the first time that energy and emissions are being measured end to end." The project is still in the pilot phase, but it already shows that improvement is possible compared with current methodologies.

## Keep communicating

When the emissions dashboards were launched for the first time, they were welcomed as a sympathetic and much-needed feature. Some of the cloud providers have major ambitions when it comes to transparency and see it as a source of differentiation. Across the board, however, the pace varies greatly.

According to Van der Zee of ABN AMRO, organisations need to make their wishes known to their IT suppliers. "They have to keep communicating about the kind of information they need." As long as information provision is not optimal, the gap is being filled by open-source tools and the services of specialised software companies.

Standards do exist, such as those of Boavizta, and the Greenhouse Gas Protocol also has IT-specific guidelines. Real obligations, however, do not exist, and that unnecessarily creates challenges for organisations wanting to green their IT. Regulation can offer relief and ensure that all players report in the same way.
