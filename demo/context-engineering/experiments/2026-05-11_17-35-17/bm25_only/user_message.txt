## **When do we use capital letters? ABN AMRO’s standards are listed below:**

- **Proper nouns:** when proper nouns are capitalised in English, only significant words 

   - in the heading are capitalised (not short words such as ‘of’ and ‘the’). Examples are the University of Amsterdam, the Secretary of State. 

- 

   - **Department names:** always capitalise, for example Group Compliance, Group Sustainable Development, Press Office. However, when referring to the business area or market segment, no capitals are needed, as in: ‘Group Sustainable Development concentrates on **sustainable development** .’ 

- 

   - **Government and politics:** organisations, ministries, departments, treaties, acts, etc., generally take capital letters when their full name is used. For example: Ministry of Finance, Health and Safety at Work Act. Do not capitalise descriptions: the French **parliament** , the American , etc. The full names of political **finance department** 

   - parties take capitals: Labour Party, Republican Party. Their members are also capitalised: Liberal Democrats, Republicans, etc.

- **Months and days of the week:** these always begin with a capital letter in English, as in: Sunday 10 November 2014. 

- **People:** no general rules apply to ranks and titles, but as a rule of thumb, capitalise when written with a name. Examples: President Obama, King Charles, the Duke of Edinburgh. 

   - **Abbreviations:** when shortened from more than one word, abbreviations are usually capitalised (PDF, AI, NGO). Note that lower case is used for measures (kg, km, mph). Acronyms are capitalised, for example UNESCO. Also see chapter 4, ‘Abbreviations’.

Risk perception is strongest among the largest companies, according to a survey by ABN AMRO and research agency MWM2 among 895 organisations. ### Privacy protection puts online advertising at a turning point  (2022-12-15, sector: Rapport)
Professionals working in the online advertising chain are generally positive about this practice, according to a survey by ABN AMRO in collaboration with PanelWizard. Of the 544 respondents, two thirds (67 per cent) are employed by an advertiser; the rest work at a publisher (19 per cent) or agency (13 per cent). More than half of advertisers and a large majority of agencies indicate that personalised ads are an effective way to reach their target audience. Two thirds of publishers believe it is an effective way to monetise their ad space. Dependence on personalised ads appears substantial on both sides; about one third of advertisers (31 per cent) and publishers (34 per cent), and even forty per cent of agencies say they are dependent on this form of targeting. “Personalisation is important,” says Rogier Hosman, Chief Marketing Officer at digital agency Youwe. The company helps businesses develop and implement a digital strategy, including in online marketing. “Companies have a whole range of segments and customer types, all with different needs. So advertisers need to serve the right story to the right person.” According to the marketing boss, personalisation is also in the interest of the person who sees the ad. “As a consumer, it is pleasant to receive a relevant message and not have to wade through all kinds of nonsensical information.”
Personalisation under fire. However, the current online advertising market focused on personalisation is changing for several reasons in favour of a – possibly – more privacy-friendly model.
