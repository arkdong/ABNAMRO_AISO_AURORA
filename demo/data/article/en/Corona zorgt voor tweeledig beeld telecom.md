# Covid creates a mixed picture for telecoms

[State of Sectors](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/stand-van-sectoren/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecoms](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

15/01/2021 • 2 minutes read

Ingrid Laane

Kamalika Patra

The telecoms sector is affected both positively and negatively by the coronavirus crisis. Mass homeworking is driving higher demand for corporate network services and customers use their phones more. Offsetting this is a sharp drop in roaming income due to less travel, and revenue from the business market is falling. In this TMT sector update, ABN AMRO highlights these and other industry developments.

## Revenue trends vary

Although growth in the IT industry was significantly lower in 2020 than in previous years, partly due to a drop in demand in consulting, the coronavirus crisis will deliver structural benefits to the industry in the long term. People shop and work more online and are expected to keep doing so after the pandemic. More online traffic gives the IT industry more work.

Publishers, advertising agencies and broadcasters saw a strong recovery in the third quarter as media spending picked up. In the first two quarters of 2020 these industries still suffered steep contraction.

For printers, the ongoing shift to digital has led to a structural fall in demand for print for many years. The coronavirus crisis came on top of that last year. Some recovery has begun, but it remains modest due to continuing restrictions on organising events and trade fairs, two major buyers of print.

## IT labour market is tight

The mismatch in the TMT sector between labour demand and supply fell only slightly due to the coronavirus crisis, as shown by ABN AMRO’s labour market indicator. At the end of 2019, 16.2 per cent of vacancies in the TMT sector were unfillable, while by the end of 2020 this had declined to 14.1 per cent.

Trends in the labour market differ widely across the various industries. In the IT industry, the share of unfillable vacancies has fallen slightly, but remains very high at 28 per cent for IT developers and 30 per cent for IT programmers. This confirms the long-standing picture of structural tightness in this industry. In marketing and communications, by contrast, the share of unfillable vacancies is low, so there is currently no mismatch between demand and supply.
