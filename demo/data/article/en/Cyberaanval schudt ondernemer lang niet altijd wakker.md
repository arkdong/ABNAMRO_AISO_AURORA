# A cyberattack does not always wake an entrepreneur up

[Report](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/rapporten/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

23/04/2024 • 4 minutes read

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

Last year, almost three-quarters of Dutch companies faced a cyberattack. Although the adverse effects of cyberattacks often make the news, many entrepreneurs only acknowledge the risks after they have suffered damage. The damage often extends beyond the company that was hacked. An overly optimistic risk assessment can put the entire chain at risk.

With new European legislation in the making, entrepreneurs must quickly get things in order together with their customers and suppliers.

Among large companies, with annual turnover of at least 25 million, as many as 86 per cent of businesses were attacked. In small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs), 71 per cent faced an attack last year; among sole traders this was 55 per cent. Unfortunately, these last two groups tend to underestimate the risks of cybercrime.

Entrepreneurs mainly recognise the risks only when an attack has caused damage. This can mean financial costs to repair systems, lost revenue due to business downtime, or reputational damage caused by leaked customer data. Research by insurer [Hiscox](https://www.hiscoxgroup.com/sites/group/files/documents/2023-10/Hiscox-Cyber-Readiness-Report-2023.pdf) shows that the Netherlands ranks second worldwide for the highest financial damage from cybercrime.

## Cybercriminals’ attack methods are changing

Meanwhile, cybercriminals’ attack methods are changing rapidly. Phishing is still a popular – and much-feared – way to gain entry to companies. But criminals and, for example, hostile governments also make massive use of ready-made login credentials to gain unnoticed access to company systems. Once inside, this can lead to the installation of malicious software. Of all companies, 37 per cent have seen their systems infected with malware; at 19 per cent this happened (among other times) last year. Some 26 per cent have experienced ransomware, of which 19 percentage points last year. Data breaches are also a real risk. More than a quarter of companies have already lost confidential data; at 15 per cent this happened in the past twelve months.

More than half of companies see advances in artificial intelligence (AI) as a threat to their organisation’s cyber security. Last year this was less than a quarter. The risk perception is strongest at the largest companies, possibly fuelled by spectacular recent media reports. For example, a finance employee at a multinational in Hong Kong transferred more than [25 million dollars](https://edition.cnn.com/2024/02/04/asia/deepfake-cfo-scam-hong-kong-intl-hnk/index.html) to fraudsters after joining a video meeting with a deepfake version of the CFO and other colleagues. At the Dutch online bank Bunq, a similar attempt was made using an AI clone of CEO Ali Niknam.

## New NIS2 directive

To improve cyber resilience in Europe, a new directive will take effect in October 2024: NIS2, the successor to the earlier Network and Information Systems Directive (NIS). It compels organisations in seventeen sectors to critically examine, among other things, the cyber resilience of their suppliers and customers. Although the Netherlands is delayed in implementing the directive, domestic companies are already being confronted with critical questions from German and Belgian customers where national laws are being shaped on schedule.

Almost three-quarters (72 per cent) of large-company respondents already question their own customers, suppliers and partners “very regularly” or “regularly” about cyber security; 66 per cent say they also receive such questions from their chain partners. Among SME respondents these percentages are lower, at 52 and 37 per cent respectively. The extent to which concrete agreements are made with chain partners is also much higher at large companies than at SMEs.

Maarten Roerink, CEO of cybersecurity service provider MMOX, recognises the importance of an approach that goes far beyond the company walls. “When our customers get hacked, it very often happens via-via. Sometimes they are the target themselves; sometimes vulnerabilities in their networks are used to penetrate the networks and data of partners. The age-old question ‘what is there to get from my company?’ is no longer relevant.”
