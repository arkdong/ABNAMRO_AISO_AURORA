# Cyber measures increasingly focused on people

[Report](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/rapporten/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

13/06/2024 • 3 minutes read

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

Of the set of measures companies can take to increase their cyber resilience, measures aimed at human vulnerabilities are getting more attention. In addition, entrepreneurs at large companies in particular are inclined to allocate more budget for cybersecurity, mainly driven by the increased threat.

A survey by ABN AMRO and research firm MWM2 among 895 organisations shows that most respondents are taking measures against cybercrime. Among self-employed people without employees, however, this share is much lower (82 per cent) than among respondents from small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) and large companies (95 and 97 per cent respectively). Preventive measures addressing technological vulnerabilities are, in line with results from previous years, the most popular across all company sizes. Examples include installing virus scanners, encrypting data and applying firewalls.

## Keeping a finger on the pulse remains necessary

Even when such measures are in place, keeping a finger on the pulse remains necessary. Hackers managed to break in almost simultaneously at 22 Danish energy companies via a critical vulnerability in Zyxel firewalls, devices that are intended to keep malicious traffic out of the corporate network. If the intruders had not been discovered earlier, they could have gained control of part of Denmark’s vital infrastructure through this [co-ordinated cyberattack](https://www.dutchitchannel.nl/news/419675/data-buitgemaakt-bij-cyberaanval-op-britse-watermaatschappij).

Although it was possible to intervene relatively early in the attack process, several energy companies had clearly dropped the ball in their security. According to a report by the Danish cybersecurity centre for vital infrastructure, several companies had failed to install security updates for the firewalls. For example, they wrongly assumed their IT supplier would install the updates, or had deferred updates because the supplier would charge installation costs. Other companies simply did not know they had the Zyxel device in their network.

## Growing focus on human vulnerabilities

Human vulnerabilities are getting more attention; the share of companies taking measures in this area rose from 40 per cent in 2022 to 53 per cent last year. Examples include training to teach employees basic hygiene in cybersecurity, or simulating [phishing](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/cybersecurity/wat-is-phishing.html) attacks to increase alertness to such emails. “You should repeat such exercises monthly,” advises Arwi van der Sluijs, Managing Director of cybersecurity service provider NFIR. “Also link a test to them to monitor knowledge of cyber safety, and have HR keep track of the results.”

Arranging help in advance for when a cyber incident occurs is a less widespread measure, at 35 per cent of respondents. The same applies to taking out insurance against the damage (32 per cent).

## Mix of measures

Maarten Roerink of cybersecurity service provider MMOX underlines the importance of a mix of measures. “The cybersecurity market has traditionally been driven by IT and technological measures,” says the CEO. “But if you take a risk-driven approach, you soon notice that interventions at process level and in the human domain are also needed. Which business data are, for example, sensitive or valuable, and which employees have access to that data? The employees concerned should then receive additional training and security measures, or their access to a particular system should be withdrawn.”

A broad approach to getting cyber risks under control can be done in three relatively simple steps. The first is to carry out a risk analysis that maps out which matters are crucial to keeping the company operating and maintaining service delivery. The second step is to take measures, such as encouraging safe behaviour among employees, recording who owns certain data and discussing cyber risks with partners and suppliers. Finally, it is essential to develop procedures to detect and handle cyber incidents.
