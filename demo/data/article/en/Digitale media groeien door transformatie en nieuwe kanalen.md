# Digital media grow through transformation and new channels

[Analysis](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/analyses/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

![Digital marketing](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/media/digital-marketing_2340752131_538_tcm16-232053.jpg)

22/08/2024 • 4 minutes read

[Mario Bersem](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/mario-bersem/index.html)

Digital media are showing strong growth in a transforming media landscape. For 2024, ABN AMRO expects revenue growth of 9%, while traditional media are shrinking. Innovations such as smart screens and retail media networks attract extra audiences and new applications of artificial intelligence also drive growth.

The media offering is increasingly digital, in other words expressible in “zeros and ones”. On the one hand, this transformation means digital media grow at the expense of traditional media, for example when paper newspaper subscriptions are converted to digital newspaper subscriptions. On the other hand, digital media grow thanks to innovations that attract extra audiences. For 2024, ABN AMRO expects revenue growth of 9 per cent for digital media, while traditional media will shrink by 1 per cent. On balance, the media sector as a whole will post revenue growth of 6 per cent, supported by a strong recovery in consumer purchasing power. In 2025, revenue growth for digital media is expected to ease slightly to 7 per cent, partly due to lower inflation.

One of these innovations is smart “digital out-of-home screens” that show ads depending on the time of day or even how busy the street is. New retail media networks are also growing strongly; retail chains are making more and more ad space available in their own channels such as the website, the app, and also the screens in shops. Search engines and social media platforms are also still growing in use, purely digital channels that have no clear analogue counterpart.

## Transformation of the media landscape

The media landscape is marked by a transformation that consists of a shift from analogue to digital, from offline to online and from live to on-demand. Where people once mainly watched live on a television screen, they now also watch “television” via an app on the smartphone, while travelling on the train, two days after a programme has been broadcast.

The digital transformation is also creating entirely new possibilities. Algorithmic and personalised selection based on artificial intelligence (AI) is gaining ground over manual selection by an editorial team. Examples include social media applications, where everyone gets a tailored media offering based on their social network, interests and preferences. Streaming services also try to keep the user’s attention with tailored recommendations. This increases user engagement and the time they spend on digital platforms. Ultimately, this retained “attention” is monetised by serving advertisements and/or by selling subscriptions to users.

## The role of artificial intelligence

One of the most transformative forces within digital media is artificial intelligence (AI). AI plays a crucial role in the transformation of digital media, for example by offering personalised content recommendations, as described above. The efficiency of digital advertising is also increased through improved data analysis and user insights, enabling media companies to respond better to consumer trends and preferences. Creative agencies are meanwhile experimenting widely with the possibilities of generative AI, both on the copywriting side and on the design side. This speeds up the creative process, shifts the emphasis from creation to selection, and opens up new possibilities to produce quickly and cost-efficiently.

Whether the growth in productivity also leads to revenue growth depends on price developments. If prices fall because there is less use of “expensive” human creativity and craftsmanship, the impact on revenue can even be negative. For this forecast, any price declines for 2024 and 2025 have not yet been included, on the expectation that these will only play a role in the medium term.

## Digital ad spend drives media sector

Developments in the advertising market are a good barometer for developments within the media sector. In 2023, around € 5.2 billion was spent on advertising in the Netherlands, of which 71 per cent was digital advertising, that is € 3.7 billion, according to the VIA/Deloitte Digital Advertising Spend report for 2023 (see chart). Revenue growth for digital advertising was 5 per cent compared with 2022. Compared with an average annual growth rate of 11 per cent in the ten years before, this was relatively low.

In 2024, ABN AMRO expects 9 per cent revenue growth for digital advertising, more in line with the historical average. Growth rates of 11 per cent or more from the past are no longer to be expected because digitalisation has now progressed further and some submarkets are saturated. Paid search, for example, is growing somewhat more slowly, while online video, social media and digital audio are growing somewhat faster. Retail media is the biggest outlier in terms of growth; for retail media, ABN AMRO expects 40 per cent growth in 2024 and total ad revenue of € 400 million.

[Also read: Advertising a lucrative side income for retail chains](/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/advertenties-lucratieve-bijverdienste-voor-winkelketens.html)

Paid search via search engines is the largest category within digital advertising and grew by 3 per cent in 2023 to around € 1.7 billion. Search engines thus accounted for one-third of total ad spend, online and offline, in 2023. Display advertising, including fast-growing categories such as online video (7 per cent growth) and social media (11 per cent growth), followed closely at € 1.6 billion and is expected to overtake search engines for the first time next year.

Digital audio grew by 16 per cent in 2023 due to the increasing popularity of podcasts and because FM radio is slowly giving way to digital DAB+ radio. In radio, the digital transformation is slower than elsewhere because people in the Netherlands enjoy listening to the radio in the car and many car radios are not yet equipped to receive DAB+. Since December 2020, every new car has been fitted with DAB+, following a European directive, but people in the Netherlands drive the [oldest car fleet in Western Europe](https://www.knac.nl/nederlands-heeft-oudste-wagenpark-in-west-europa-114-jaar/), with an average age of 11.4 years. FM stations are expected to continue broadcasting their analogue signal for the time being, as is also evident from the successful auction of FM frequencies in July 2023, which raised € 150 million for the government.
