# Management insufficiently involved in cyber security

[Report](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/rapporten/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

06/06/2024 • 3 minutes read

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

Cyber attacks can paralyse business operations and seriously disrupt relationships with customers. Cyber security is therefore a board-level issue, also because European legislation makes management responsible for it. Yet this awareness has not fully reached the boardroom, according to a survey by ABN AMRO and research agency MWM2 among 895 organisations.

The new European NIS2 directive places ultimate responsibility for cyber security at management level. If it fails to meet its obligations under this law, individual board members can even be held personally liable. Large companies still have work to do. Among these respondents, only 40 per cent already have the management team as the ultimate owner for cyber security. At almost as many companies (35 per cent), that role lies with an internal IT or security specialist. Half of large companies discuss cyber security “very regularly” at management level and more than a third (37 per cent) do so “regularly”.

## Gap between management and specialists

Compared with large companies, the management teams of small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) appear to have a slightly firmer grip on the wheel — possibly due to these companies’ smaller scale. Half of SMEs (49 per cent) see the management team as ultimately responsible for cyber security. The topic is discussed less often at management level than in large companies, though. A quarter do this “very regularly” and about half “regularly”.

According to Arwi van der Sluijs, Managing Director of cyber security service provider NFIR, there is still a gap to bridge between management on the one hand and IT and security experts on the other. Specialists do not always succeed in conveying the urgency of certain measures. “They should not only talk about the technical solution. It is better to speak in terms of concrete risks to the company. Otherwise, there is a risk that a good security initiative will be shot down purely on the basis of cost.” Conversely, many management teams labour under the false assumption that IT and security departments can be fully in control with a limited budget. “If, after a cyber attack, the IT staff are then blamed for dropping the ball, that is very painful.”

## Paper reality as a response

Although ownership at management level is essential for better-protected companies, the NIS2 obligation also brings a new kind of risk. “I foresee a situation in which companies will focus to an extreme degree on compliance, simply to prevent individual management and board members being held liable for cyber incidents,” says Daan Hoogendijk, Programme Director of Samen Digitaal Veilig. The platform, a partnership of major industry associations, helps companies with information and solutions in the area of digital security. “That would create a paper reality rather than people working in the real world to raise cyber resilience to a higher level. It is important that companies recognise that risk.”

All the more reason for companies to arm themselves properly against cyber attacks and have management take the lead. ABN AMRO says this can be done in three relatively simple steps. First, carry out a risk analysis to map out which matters are critical to keeping the company running and services up to standard. Second, take measures such as encouraging safe behaviour among employees, defining who owns certain data, and discussing cyber risks with partners and suppliers. Finally, it is essential to develop procedures to detect and handle cyber incidents.
