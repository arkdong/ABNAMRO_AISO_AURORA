# More cyberattacks using stolen login details

[Report](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/rapporten/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

27/05/2024 • 3 minutes read

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

Cybercriminals are increasingly starting their attack by logging in to company systems with a username and password. This strategy is now used as often as phishing, which over half of companies faced in 2023, according to a survey by ABN AMRO and research agency MWM2 among 895 organisations.

This increase shows how easy it has become for cybercriminals to obtain combinations of usernames and passwords. In many cases, they no longer even need to set up a separate [phishing](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/cybersecurity/wat-is-phishing.html) campaign, where only a small share of recipients will take the ‘desired’ action. Malicious actors are therefore willing to pay top money for ready-to-use datasets. This is one reason why criminal groups that previously focused on [ransomware](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/cybersecurity/wat-is-ransomware.html) are shifting their hunting ground to creating malware designed specifically to steal information.

## Getting in unnoticed

Attackers can obtain those login details in different ways. Sometimes they get hold of ready-made lists of leaked user accounts, often offered via the ‘dark web’. They then try to log in to company systems with these details, taking advantage of the fact that people often use the same combinations of usernames and passwords for different accounts. In other cases, the leaked lists lack passwords and attackers guess them using enormous computing power. This latter method also marked the start of a Chinese cyber-espionage operation at the Dutch chip manufacturer NXP, which began in 2017 but went unnoticed until 2020. The hackers therefore had long-term access to the manufacturer’s intellectual property, such as chip designs.

## Exploiting vulnerabilities

Vulnerabilities in internet-connected programmes were also a popular starting point for cyberattacks in 2023. Such backdoors can be abused to break into systems before a fix or ‘patch’ is available. Abuse occurred via newly found [vulnerabilities](https://blog.google/technology/safety-security/a-review-of-zero-day-in-the-wild-exploits-in-2023/#:~:text=In%202023%2C%20Google%20observed%2097,of%202021) in the Windows, iOS and Android operating systems, and the Chrome and Safari web browsers. Cybercriminals are often quick off the mark; in 2023, attempts to exploit a [quarter](https://blog.qualys.com/vulnerabilities-threat-research/2023/12/19/2023-threat-landscape-year-in-review-part-one#:~:text=MITRE%20ATT%26CK%20tactics.-,2023%20Statistics,found%20than%20the%20year%20before.) of these ‘zero-days’ were made within a day.

## Combination of measures matters

This top three of ‘initial access vectors’ underlines the importance for companies of applying a combination of measures. Training employees to recognise phishing can reduce the number of successful attempts, and applying ‘multifactor authentication’ is important to prevent leaked or stolen login details being misused. With this authentication method, one or more extra checks take place, for example via a confirmation in a mobile app or a code sent by SMS. The abuse of vulnerabilities in programmes and devices highlights the importance of regular software updates and a solid check of IT suppliers’ cybersecurity policies.
