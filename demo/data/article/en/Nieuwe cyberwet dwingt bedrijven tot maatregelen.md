# New cyber law forces companies to take measures

[Report](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/rapporten/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

29/05/2024 • 3 minutes read

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

To improve cyber resilience in Europe, a new law will take effect in October 2024: NIS2, the successor to the earlier Network and Information Systems Directive (NIS). This law forces organisations to take a critical look at the cyber resilience of suppliers and customers. Smaller companies in particular are not yet prepared for critical questions from the chain, which could cost them clients.

The law comprises a set of rules primarily aimed at companies whose disruption could have a broad social impact. Both Europe’s energy infrastructure and the rail network, for example, are the [target](https://tweakers.net/nieuws/220560/minister-tsjechie-rusland-voert-cyberaanvallen-uit-op-europees-spoornetwerk.html) of Russian state hackers. Where NIS focused on six sectors, including energy supply, digital infrastructure and healthcare, successor NIS2 reaches much further. Food companies, government and the chemical industry now also fall under the new law, for example. In total, 10,432 Dutch companies across seventeen sectors must comply with the new rules.

Companies in the relevant sectors will be required, among other things, to apply basic security measures, including the use of strong passwords and carrying out software updates. They must also be able to guarantee organisational continuity after a serious attack, for example through back-ups and crisis management. In addition, they must register and are obliged to report cyber incidents in their company to the supervisor. These are existing national supervisors, which may differ by sector.

## Sprinting before implementation

Although there is movement in the market, very little is happening at most small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) according to Daan Hoogendijk, programme director of Samen Digitaal Veilig, and what is happening is “painfully slow”. Hoogendijk: “I fear that in the months before NIS2 is implemented everyone will be sprinting. We saw the same thing when the GDPR privacy law was introduced.” Only 38 per cent of the SMEs surveyed have looked into the NIS2 legislation “very much” or “a lot”, compared with 58 per cent of large companies.

Even so, the programme director views the new law with optimism. “Ultimately, the chain approach will become the standard,” Hoogendijk looks ahead. “And I expect that within about three years SMEs will also have their cyber hygiene in order.” He refers to a set of basic security measures that every company should apply as standard, including the use of strong passwords and carrying out software updates. “Compare it to locking your door; that is also a routine you apply automatically every day.” Of the large organisations surveyed, 40 per cent say they already apply these measures in full; among SMEs and sole traders this is about a quarter.

## Insight into suppliers

As noted, one of the key points of the new law is that companies must also have a clear view of the cyber risks arising from their own supply chain, and that agreements on cyber security must be laid down contractually with these chain partners.

This is a reality that smaller companies will also have to take into account. For example, a large supermarket chain has already made an urgent request to all its Dutch suppliers to explain their cyber security policy, says Hoogendijk. “The message was: either you show a certificate, or we stop the collaboration. Unfortunately, most of these food companies still have too little focus on cyber security.”

Smaller companies therefore also need to step up to get their cyber security in order and comply with the new European law.
