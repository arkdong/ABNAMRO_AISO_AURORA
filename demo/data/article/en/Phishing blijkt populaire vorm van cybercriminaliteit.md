# Phishing proves a popular form of cybercrime

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

Phishing is a popular method for cybercriminals to gain access to companies, and entrepreneurs fear it. Attackers send messages that prompt the recipient to take an action that later turns out to be harmful. Attacks are becoming more targeted and more sophisticated.

[Phishing](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/cybersecurity/wat-is-phishing.html) is the most reported type across all company sizes, according to a survey by ABN AMRO and research agency MWM2 among 895 organisations. In 2023, well over half of companies faced phishing emails or texts. These messages tempt the recipient to open a link that triggers an automatic download of malicious software, or to visit a fake login page where data are shared with malicious third parties.

## Careful research for a targeted attack

CEO fraud is a specific form of phishing, also known as “whaling”. Fraudsters pose as the CEO or a senior manager and approach employees in their name. The channels range from email to WhatsApp and even phone calls. These are usually urgent requests to transfer money, pay invoices or provide sensitive information. The attackers have often already done careful research. First to find the right contact details, and also to imitate the manager’s communication style. No fewer than 33 per cent of large-company respondents faced such an attack last year.

## Deepfakes of colleagues

Artificial Intelligence (AI) further expands the attack options. By using deepfake technology, which creates images or audio that are indistinguishable from real, bad actors can make a victim believe they are dealing with someone else. In Hong Kong, a finance employee at a multinational transferred more than [25 million dollars](https://edition.cnn.com/2024/02/04/asia/deepfake-cfo-scam-hong-kong-intl-hnk/index.html) to fraudsters after joining a video meeting with a deepfake version of the CFO and other colleagues.

In October last year, there was a similar attempt at Dutch online bank Bunq, when an employee was invited to a video call with what later turned out to be an AI clone of chief executive Ali Niknam. In a [LinkedIn post](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7114874320627085312/), Niknam calls the clone “convincing”.

## Ready-made login details

Phishing is a popular way for bad actors to start a cyberattack, but it only causes damage if the recipient actually takes the bait. That is why attackers now often start their journey through the target’s IT landscape by simply logging in to one of the systems with a username and password. In 2023, almost one-third (30 per cent) of cyberattacks began this way, up from just 16 per cent in 2022, according to [IBM X-Force](https://www.ibm.com/reports/threat-intelligence). These login details can be bought ready-made, or guessed using brute computing power. It is an unobtrusive way to get in, which gives the intruder plenty of time to look for further vulnerabilities and information.

## Measures to reduce risks

Encouraging safe behaviour among employees is crucial to counter phishing. It is one of three steps entrepreneurs should take to protect themselves against cyberattacks. The first step is to carry out a risk analysis to map what is crucial to keep the company running and services operational. The second step is to take measures, such as promoting safe behaviour among employees, assigning ownership of specific data, and discussing cyber risks with partners and suppliers. Finally, it is essential to develop procedures to detect and handle cyber incidents.
