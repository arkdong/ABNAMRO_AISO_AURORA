Will Europe be crushed between the US and China when it comes to technology? Will we lose both our digital autonomy and our role in the tech economy? Far from it, predicts ABN AMRO sector specialist Julia Krauwer. In a Europe that is waking up digitally, she sees abundant opportunity. "A lot of great things are already happening, but the internal barriers have to go. And we need to speak our ambitions out loud."

When it came to technology, Europe long walked hand in hand with big brother America. Software, servers, cloud storage: all our data and systems felt familiar, but they were completely dependent. At first that was fine, until geopolitical relations were turned upside down and the rise of AI began to shake virtually every layer of our business community and society. "The rapid breakthrough of AI is transforming business models. It affects every company and certainly those in tech, media and telecom," says Julia Krauwer, ABN AMRO's sector specialist in this area. "The question is not whether you will change, but whether, as an entrepreneur, you can keep up with the pace."

## Dependence creates vulnerability

The European tech sector is posting strong growth figures, but it also faces major uncertainties. Behind digital progress lies an enormous dependence that remains largely invisible to end users. Virtually all the servers, chips and software on which companies, sectors and even societies run come from the US or Asia. "If that supply stops, our entire digital economy is affected. The war in Ukraine, recent developments involving Nexperia and the continuing tensions between China and Taiwan have accelerated that awareness."

That dependence is reflected in Synergy Research's figures. Between 2017 and 2022, the European cloud market grew fivefold, yet the combined market share of European providers still fell from 27 to just 13 per cent. The reason: Amazon, Microsoft and Google grew much faster. They too have invested billions in data centres on European soil in recent years. "So while we are growing ourselves, the outside world is growing much faster still," Krauwer says, summing up the urgency.

> "The future belongs to companies that provide technology that is hard to reproduce and that can be integrated intelligently into other people's ecosystems."

## AI as a catalyst and a threat

According to Krauwer, artificial intelligence is both the most important driving force and the greatest disruptor of the moment. "AI changes everything, from the way we develop software to the way we advertise and sell products."

She describes a two-sided movement: on the one hand, AI is driving an explosion in productivity. IT service providers are helping clients with automation, forecasting and bespoke solutions, opening up new markets. On the other hand, artificial intelligence threatens to undermine parts of the software industry itself. "Software companies are in a strange bind: AI can make your product smarter, but it can also make it redundant. The winners have a strong core, unique data or deep domain knowledge." Companies whose value rests on unique algorithms or datasets are better protected against generative AI that can easily copy standard features.

According to Krauwer, a new distinction is emerging in which scale is no longer the only factor; what matters above all is whether you offer a very specific and ingenious solution. "Small tech companies can scale very quickly too; just look at Sweden's Lovable. But anyone who only delivers a nice interface runs the risk of an AI agent taking that over tomorrow. The future belongs to companies that provide technology that is hard to reproduce and that can be integrated intelligently into other people's ecosystems."

## From risk to opportunity

Where dependence on foreign players used to be seen mainly as a risk, Krauwer now also sees an opportunity. "Because of the changing relationship between America and Europe, we in Europe have become acutely aware of the importance of cloud sovereignty. As a European company, you now want more than ever to know where your data sits, who can access it and who controls the master switch. Companies that can demonstrably show they handle privacy and transparency, data sovereignty and therefore the continuity of their services with care have an important USP in their hands: your data is stored safely in Europe, under European law and regulation, and is therefore less dependent on geopolitical unrest."

## European Google Maps alternative charts its own course

The Amsterdam-based company Magic Lane shows that digital sovereignty and commercial success can go hand in hand with its white-label Location Intelligence Technology: technology that allows other platforms to integrate navigation or shared-mobility solutions easily, from electric bicycles to shared cars, and with smart features such as offline navigation and live traffic data. CEO Raymond Alves says: "And the technology safeguards privacy: unlike American providers, users are not tracked; all data remains anonymous." The figures also show that this approach is paying off: in the spring, the number of users of the consumer app rose by 250% in six weeks. "This shows that privacy and performance do not have to be mutually exclusive."

> "We are excellent at R&D in the Netherlands and in Europe, but we still find it daunting to say out loud that we are going to conquer the world."

## Culture and capital

There is no shortage of promising initiatives, but Europe is still a long way from where it wants to be. Krauwer says: "We are excellent at R&D in the Netherlands and in Europe, but we still find it daunting to say that we are going to conquer the world." That modesty does not work in our favour, especially when it comes to raising sufficient capital, which is essential if good ideas are to flourish. Tech companies in Europe find it far harder to secure large investments than their counterparts in America. According to the State of European Tech 2024 report, the cumulative gap has now risen to USD375 billion. "In the US, start-ups raise more than USD15 million twice as often," says Krauwer. "That is a huge difference. And in the end, capital determines whether you can scale." Fortunately, Krauwer also sees the first signs of a broader opening there, albeit cautiously. "Investors are seeking one another out more often," she notes. "Brussels is also working on a so-called 28th regime to simplify investment and scaling across borders. With this, Europe is developing a single European framework for company law, insolvency law, employment law and tax law, to put an end to the current fragmentation of 27 countries each with their own rules."

## Cyber resilience as a test of maturity

Alongside economic uncertainty, the security risk is increasing. Tech companies are attractive targets for hackers and espionage groups, especially now that digital infrastructure has become part of geopolitical power. "As a tech company, you have to assume that you will be hacked at some point," says Krauwer. "The question is how you deal with it." In her view, cyber resilience is part of strategic maturity. "It is not only about measures to keep attackers out of your systems, but also about communication and recovery capacity if you are hacked despite all precautions. Being resilient means being able to get back on your feet quickly. Businesses that embed safety and transparency in their brand win trust from both customers and investors."

## Fronts of growth

Krauwer sees European tech companies able to grow on several fronts, provided they dare to act boldly. "We should not only want to be less dependent on Big Tech; above all, we should want to become stronger ourselves." The first growth layer lies in AI integration: companies that weave artificial intelligence intelligently into their products or processes. "You see software companies integrating AI cleverly into their products themselves, and in doing so developing specialist, and preferably highly intelligent, domain-specific applications that are ideally highly scalable but not easy for others to imitate."

The second growth layer is technical infrastructure, from chips to data centres. Krauwer says: "Demand for computing power is growing faster than the supply of data centres, while energy and grid capacity also impose limits. That pressure has already led to some great innovations. Take Axelera AI, a Dutch start-up in chips, which develops powerful and energy-efficient AI processing units. Or Asperitas, which has developed a cooling system for data centres in which servers are immersed in cooling liquid, making them more compact, more energy-efficient and more sustainable."

## Building blocks for developers

Thanks to Magic Lane's modular architecture, developers can now build solutions even faster with the support of AI. "We do not deliver one app, but separate building blocks that developers can use. In addition, our software development kit can even be called by AI development tools. We deliberately position ourselves at the back end of an application," Alves says. "That is where the strength lies: technology that is hard to replicate, built on European values. In this way we show that this kind of technology, like Signal and Protonmail, which offer alternatives to messenger and email applications respectively, is not idealism but a realistic business model."

> "We offer technology that is hard to replicate, built on European values."

The third growth layer lies in the focus on European values. Providers of innovative products that are not only smart but also privacy-friendly, respect copyrights, are energy-efficient, run on local servers and operate without bias, win trust within and beyond Europe. Krauwer cites Dutch company Ab Ovo as an example. "They have integrated AI into both the development process and their actual software, while also focusing on building software that works more flexibly and more sustainably. That is exactly the step more companies need to take."

## Talent and ecosystems

Ultimately, the success of the sector stands or falls with people. "Talent is drawn to places where ambition is tangible," says Krauwer. "Universities, start-ups, investors: you need concentration. We need to create more breeding grounds where science and business meet. Only then can you retain the talent that is currently leaving for abroad." She points, among other things, to TU Delft and TU Eindhoven, which are already helping dozens of spin-offs to scale. "Those are encouraging signals. But it all has to be faster, more ambitious and more international, with more boldness and courage. In Silicon Valley, failure is mainly seen as a learning moment; here it still seems more like a disgrace. If you dare to state your big ambitions out loud, you are more likely to achieve them."

> "In Silicon Valley, failure is mainly seen as a learning moment; in the Netherlands it still seems more like a disgrace."

## More equal footing

All things considered, Krauwer expects the European Big Tech and AI ecosystems to grow strongly in the years ahead. Not with the primary aim of becoming completely detached from American tech, but of becoming more equal. "We are building a plan B by strengthening our own alternatives. Anyone who dares to invest in that independence is helping to build the next generation of European tech. At the same time, you should not assume that what worked five years ago is still good enough for the next five. The entrepreneurs who understand that are tomorrow's winners. Anyone who dares to link their own values to innovation and scale can become a global player even now."
