Marie Marie Amsterdam is choosing local craftsmanship and connection. The crocheted bags, made by Dutch hobbyists, are finding their way to consumers at home and abroad. Together with the Made by Marí platform, nominated for the ABN AMRO Newcomer Retail Award 2025-2026, the founders are also making local craftsmanship scalable for other brands.

The story of [Marie Marie Amsterdam](https://marie-amsterdam.com/), founded in 2022, begins with a bag that Marthe De Groot bought from her grandmother's crochet club. "I got so many compliments on it at home in Amsterdam," she says. "Then I thought: what a shame that this kind of craftsmanship stays hidden. How can I bring these crocheters together with people who want to buy their work?"

## From hobby to community

During the coronavirus period, De Groot decides to take the idea seriously. She posts calls in Facebook groups and within no time dozens of crocheters sign up. Marie Marie Amsterdam now has a community of 200 crocheters, divided into hubs around the country. Each hub has a coordinator who distributes materials and patterns. "For many women, it is valuable to connect their creativity to something bigger," says De Groot. "It shows that their handiwork has impact far beyond the hobby. All our bags come with a handwritten card bearing the maker's name.

Not just me, but Joke, Corrie and Jolanda are incredibly proud of our bags now being stocked at De Bijenkorf."

The colourful crocheted bags from Marie Marie Amsterdam are a hit. Alongside the company's own webshop and shop in De Pijp, the bags are now stocked by various international clients such as The Iconic in Australia and Le Bon Marche in Paris. This spring, the brand launched a collaboration with Dutch label Fabienne Chapot. This year, it also ran a promotion at Lowlands.

> "The colourful crocheted bags from Marie Marie Amsterdam are a hit."

## Active in 60 WhatsApp groups

That popularity also brings challenges. "I was overwhelmed by the influx of crocheters and the demand from shops," De Groot reflects. "The hardest part was organising everything operationally and making it scalable. I was in 60 WhatsApp groups and together with my mother I was working full time on community management."

That is when Rinke Adema enters the picture. With her experience at tech companies and start-ups, she immediately sees an opportunity. "I met Marthe and thought: this needs to be digitised. With a platform that automates everything, from onboarding makers to project management and payments."

## Making craftsmanship scalable

The two quickly decide to think beyond crocheted bags alone with their platform. "It escalated a bit, really," Adema laughs. "We saw that many makers have several hobbies, not just crocheting, but also ceramics, furniture making or clothing. In addition, Marthe noticed in her contacts with the fashion industry that many brands do want to work with craftsmanship, but get stuck in their own models. That is why we launched Made by Marí in 2024: a platform that brings makers and brands together and makes craftsmanship scalable."

> "This needs to be digitised. With a platform that automates everything, from onboarding makers to project management and payments."

Rinke Adema

Founder Made by Marí

The founders also see their platform as an answer to the growing pressure from legislation and regulation. Adema says: "Government is asking for more and more sustainable solutions, and many brands are struggling with that. With our platform, we make it possible to produce on a small scale and locally, exactly where the sector needs to go."

## The point on the horizon

Over the past year, the entrepreneurs have been working on developing that platform. A first version is now in place. "With Marie Marie Amsterdam as our first client, we are now testing the system, ironing out the bugs and learning how to make this work properly in the long term. You start small, but you already need a vision for five to ten years ahead," says Adema.

That point on the horizon is clear. Adema says: "We want to be a partner for major international brands with an international community of makers. From Tokyo to Terneuzen. Imagine a brand like Uniqlo working with local embroiderers in every city. Or Nike keyrings made by local makers. I also believe in Made by Marí evenings with makers all over the world."

De Groot adds: "We believe this platform can shake up the fashion industry and inspire it to revise its production models. Back in time, but with a hint of the future. The fashion industry is currently large-scale, polluting and complicated. We want to bring it closer to people."

## A change in mentality is badly needed

According to the founders, shaking up the industry is badly needed. "Some people think Marie Marie's bags are expensive. But when you consider how many hours, love and care go into them, they are actually incredibly cheap. It really requires a change in consumer mentality. We believe in inspiring people, rather than imposing things on them." According to Adema, that change starts with the big brands. "If the very brands that made fast fashion so big now start including small-scale, local collections, that sets an enormous movement in motion."

> "We believe this platform can shake up the fashion industry and inspire it to revise production models."

Marthe de Groot

Founder Made by Marí

The nomination for the ABN AMRO Newcomer Retail Award 2025-2026 feels to De Groot like confirmation that they are on the right track with Marie Marie Amsterdam and the Made by Marí platform. "We have not been at it for very long, so it is special that we are being seen by people in the field." Adema adds: "It is also the perfect stage for getting in touch with other retailers who can use our platform."

## The most enjoyable and the toughest trial

What would they like to pass on to other start-up entrepreneurs? Adema says: "Do not give up. You always run into hassle and setbacks, but persistence wins. Keep believing in your vision. We are not making money from the platform yet, which is why I am still working full time alongside it."

De Groot concludes: "You have to do something you are passionate about, otherwise you will not keep going. Of course there are risks, but we are not afraid of them. There is always a way. This is the most enjoyable, but also the toughest thing we have ever done."
