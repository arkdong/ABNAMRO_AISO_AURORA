# 5G essential for the Netherlands’ earning capacity

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

Kasper Buiting

Technology, Media and Telecom (TMT) is the sector where the new economy first becomes visible. IT services, the main industry within the sector, continues to grow strongly and has emerged as a driving force in the Dutch economy.

## Download the full report

(Can’t see a download or unable to download the report? Please upgrade your browser to the latest version.)

- [State of Technology, Media and Telecom (TMT) (13 January 2020)

  463 KB](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/media/Sectorprognose-TMT-2020-1-_tcm16-66049.pdf)

To keep contributing to the Netherlands’ earnings model, the preconditions must be in order. The first major challenge is 5G, for which the first Dutch auctions will take place this year. Unfortunately, the Netherlands is lagging behind many other countries. It is vital that the Dutch IT cluster and other segments of the economy make full use of the benefits of this new development.

## IT sector to grow by 4.5 per cent in 2020

IT services – the largest industry in the Technology, Media and Telecom (TMT) sector – is an important driver of growth in the Dutch economy. In the first three quarters of 2019, revenue in this segment even grew by 7 per cent year-on-year. Software companies and hosting companies in particular – driven by rising demand for cloud-based services – are a major growth engine of IT services. Although ABN AMRO expects GDP in the Netherlands to grow by less than one per cent in 2020, revenue in the TMT sector is expected to rise by 3 per cent and the IT industry will perform even better (+4.5 per cent). The strong growth of this industry has led to a sharp increase in the number of employees in recent years. At the same time, this is also its biggest problem, as there is still a large shortage of IT staff.

In telecoms, 2019 actually saw contraction (-0.5 per cent), partly due to digitalisation, the focus on data traffic and falling prices for telephony services. In 2020, ABN AMRO expects a further decline in telecoms revenue: -2.0 per cent. Other industries within the sector, such as publishers and printers, will also see a slight decline in revenue in the coming years.

## Introducing 5G is required to maintain the strong position of the Dutch IT sector

The Dutch IT industry holds a very strong position worldwide thanks to robust infrastructure with fast internet connections. However, the Netherlands is lagging behind other countries, especially in Asia, when it comes to adopting 5G. This network offers much higher speeds than the current 4G network. For many applications, 4G still seems adequate at present and the Netherlands also has one of the fastest 4G networks in the world. That is why a rapid introduction of 5G does not seem necessary right now. However, the Netherlands cannot afford to be at the back when 5G developments truly gain momentum. “The first auctions for 5G will not take place in our country until 2020. It is vital that the Dutch IT sector catches up and makes full use of the benefits of 5G,” emphasises Kasper Buiting, Sector Economist Technology, Media and Telecom at ABN AMRO. “The much higher speeds of 5G, for example, bring promising revenue models and new applications, such as the self-driving car and VR services, within reach. Too slow a development of 5G could eventually harm competitiveness and the favourable business climate for IT companies in the Netherlands.”

# AI is the creative brain of the future

[Headlines & Insights](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/headlines-insights/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

Kamalika Patra

With shrinking research productivity and declining innovation, it is getting harder to achieve economic growth. There is hope, though; algorithms that combine existing inventions into new ones can accelerate innovation. A new Google model can now make a tasty ‘cakie’ by blending cake and biscuit recipes.

## Insight

Conventional theory holds that long-term economic growth can only continue through technological improvements that raise labour productivity; improvements driven by inventions and innovations. After the industrial revolutions of past centuries, some economists fear that the US economy has already [seen its best days](https://www.nber.org/papers/w18315) due to a lack of innovations.

The suspicion that growth cannot continue is confirmed by [research](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w23782/w23782.pdf); the so-called ‘research productivity’ of scientists and researchers is falling by an average of 5 per cent a year. Current economic growth is only thanks to the high inflow of new researchers. For example, far more researchers are needed than in the 1970s to generate ideas to keep Moore’s Law going, the observed pattern that every two years the number of transistors on a chip doubles.

Moreover, [research](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsif.2015.0272) by, among others, the University of Oxford indicates that the number of inventions is shrinking. Patent applications largely combine and adapt earlier inventions. This is called ‘combinatorial innovation’.

However, this limited pool of ideas is also running dry. The more combinatorial innovations there already are, and the fewer inventions, the harder it becomes to keep innovating combinatorially. Combinatorial innovation is limited by a person’s knowledge and experience, as well as creativity, which cannot be forced. In the end it is largely a matter of chance that exactly the right elements of existing inventions are brought together.

As research productivity dries up, artificial intelligence (AI) comes into view. AI first of all has no shortage of ‘input’. An AI programme that uses so-called neural networks has all the knowledge that humanity has stored digitally at its fingertips. The high processing speed of computers then ensures that this knowledge can be combined and processed within seconds. This rules out all chance; in a flash, computers can assess all possible combinations for feasibility without overlooking anything.

Take IBM’s Deep Blue, for example, the computer programme that beat world chess champion Garry Kasparov in 1997. Deep Blue evaluates 200 million chess positions per second. Compared with Kasparov’s two chess positions per second, the computer has a colossal advantage over a human, says Professor Jonathan Shaeffer, developer of the AI world champion in draughts, in an [interview](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fLZyIbuh7hU&feature=youtu.be&ab_channel=StarTalk).

Programmes that really think along creatively are more recent. In December 2020, Google AI expert Sara Robinson showed a [machine learning model](https://cloud.google.com/blog/topics/developers-practitioners/baking-recipes-made-ai) that merges existing cake and biscuit recipes into new ones. The experiment produced the ‘cakie’, a tasty cake–biscuit hybrid. An [AI programme](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1706.05585.pdf) developed by researchers at Carnegie Mellon now combines inventions recorded in the US patent database.

These ‘artificial inventors’ are unfortunately still far from perfect. AI needs concrete rules to deliver viable ideas, but the nature of innovation is the opposite; it has no rules. Programming strict rules involves a trade-off: viability is weighed against the constraints that rules place on innovation opportunities. As a result, the strength of computers is also their weakness; they overlook nothing, including the ‘bad’ inventions. For example, alongside the cakie, Robinson’s algorithm also produced an abundance of inedible fare, just like a [similar algorithm](https://venturebeat.com/2021/01/07/why-we-need-augmented-creativity-to-reignite-growth-in-2021/) from Accenture.

For programmers, the challenge is to filter good ideas from the sea of bad ones, and to strike the right balance in the amount of rules assigned to AI programmes. For now, exploring uncharted territory is up to humans. The good news is that humans no longer have to do it alone.

# Advertising model under pressure due to new European legislation

[Analysis](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/analyses/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

[Mario Bersem](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/mario-bersem/index.html)

With the Digital Services Act, the days when personal data could be freely collected and traded for advertising purposes seem to be over. Although this new European legislation is primarily aimed at large tech companies such as Google and Facebook, smaller parties in the online advertising chain will also need to adjust their strategy.

On 23 April, the Council of the European Union reached agreement on the Digital Services Act (DSA), a new set of rules intended to create a safe online environment for both citizens and businesses. Among other things, the DSA will make it harder to track internet users for advertising purposes. The revenues that websites now generate by offering space for personalised ads will no longer be a given. This may affect many parties; almost half of the most-visited websites earn money through advertising. The new law also means a switch for advertisers; in 2021, almost three-quarters of online advertising spend went to personalised ads. It is therefore important for both ‘publishers’, in advertising terms, and advertisers to try alternative advertising strategies. Contextual advertising can help, as can smart use of less privacy-sensitive data.

## Legislation for a safer internet

With the Digital Services Act and the ‘sister legislation’ Digital Markets Act (DMA), which was adopted earlier, the European Union is taking the lead in regulating the digital market, protecting personal data and curbing the power of large technology companies. The United States, Canada and Singapore are expected to follow shortly with their own initiatives. The DSA addresses two major challenges in today’s digital world: the fact that online platforms are being misused to distribute illegal products, disinformation and hate speech; and the overall lack of transparency in, and oversight of, data use by digital service providers.

Given this second challenge, it is not surprising that the DSA also includes various rules relating to online advertising. Data are an essential raw material for many digital advertising campaigns. According to the ‘Digital Ad Spend Study’, an annual report by consultant Deloitte and industry association VIA Nederland, 74 per cent of spend on online display ads goes to variants based on personal data. The data for these personalised ads often come from different sources; from visited websites and downloaded apps to search terms used and videos viewed. Various parties in the market consolidate these data and infer a complete profile from them; from age and place of residence to interests and buying behaviour.

Personalised ads can neatly match the needs of the website visitor, but according to various studies the drawbacks outweigh the benefits for many people. Terms such as “manipulative”, “invasive” or even “creepy” came up when people were [surveyed](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-658-24878-9_5 "Consumer View on Personalized Advertising: Overview of Self-Reported Benefits and Concerns") for a scientific study about this type of advertising. According to [research](https://www.amnesty.org/en/latest/news/2019/12/big-tech-privacy-poll-shows-people-worried/ "New poll reveals 7 in 10 people want governments to regulate Big Tech over personal data fears") by Amnesty International, 77 per cent see profiling people for online advertising purposes as a problem. More than a third of respondents (37 per cent) even avoid certain search terms or web pages for fear of being tracked.

At the same time, personalised ads are more effective than fully generic ones; the chance that someone clicks on an advert tailored to online behaviour turns out to be more than [five times as high](https://datadrivenadvertising.eu/wp-content/uploads/2017/09/BehaviouralTargeting_FINAL.pdf "THE ECONOMIC VALUE OF BEHAVIOURAL TARGETING IN DIGITAL ADVERTISING") as with general ads. Advertisers are therefore willing to pay more for this variant. When Apple made a technical change to the Safari web browser, meaning users could no longer be tracked by default for personalised ads, the ad price fell by [60 per cent](https://www.theinformation.com/articles/inside-apples-decision-to-blow-up-the-digital-ads-business "Inside Apple’s Decision to Blow Up the Digital Ads Business").

## Tracking internet users will be made more difficult

The Digital Services Act does not ban tracking. However, the new legislation makes it harder to track internet users for advertising purposes. That is primarily due to specific restrictions. As part of a broader set of measures to protect young people on online platforms, minors may no longer be shown personalised ads at all. For adults, this is in principle allowed, as long as the personalisation is not based on sensitive data. This includes, for example, information about health, sexual orientation, religion or political opinions.

The new European law also sets requirements in the area of transparency. Platforms will be obliged to state for each advert which data were used to decide to show it to the user in question. Users must also receive clear information about how their data are used for advertising at the moment their consent is requested.

Refusing personalised ads must also not be harder than accepting them. At present, website users who do not want to be tracked often have to click several times before they can make this known. The consent process also often demands users’ utmost concentration. Through sophisticated tricks, they are enticed to agree – unintentionally – to have their browsing behaviour tracked by numerous external parties. For example, by making the “Accept all” button green and placing it where the user’s cursor already was. The DSA restricts such strategies, also called ‘dark patterns’.

The DSA also enshrines that “no” means “no”. A website visitor may not be asked again for consent to use their personal data if they have already rejected this request. Such a request may not be shown at all when the user has already indicated via the browser that they do not wish to be tracked.

Finally, refusing personalised ads must not affect the functionality of the website. A website may not deny a user access if they do not wish to be tracked online. Where some parties now offer their visitors a choice between agreeing to tracking or paying for the service, this will no longer be allowed. The user must be able to choose non-personalised ads without restrictions.

The DSA enters into force twenty days after publication in the Official Journal of the European Union. Fifteen months later, the DSA will also become effectively applicable, but at the earliest on 1 January 2024. Companies that do not comply with the rules at that point risk a fine of up to six per cent of annual turnover.

## Duopoly is decisive

The new rules on tracking in the DSA apply to ‘very large online platforms’, with a reach of more than 45 million users in the European Union, and ‘online platforms’. The ‘very large online platforms’ category includes the channels of international tech giants, led by the ‘duopoly’ Meta – the parent company of Instagram and Facebook – and Alphabet, the company behind search giant Google. It is not surprising that the DSA regulates the large platform companies; together they pocket the majority of global digital advertising revenues. In 2021, Google and Facebook held the largest share of the pie with 28.6 and 23.7 per cent respectively.

Part of their revenue stream comes from the ad space they offer to advertisers for a fee. In the Google ecosystem, this could be a YouTube advert or a listing among the search engine’s ‘sponsored results’. On Instagram and Facebook, these are the ads that users see when they scroll through their feed. Because of the large amount of data these big tech companies collect about their users, they can easily give advertisers access to very specific target groups.

The other revenue stream derives from their role as intermediaries; many companies rely on Google AdSense – and to a lesser extent the Facebook Audience Network – to fill the ad space on their own website or mobile app. These ads are in many cases personalised based on behaviours on, and data shared with, the platforms in question.

It is therefore evident that restrictions on tracking pose a threat to the ad-based business model of Meta and Alphabet. This is also evident from the fact that both companies actively lobbied when the initial proposal spoke of a total ban on personalised ads. At the same time, it is clear that it will be easiest for these two dominant parties to adapt to the new rules. They have the most resources and first-party data at their disposal to continue to play an important role in the online advertising domain within the limits of the DSA.

## Not only platform companies are affected

The second category of companies concerns ‘online platforms’, websites that bring supply and demand together, such as online retailer Bol.com, online marketplace Marktplaats and travel platform Booking.com. They will also need to take a critical look at the type of advertisements they offer their visitors, and set up the process around them in line with the rules.

However, this does not mean that companies without an explicit platform business model can sit back. According to Saskia Baneke, Director of marketing industry association VIA Nederland, the ‘online platforms’ category is broader than the name suggests. “We assume that any company that makes ad space available to advertisers on its website will have to comply with the rules,” says Baneke. The new rules will therefore probably also require vigilance from, for example, news websites and online retailers. Baneke does stress that it is difficult to give a definitive answer at this stage. “Because the law has not yet entered into force, there is also no case law in which the term ‘online platform’ is further specified.”

The scope of the European law is therefore broad. Although it is not yet possible to determine exactly how many companies in the Netherlands will be affected, it is already clear that many websites depend on advertising revenue. Of the ten million most-visited websites worldwide, almost [47 per cent](https://w3techs.com/technologies/history_overview/advertising/all "Historical trends in the usage statistics of advertising networks for websites") use an ‘ad network’. This is a technological solution that aggregates online ad space from publishers and offers it to advertisers.

The extent to which these websites will still be able to generate revenue through personalised advertising is likely to decline sharply. The new process requirements make it easier for internet users to refuse tracking. The consequences of redesigning such a process should not be underestimated. Since 2021, apps from the Apple App Store have had to explicitly ask their users for permission to track their activity in the app and beyond. Only 16 per cent of users allow this, although the number appears to have [risen](https://9to5mac.com/2022/04/14/number-of-users-opting-in-to-app-tracking-on-ios-grows-significantly-since-last-year/ "Number of users opting in to App Tracking on iOS grows significantly since last year") recently.

The Dutch Public Broadcaster (NPO) has experienced what it means to be explicit. Unlike the way many other websites presented tracking choices to their visitors, on the NPO website both consent and refusal took just one click. As a result, nine in ten website visitors refused personalised ads when a pop-up allowed them to indicate their choice. Such windows had been mandatory since the introduction of the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) in 2018, but interpretations varied. This strict interpretation of the privacy law, in which refusing tracking is just as easy as accepting it, will now become the market standard under the new rules within the DSA.

## Search for alternatives

For advertisers who rely on personalised ads and publishers who depend on the revenues from such ads, the partial loss of this option may feel unsettling. However, there are also effective advertising strategies that do not depend on tracking individual internet users.

For example, due to the clear preference of its website visitors, the NPO switched entirely to ‘contextual advertising’ in 2020. The person themselves is not decisive, but rather the type of website, article or video being viewed determines the ad that is placed. This allows advertisers to infer the viewer’s interests, demographics and needs without using data about that specific person. Examples of contextual advertising include a lipstick advert on a beauty blog, or an offer for a family pack of ice cream alongside a news item about hot weather.

This strategy proved beneficial for both broadcaster and advertisers; advertisers saw the effectiveness of their campaigns [remain the same or even increase](https://www.ster.nl/media/quakpy4e/ster_een-toekomst-zonder-advertentiecookies.pdf "EEN TOEKOMST ZONDER ADVERTENTIECOOKIES?"), and the NPO saw online advertising revenues rise. In [De Volkskrant](https://www.volkskrant.nl/wetenschap/pop-ups-voor-toestemming-cookies-illegaal-bevonden-je-kunt-niet-vragen-om-akkoord-te-gaan-met-een-datalek~b578a408/ "Pop-ups voor toestemming cookies illegaal bevonden: ‘Je kunt niet vragen om akkoord te gaan met een datalek’"), Joost Negenman, a privacy officer at the broadcaster, said earlier this year that while the number of ads placed has gone down, revenue per ad has increased because the NPO stopped using Google as an intermediary for ad placement. Instead, the NPO started dealing directly with advertisers. “An enormous amount of money gets stuck with intermediaries in the entire chain,” Negenman said in the newspaper. “If you make a direct link between the advertiser and the platform, almost all the amount the advertiser spends can also benefit the platform.” According to Google, the share of ad revenues it retains is around [thirty per cent](https://blog.google/products/admanager/display-buying-share-revenue-publishers/ "How our display buying platforms share revenue with publishers").

By placing ads smartly in context and doing business directly with advertisers, the NPO essentially reverted to an old advertising model from the offline world: the advertising model of printed newspapers. Newspapers, platforms avant la lettre, have been selling ads for hundreds of years to advertisers who in turn want to reach the newspaper’s readers. The NPO’s experience shows that this old advertising model can work well through the use of contextual advertising.

There are also alternatives that still try to target people, but use far less granular and privacy-sensitive data to do so. Topics, developed by Google, assigns a limited number of topics to an internet user based on browsing behaviour. This could be, for example, ‘pets’, ‘smartphones’ or ‘weddings’. Advertisers can use those data to decide whether or not to show their advertising message to a website visitor. The browsing history is therefore no longer shared, although it still serves as the basis for a generic user profile.

The precise effects of the Digital Services Act on the online advertising industry are uncertain, but they will become increasingly clear in the run-up to 1 January 2024. The time when personal data could be freely collected and traded appears to be coming to an end. Broadly speaking, the new legislation is part of a wider social and political undercurrent in which data privacy is being defended and the internet user is given more rights regarding the use of the data they generate.

# Advertising a lucrative side income for retail chains

[Headlines & Insights](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/headlines-insights/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Mario Bersem](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/mario-bersem/index.html)

Retail chains are increasingly offering advertising space in their own channels: on the website, in the app, and also on screens in stores. Hema recently launched its own media network that connects all screens in its 280 stores. Hema is following supermarket chains like Albert Heijn and Plus, for whom retail media is already a lucrative side income.

Where retailers have traditionally ranked among the biggest advertisers, they now also sell advertising. Retailers are increasingly offering suppliers ad space to advertise via their own channels. And of course retailers use the channels themselves too: “Hamsteren!”, as a well-known slogan from a supermarket chain goes. It is not new that retailers highlight some suppliers’ products for a fee; that has happened for years in analogue form, for example through special promotions or shelves in the store. What is new is the addition of their own digital channels and physical screens on the shop floor. Entire new media networks are emerging: so-called retail media.

Using retail media to offer digital advertising is part of an international trend that, for once, did not start in the United States but in China. In China, e-commerce platforms like Alibaba and JD.com started selling digital ad space more than twenty years ago. Retail media has also penetrated further in the United States than in Europe. For example, retail giant Amazon already earns more from online advertising revenue than broadcasting giant YouTube. In 2022, it involved almost 38 billion dollars for Amazon, while YouTube stalled at about 30 billion dollars. For 2023, Amazon expects 20% growth for its advertising arm.

## Retail media spend up 42%

In the Netherlands too, advertising spend via retail media is skyrocketing. According to Rogier Lammers, chair of the retail media taskforce at industry association VIA, spend rose by 42% in 2022. Advertising spend thus reached around 210 million euros, equal to 6% of total digital advertising spend. This is still relatively low compared to, for example, Germany or the United Kingdom. ABN AMRO therefore expects solid growth in the coming years, driven in part by the current catch-up, see chart.

In Europe, Germany is ahead — not surprising, since an average German internet user has Amazon as their start page. But Europe as a whole is about three years behind the United States, says Lammers, not to mention China where retail media has penetrated much further. Figures from Daniel Knapp of IAB Europe show that retail media spend in Europe averages 11% of total digital advertising spend, while in the US it is already heading towards 17% and China now leads with as much as 30%.

## Growing pains in retail media

All major retailers have discovered the potential of retail media and are setting up teams to develop their advertising arm. In some cases, these new teams must compete with their e-commerce colleagues who, until now, had the webshop and the online shopping experience to themselves. Retail media teams want to sell ads. That sometimes leads to friction. These are ads that, even if seamlessly woven into the webshop’s house style, can still detract from the consumer’s shopping experience. More conspicuous campaigns could even rub non-advertising suppliers up the wrong way. These frictions will prove to be growing pains because the retail media trend will not be stopped. The United States and China show as much.

For advertisers, the key is to weigh up whether the costs of a retail media campaign outweigh the benefits. The benefits are fairly easy to measure — at least for the online part — via clicks and realised sales. In addition, data availability is one of the main reasons for a supplier to advertise with a retailer. Retailers hold rich customer data that are becoming harder to obtain elsewhere as third-party cookies disappear. Advertisers can target, for example someone who likes eating biscuits, and do so close to the point of sale. This combination means retail media has the wind firmly in its sails at the moment, especially in Europe where data policy is becoming more restrictive.

## Search results: sponsored products

As noted, retail media has different formats: the screen in the store, display or video ads on the website or in the app. But the lion’s share of retail media revenue consists of sponsored products. These are the first search results a customer sees after searching on a platform like Amazon or Bol. The business model is much like Google Search, where the first search results are often sponsored too. But much larger amounts are paid per click for retail media campaigns than for Google Search campaigns. Lammers says: “Google knows what you are searching for, but retailers know what you want to buy.”

Retail media ads sit low in the marketing funnel compared with other campaigns. The funnel starts broad at the top with ‘awareness’ and ends at the bottom with the purchase of a product or service. Lower funnel placements command relatively high prices because the chance of conversion (sale) is greater when you advertise close to the point of sale. Retailers can add further personalisation based on their customer data, which makes the ads even more effective and therefore more valuable.

The cost side also looks favourable for retailers, since the infrastructure already exists in the form of website, app and store space. On top of that, they can use retail media channels for their own advertising messages. High revenue and low costs mean high margins. According to an [estimate by Boston Consulting Group](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2022/how-media-is-shaping-retail), online could generate a gross margin of 70-90%. Enough reason for Bol to make its advertising arm a [strategic priority](https://www.emerce.nl/nieuws/bolcom-advertising-strategische-prioriteit), alongside e-commerce and logistics services. Retail chains too are piling into this lucrative side income.

# Companies risk losing European customers due to cyber law

[Report](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/rapporten/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

The Netherlands is behind on implementing the European NIS2 directive, which will take effect in October 2024. Dutch companies cannot sit back. They may now face critical questions from customers in Germany and Belgium, where the legislative process is on schedule. NIS2 requires companies to make sure that the cyber security of all their suppliers and customers is in order.

NIS2 succeeds the earlier Network and Information Systems directive (NIS). Although it is a European law, countries are moving at different speeds. Germany and Belgium are almost ready for national implementation, but the Dutch government has indicated it will most likely miss the 17 October deadline. The consultation, where market participants can give feedback on the draft laws, has been postponed several times. This puts the Netherlands behind its largest trading partners; Germany is finalising the legal texts, and Belgium will soon present its legal texts for approval.

## No postponement

This does not mean a postponement for Dutch companies. It is unwise to delay the time-consuming preparations. Customers elsewhere in Europe will also require their Dutch suppliers to be NIS2-compliant. If they cannot guarantee this, these suppliers risk losing their European customers. Large sums are at stake. According to Statistics Netherlands ([CBS](https://opendata.cbs.nl/statline/#/CBS/nl/dataset/85428ned/line?dl=A21FA)), in 2023 the Netherlands exported around € 500 billion of goods within the European Union, of which € 241 billion went to Germany and Belgium.

Germany is the Netherlands’ most important export destination, with a total value of [€ 165.3 billion](https://opendata.cbs.nl/statline/#/CBS/nl/dataset/85428ned/line?dl=A21FA) in 2023. Goods exports to Belgium amounted to € 75.9 billion. These are mainly chemical products and industrial (semi-)manufactures, machinery, equipment and means of transport, and agricultural and food products.

## Able to demonstrate cyber resilience

Given Germany’s culture of formality and strict rule compliance, supply chains are likely to be scrutinised closely — regardless of whether the NIS2 laws are already in force in the Netherlands. To succeed in Germany, Dutch companies should not only accelerate their NIS2 preparations. They should also keep an open and effective communication line about their compliance efforts. “Make sure you can at least demonstrate, for example with a certificate, that you are investing in your organisation’s resilience,” says Daan Hoogendijk, programme director of Samen Digitaal Veilig.

CEO Maarten Roerink of cybersecurity service provider MMOX confirms this from his own experience. “We see that companies are looking for a stamp to show their partners that they are compliant. An ISO 27001 or Cyber Essentials certification helps with this. Some entrepreneurs also get certified to gain a competitive advantage.”

Because such a certification process is demanding, industry organisations, specialists and companies have developed a new quality mark specifically for small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs). The so-called [NIS2 Quality Mark](https://www.samendigitaalveilig.nl/nis2-quality-mark/) is available via the Samen Digitaal Veilig platform and comes in different levels. Smaller organisations can thus demonstrably boost their cyber resilience without having to commit to a heavy ISO certification by default.

## Measures to increase cyber security

Entrepreneurs should get their cyber risks in order. You can do this in three relatively simple steps. The first is to carry out a risk analysis. Map which matters are critical to keep the company operating and services running. The second step is to take measures, such as encouraging safe behaviour among employees, recording who owns specific data, and discussing cyber risks with partners and suppliers. Finally, it is essential to develop procedures to detect and handle cyber incidents.

# Privacy protection puts online advertising at a turning point

[Report](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/rapporten/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

[Mario Bersem](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/mario-bersem/index.html)

Restrictions on the use of privacy-sensitive information are turning the online advertising market upside down. Advertisers, publishers and agencies are looking for alternatives to today’s personalisation practice.

## Download the full report

(Can’t see a download or unable to download the report? Then upgrade your browser to the most recent version.)

- [Privacy protection puts the online advertising market at a turning point (December 2022)

  2 MB](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/media/bescherming-privacy-zet-markt-voor-online-adverteren-op-keerpunt_tcm16-179811.pdf)

Based on a survey of 544 industry respondents and in-depth interviews with experts, ABN AMRO sets out a vision for the future of online advertising. Advertisers, publishers and agencies are exploring alternatives to the current personalisation practice, such as contextual advertising, generating their own data and more collaboration on data. Professionals in the online advertising chain also think the big tech players will come up with new solutions and so manage to retain their power.

## Advertising practice under pressure

Person-specific – also known as personalised – ads have become the norm, at least online; by some estimates, a form of personalisation is used for 80 per cent of online ads.

But this personalisation practice is now under heavy pressure. Europe is trying, with new laws and regulations, to curb the personalisation of ads and even ban it for minors. The new rules, mainly set out in the Digital Services Act (DSA) which took effect on 16 November 2022, are driven by the desire to protect users’ privacy, especially because of the unbridled trade in personal data that lies behind personalised ads. In the words of MEP Paul Tang: “The time when personal data are freely collected and traded for advertising purposes is coming to an end.”

At the same time, the big tech companies also seem to have heard their users’ call for privacy. On Apple devices, users can now choose to switch off tracking, and Alphabet has announced that in 2024 it will stop supporting third-party cookies in Google Chrome, i.e. by far the world’s most used web browser will stop supporting the most used tracking method. Regulation and market forces are therefore pointing in the same direction: an online advertising market that is less dependent on privacy-sensitive data and where personal data are collected with the conscious and explicit consent of the end user.

## ABN AMRO’s vision for the future

ABN AMRO research shows that as many as one third of the professionals surveyed who work at advertisers and publishers consider their organisation dependent on personalised online ads. Contextual advertising, where only the content of a web page or, for example, the time of day determines the specific ad, can offer them a solution, as various examples show. As an alternative, advertisers and publishers can develop a data strategy – separately or possibly together – that relies on personal profiles created with informed consent and that the person in question can also view and adjust. In other words: conscious and explicit consent that can also be withdrawn. A majority of respondents indicate they do not yet have enough of their own data to offer personalised ads.

An in-house data strategy is not expected to be feasible for all advertisers and publishers, due to the costs, compliance risks and required scale involved. This is one reason why the advertising giants, as now, will continue to offer solutions to enable personalised advertising, although these will be based on less privacy-sensitive data and will no longer target minors, at least in Europe.

The online advertising market is at a turning point. Because of increasing restrictions on collecting and using personal data, publishers, advertisers and agencies are forced to rethink their strategy. At the same time, the major technology companies will do everything they can to retain their power. In this report, ABN AMRO sets out a vision for the future based on a survey conducted with PanelWizard and in-depth interviews with experts from the online advertising industry.

## The online advertising market

In 2021, people spent on average about [seven hours](https://datareportal.com/reports/digital-2022-october-global-statshot "DIGITAL 2022: OCTOBER GLOBAL STATSHOT REPORT") a day in online channels. Advertisers therefore spend billions a year to show their message to website visitors and app users. This market is growing fast; in 2021 this amount in Europe had risen to [92 billion euros](https://iabeurope.eu/wp-content/uploads/2022/06/adex-2021-advertising-report.pdf "DIGITAL ADVERTISING IN EUROPE") – an increase of 30.5 per cent compared with the previous year.

In the Netherlands, digital ad spend exceeded advertising spend in traditional media for the first time in 2016 and by now the digital ad spend is already more than twice as large. For the owners of websites, apps and other content – also known as ‘publishers’ – the internet offers opportunities to monetise their ad space. For example, almost half of the largest websites use a so-called ad network. This is a technological solution that collects online ad space from publishers and offers it to advertisers.

Besides advertisers and publishers, there are numerous intermediaries and other service providers active in the online advertising market. Advertisers are supported by media, marketing and advertising agencies, which help determine the most effective channels for online ad campaigns and often also play a role in the associated purchase of ad space. There are also numerous so-called AdTech players, which use technological solutions to facilitate the buying and selling of online ad space.

## Personalised advertising as standard practice

This process is in most cases fully automated and works by means of an auction – also called Real Time Bidding (RTB). When someone visits a specific part of a website or app, a technological solution on the advertiser’s side determines whether to bid for the attention of the person in question. This is based on predefined criteria, which often relate to the individual – for example ‘woman’, ‘living in South Holland’, ‘interested in pregnancy and babies’. Large amounts of data on internet users are used to determine the best-fitting ad for each individual. Such data often come from various sources: from visited websites and downloaded apps to search terms used and videos watched.

Professionals working in the online advertising chain are generally positive about this practice, according to a survey by ABN AMRO in collaboration with PanelWizard. Of the 544 respondents, two thirds (67 per cent) are employed by an advertiser; the rest work at a publisher (19 per cent) or agency (13 per cent).

More than half of advertisers and a large majority of agencies indicate that personalised ads are an effective way to reach their target audience. Two thirds of publishers believe it is an effective way to monetise their ad space. Dependence on personalised ads appears substantial on both sides; about one third of advertisers (31 per cent) and publishers (34 per cent), and even forty per cent of agencies say they are dependent on this form of targeting.

“Personalisation is important,” says Rogier Hosman, Chief Marketing Officer at digital agency Youwe. The company helps businesses develop and implement a digital strategy, including in online marketing. “Companies have a whole range of segments and customer types, all with different needs. So advertisers need to serve the right story to the right person.” According to the marketing boss, personalisation is also in the interest of the person who sees the ad. “As a consumer, it is pleasant to receive a relevant message and not have to wade through all kinds of nonsensical information.”

## Personalisation under fire

However, the current online advertising market focused on personalisation is changing for several reasons in favour of a – possibly – more privacy-friendly model. New laws and regulations play a decisive role here. For example, the European Digital Services Act (DSA) prohibits ads based on sensitive data such as sexual orientation and political preference. No personalised ads may be shown to minors at all. For adult internet users, it becomes easier to say “no” to tracking. The sister law, the Digital Markets Act (DMA), also raises various barriers.

Since most privacy-restricting legislation is still recent, the effects are only becoming clear over time. For example, according to civil rights groups and privacy watchdogs, it is questionable whether the RTB system complies with the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) of 2018. Consent by the internet user must, among other things, be informed, which means that in advance it is clear who will process the data, for what purpose and in what way. Due to the complexity of the auction system and the large number of parties with whom personal data are shared, informed consent in the context of current online ad auctions is, according to critics, not feasible in advance.

Legislation is seen as the main growth barrier for the market for personalised ads, the survey shows. Growing consumer awareness of their privacy and rights is, according to respondents, the second most important barrier. That internet users do indeed make use of the option to refuse personalised ads if that option is accessible enough was experienced by Nederlandse Publieke Omroep (NPO). When the broadcaster introduced a new type of pop-up that made both refusal of and consent to personalised ads possible with one click, nine out of ten website visitors did not agree. iPhone users have also overwhelmingly chosen not to be tracked since this became possible; 75 per cent of tracking requests by apps were answered with a “no” in early 2022.

## Consumers demand privacy

A related development at the expense of personalised advertising is the fact that the third-party cookie, the dominant technology for tracking internet users, will soon no longer be supported by the largest web browsers. After Safari – found on Apple devices – and Firefox, Google Chrome will also stop supporting this tracking vehicle from 2024. Although around eighty per cent of online ads are currently personalised based on information collected via third-party cookies, the professionals surveyed do not seem alarmed. A small minority indicate they are not well prepared for a future without third-party cookies.

This interplay between binding laws and explicit consumer preferences may lead to a larger number of refusals to use personal data for advertising purposes. Combined with the disappearance of the third-party cookie, it becomes more difficult to create a user profile based on previously visited websites and behaviour on those websites. In the end, less data will be available to tell an advertiser or intermediary what kind of visitor they are dealing with. A more privacy-friendly advertising model therefore not only fits the spirit of the times, it is also the only option.

## The future of online advertising

Changing social standards and associated laws and regulations are putting various principles of the online advertising market under pressure. Full transparency and control over the use of personal data will become the new standard, and contextual advertising will gain – or rather regain – ground due to new restrictions, given its history in traditional media.

## The unbroken power of big tech

Big tech remains dominant, according to the consensus among respondents. A large majority (77 per cent) expect that the share of ad spend flowing to these parties will be the same or higher in five years’ time (currently the share is more than half, see box D on page 21). Google has a solid position due to its activities across the entire AdTech chain (see box A). The channels of both Google and Meta are also particularly attractive to advertisers due to their extensive data collection. For example, Google knows perfectly what someone’s interests are based on their search behaviour, and social medium Instagram has insight into users through likes, posts and followed accounts. Based on such data, targeted ads can be shown.

Most other websites, unlike such walled gardens, are not autonomously able to collect so many insights about their users. That is why personalised ads at smaller publishers are often created based on third-party data – including browsing behaviour on previously visited websites. The use of such tracking data will soon be made more difficult by the DSA. “Truly personalised advertising will be confined within the walled gardens,” expects Havik of DPG Media.

## Small publishers under pressure

Google will continue to play an important role for advertisers and publishers, expects Havik of DPG Media. This mainly concerns small publishers, whose potential reach and the amount of available visitor data are both insufficient to interest advertisers one-to-one. That is why small publishers rely heavily on platforms that bring together supply of and demand for ad space. Google is the market leader in this.

According to Havik, smaller publishers must accept that dependence, or revert to the traditional subscription model. “They can choose to consider their ad revenue lost and derive their income from the consumer market. Through a paywall they can then have visitors pay for access to articles.” JustPremium co-founder Visser believes the earning potential from advertising is limited anyway for websites with relatively few visitors. “If you don’t have serious scale, you can’t earn serious money with online ads,” he says. “People no longer browse endlessly, but go purposefully to a website where they know exactly what they can get. For example, reading news or buying a product.”

## Transparency as a precondition

In addition to the power of large technology companies, the will of the consumer also determines which way the online advertising market ultimately goes. Their privacy awareness will likely be further strengthened in the coming years by stricter enforcement of the European privacy law GDPR and the requirements of the Digital Services Act. The latter requires clear communication about data use for online ads and makes it easier to say “no” to tracking. Both requirements put the power – or at least part of it – back in the hands of the internet user.

Almost one third of respondents (30 per cent) expect to see an increased willingness among consumers in five years’ time to share their data for advertising purposes; a slightly higher percentage (35 per cent) expect a decrease. Transparency is in any case essential to be able to continue offering personalised ads in the future, thinks Hosman of Youwe Digital Agency. “In a few years, internet users will no longer accept that they have to read somewhere in a sub-sub-agreement how their data are used exactly,” he says. “We should not underestimate the power and will of consumers. Otherwise you will see the tide turn.”

DPG Media is developing an online environment where every reader can change their user settings. There people will soon also be able to indicate how their data may be used for advertising purposes, says Havik. “For everything we do in the field of advertising, the rule is: you must be able to explain it to your mother. And she must also agree with it.”

## A key role for creativity

Although personal data have in recent years become the main fuel in the online advertising world, creativity also has a clear role. An ad must also be visually appealing, Visser notes. “A large part of a campaign’s success can be traced back to the creative,” he says. The entrepreneur refers to the combination of image and text of an ad, an area where advertising agencies have proven their added value for many years. “Data are much less important than was long thought.” Kroontje of Pangaea Digital Agency agrees that reduced availability of personal data does not necessarily undermine an ad strategy. “Most of the result comes from traditional marketing. That starts with a good understanding of your target audience.”

More than sixty per cent of respondents believe that the importance of creativity will have somewhat or even strongly increased in five years’ time. This applies even more to agencies: as many as 75 per cent expect an increase. It seems they are most clearly preparing for a future in which the boundless availability of personal data is not a given.

## A mix of strategies

The market is not moving towards one specific strategy, as the survey on solutions already showed. This is also reflected in the respondents’ vision for the future, who can imagine a growing role for both contextual and personalised advertising. More than half expect a higher degree of personalisation of online ads in five years’ time, despite legal restrictions. This matches the vision of the future held by Havik of DPG Media: “The principle of value exchange – in this case good content in return for data – will continue to exist on websites that know their own visitors well.” Visser takes the same view. “Within walled gardens, there will still be endless personalisation in future. But,” he qualifies, “outside them, contextual advertising will gain ground, or personalisation will take place at a less granular level – such as with Google Topics.”

The future is in any case not black and white, Kroontje of Pangaea also thinks. The entrepreneur expects that information about individuals will continue to play a role in the context of online advertising. “I believe there will be a balance. If you know nothing about someone, then you are sailing blind. You actually go back to TV advertising in terms of the level of personalisation. I don’t expect that to happen.” Kroontje stresses that personalisation of ads does not necessarily equal online tracking. “If you can indicate your own preferences and interests, many users will not mind. For example, I don’t like football but I do like golf. If I can tick that I only want to see ads about golf, then that’s only good.”

According to Hosman of Youwe Digital Agency, it is important for marketers to keep an open mind at least. “As a marketing team, you should not become addicted to one solution. Current developments in the market are leading to new solutions, each of which is worth considering.”

# Out-of-home advertising flourishes in the digital era

[Analysis](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/analyses/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Mario Bersem](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/mario-bersem/index.html)

Digital out-of-home advertising is making remarkable headway. New screens are appearing in shopping centres, bus shelters and railway stations. Analogue out-of-home also remains popular thanks to mass reach. ABN AMRO expects total out-of-home revenues to grow by 5 per cent in 2025 and 6 per cent in 2026. Up to 2030, out-of-home revenues are expected to grow by an average of 5 per cent a year and overtake radio as an advertising channel.

The Netherlands has 83,000 out-of-home objects, such as bus shelters, motorway masts, transformer houses, scaffold wraps, blank walls, billboards, information pillars and lamp posts. Even buses, trams and ferries are used as advertising platforms. The number of objects is growing due to the addition of new digital screens. At the same time, revenue per object is rising as existing objects are digitised.

The most common size is the abri format (1,185 mm x 1,750 mm, around 2 m²), accounting for 65 per cent of all advertising carriers, found in bus shelters and information pillars. The A0 size (841 mm x 1,189 mm) accounts for 32 per cent and can be found on three-sided boards and electricity boxes. Larger objects are rare and vary in size; only 1,300 objects are larger than the abri format. These are in high-reach locations, such as the LED facade of the Ziggodome in Amsterdam (23 metres high and 400 metres long) or the NEO screen at Hoog Catharijne in Utrecht (193 m²).

## Digital out-of-home is booming and offers new possibilities

In three years, digital out-of-home revenue has more than doubled to 172 million euros in 2024, according to VIA/Deloitte’s Digital Ad Spend 2024 report. In 2025, revenue will grow by 4 per cent to around 179 million euros, ABN AMRO expects. That is 56 per cent of total out-of-home revenue in 2025, which is expected to be 317 million euros. This is striking, as only 17 per cent of all advertising objects are digital; the remaining 83 per cent are analogue. Digital screens generate more revenue than analogue ones because they are on average larger, in the busiest locations and attract more attention.

Digital screens attract more attention thanks to moving images and rotating ads. They are also larger and in busy spots in big cities and along busy roads, where many passers-by ensure high reach. Advertising objects in these locations were digitised first to recoup the high investment costs. Analogue objects are digitised when reach is large enough to justify the costs. The new BRO Next study is the new standard for measuring reach.

Most new digital out-of-home screens are indoors, in shop windows. As long as the screens are visible from the street or public space, they count towards the out-of-home inventory. This phenomenon is part of the [rise of retail media](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/advertenties-lucratieve-bijverdienste-voor-winkelketens.html), where grocers and other retailers are turning to advertising as a lucrative side business. For example, HEMA has almost 330 digital screens with a range of campaigns that generate money in the evenings when the shops are closed. Global, a large out-of-home operator, manages ad sales for HEMA.

For advertisers, digital screens offer more flexibility. Campaigns can be tailored to conditions, such as advertising ice cream on warm days. Advertisers can also better reach specific target groups, such as young people who see ads for matcha tea at a railway station on their way to school. Around 30 per cent of digital ad space is placed automatically using algorithms that take into account factors such as time of day, location and weather. This share is rising and points to more efficient and flexible use of digital ad space.

## Digital advertising dominates the media landscape

Digital ads, especially those via web pages, social media, streaming services and the digital screens mentioned, dominate the Dutch media landscape and accounted for 4.1 billion euros in 2024 — almost three-quarters of all ad spend. Big tech companies, with a market share of 80 per cent (3.5 billion euros), dominate the digital ad market. The digital transformation of the media is not yet complete, so the share of digital ads is still rising.

While traditional mass media such as television, radio and newspapers are losing reach, out-of-home — also known as ‘out of home’ — is gaining importance. Johan Smit, Director of PMA, the industry association for media agencies, explains: “Out of home is actually the only mass medium left and is thereby taking over the leading role of linear TV.”

Digital out-of-home stands out from other digital media, such as online ads, because it is usually not perceived as intrusive. Online, ad blockers can be used and some people experience ad fatigue. “Out-of-home is, on the one hand, not intrusive and, on the other, impossible to miss,” says Sita Bakker, Director of OUTREACH, the industry association for out-of-home operators. “It does not interrupt people during their activities, which contributes to a positive experience,” Bakker adds.

## Analogue out-of-home also remains strong in the digital era

Analogue out-of-home remains relevant and is even growing, with a projected 6 per cent rise in revenue in 2025. That puts analogue out-of-home revenue at around 138 million euros, ABN AMRO expects. Although digitisation slightly reduces the number of analogue objects, traditional forms such as paper posters and static billboards remain popular with advertisers because of mass reach. New measurement methods from the BRO Next study also play a role in this (re)appraisal, as advertisers can increasingly estimate their reach precisely. As the “only mass medium left”, out-of-home offers an alternative to channels such as television that are seen as more expensive.

Out-of-home remains untouched by the online competition affecting other media. When travelling, for example to school or work, people come into contact with out-of-home. Bus shelters, motorway masts, billboards and A0 frames reach 80 per cent of the Dutch population each week. By comparison, linear television reaches 80 per cent of the Dutch population in a month.

About 5 per cent of all ad spend goes to out-of-home. This share varies between advertisers: McDonald’s spends as much as 20 per cent of its budget on out-of-home, and WPP Media, the largest media agency, spends about 10 per cent on out-of-home on behalf of advertisers. These examples show further growth potential for out-of-home, which is also evident from experience in other countries. In a positive scenario, in which current trends continue strongly, the out-of-home share could rise to 7.5 per cent and revenues could reach 600 million euros in 2030. In the base case, however, the out-of-home share remains stable at around the current 5 per cent.

## Municipalities set out-of-home policy

Out-of-home targets passers-by, on foot or by bike, in cars or on public transport. Municipalities apply strict rules on where and how advertising is allowed, that is, in which size and, for example, the brightness of digital screens. Some municipalities levy a municipal advertising tax. Because municipalities are responsible for the design of public space, rules vary widely: the Netherlands has 342 municipalities, each with its own policy on out-of-home. For instance, the Municipality of The Hague has banned ‘fossil advertising’. Through a general municipal by-law (APV), The Hague bans ads for air travel, petrol cars and fossil energy contracts. In Haarlem, a tender is under way in which advertising for meat and fish is not permitted.

Municipalities award multi-year concessions for out-of-home through public tenders. In return, they receive a financial contribution, often combined with payments in kind such as street furniture or media space for their own campaigns and charities. JCDecaux is an example of an operator that invests in bus shelters and maintains them in return for advertising exploitation. Notably, JCDecaux has more staff working in the technical department than in ad sales. A [PWC study](https://www.outsmart.org.uk/resources/ooh-supporting-uk-society) in the United Kingdom shows that 46 per cent of out-of-home ad revenue flows back to municipalities. Applied to the Dutch market, this amounts to around 146 million euros in 2025.

Out-of-home largely depends on municipal policy and concessions, which vary widely between the 342 Dutch municipalities. While some municipalities impose specific restrictions, such as the fossil ad ban in The Hague, there is no national guideline. This leads to variation in rules and uncertainty for operators. Central rules on tobacco, alcohol and gambling ads show that a uniform approach can offer benefits. Without such guidelines, individual municipalities can impose stricter restrictions, which is a risk for the out-of-home sector.

The Dutch out-of-home market is relatively fragmented, with 52 operators, compared with neighbouring countries such as Belgium and Germany, where only two large players are active alongside a few specialists with their own niche. The acquisition of the Dutch arm of Clear Channel by Bauer Media Group in April 2025 is an example of consolidation that will continue. Internationally, the Netherlands still lags behind countries such as the United States and China, especially in digital out-of-home and retail media. Based on international trends and the current market structure, ABN AMRO expects further consolidation and digital growth.

## Rise of the abri

In 1964, Jean Claude Decaux introduced his first bus shelters in Lyon. In return for funding, installation and maintenance, his company, JCDecaux, obtained the rights to exploit advertising in these ‘abribus’ (French for bus shelter). This public-private partnership model spread worldwide, and the term ‘abri’ became synonymous with a widely used ad format: 1,185 mm x 1,750 mm, around 2 m². Advertising in abri format accounts for 65 per cent of all out-of-home in the Netherlands and can be found in bus shelters across the country, stations, shopping centres and along busy streets.

# Cloud power will remain outside Europe for now

[Analysis](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/analyses/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

The European cloud market is dominated by US technology companies. Local players barely rise above a few per cent market share. Yet awareness is growing that Europe must gain control over crucial digital services itself.

A majority of European governments and companies run on cloud infrastructure of US origin: Amazon Web Services, Microsoft Azure and Google Cloud. Together these parties control about 70 to 80 per cent of the European [cloud market](https://sciencebusiness.net/news/ict/new-franco-german-cloud-computing-project-set-standards-and-challenge-us-market-leaders#:~:text=However%2C%20with%20almost%2070%20per,for%20Cyber%20Studies%20%26%20Research). The rest is fragmented across dozens of smaller providers, including European parties such as SAP, Deutsche Telekom, OVHcloud and Orange. No European player achieves more than a few per cent market share. SAP and Deutsche Telekom top the European ranking with only [2 per cent of the market](https://www.srgresearch.com/articles/european-cloud-providers-continue-to-grow-but-still-lose-market-share#:~:text=been%20Amazon%2C%20Microsoft%20and%20Google,list%20of%20national%20and%20regional) each.

## Applying pressure via IT

The Dutch government also appears to be highly dependent on the cloud services of the US tech giants. Almost half (44 per cent) of the cloud services the government uses fall into the ‘public cloud’ category and therefore rely on these parties’ infrastructure. A risk assessment has not been carried out in many cases, according to a recent report by the Algemene Rekenkamer with the ominous subtitle Gathering dark clouds. According to Simon Besteman of the industry association Dutch Cloud Community, the risks are substantial. “If your data are in a data centre of a cloud provider with a US UBO, then the US government can access them. It does not make a blind bit of difference if the data centre is in Europe.”

The industry association warns that technological dependence puts countries and companies in a tight spot, especially if geopolitical relations worsen. For example, Amsterdam Trade Bank (ATB), a subsidiary of the Russian Alfa Bank, was cut off from IT services by Amazon and Microsoft after the invasion of Ukraine, with major disruption as a result: employees lost access to applications and email, and digital payments came to a standstill. It feeds fears of a scenario in which US tech companies stop providing services to Europe as political pressure mounts. “If the United States want to put pressure on us, they can do it via IT,” says Besteman.

## European providers losing market share

The US clouds have grown steadily in recent years, even faster than local providers. Synergy Research calculated that between 2017 and 2022 the European cloud market became five times larger, but the combined market share of European providers fell from 27 per cent to 13 per cent. Amazon, Microsoft and Google invest billions every quarter in new European data centres.

European providers are therefore retreating to niches. They serve specific local needs or act as partners of the big three in hybrid set-ups. For example, the French OVHcloud provides cloud services that comply with strict European regulation. In some cases organisations combine these services with those of Amazon Web Services or Microsoft Azure. They benefit from the scale and functionality of US platforms, while sensitive data stay within Europe and fall under European rules.

The dominance of US cloud providers also extends to the backbone of artificial intelligence: the advanced servers on which AI models are trained and run. The United States accounts for about [70 per cent](https://geopolitique.eu/en/2025/02/10/financing-infrastructure-for-a-competitive-european-ai/#:~:text=A%20risk%20of%20strategic%20dependence,higher%20than%20in%20the%20US.) of this global AI compute capacity, of which the vast majority – 80 per cent – is in the hands of the hyperscalers. Europe has to make do with only 4 per cent of global capacity.

## Government cloud under national control

Yet things are moving. On several fronts awareness is growing within Europe that technological autonomy is only achievable with robust investment, smart regulation and local alternatives. “Within the Dutch government, the question is no longer whether we should reduce dependence on US cloud companies but how,” says Besteman of the Dutch Cloud Community. The House of Representatives recently passed a motion calling on the government to launch a tender for a “government cloud under full Dutch control”.

Besteman does not expect such a government cloud to appear overnight, but such an intention can set the local cloud market in motion. “If you want European cloud providers to invest further in their services, there must first be a buying signal.” Similar discussions are now taking place in the business community, Besteman notes. “There they are also considering how secure their data actually are, and what a plan B could be, an exit strategy.”

## Network of providers

From Europe, the focus is not on a single alternative cloud solution that can match Microsoft Azure or Amazon Web Services in scale, but on a network of providers between which data and workloads can be moved and distributed easily. ECOFED is an example of such a ‘federated cloud’. The Dutch project is developing a solution that decouples software from the physical cloud infrastructure, which gives users more freedom when choosing and combining cloud services.

The Dutch LeaseWeb is also making itself heard. In March the cloud provider presented the first results of its contribution to the IPCEI-CIS project — the same initiative that includes ECOFED. The company is building a cloud platform designed specifically to comply with European laws and regulations. The platform forms the heart of LeaseWeb’s so-called European Cloud Campus — a broader investment in sovereign cloud technology, local data centres and cooperation with knowledge institutions.

Full sovereignty seems unrealistic, but awareness is growing that Europe must get a firmer grip on its digital infrastructure. Exit strategies and alternative scenarios are gaining a more prominent place on the agenda in government and business. This gives local cloud companies room to stand out, with security and compliance as their trump card.

# Cyberattacks more dangerous due to artificial intelligence

[Report](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/rapporten/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

More than half of companies view innovations in artificial intelligence (AI) as a threat to the organisation’s cyber security. Last year it was not even a quarter. Risk perception is strongest among the largest companies, according to a survey by ABN AMRO and research agency MWM2 among 895 organisations.

AI seems to increase offensive power mainly in the area of social engineering. This manipulates people into sharing confidential information or taking another harmful action. AI makes this manipulation even sneakier and more convincing. Generative AI, for example, makes it very easy to create [phishing](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/cybersecurity/wat-is-phishing.html) emails. This form of AI can write texts on the basis of a few instructions — grammatically correct, without spelling mistakes and in a language of choice. According to IBM X-Force, the development time for a phishing email drops from [sixteen hours](https://securityintelligence.com/x-force/ai-vs-human-deceit-unravelling-new-age-phishing-tactics/) to five minutes.

Interactive conversations can also now be outsourced to AI models. An automated chat can, for example, extract login details or set payments in motion. The use of deepfake technology — which creates image or audio material that is indistinguishable from the real thing — can make victims believe they are dealing with someone else.

[Listen to the podcast as well: Generative AI: The difference between real and fake](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/in-gesprek/podcast/de-innovatiegolf/generatieve-ai-het-verschil-tussen-echt-en-nep.html)

## Convincing clone of the CEO

In Hong Kong, a finance employee at a multinational transferred more than [25 million dollars](https://edition.cnn.com/2024/02/04/asia/deepfake-cfo-scam-hong-kong-intl-hnk/index.html) to fraudsters after joining a video meeting with a deepfake version of the CFO and other colleagues. In October last year, Dutch online bank Bunq faced a similar attempt when an employee was invited to a video call with — as it later turned out — an AI clone of CEO Ali Niknam. In a [LinkedIn post](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7114874320627085312/) Niknam calls the clone “convincing”.

[Read also: Sharp rise in CEO fraud](/nl/zakelijk/insights/cybersecurity/phishing/sterke-toename-in-ceo-fraude.html)

According to the UK’s [National Cyber Security Centre](https://www.ncsc.gov.uk/report/impact-of-ai-on-cyber-threat#section_5) (NCSC), all types of attackers will benefit from these new social engineering possibilities, from advanced state hackers to less capable cybercriminals. Automatic scanning of company data for value will also become easier for all malicious actors, so the damage from data breaches is likely to grow in the coming years.

## AI makes capable attackers even stronger

Generative AI is also used to write malware. The British estimate that the already highly capable attackers will benefit most from this. State hackers who, for example, already have large datasets with malware at their disposal can use them to train AI models to create new malicious software that current security systems cannot detect.

The NCSC points out that developing malware still requires human expertise — something they say also applies to lateral movement, where intruders jump from one system to another. This means less professional hackers will not suddenly become exponentially more effective across the board thanks to AI. “It can provide a helping hand,” says Maarten Roerink, CEO of cybersecurity service provider MMOX. “Beginner hackers can reach malicious code more quickly.”

## Data breaches caused by chatbots

Companies that use smart chatbots based on generative AI risk employees sharing confidential information in these conversations. Samsung halted employees’ use of generative AI tools after it emerged that one of them had entered a piece of source code into ChatGPT in the hope the chatbot could find a solution for a bug in the code. Transcripts of internal meetings also ended up in the chats.

In this way, sensitive data can leak not only to the AI provider, but information may also be shared unintentionally with other users. In March 2023, several ChatGPT users reported that they could see [topics from other users’ chat histories](https://www.pcmag.com/news/openai-confirms-leak-of-chatgpt-conversation-histories) in their own account. In addition, conversations can be used again as training data to refine the AI model, and that training data does not always appear to be well locked down. At the end of last year, Google researchers revealed how they had coaxed competitor OpenAI’s ChatGPT into [disclosing](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.17035) training data. The risk of data breaches via chatbots is therefore real.

## AI as a weapon against cybercrime

AI can also be used to improve cyber security. “It is always the crooks who are first to embrace a particular technology, but it never takes long before the defence starts working with it too,” says Arwi van der Sluijs, Managing Director of cybersecurity service provider NFIR. AI can help to identify new patterns that indicate suspicious activity in a network. Previously, people identified such patterns by manually studying large amounts of data and doing desk research. Now this can be largely automated. “Stronger still: thanks to AI we can start to predict incidents.”

The current dynamic is increasingly starting to look like an arms race. In a world where AI-generated malware and phishing emails are a reality, security systems must in turn learn to recognise these new threats. AI can do that too. The underlying model must then have seen enough examples of this new generation of wrongdoers. Well-intentioned companies therefore first use generative AI to write malware and phishing emails, and then use these to train an effective recognition model.

# A cyberattack does not always wake an entrepreneur up

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

Last year, almost three-quarters of Dutch companies faced a cyberattack. Although the adverse effects of cyberattacks often make the news, many entrepreneurs only acknowledge the risks after they have suffered damage. The damage often extends beyond the company that was hacked. An overly optimistic risk assessment can put the entire chain at risk.

Read all facts and developments in the report ‘Ever more sophisticated cyberattacks’.

With new European legislation in the making, entrepreneurs must quickly get things in order together with their customers and suppliers.

Among large companies, with annual turnover of at least 25 million, as many as 86 per cent of businesses were attacked. ## Cybercriminals’ attack methods are changing

Meanwhile, cybercriminals’ attack methods are changing rapidly. Phishing is still a popular – and much-feared – way to gain entry to companies. Some 26 per cent have experienced ransomware, of which 19 percentage points last year. Data breaches are also a real risk. More than a quarter of companies have already lost confidential data; at 15 per cent this happened in the past twelve months.

More than half of companies see advances in artificial intelligence (AI) as a threat to their organisation’s cyber security. Last year this was less than a quarter. The risk perception is strongest at the largest companies, possibly fuelled by spectacular recent media reports. For example, a finance employee at a multinational in Hong Kong transferred more than [25 million dollars](https://edition.cnn.com/2024/02/04/asia/deepfake-cfo-scam-hong-kong-intl-hnk/index.html) to fraudsters after joining a video meeting with a deepfake version of the CFO and other colleagues. At the Dutch online bank Bunq, a similar attempt was made using an AI clone of CEO Ali Niknam.

## New NIS2 directive

To improve cyber resilience in Europe, a new directive will take effect in October 2024: NIS2, the successor to the earlier Network and Information Systems Directive (NIS). It compels organisations in seventeen sectors to critically examine, among other things, the cyber resilience of their suppliers and customers. Although the Netherlands is delayed in implementing the directive, domestic companies are already being confronted with critical questions from German and Belgian customers where national laws are being shaped on schedule.

Almost three-quarters (72 per cent) of large-company respondents already question their own customers, suppliers and partners “very regularly” or “regularly” about cyber security; 66 per cent say they also receive such questions from their chain partners. Among SME respondents these percentages are lower, at 52 and 37 per cent respectively. The extent to which concrete agreements are made with chain partners is also much higher at large companies than at SMEs.

Maarten Roerink, CEO of cybersecurity service provider MMOX, recognises the importance of an approach that goes far beyond the company walls. “When our customers get hacked, it very often happens via-via. Sometimes they are the target themselves; sometimes vulnerabilities in their networks are used to penetrate the networks and data of partners. The age-old question ‘what is there to get from my company?’ is no longer relevant.”

# Cyber threat prompts entrepreneurs to take action

[Analysis](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/analyses/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

The number of companies that have faced a cyberattack has risen sharply over the past year. Although entrepreneurs’ risk perception has stayed almost the same, research by ABN AMRO among 233 companies shows a growing willingness to take measures. That is positive, because cyber threat has become a real operational risk.

This is first of all due to a larger attack surface from far-reaching digitalisation, combined with the professionalisation of cybercriminals. The fact that geopolitical tensions are increasingly playing out in digital [attacks](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/cybersecurity/wat-is-een-ddos-aanval.html) gives extra cause for concern. For example, the war between Russia and Ukraine is also leading to a higher cyber risk for Dutch enterprises. “On top of the structural increase in cyberattacks, we saw a clear spike in the run-up to the escalation of the conflict. This was even before the general public heard in the news which way things would go in Ukraine,” says Bastiaan van Nunen, Managing Director at cybersecurity company MMOX.

The percentage of companies that have faced cybercrime, regardless of the war, has increased. While last year this was still 29 per cent of all respondents, this year it is 45 per cent. This is according to research by ABN AMRO in collaboration with research agency MWM2 among 233 business clients who are ultimately or jointly responsible for their company’s cybersecurity.

\* Significant increase (p<0.05)   
 Question: Has your organisation or the organisation you work for ever faced cybercrime?

Other sources confirm this picture. For example, the Dutch police registered 14,000 cases of cybercrime last year, an increase of almost one-third compared with 2020 and a tripling compared with 2019. At European level too, according to the cybersecurity agency [ENISA](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/publications/enisa-threat-landscape-2021), there has been an increase in cyberattacks over the past two years.

ABN AMRO’s research shows that large corporates in particular encounter cyberattacks remarkably often; 63 per cent of respondents in this category – defined as companies with annual turnover higher than ten million euros – have faced cybercrime at some point, 22 per cent of whom in the past year.

## Damage not limited to large companies

Still, smaller companies must also stay alert. They too are increasingly faced with cybercrime. According to the February 2022 survey, the percentage of self-employed without employees (sole traders) with experience of cyberattacks is 32 per cent, whereas in April 2021 this was only 13 per cent. A rise is also observed among small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs), although this is not statistically significant.

The trend is clear in any case, according to Van Nunen: not only large companies are victims. He says there is a clear strategy behind this. “A group of cybercriminals would rather attack a few hundred small companies with a smaller return and a lower chance of being caught than a handful of large companies that are already on high alert,” says Van Nunen. Sitting back is not an option, he adds: “It would be naïve to think that cyberattacks would not affect sole traders. People thought the same about SMEs a few years ago.” Trish McGill, a cybersecurity expert at Dutch IT service provider Ilionx, agrees: “In the end, everyone gets hacked at some point, it is only a matter of time.”

[Also read: Threat Intelligence: Essential tips for SMEs](/nl/zakelijk/insights/cybersecurity/beveiliging/threat-intelligence-essentiele-tips-voor-het-mkb.html)

## Interconnected digital landscape creates risks

When a company faces a cyberattack, this is often not an isolated event. Attacks on larger companies can have far-reaching consequences for smaller parties. This is certainly the case when large technology providers are targeted. This became clear once again in 2021, when hackers exploited a security flaw in Microsoft Exchange email software to gain access to at least 1,200 Dutch mail servers. Confidential information from all kinds of companies could be stolen in this way.

The risk has undeniably grown for all companies. “Malicious actors are making use of the possibilities that digitalisation brings more than ever,” says Van Nunen. This is a structural development that can be directly linked to the ever-growing attack surface. More and more companies are digitalising their operations, a development that accelerated during the coronavirus crisis. The many cloud services – often from a wide range of providers – and devices involved all form potential entry points for cybercriminals.

## Hackers are becoming more professional

At the same time, cybercriminals are becoming more professional. This can be seen in the way they make their trade-offs. Both experts indicate that there is often a ‘business case’ behind it. “More and more often, a cyberattack starts with thorough research to determine, for example, what data can be taken from a company. Their own accountant then maps out the potential value of the attack,” says McGill. Van Nunen recognises this principle: “When cybercriminals have a company in their sights for a [ransomware attack](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/cybersecurity/wat-is-ransomware.html), they will happily do the maths to determine the best amount to demand from a party. What does it cost per day if the operations of a company with eight million in turnover come to a standstill? What is the entrepreneur therefore willing to pay?”

Today’s hackers are not comparable to those of the past, says McGill. “In the past, hackers were just ‘script kiddies’ coding in their mother’s basement. The same kind of people can now channel their energy into making TikTok videos. The type of attacker active today is of a completely different order; these are professionals.” According to McGill, cybercriminals’ skills are developing in line with technological developments. “Phones and laptops are constantly evolving in terms of security. Where in the past a virus could easily shake a company to its foundations, nowadays much heavier artillery is needed. Attackers cannot lag behind; they have to keep innovating to be able to cause damage.”

In addition, hackers are not only organising themselves more and more, they are also increasingly working in organisations that are specifically dedicated to cybercrime. Such organisations offer all kinds of attacks to malicious actors as a ready-made package. Ransomware is also available through this route. The associated ‘Ransomware-as-a-Service’ companies have everything that a legally operating technology company has, from a research and development department to customer service that helps the purchasing party implement the software. The difference compared with regular technology companies is the so-called ‘victim service’, which guides hijacked enterprises in paying the ransom.

## Different motives for a cyberattack

Hackers’ motives vary. One is after direct financial gain and uses ransomware to demand large sums of money from the company whose system has been shut down, while another acts primarily from political motives. In the latter case, this may involve, for example, breaking into government systems to steal sensitive military information, or shutting down important local companies to force a political opponent to its knees. Others fall into the ‘ethical hacker’ category. They use their skills to discover vulnerabilities in systems and use this information to help raise the security level. Hackers sometimes work on behalf of the government, and at other times entirely on their own initiative.

The war between Russia and Ukraine means that the threat of politically motivated cyberattacks is increasing. According to Van Nunen of MMOX, the digital attack is one of the weapons in the arsenal: “Cyber warfare is part of modern warfare. It is used, for example, to destabilise a country or region. State actors have previously tried to shut down nuclear reactors in Iran through a cyberattack. It is simply one of the ways to impose your will on another country.”

## War creates threat, but no substantial damage yet

As far as is known, there have not yet been any direct Russian attacks on Dutch companies, for example in retaliation for European sanctions against Russia or European arms deliveries to Ukraine. The National Cyber Security Centre (NCSC) estimates the likelihood of this to be low, but [advises](https://www.ncsc.nl/onderwerpen/oekraine) organisations “to remain alert due to the unpredictability of the situation”.

Dutch enterprises have already become involved in the conflict in a more indirect way. In March it became known that several hundred routers of Dutch companies had been hacked to be incorporated into a ‘botnet’. This is a network of infected devices that are used to send large numbers of requests to third-party servers. In March, the malware ‘Cyclops Blink’ was used to create a botnet that was used to attack Ukrainian companies and governments. According to insiders, this malware was almost certainly developed by a group of hackers linked to a Russian intelligence service.

Although it will be an uncomfortable idea for entrepreneurs that their IT equipment is being used in a cyberwar, companies will not easily notice that they have been incorporated into a botnet. Their equipment may send a few requests per hour. This has no impact on their daily operations.

## Direct attacks elsewhere can also have an effect here

A greater risk is posed by so-called ‘spillover effects’. This is the damage that is initially inflicted only on the IT systems of companies or governments in the conflict area, but spreads to parties elsewhere. A well-known example is the ‘NotPetya’ malware, which in 2017 was spread via an update of a locally widely used accounting program. In this way, the systems of all kinds of companies in Ukraine were infected. The malware then spread worldwide via these companies. For example, several container terminals of shipping company Maersk in the port of Rotterdam were shut down by NotPetya and a large part of pharmaceutical company Merck’s operations came to a standstill. The infection cost both companies many millions in recovery costs and lost revenue.

Similar malware was also used in the run-up to the Russian invasion of Ukraine. These were so-called ‘wipers’, highly destructive software that can destroy all files on an infected computer and often even prevent it from starting up. Therefore, alertness is required for any company that has a branch in the conflict area, uses IT services hosted there, or has customers or suppliers there.

Another way in which the war leads to increased threat is the fact that cybercriminals are abusing the willingness to give help. Such groups are not parties to the war itself, but exploit the circumstances in, for example, ‘[phishing](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/cybersecurity/wat-is-phishing.html) campaigns’. For instance, fraudsters are active who send email, WhatsApp or text messages on behalf of Giro555, a partnership between Dutch aid organisations, asking for a donation.

## Risk perception lags behind the actual threat

Cyber threat has therefore increased significantly for several reasons. However, entrepreneurs’ risk perception is lagging behind. While last year 30 per cent of respondents saw cybercrime as a “high” or “very high” risk for their own organisation, at 33 per cent in 2022 this has not increased significantly.

Question: To what extent do you think cybercrime is a risk to your organisation? (1 = no risk at all; 5 = very high risk)

Van Nunen also sees that entrepreneurs’ risk perception is often still not in line with reality. According to him, however, cyberattacks are a risk that companies need to take into account. “Companies insure themselves against fire and theft, but less quickly against cybercrime. The risk of fire is 1 in 8,000, while the risk of encountering a cyberattack, or attempts to do so, is at least 1 in 8.”

The discrepancy between risk perception and the actual threat can, according to McGill, be partly explained by cultural factors. “The Netherlands is only a small player on the world stage. That shapes companies’ risk perception here. The fact that they too can sometimes fall victim to hackers outside their own borders is felt less strongly.” She resolutely rejects the concept of ‘local companies’: “The moment a company is connected to the internet, it is an international company. With all the associated risks and challenges.”

According to her, it does not help that the Netherlands has little legislation and regulation in the field of cybersecurity. “Here there are, for example, no laws that require you to report if you have been hit by a cyberattack.” McGill makes a comparison with the United Kingdom: “There, unlike in the Netherlands, there is a clear enforcement culture. The result is that there are also standards and government bodies that help companies very concretely to get their security in order. In the Netherlands they do not have that, only guidelines and soft recommendations.”

## Risk perception determines whether measures are taken

The minimal change in risk perception unfortunately also means a limited improvement in entrepreneurs’ digital resilience. Statistical analyses show that respondents with a higher risk perception (“high” or “very high risk”) take more measures than respondents who estimate the cyber risks for their organisation lower (“low” or “no risk at all”).

Question: In which area is your organisation currently taking measures regarding cybercrime?   
 Sample: sole traders (n=64) | SMEs (n=139) | large corporates (n=27; results for this group indicative in nature)

The top three most taken measures are entirely preventive. The most popular are technological measures such as antivirus software, firewalls and making backups; 79 per cent of respondents take these. Preventive measures aimed at human behaviour are used by 51 per cent. Large corporates in particular attach great importance to the human role; no fewer than 81 per cent of respondents in this category say they take preventive measures focused on human behaviour.

In third place is conducting a cyber scan that provides insight into the company’s vulnerabilities. Of the respondents, 25 per cent make use of this. Less popular is arranging support in case the company faces a cybercrime incident (18 per cent) or taking out insurance (12 per cent). Of the respondents, 11 per cent indicate that they take no measures at all. This concerns sole traders and SMEs only.

## Different causes for high risk perception

A higher risk perception ultimately leads to more barriers for cybercriminals, but in some cases it means that the damage has already been done. About one-third of entrepreneurs indicate that their risk assessment stems from a previous experience with a cyberattack or phishing emails. McGill also sees this in practice: “For some people, they only really recognise the risk when they have suffered significant damage from an attack.”

Respondents themselves cite other reasons as more decisive for taking action. About two-thirds of them attribute the high risk perception to their role within the company – often owner or director – and the associated insight into the risks. News reports about cyberattacks also prove to be very decisive. Among sole traders, as many as 63 per cent cite this as a trigger. The same reason is cited by 55 per cent of respondents from large corporates, and by 37 per cent of respondents from SMEs.

## Media reports about cyberattacks increase risk awareness

Cyber threat has been widely covered in the media over the past year. For example, it emerged last year that a ransomware attack had shut down various systems of electronics retailer Mediamarkt, and De Mandemakers Groep – known for kitchens, bathrooms and furniture – had also fallen victim to ransomware. An accident can literally be around the corner, as evidenced by the recent crisis around ‘Log4J’ which received a lot of media attention. This is positive, because initial access to systems is often gained via the human route. # Cyber measures increasingly focused on people

[Report](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/rapporten/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

Of the set of measures companies can take to increase their cyber resilience, measures aimed at human vulnerabilities are getting more attention. In addition, entrepreneurs at large companies in particular are inclined to allocate more budget for cybersecurity, mainly driven by the increased threat.

A survey by ABN AMRO and research firm MWM2 among 895 organisations shows that most respondents are taking measures against cybercrime. Among self-employed people without employees, however, this share is much lower (82 per cent) than among respondents from small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) and large companies (95 and 97 per cent respectively). Preventive measures addressing technological vulnerabilities are, in line with results from previous years, the most popular across all company sizes. Examples include installing virus scanners, encrypting data and applying firewalls.

## Keeping a finger on the pulse remains necessary

Even when such measures are in place, keeping a finger on the pulse remains necessary. Hackers managed to break in almost simultaneously at 22 Danish energy companies via a critical vulnerability in Zyxel firewalls, devices that are intended to keep malicious traffic out of the corporate network. If the intruders had not been discovered earlier, they could have gained control of part of Denmark’s vital infrastructure through this [co-ordinated cyberattack](https://www.dutchitchannel.nl/news/419675/data-buitgemaakt-bij-cyberaanval-op-britse-watermaatschappij).

Although it was possible to intervene relatively early in the attack process, several energy companies had clearly dropped the ball in their security. According to a report by the Danish cybersecurity centre for vital infrastructure, several companies had failed to install security updates for the firewalls. For example, they wrongly assumed their IT supplier would install the updates, or had deferred updates because the supplier would charge installation costs. Other companies simply did not know they had the Zyxel device in their network.

## Growing focus on human vulnerabilities

Human vulnerabilities are getting more attention; the share of companies taking measures in this area rose from 40 per cent in 2022 to 53 per cent last year. Examples include training to teach employees basic hygiene in cybersecurity, or simulating [phishing](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/cybersecurity/wat-is-phishing.html) attacks to increase alertness to such emails. “You should repeat such exercises monthly,” advises Arwi van der Sluijs, Managing Director of cybersecurity service provider NFIR. “Also link a test to them to monitor knowledge of cyber safety, and have HR keep track of the results.”

Arranging help in advance for when a cyber incident occurs is a less widespread measure, at 35 per cent of respondents. The same applies to taking out insurance against the damage (32 per cent).

## Mix of measures

Maarten Roerink of cybersecurity service provider MMOX underlines the importance of a mix of measures. “The cybersecurity market has traditionally been driven by IT and technological measures,” says the CEO. “But if you take a risk-driven approach, you soon notice that interventions at process level and in the human domain are also needed. Which business data are, for example, sensitive or valuable, and which employees have access to that data? The employees concerned should then receive additional training and security measures, or their access to a particular system should be withdrawn.”

A broad approach to getting cyber risks under control can be done in three relatively simple steps. The first is to carry out a risk analysis that maps out which matters are crucial to keeping the company operating and maintaining service delivery. The second step is to take measures, such as encouraging safe behaviour among employees, recording who owns certain data and discussing cyber risks with partners and suppliers. Finally, it is essential to develop procedures to detect and handle cyber incidents.

# Cybersecurity in the TMT sector

[Report](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/rapporten/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Amad Khan](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/amad-khan/index.html)

IT, media and telecom companies are essential for almost every organisation. Their software and infrastructure are the gateway to a broad audience. That central role in our digital landscape makes them vulnerable. Cybercriminals can cause major damage to them, their clients and society. This may be why there are more hacking and deception attempts at companies in TMT than in other sectors.

## Recent cyberattacks in the sector

In mid-2023 the notorious ransomware group LockBit [claimed](https://www.securityweek.com/tsmc-says-supplier-hacked-after-ransomware-group-claims-attack-on-chip-giant/) they had hacked chip giant Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC). According to the company itself, that was not correct. It said hardware supplier Kinmax had been hit, not TSMC. TSMC halted data exchanges with its supplier immediately after the incident.

In April 2023, hardware and software developer MSI reported it had fallen victim to a ransomware attack. [Private keys were stolen](https://www.ncsc.nl/actueel/nieuws/2023/mei/7/msi-ransomware-aanval), including Intel Boot Guard keys. These are used to digitally sign and verify motherboard firmware. In theory, criminals could use the stolen keys to run malicious firmware and gain deep access to systems. Even so, the National Cyber Security Centre considers the chance of abuse small.

At the end of 2022, hackers [stole the personal data of tens of thousands of Delta Fiber customers](https://www.telegraaf.nl/financieel/843971726/grote-roof-persoonsgegevens-bij-delta-fiber). Swift action kept the damage relatively limited. The company said it involved only names, addresses, email addresses, dates of birth, phone numbers and bank account numbers — no sensitive data such as passwords and credit card details. Even so, customers should be extra alert to possible email and phone fraud, such as [phishing](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/cybersecurity/phishing/index.html).

You can have your own cyber defences in good shape, yet still be embarrassed when hackers attack your supplier — TV providers like Ziggo and KPN have experienced this since March 2024. Hackers managed to [broadcast Russian propaganda videos on BabyTV](https://www.nu.nl/tech/6308299/tv-wereld-neemt-maatregelen-na-russische-propaganda-op-babytv.html). They used a jammer to break into Eutelsat’s satellite signal. Because this type of attack had never happened before, no one was prepared, and it took a while before the hack was even noticed.

## NIS2: new European legislation to improve cyber resilience

To improve cybersecurity in Europe, a new law will take effect in October 2024: NIS2, the successor to the Network and Information Security Directive (NIS). Organisations that are important or essential to society must comply with this directive to limit the impact of cyberattacks and disruptions. This also applies to companies in the chain of these vital organisations, such as clients, partners and suppliers.  
The intention is that European member states translate the directive into national legislation, but the Netherlands is running behind schedule. It is still wise to prepare your organisation for implementation now. The law will come anyway, and clients or suppliers in other European countries will follow the directive from October 2024.  
Within TMT, several companies fall under NIS2, including:

- Management of ICT services
- Digital infrastructure
- Digital service providers

Wondering whether your organisation falls under the NIS2 directive? Read our report ‘Cyberattack does not always wake entrepreneurs up’, or complete the National Cyber Security Centre’s [NIS2 Self-assessment](https://www.ncsc.nl/actueel/nieuws/2023/oktober/18/zelf-evaluatie-nis2-gelanceerd).

# Cybersecurity for small businesses is crucial to retain customers

[Report](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/rapporten/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

The European NIS2 law, the successor to the earlier Network and Information Systems Directive (NIS), forces organisations to take a critical look at the cyber resilience of suppliers and customers. This means smaller businesses, which usually do not fall directly under the law, will also face critical questions from the chain. This awareness has not yet fully sunk in.

The new law, which takes effect from October 2024, stipulates, among other things, that companies must also map cyber risks from their own supply chain, and that agreements on cybersecurity must be laid down contractually with these chain partners. According to CEO Maarten Roerink of cybersecurity service provider MMOX, this is no luxury. “If our customers are hacked, it very often happens indirectly. Sometimes they are the target themselves, and sometimes vulnerabilities in their networks are used to penetrate the networks and data of partners. The age-old question ‘what is there to get from my company?’ is no longer relevant.”

A survey by ABN AMRO and research agency MWM2 among 895 organisations shows that almost three-quarters (72 per cent) of respondents from large companies already question their own customers, suppliers and partners about this “very regularly” or “regularly”. Of them, 66 per cent say they also receive such questions from their chain partners. Among respondents from small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs), these percentages are lower, at 52 and 37 per cent respectively. The extent to which concrete agreements are made with chain partners also differs by company size. Of respondents from large companies, 75 per cent do this, compared with 56 per cent of SMEs.

## Customers will set requirements

Even so, the broad chain responsibility is a reality that smaller businesses must also take seriously. For example, a large supermarket chain has already made an urgent request to all its Dutch suppliers to explain their cybersecurity policy, says Daan Hoogendijk, programme director of Samen Digitaal Veilig. “The message was: either you show a certificate, or we will end the cooperation. Unfortunately, most of these food companies still have too little focus on cybersecurity.” According to Hoogendijk, this is typical of the attitude of most SME companies. They tend to wait and see, and other priorities block serious steps.

NIS2 comprises a set of rules primarily aimed at companies whose disruption can have a broad social impact. Broadly speaking, these are large and medium-sized companies with at least 50 employees or annual turnover and total assets of more than 10 million euros, and that are active in a sector of social importance. In total, this concerns 10,432 Dutch companies that must comply with the new rules, spread across seventeen sectors such as energy supply, digital infrastructure and government. The companies in scope are therefore expected to look critically at the cybersecurity of all their suppliers, including small ones.

# The future of television: sitting still is not an option

[Report](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/rapporten/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

Steven Peters

Television is changing at breakneck speed. For a long time, linear television used the internet to expand its reach. Television suffered little from the disruptive power of the internet, unlike newspapers and magazines. That is changing.

## Download the full report

(Do you not see a download or are you unable to download the report? Then upgrade your browser to the most recent version.)

- [The future of television (19 December 2017)

  560 KB](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/media/20171219-ABN-AMRO-update-tv_tcm16-70487.pdf)

John de Mol recently sounded the alarm about the inability of Dutch media companies to compete on the advertising market with, for example, Google and Facebook. The Dutch television sector also faces competition from Netflix. It took about a year, but the growth of streaming video now has a strong negative impact on traditional television. The number of minutes watched of linear television is falling. And last year, income from regular spot advertising fell. This is while the economy is in rude health. Falling advertising spending in good economic times? Something special must be going on here.

ABN AMRO is therefore investigating: what does the future of linear television look like? What are the opportunities for traditional television broadcasters in the new television world? One thing is certain: sitting still is not an option.

## Watching television

Trends in television have been pointing to major changes in viewing behaviour for several years. But until recently this was only partly visible in the figures. For the entire television industry, the number of minutes watched per day is a crucial figure. Because these almost directly determine advertising income. And the number of minutes watched per day has now been falling for three years in a row.

### Structural break in the trend

For years, the number of minutes watched for linear television hovered around three hours per day. In even years with major sporting events such as the FIFA World Cup or the Olympic Games, the number was slightly higher than in odd years. From 2015, however, the number of minutes watched began to fall. ABN AMRO estimates the number of linear minutes watched for 2017 at 150 minutes. This would mean a fall of 16% in three years. A structural break in the trend. Part of the explanation is that younger people in particular are spending more time on Facebook and other social media. YouTube also has a major impact on video consumption. But these trends have been going on for longer and do not explain the sudden fall from 2015.

### Paying for streaming

Another important new player entered the Dutch television market in September 2013: the paid American streaming service Netflix. Within a year, the company gained one million Dutch subscribers. By mid-2017, this number had grown to 2.35 million subscribers, according to research agency [Telecompaper](https://www.telecompaper.com/pressrelease/toename-gebruik-video-on-demand-vooral-via-netflix-2--1208460) . The number of minutes watched of streaming video-on-demand (vod) more than doubled between 2015 and 2017, from an average of 11 minutes per day to 23 minutes per day. People in their twenties spend 43 minutes watching and therefore almost twice as much time on streaming vod. One explanation is that they are doing this more often via smartphone, tablet, laptop or desktop, about 40% of the time.

### Regular spot advertising falls

The fall in the number of minutes watched is beginning to have a clear impact on income from spot advertising. The effectiveness of spot advertising drops sharply with fewer viewers, especially compared with online advertising, which is much more targeted at the individual consumer. In 2016, revenue from regular spot advertising already showed a small fall of 2% in an upswing of the economic cycle.

### Total income does rise

The positive news is that income from online spot advertising (+17%) and non-spot sponsorship (+15%) rose sharply last year. Although still limited in size, they offset the fall in regular spot advertising. Total income from television advertising therefore rose by 2.7% in 2016 to EUR 991 million. The question is how long this will continue. Time-shifted viewing and sponsorship are derivatives of the linear television offering. And if this offering comes under pressure – due to far fewer viewers – this could also start to affect online spot advertising and sponsorship.

### Contraction in 2017

There are very negative signals in the market. RTL Nederland reported a fall of 6.3% in income from spot advertising over the first nine months of 2017. This was partly offset by growth in online income from, for example, online video platforms such as Videoland and RTL XL. Total revenue fell by 4.9%. ABN AMRO forecasts for 2017 a fall of 5% in the Dutch market for regular spot advertising. This then results in a fall of around 3% in total television income.

### TV connections falling

Streaming video undermines the principle of linear television. Scheduling is a word that means hardly anything to young viewers. From now on, consumers prefer to decide for themselves when they want to watch a programme. This is reflected in a fall in the number of TV connections in the Netherlands. According to Telecompaper, the number of connections stood at 7.4 million at the end of Q1 2017. This is a fall of around 1% within a year, while the number of households is actually increasing.

### Largest source of income under pressure

In any case, TV broadcasters lost their monopoly on video a long time ago. Until now this had no decisive impact on the television market. But now income from regular spot advertising – by far the largest source of income for TV broadcasters – is under structural pressure.

## Increase in use of Video on Demand, mainly via Netflix

People in the Netherlands are watching more and more Video on Demand (VoD). Netflix seems to be the major driver of this development. Read more about this development via [Telecompaper.](https://www.telecompaper.com/pressrelease/toename-gebruik-video-on-demand-vooral-via-netflix-2--1208460)

## Making television

It is a different story on the production side of television. Film and television producers have shown strong growth figures in recent years. Together with IT companies, they were the fastest-growing companies within the Technology, Media and Telecom sector.

### Strong demand

Video has been booming business for several years. Production companies serve the entire audiovisual market and therefore have many clients. Demand for video content is high, especially from online media companies, but also from regular business. Television broadcasters are important clients in that respect, but by no means the only ones. Worldwide, Netflix, Amazon and, following in their wake, many other content providers have given the market for video production a strong boost.

*Source: Telecompaper*

### Streaming changes the market

Even so, a structural contraction in television advertising also affects the makers of television programmes. If Dutch broadcasters have less money for new programmes, this will affect the revenue (growth) of TV producers. The strong growth of streaming vod worldwide is also causing changes in the markets for content. Since Dutch producers are often active internationally, they too will have to adapt to structural changes.

### Large producers taken over

The largest TV producers in our country have all ended up in foreign hands over the past three years. A large part of their income comes from selling programme licences and content rights abroad. The Netherlands is seen by these producers as a good test market for new television trends and formats. Fewer television viewers can have a negative impact on the launch and testing of new formats.

### More competition

International competition in online content continues to increase. In addition to YouTube, there are other new creators and providers of free content. The influence of vloggers is clearly increasing, for example. And now Facebook is also stepping into this market with Watch. On the other hand, advertisers such as Albert Heijn are starting their own online TV channels. Traditional media companies are trying to attract more visitors to their websites with more video. The counter movement exists too: online media company Vice is going to start a new TV channel. Finally, Netflix also sees new competition in the subscription market when Disney soon launches its own streaming service.

### Sharpening strategy

It is clear that TV producers also face a rapidly changing market and must sharpen their strategy. Creativity can come from anywhere and the barrier to entry for TV production is relatively low. Demand for content is high, but also subject to strong change. The internet gives niche content aimed at smaller target groups a better chance. Increasing competition is also putting pressure on producers to pre-finance part of a new production themselves. This reduces the risk for the buyer, but increases it for the producer.

### Oversupply

The term ‘Golden Age of Television’ appears regularly in the media: the international supply of high-quality TV drama is large. Many parties use quality drama to attract new viewers and/or subscribers. This logically leads to a counter reaction. In mid-2017, consultancy PwC warned in its annual media forecast of a possible oversupply of television content. Consumers naturally do not worry about this. But for providers, retaining the viewer is an increasingly important topic.

## Television strategy

It is clear that television viewing is subject to strong change. Providers and producers of linear television programmes will have to adapt to increased competition. A number of current trends have a major impact on the future of linear television. ABN AMRO identifies the following crucial trends:

- Behavioural change towards streaming video on demand
- Adoption of fibre and 5G mobile networks
- Content renewal: interaction and reality
- Personalised content and advertising

### 25% time-shifted and online already by 2020

It is not hard to predict a strong rise in streaming video-on-demand for the coming years. [In mid-](https://insights.abnamro.nl/2016/07/online-en-uitgesteld-tv-kijken-naar-25-in-2025/) 2016, ABN AMRO already stated the expectation that in 2025 the share of time-shifted and online TV viewing would come to 25% of viewing time. Based on new figures, ABN AMRO now expects this 25% to be reached as early as 2020.

### Ever more options

More and more people have a television with an internet connection, also known as a smart TV. By now about 35% (GFK, 2017). As a result, the television is increasingly used for online services, for example via apps. On the other hand, the viewing landscape is widening, because people are watching live television more often via smartphones and tablets. Overall, people consume the same amount of media, but the number of available options has increased sharply. This is a complex landscape for traditional television broadcasters, who have to compete with new players specialised in the online world.

### From advertising to subscriptions

The lasting shift in consumers’ viewing behaviour has an important strategic consequence in the long run: television is partly changing from a public advertising medium to a paid subscription without advertising. In other words: in future, the television industry will get less money from advertising and more from subscription fees.

### Own streaming strategy

In response to the arrival of Netflix, Dutch TV broadcasters have launched their own initiatives in streaming video-on-demand. RTL operates Videoland and also takes part in NLziet, a partnership with NPO and SBS. Telecompaper estimates the number of subscribers for Videoland at around 390 thousand, and for NLziet at 150 thousand. These are not yet numbers that pose serious competition to Netflix. Even so, a streaming strategy is crucial for the Dutch broadcasters now that spot advertising has begun a structural contraction.

### Archive treasures

The Dutch broadcasters are sitting on a treasure trove of Dutch-language drama content in their archives, produced over many years. Making these television series available can be a competitive advantage. Netflix keeps trying to improve its Dutch-language offering, but only succeeds to a limited extent (films, cabaret, but no series). Many Dutch-language films have, incidentally, been financed with broadcaster money, so there is an advantage here too. Traditional TV broadcasters need a good subscription-based earnings model to offset the loss of advertising revenue.

*Source: IMDB, ABN AMRO*

### Opportunity for local quality drama

Netflix has made local content for a few countries, such as the series Lillehammer (Norway), Marseille (France) and Cable Girls (Spain). It is doubtful whether this is feasible for all the countries where the company is active. Even so, Netflix has announced the first Dutch-language production, ‘Undercover’. Dutch television companies can respond to the need for local quality drama. This does not even have to be in hard-nosed competition. A good marketing story could be: alongside a subscription to Netflix (or soon also Amazon), you need a subscription to a local streaming service for good Dutch-language drama.

### Must see now

However, broadcasters and producers face powerful competition from large international players. They will have to make sharper choices in investment in producing both television drama and reality formats. Put simply: quality needs to go up further. To retain viewers, we need high-quality television drama and more innovative reality formats. Television needs more “must see now” moments. For example, by building more interaction with viewers into reality formats. At present this is still mainly limited to voting for favourite candidates.

### Limiting supply

Linear television – in the sense of live broadcasting – will not disappear quickly. News and sport will continue to attract viewers. Over the next twenty years, the traditional (older) TV viewer will also remain glued to the box. However, TV broadcasters will have to adapt to a shrinking linear viewing audience. A logical way to improve quality is to limit the number of programmes. Producing fewer programmes means more budget per programme. And hopefully, therefore, higher quality. More viewers per programme is also more attractive for advertisers.

### Massively online

NLziet has recently also started offering live television via the internet. This seems a small step, but it can have major consequences in the longer term. With the further roll-out of fibre and, in future, the step from 4G to 5G mobile networks, watching television en masse via the internet becomes technically possible. And perhaps quite commonplace. And a new way of watching television may emerge in which television channels and linear scheduling disappear in their current form.

### Internet as a lifeline

For traditional TV broadcasters, this is an opportunity: the internet offers many new possibilities. Not only in terms of a wide range of streaming programmes. Online viewing also offers more choice when watching live sports matches (camera angle, selecting matches yourself in a tournament). It is also possible to experiment with personalised content and advertising. More intensive use of the internet therefore seems to be a lifeline.

### Rapid strategic change needed

But embracing an online strategy is not easy for traditional broadcasters. Speed is essential: competition continues to increase strongly and spot advertising no longer offers a foothold for the future. It is no coincidence that NPO has announced that it will triple the budget for online from EUR 20 million to EUR 60 million per year. For traditional television companies, sitting still really is no longer an option.

# The two faces of Agentic AI: weapon and shield

[Analysis](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/analyses/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Amad Khan](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/amad-khan/index.html)

Criminals are using artificial intelligence more often to carry out lightning-fast attacks. At the same time, companies are deploying the same technology to defend themselves. The urgency to act is growing, but many organisations do not know where to start within this complex arms race.

The world of cyber security is at a tipping point. Agentic AI — artificial intelligence in which systems operate autonomously and make decisions without human intervention — is fundamentally changing how both defenders and attackers work. Unlike generative AI, which depends on pre-defined instructions, these “agents” can initiate, execute and improve actions on their own based on what they learn.

Recent research by Palo Alto Networks, a US cyber security company, shows how profound this shift is. Researchers managed to simulate a full ransomware attack in just 25 minutes using Agentic AI — a hundred times faster than traditional methods. “The scenario they outline is disturbingly realistic,” says Matthijs Mons, cyber security specialist at ABN AMRO. “Criminals will develop agents that are tailored to targeted attacks.”

## From science fiction to reality

The process described by Palo Alto Networks illustrates the power of Agentic AI. Agents start by gathering public information about their targets via social media and corporate databases. Based on current data, they continually adapt their strategy and try to gain access to networks through personalised phishing attacks, LinkedIn messages or fake invitations to video conferences. Once inside, they analyse patterns to identify the most valuable systems and choose attack techniques that align seamlessly with their target’s vulnerabilities. “For many people this scenario sounds fictional, as if everything happens by itself,” says Mons. “But with traditional AI a human still had to perform at least an initial action. With Agentic AI, things now happen fully autonomously, without anyone being involved. And that applies to both sides.”

The other side is the protective measures that organisations implement. At large financial institutions, hundreds of cyber security specialists work to protect vital infrastructure. The staff shortage in this sector is significant, and Agentic AI offers solutions here. “In an incident, a security expert often has to look up information in different systems,” Mons explains. “That can take hours. An agent for security tasks can, based on a single instruction, independently examine data from system reports and present a complete picture, while an analyst has to follow an entire step-by-step plan.”

> “With Agentic AI, things now happen fully autonomously, without anyone being involved.”

Matthijs Mons

ABN AMRO

## Simulating an attack

The most promising applications are in simulating attacks on one’s own organisation, so-called “red team” actions. Traditionally, security teams spend days or even weeks on this: mapping systems, finding access routes and testing different paths.

By learning from previous simulated attacks, Agentic AI can break in much faster and map which attack sequence is most effective — exactly what criminals will do as well. There are already companies that offer this as a service. They deploy simulations for their clients and learn from both the attacking “red teams” and the defending “blue teams”. This combined approach, also known as “purple AI”, enables organisations to learn from both perspectives and continuously improve their security. It is important to note that Agentic AI uses existing methods. It executes attacks faster than ever, but it does not devise new methods. So far, Agentic AI is mainly a leap in efficiency. “The major cyber security companies are investing heavily in AI,” says Mons. “But criminals are just as active. The romantic image of hackers as lonely boys in basements is completely outdated. Cyber attacks are carried out by professional organisations with specific roles focused on gaining access, building malware, installing malware, stealing data, negotiations, etc. They even have HR departments and call centres, with a solid business case based on the proceeds they generate from stolen data.”

## The paradox of adoption

The use of Agentic AI within organisations — for both security and regular business processes — also creates new vulnerabilities. Hackers can, for example, try to manipulate the AI agents that already have access to systems.

This leads to a striking paradox, revealed by a survey by SailPoint (a US technology company focused on identity and access management) among 353 cyber security professionals worldwide. Although 96 per cent of respondents consider agents to be a growing security threat, as many as 98 per cent of organisations plan to expand their use of Agentic AI in the coming year. The study shows that 82 per cent of companies already use agents, with more than half reporting that these have daily access to sensitive data. Nevertheless, only 44 per cent have implemented policies to protect these agents against external threats. Perhaps most alarming: nearly one in four companies reported that agents can be manipulated to reveal access credentials.

“The use of this technology is a risk in itself,” warn Hans van Haarlem and Steven Ottenhoff of MMOX, a Dutch cyber security consultancy. They point to a crucial point that many entrepreneurs overlook. “In the past, attacking large companies took enormous time and resources. That threshold has now been drastically lowered. Criminals can now work much more efficiently, which makes medium-sized and smaller organisations attractive targets as well.”

> “Hold a regular digital fire drill, so everyone is prepared and knows their role in a crisis.”

Hans van Haarlem

MMOX

## Volume and quality are increasing

The lower threshold for attacks is already visible in practice, with attackers expanding their scope. “We receive buckets full of phishing emails to analyse,” says Van Haarlem. “What stands out is that both the volume and the quality are increasing. Spear phishing — targeted attacks on specific individuals — is carried out much better. We regularly see new employees receive texts from ‘our’ CEO in no time, with requests to transfer money or get in touch. On their very first working day. It is hard to believe this is not automated in the background.”

The number of cyber attacks has not yet risen explosively, and there is a reason for that. “Agentic AI systems are still super expensive,” the ABN AMRO cyber security specialist explains. “They require a lot of computing power and training models costs money. Criminals therefore continue to focus on relatively easy targets. Hacking a large bank can take years, but a small accountancy firm can be cracked within a week with a substantial haul. Generative AI has caused the number of phishing emails to increase drastically. That is cheap and easy. Full Agentic AI attacks, in which systems independently screen social media and carry out spear phishing, are certainly coming, but are not yet widespread.”

## Internal security risks

In addition to external threats, agents deployed for both security and other business processes pose specific security risks within organisations. “If such an agent is poorly configured or insufficiently monitored, situations can arise that you do not want,” warns Mons. “Imagine: an agent detects that you clicked on a phishing email. The normal procedure is to isolate that computer from the network so it cannot cause damage. But if that agent accidentally isolates all the computers in your network instead of one, no one can work.”

There is a more fundamental problem. Agentic AI can act based on data and prior experience without human control, but it can also “hallucinate”: draw wrong conclusions or perform unexpected actions. “You then do not know exactly what it has done,” Mons explains. “It learns by itself and acts by itself. If things go wrong, you have to find out everything that happened and what you need to restore. That is why it is crucial to build in good control mechanisms and keep options open that make it possible for people to intervene themselves.”

The experts at MMOX add nuance. “Agentic AI has no stress or workload under which someone becomes sloppy. It can do something wrong with good intentions because the underlying model is inadequate, but the margin of error is often smaller than with people.”

## What can companies do?

The course of action for organisations is clear but wide-ranging. “Make sure you are better protected than the neighbour,” says Mons. “On a street, burglars break into the worst-protected house. That basic principle also applies in the digital world. It is not about absolute security, but about relative resilience.” Concrete measures start with fundamentals: good device security and two-factor authentication. But the latter is not watertight. “There are scenarios in which that double check can still be bypassed. It is a constant cat-and-mouse game between security makers and malicious parties.”

Van Haarlem therefore argues for a strategic approach that goes beyond technology alone. “AI does not change the methodology of cyber criminals, it only changes efficiency and thus scope,” he states. “We are proponents of the NIST framework: a widely recognised set of guidelines that helps organisations manage and reduce cyber security risks. Invest in employees and technology, in network segmentation and strong data analysis.” Impact-mitigating measures are a crucial but underestimated element. “Instead of focusing only on keeping hackers out, you must ensure they cannot cause damage once they are in,” Van Haarlem and Ottenhoff emphasise. “Segment your network so an attacker cannot move freely through your entire systems. Try to build a high wall, but realise that if someone climbs over it, there must be more barriers inside.”

> “The threshold for cyber attacks is lower than ever. Companies that do not act now face major risks.”

Steven Ottenhoff

MMOX

## The human factor

The role of employees remains complex. While some see people as the weakest link, ABN AMRO’s Mons thinks differently. “We consider people the last resort, the strongest link. Technology may have failed, but a vigilant employee can still stop an attack. That is why we promote safe behaviour across the entire organisation.” Van Haarlem and Ottenhoff go deeper into this aspect. “Changing human behaviour is extremely difficult, especially when the digital skills of the current population lag behind and employees are not intrinsically motivated to work safely.”

The experts at MMOX distinguish three essential factors. First, organisations must set clear guidelines for safe behaviour and provide user-friendly security tools. They warn: “Many tools wither on the vine because adoption lags.” Second, the urgency of cyber risks must be emphasised continuously. “This is not something for a single training session, but must be institutionalised. Discuss it monthly or quarterly during meetings, repetition is essential.” Third, organisations must understand the working environment. “Map which systems you have. Listen carefully to your employees. If they download software or extensions themselves because they are missing something in the official systems, you need to take that seriously. By understanding their needs and meeting them, you prevent them from seeking unsafe solutions. Make the topic open for discussion.”

## Resilience rather than perfection

The central message from all experts is clear: perfect security does not exist. “The question is not whether you will be hacked, but when,” say the experts at MMOX. “The costs of attacks have become so low that every company is a potential target. We therefore advise clients: be prepared. Develop resilience.” Concretely, that means: ensure backups that actually work, develop an incident response system — a structured step-by-step plan to respond quickly and effectively to a cyber incident — and practise regularly with scenarios. “Simulate that you have been hacked,” they advise. “What will you do? How will you communicate? What if your data are leaked? Hold a regular digital fire drill, so everyone is prepared and knows their role in a crisis.”

For small and medium-sized enterprises, the challenges are different from those of large organisations. “The blue team agents and advanced systems that are now available require such investment that they are not relevant for most entrepreneurs.” Entrepreneurs often do not know where to start. That is why we focus on helping lay a solid foundation: getting basic measures in order so you are better protected than the neighbour.”

## Opportunities and threats

Despite all the risks, Agentic AI also offers substantial opportunities. ABN AMRO cyber security specialist Mons underlines this. “Red team actions that used to take weeks can now take days. Analyses that took hours now take minutes. With Agentic AI, we can test ourselves better, find vulnerabilities faster and respond more effectively to incidents,” he says. But both parties also stress the need for caution. Ottenhoff: “Agentic AI is not necessarily transparent. Agentic systems can do something wrong with good intentions. Sometimes a simple, predictable system is a better choice than complex AI.”

## The urgency is growing

Cyber attacks will in future be faster, on a larger scale and more under the radar. This calls for security systems that continually adapt. Old systems that rely on fixed controls and slow updates fall short. A new approach is needed in which vulnerabilities in the cloud and other digital systems are continuously monitored, and in which simulating realistic threats helps identify and fix weak spots. Within organisations, all Agentic AI systems — whether used for security or other business processes — should not be regarded as ordinary system components, but as separate entities with their own security needs. This requires a strategy that identifies, monitors and controls all agents. In this way, companies can not only comply with regulation, but also strengthen their overall security posture.

At the same time, agents deployed by malicious actors form a growing external threat. The rise of Agentic AI does not introduce entirely new attack methods, but makes existing techniques faster and more effective. The speed and adaptability of these systems pose a major challenge. Companies must therefore innovate proactively and anticipate agent-driven attacks.

“It is very difficult to move cyber security from ‘important but not urgent’ to ‘urgent’,” Ottenhoff observes. “But with the rise of Agentic AI that urgency is real. The industry behind cyber crime has matured, with solid business cases. The threshold to attack is lower than ever. Companies that do not act now face major risks.”

The two faces of Agentic AI — as weapon and as shield — force organisations to rethink. The technology offers unprecedented opportunities for efficient security and scaling up cyber security teams. But that same power is also in the hands of criminals who are becoming more professional. The challenge is clear: companies must now invest in fundamental security, involve and prepare their employees, and realise that absolute security does not exist. Resilience and preparation are the new watchwords in an era where the question is not whether, but when you will be attacked.

# The virtual human as a commercial opportunity

[Analysis](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/analyses/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

From virtual replicas of celebrities to social media personalities generated from scratch – virtual humans are popping up everywhere. The public seem open to virtual brand ambassadors and the underlying technology is also becoming more widely available. This creates opportunities for brands, technology providers and forward-looking advertising agencies

Model Eva Herzigova has had an exact replica of herself created, from the tiny hairs on her face to her facial expressions. The model’s digital counterpart can appear in photo shoots and videos and even walk catwalk shows; Herzigova’s walk has been copied as well. If the real Herzigova no longer has to travel the world for every job, the model told [Vogue Business](https://www.voguebusiness.com/technology/brands-can-now-hire-supermodel-eva-herzigovas-digital-twin), she can spend more time with her children.

That makes Herzigova one of the first celebrities to actively market her replica. In this way she not only increases her productivity. It is also a step towards more control over her digital identity. In a world where artificial intelligence (AI) can easily create fake material of people, one remedy may be to pre-empt this by presenting the most convincing replica yourself.

## A scalable and personal message

Brands do not seem to shy away from such innovations either. Holidaymakers who want to persuade their friends to join them on a Virgin Voyages cruise can send their prospective travel partners a personalised invitation in which Jennifer Lopez paints them a picture of a dream holiday. Jen AI addresses them by name and knows the occasion and the group’s favourite activities. In a campaign by crisp brand Lay’s with Lionel Messi, the real version of the footballer appeared in commercials while a digital twin of the footballer was simultaneously available for personalised online video messages. In these so-called Messi Messages, a virtual Messi addresses the viewer by name, in ten possible languages.

Chocolate brand Cadbury created a win-win in India through an advertising campaign that applied ‘deepfake’ technology in an innovative way. Small business owners, from clothes shops to electronics stores, could have film star Shah Rukh Khan promote their own business in an online advert by the chocolate brand. Cadbury itself plays only a small role in the video, but strengthens its name in a sympathetic way by giving space to the local shopkeeper.

For brands, such campaigns are a way to tie a big name to them while also narrowing the distance between superstar and target group. It fits seamlessly with the present day, in which celebrities are closer than ever through social media; it is easy to follow their ups and downs and sometimes even watch them live. A scalable yet ‘personal’ message is a logical next step.

## The rise of virtual influencers

But however perfect and choreographed such videos are, the celebrity remains a human being with associated whims. That carries risks, as every brand that has ever associated itself with a famous person knows. In 2001, for example, Britney Spears was photographed by paparazzi with a can of Coca Cola, while at that time she had a costly sponsorship deal with Pepsi. Even now, any crack in the reputation of a carefully chosen ambassador is also a vulnerability for the brand. The solution: partner with a fully virtual ‘influencer’, without a compromising past or present.

Miquela is a frequently cited example. Her full name is Miquela Sousa and she was fully created by marketing agency Brud. Sousa’s identity has been built in detail, with her own background, personality and preferences. On video platform TikTok, where she has no fewer than 3.5 million followers, she posts about her daily life and answers questions. “Hormones,” Miquela replies when asked what she really doesn’t understand about people. She has created content with brands such as Samsung and Calvin Klein, and last year started a long-term partnership with the US retailer Pacsun.

## Branding opportunities

iIt turns out to be a lucrative business. US research shows that about [35 per cent](https://theinfluencermarketingfactory.com/virtual-influencers-survey-infographic/?utm_source=pr&utm_medium=link&utm_campaign=virtual_influencers_PR) of internet users have at some point bought a product or service after a virtual influencer drew attention to it. People also seem to enjoy interacting with these created individuals; research shows that internet users have [three times](https://hypeauditor.com/blog/the-top-instagram-virtual-influencers-in-2021/) as much engagement with posts by virtual influencers as with those by real people – think likes and comments.

A textbook success story is Lu, the much-loved ambassador of Brazilian retailer Magalu. She grew from virtual assistant in the webshop to authoritative [social media personality](https://www.ogilvy.com/work/lu-magalu). She even appears in music videos by real artists, and the outfits she wears there then invariably sell out. The label ‘influencer’ is therefore anything but empty in Lu’s case.

Brand-specific personalities are not new, but by transforming them into 3D models that can showcase their character and lifestyle across many channels, the possibilities for branding increase significantly. From social media to games and virtual reality worlds, they can make an appearance everywhere.

## Proceed with caution

Even amid the hype, caution is advised; many people are not waiting for this. When retailer Marks & Spencer introduced its virtual personality Mira at the end of 2022, the public were not enthusiastic. In the comments under her Instagram posts, people called Mira “creepy” and expressed incomprehension at M&S’s choice of a perfect appearance. If you do opt for a virtual brand ambassador, the criticism ran, then choose someone whose appearance is a better reflection of the brand’s customer base. No new posts from Mira have appeared since November.

Although it is an experimental – and often successful – way to connect mainly with Gen Z and younger, the virtual influencer will be a bridge too far for most smaller brands. It remains to be seen whether the advantages over regular influencer marketing currently outweigh the risks and investments. For now, the opportunities seem to be concentrated mainly among larger brands that are actively experimenting with their branding efforts and want to reach a younger target group.

Knowledge and expertise are needed to steer such initiatives in the right direction. That in turn creates opportunities for agencies that know the field. Advertising agency Ogilvy was the brain behind the successful Cadbury campaign in India and was also the party that managed to turn Lu into a true influencer. Recently, advertising agency Dentsu even created a specific service: Dentsu VI, where the two-letter abbreviation stands for ‘virtual identity’. The division creates completely new virtual personalities on behalf of brands, or adapts already developed models to the client’s wishes.

## AI-generated models

Smaller brands would nevertheless do well to read up further; technological developments are moving fast, and virtual humans can also add value behind the scenes of business operations. So says Sharief Mohamed, CFO and COO of Lalaland AI, which develops lifelike 3D models for fashion designers. Via a plug-in in a widely used programme, designers can have their three-dimensional clothing designs virtually worn by models with different ready-to-wear sizes and skin tones. “This allows them to see straight away whether a design also looks good on people with a size other than small,” says Mohamed. “And whether the chosen colour of the garment works with a particular skin tone.”

This also offers benefits on the purchasing side. Retailers can more easily form a picture of the individual garments in brands’ collections when they are worn by models. Due to the large number of designs, catalogues show most garments without a model, photographed against a white background. By using virtual models, buyers know better what they are bringing into their shops.

The models’ appearance can be fully adapted to the brand’s identity, from hairstyle to accessories. According to Mohamed, they are certainly no replacement for brands’ more creative campaign images. “Towards consumers you will still need real people,” he says. “Our added value at present is behind the scenes, in the design phase.”

[Listen to the podcast too: Virtual humans: AI-generated fashion models](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/in-gesprek/podcast/de-innovatiegolf/virtuele-mensen-ai-gegenereerde-fashionmodellen.html)

## Major investments in technology companies

Lalaland is not the only company to have jumped on this wave of innovation. A growing number of technology companies offer services for creating virtual humans, whether replicas of existing individuals or fully invented figures. Recently, Synthesia, the technology company behind the Messi Messages, reached unicorn status after raising 90 million dollars from investors. Yoom, which creates 3D versions of people in a special studio with cameras on all sides, has raised 50 million dollars in funding since its inception. It is an interesting proposition for, among others, artists who want to have their performances recorded in three dimensions and brands that want to bring their ambassadors into people’s homes via augmented reality.

Hyperreal is another player facilitating the creation of virtual humans and showing highly convincing results. For example, their website features a video clip of a performance by rapper Biggie Smalls, who died in 1997. Only, it is not a real concert but a [lifelike reconstruction](https://www.technologyreview.com/2022/12/16/1065393/biggie-smalls-metaverse/) of the artist. Ownership and control from the real world are explicitly part of Hyperreal’s proposition; the rapper’s heirs have full say over the use of the underlying 3D model. In doing so, the company is preparing for a world in which everyone can have their own virtual replica and in which digital identities also need protection against theft and misuse.

## Technology is becoming more accessible

Due to their level of detail and versatility, replicas like those of Biggie Smalls and Eva Herzigova are time-consuming and costly to make. Even so, a host of technologies are available that also open doors for people and brands with less deep pockets. This allows them to experiment, in a low-threshold way, with the technological possibilities and limitations. Gaming giant Epic Games, for example, offers a free web application, MetaHumans, with which users can create virtual humans. It is even possible to import your own 3D scans – a functionality available in many mobile apps – and refine them on the platform into a convincing digital twin.

Rotterdam-based social media agency RauwCC also started experimenting with the available building blocks. Images of their creation Esther Olofsson used to be made by projecting her virtual face onto photos of a real model, but the influencer is now entirely ‘[AI-based](https://www.emerce.nl/nieuws/esther-olofsson-evolueert-tot-volledig-aifotomodel)’. New photos of Olofsson can now be created via a simple textual instruction – also called a ‘prompt’. Stable Diffusion, the underlying AI model, was trained on a 3D version of the influencer for this purpose. Video footage of Olofsson also appears regularly, with photos animated afterwards using Movio and a synthetic voice added with Replica Studios’ software.

In the near future, it is unlikely to be technology that is the biggest challenge, but the ability of brands and their agencies to forge unique partnerships with existing celebrities – real or created – and shape new personalities that resonate with their audiences. In a world where technological possibilities blur the line between real and fake, brands must have a clear vision of their own core and of creative ways to put that across to their target group.

# Deregulation is not the end of RegTech

[Analysis](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/analyses/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

Stricter regulation gave innovative start-ups that help companies comply with legislation a growth spurt, but with the global call for deregulation the market seems under pressure. The RegTech sector must reinvent itself to stay relevant.

In recent years, start-ups and scale-ups have focused on developing smart technology that helps companies comply with laws and regulations. This is also called ‘regulatory technology’, often shortened to RegTech.

The need for such technology grew, among other things, when the European Union (EU) introduced the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), a directive to safeguard citizens’ data privacy, and developed the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD), which mandates sustainability reporting. Demand also increased in the financial sector, partly due to guidelines that require financial institutions to carefully screen suspicious transactions and customers.

## Deregulation as a political and economic reality

A political shift is now under way. In both the United States and the EU, the call for deregulation and for reducing administrative burdens is growing, driven in part by geopolitical competition and economic uncertainty. President Trump is pushing for a drastic slimming down of regulation, with the principle that for every new federal law, ten existing rules must be scrapped. His goal is to reduce regulatory pressure and stimulate economic growth. Especially in the area of ESG compliance, companies’ obligations are expected to decrease sharply, and financial institutions are expected to experience less regulatory pressure as well.

On our side of the ocean, the European Commission proposed the Omnibus reform package in February 2025, which is estimated to reduce the administrative burden of sustainability reporting by more than € 6 billion. This reform package is intended to make compliance simpler and less costly for companies.

As a result, an estimated 80 per cent of companies, contrary to earlier proposals, will no longer have to comply with CSRD’s reporting requirement. Although some will continue to report out of intrinsic motivation, this development is a hard blow for RegTech companies specialised in ESG reporting.

## Investor confidence

RegTech is a niche, as shown by the relatively modest sums that have flowed to start-ups and scale-ups in this space. Capital flows peaked in 2021, with a global investment volume of more than 1 billion dollars. A similar peak was also visible in the broader tech sector, driven by low interest rates and the digitisation push in the wake of the pandemic. But while tech investment fell sharply across the board in 2022, the RegTech niche saw only a minimal correction that year — possibly a sign of confidence that laws and regulations would remain a driving force behind the market. Although a correction followed the year after, investment has since shown a cautious recovery.

The RegTech sector includes a wide range of services that help companies integrate laws and regulations into their operations efficiently. Some providers develop software that scans business processes and systems for compliance with all relevant laws and regulations in a given sector. Others take specific regulation as a starting point and offer tools that make it easier for companies to meet the associated obligations. Examples include software that identifies violations of human rights and environmental standards in supply chains, and systems that scan financial transactions for suspicious patterns.

## RegTech between growth and uncertainty

The rise of RegTech is closely linked to the financial sector. After the 2008 financial crisis, governments worldwide introduced stricter regulation to better oversee financial institutions, which led to strong demand for technological compliance solutions. Solutions for Know Your Customer (KYC), transaction monitoring and anti-money laundering regulation (AML) initially dominated the market. The advent of the GDPR in 2018 and the broader focus on data privacy and sustainability led RegTech to expand into other sectors, attracting new investment flows.

Laws and regulations are often a catalyst for innovation and new business opportunities. “Europe is known for its tendency to regulate, and that creates an interesting market for RegTech,” says Sophie Heijenberg, investor at No Such Ventures. For example, in 2022, the year after the CSRD was first announced, 1.8 billion dollars was promptly invested in sustainability measurement software, while a year earlier this amount was only 0.5 billion dollars. After this peak, however, a decline followed that has continued since. Although this type of software does not always fall directly under RegTech, it does show how regulation can set a market in motion. The question remains whether this niche will recover now that the political wind has turned, or whether investor interest will decline further.

## From reporting to risk management

Dependence on laws and regulations places RegTech companies in a dynamic playing field. That makes some players vulnerable. “Tools that only serve to generate reports will struggle,” says Heijenberg. “But broader platforms that focus on risk management in the areas of sustainability, privacy and cybersecurity remain relevant. Legislation may be slimmed down, but the underlying risks do not disappear and can come very close. Just look at the floods in Limburg.”

The Austrian scale-up Prewave develops software that helps manage those underlying risks. Under the banner of “supply chain superintelligence”, the company uses public data to identify risks in the supply chain, such as natural disasters, cyber incidents and financial stress. Although the platform simultaneously helps its users comply with laws and regulations using this data, co-founder Harald Nitschinger places his product in a broader perspective. “As laws and regulations become more complex, companies should not simply tick boxes, but rather use compliance to become stronger and more resilient,” he said last year when Prewave secured € 63 million in investment.

## Sector-specific compliance as a growth market

It can also pay to focus on specific niches, as proven by the Dutch-British Naq, which developed a compliance platform for tech companies active in the medical sector. There are strict requirements for the use of medical devices, and privacy legislation is extra relevant due to the large amount of sensitive personal data being processed. The medical sector is also designated as ‘critical’ under the NIS2 cyber law, which brings with it all kinds of stricter cybersecurity requirements.

“MedTech companies must be compliant from day one if they want to sell their services to, for example, hospitals,” explains Heijenberg of No Such Ventures, one of the investors behind Naq. “If, as a start-up, you can take out a software subscription instead of paying tens of thousands of euros to a consultant, that really solves a pain.”

[Also listen to the podcast with Nadia Kadhim of Naq about off-the-shelf compliance solutions for medical innovators](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/in-gesprek/podcast/de-innovatiegolf/kant-en-klare-compliance-oplossing-voor-medische-innovators.html)

## Keeping a finger on the pulse

Even in a context of deregulation, laws change at a rapid pace; they shift, are relaxed or tightened in response to economic and political developments. For companies active in multiple jurisdictions or within complex global supply chains, it is important to keep a constant finger on the pulse. New compliance requirements can emerge suddenly, while existing obligations can change or lapse.

Platforms such as Regology respond to this by giving companies insight into legal changes and translating them directly into practical action points. This enables organisations to implement changes in good time, without having to keep track of legal developments themselves continuously. To keep up, it is important for RegTech companies to embed such intelligence in their services.

## Agility and strategic risk management

Regulation can set a market in motion, as seen in the explosion of investment in sustainability measurement software following the CSRD. But laws come and go, and anyone who sees RegTech purely as a reaction to new obligations is building on quicksand. The essence of regulation remains: managing risks, ensuring transparency and keeping markets fair and safe. RegTech companies that focus on this, and that can adapt quickly to changing risks and requirements, create lasting value — regardless of whether regulation is tightened or slimmed down.

# Digital subscriptions set media companies on a growth path

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

Ingrid Laane

Driven by the coronavirus crisis, media companies are making a rapid digital transformation. Because of the lockdowns, media consumption has shifted faster to the internet; the number of digital subscribers to newspapers and on-demand television services has risen sharply and consumers are buying online en masse. The increased internet traffic gives more branches in the TMT sector a chance to get off to a good start when the economy picks up again.

## Shift to online

Consumers have been ‘stuck’ at home for almost a year, but the internet provides a virtual way out. As a result, data traffic has increased more strongly than in previous years, according to figures from the Amsterdam Internet Exchange (AMS-IX). This internet hub processed 35 per cent more data last year than the year before, while in 2019 growth was ‘only’ 17 per cent. The number of digital subscribers to newspapers benefited from this trend. For example, the number of digital subscriptions at Mediahuis, publisher of NRC and De Telegraaf among others, rose by almost 50 per cent last year.

Led by new streaming services and demand for home entertainment due to the many hours spent at home, revenue growth in the Dutch market for TV and video services has also accelerated. While revenue from traditional pay TV remained the same, income from over-the-top video services in the third quarter of 2020 rose by 48 per cent compared with the year before. Online shopping increased by almost 36 per cent. As a result, advertising campaigns are also shifting to the internet, where consumers are increasingly easy to reach.

## Momentum to strengthen digital propositions

Media companies are using the current crisis to implement structural changes: less dependence on advertising income and more propositions that tap into increased digital consumption. For example, DPG Media in Belgium recently set up its own streaming platform, Streamz, to offer a local alternative to foreign streaming services. Media companies are also differentiating more in their subscription offering. For instance, Videoland now offers a cheaper package because it also contains advertising. Media spending is also expected to increase again in 2021, which gives extra scope to strengthen propositions further.

The shift to online is not new. Media companies have been working on online propositions for years, but due to strong competition from Google and Facebook in the advertising market and the wide availability of free content, it is difficult to make money from this. However, the call to curb the market power of these tech giants is growing louder. The first concrete steps will soon be taken at European level. A new European directive to be published in June this year should ensure that publishers gain more control over the use of their articles by Google and Facebook and can, if necessary, enforce payment for this. This gives publishers’ online revenue model a helping hand.

Advertising agencies have also responded quickly over the past year to increased digital consumption. Due to the lockdowns, consumers have been buying even more online. As a result, advertising campaigns have increasingly shifted to the internet. During the first lockdown in spring 2020, a very different route still seemed to be taken; campaigns were then withdrawn or postponed en masse due to economic uncertainty. Advertising agencies therefore faced a sharp fall in revenue. The fact that the current lockdown is being felt less does not mean that advertising agencies are free of concern. As long as shops remain closed, people do not travel and events do not take place, media budgets will remain at a lower level.

## Strong growth in the IT industry

Not only in media companies and advertising agencies, but also in other sectors, the coronavirus crisis has accelerated the digital transformation; for example in health care and education. The IT industry is benefiting from this and growth will therefore pick up again in 2021. Because organisations must also invest carefully in IT in 2021 due to economic uncertainty, growth in this industry will for the time being remain below the long-term average. In the long term, the outlook for the IT industry is excellent. The digitalisation of society continues unabated, which means that companies in all sectors will continue to rely on the technology sector.

## TMT sector to grow in 2021

For the entire TMT sector, the outlook is particularly good for the second half of this year. Because a large part of the vulnerable population is then expected to be vaccinated, the restrictive measures will by then have been largely lifted. This will cause media budgets in hard-hit sectors such as travel and events to grow substantially again, business investment to increase and consumer confidence to rise. After growth stagnated in 2020, the TMT sector will grow again as a result. ABN AMRO expects growth of 3 per cent in 2021. This growth is driven primarily by rising demand for IT services, by far the largest branch within the TMT sector. But the recovery of publishers, broadcasters, printers and the advertising industry compared with the low year of 2020 will also contribute. As a result, the sector will once again be well above the level of end-2019. However, due to uncertainty about the course of the coronavirus, these expectations are surrounded by considerable uncertainty.

# Digital media grow through transformation and new channels

[Analysis](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/analyses/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Mario Bersem](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/mario-bersem/index.html)

Digital media are showing strong growth in a transforming media landscape. For 2024, ABN AMRO expects revenue growth of 9%, while traditional media are shrinking. Innovations such as smart screens and retail media networks attract extra audiences and new applications of artificial intelligence also drive growth.

The media offering is increasingly digital, in other words expressible in “zeros and ones”. On the one hand, this transformation means digital media grow at the expense of traditional media, for example when paper newspaper subscriptions are converted to digital newspaper subscriptions. On the other hand, digital media grow thanks to innovations that attract extra audiences. For 2024, ABN AMRO expects revenue growth of 9 per cent for digital media, while traditional media will shrink by 1 per cent. On balance, the media sector as a whole will post revenue growth of 6 per cent, supported by a strong recovery in consumer purchasing power. In 2025, revenue growth for digital media is expected to ease slightly to 7 per cent, partly due to lower inflation.

[Also read: Media sector grows despite stagnating economy](/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/sectorprognoses/technologie/mediasector-groeit-ondanks-stagnerende-economie.html)

One of these innovations is smart “digital out-of-home screens” that show ads depending on the time of day or even how busy the street is. New retail media networks are also growing strongly; retail chains are making more and more ad space available in their own channels such as the website, the app, and also the screens in shops. Search engines and social media platforms are also still growing in use, purely digital channels that have no clear analogue counterpart.

## Transformation of the media landscape

The media landscape is marked by a transformation that consists of a shift from analogue to digital, from offline to online and from live to on-demand. Where people once mainly watched live on a television screen, they now also watch “television” via an app on the smartphone, while travelling on the train, two days after a programme has been broadcast.

The digital transformation is also creating entirely new possibilities. Algorithmic and personalised selection based on artificial intelligence (AI) is gaining ground over manual selection by an editorial team. Examples include social media applications, where everyone gets a tailored media offering based on their social network, interests and preferences. Streaming services also try to keep the user’s attention with tailored recommendations. This increases user engagement and the time they spend on digital platforms. Ultimately, this retained “attention” is monetised by serving advertisements and/or by selling subscriptions to users.

## The role of artificial intelligence

One of the most transformative forces within digital media is artificial intelligence (AI). AI plays a crucial role in the transformation of digital media, for example by offering personalised content recommendations, as described above. The efficiency of digital advertising is also increased through improved data analysis and user insights, enabling media companies to respond better to consumer trends and preferences. Creative agencies are meanwhile experimenting widely with the possibilities of generative AI, both on the copywriting side and on the design side. This speeds up the creative process, shifts the emphasis from creation to selection, and opens up new possibilities to produce quickly and cost-efficiently.

Whether the growth in productivity also leads to revenue growth depends on price developments. If prices fall because there is less use of “expensive” human creativity and craftsmanship, the impact on revenue can even be negative. For this forecast, any price declines for 2024 and 2025 have not yet been included, on the expectation that these will only play a role in the medium term.

## Digital ad spend drives media sector

Developments in the advertising market are a good barometer for developments within the media sector. In 2023, around € 5.2 billion was spent on advertising in the Netherlands, of which 71 per cent was digital advertising, that is € 3.7 billion, according to the VIA/Deloitte Digital Advertising Spend report for 2023 (see chart). Revenue growth for digital advertising was 5 per cent compared with 2022. Compared with an average annual growth rate of 11 per cent in the ten years before, this was relatively low.

In 2024, ABN AMRO expects 9 per cent revenue growth for digital advertising, more in line with the historical average. Growth rates of 11 per cent or more from the past are no longer to be expected because digitalisation has now progressed further and some submarkets are saturated. Paid search, for example, is growing somewhat more slowly, while online video, social media and digital audio are growing somewhat faster. Retail media is the biggest outlier in terms of growth; for retail media, ABN AMRO expects 40 per cent growth in 2024 and total ad revenue of € 400 million.

[Also read: Advertising a lucrative side income for retail chains](/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/advertenties-lucratieve-bijverdienste-voor-winkelketens.html)

Paid search via search engines is the largest category within digital advertising and grew by 3 per cent in 2023 to around € 1.7 billion. Search engines thus accounted for one-third of total ad spend, online and offline, in 2023. Display advertising, including fast-growing categories such as online video (7 per cent growth) and social media (11 per cent growth), followed closely at € 1.6 billion and is expected to overtake search engines for the first time next year.

Digital audio grew by 16 per cent in 2023 due to the increasing popularity of podcasts and because FM radio is slowly giving way to digital DAB+ radio. In radio, the digital transformation is slower than elsewhere because people in the Netherlands enjoy listening to the radio in the car and many car radios are not yet equipped to receive DAB+. Since December 2020, every new car has been fitted with DAB+, following a European directive, but people in the Netherlands drive the [oldest car fleet in Western Europe](https://www.knac.nl/nederlands-heeft-oudste-wagenpark-in-west-europa-114-jaar/), with an average age of 11.4 years. FM stations are expected to continue broadcasting their analogue signal for the time being, as is also evident from the successful auction of FM frequencies in July 2023, which raised € 150 million for the government.

# Digital sovereignty offers major opportunities for Europe

[Interview](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/interviews/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

Will Europe be crushed between the US and China when it comes to technology? Will we lose our digital say and our role in the tech economy? Far from it, predicts ABN AMRO sector specialist Julia Krauwer. In a Europe that is waking up digitally, she sees plenty of opportunities. ‘A lot of great things are already happening, but the internal hurdles must go. And we must state our ambitions out loud!’

When it came to technology, Europe long followed big brother America. Software, servers, cloud storage: all our data and systems were familiar, yet totally dependent. At first that was fine, until geopolitical balances were upended and the rise of AI put almost every part of our business world and society on shaky ground. ‘The lightning-fast breakthrough of AI is transforming business models. It affects every company, and certainly firms in tech, media and telecom,’ says Julia Krauwer, sector specialist in that field at ABN AMRO. ‘The question is not whether you change, but whether you as an entrepreneur can keep up the pace.’

## Dependence makes you vulnerable

The European tech sector has solid growth figures, but also great uncertainties. Behind digital progress lies huge dependence that is barely visible to end users. Almost all the servers, chips and software on which companies, sectors and even societies run come from the US or Asia. ‘If that supply falters, it hits our entire digital economy. The war in Ukraine, recent developments with Nexperia and the ongoing tensions between China and Taiwan have accelerated that awareness.’

You see that dependence in figures from Synergy Research. Between 2017 and 2022, the European cloud market did become five times larger, but despite this the combined market share of European providers fell: from 27 to just 13%. The cause: Amazon, Microsoft and Google grew much faster. They have also invested billions in recent years in data centres on European soil. ‘So while we are growing, the outside world is growing even faster,’ is how Krauwer sums up the urgency.

> “The future lies with companies that supply technology that is hard to reproduce and that integrates smartly into someone else’s ecosystem.”

Julia Krauwer

Sector Banker Technology, Media & Telecom

## AI as crowbar and threat

According to Krauwer, artificial intelligence is both the main driving force and the biggest disruptor right now. ‘AI changes everything: from the way we develop software to the way we advertise and sell products.’

She sketches a double movement: on the one hand, AI triggers an explosion in productivity. IT service providers help clients with automation, prediction and customisation, which opens new markets. On the other hand, artificial intelligence threatens to undermine parts of the software industry itself. ‘Software companies are in a strange split: AI can make your product smarter, but also redundant. The winners have a strong core, unique data or deep domain knowledge.’ Companies whose value is based on unique algorithms or datasets are better protected against generative AI that can easily copy standard functions.

According to Krauwer, a new distinction is emerging, where it is not only about scale, but above all about whether you offer a very specific and ingenious solution. ‘Small tech companies can also scale very quickly; just look at Swedish Lovable. But those who only deliver a nice interface risk an AI agent taking that over tomorrow. The future lies with companies that supply technology that is hard to reproduce and that integrates smartly into someone else’s ecosystem.’

## From risk to opportunity

Where dependence on foreign players used to be seen mainly as a risk, Krauwer now also sees an opportunity. ‘Because the relationship between America and Europe is changing, we in Europe are acutely aware of the importance of cloud sovereignty. As a European company you want more than ever to know where your data sits, who can access it and who controls the main switch. Companies that can demonstrate a conscious approach to privacy and transparency, to data sovereignty and thus to the continuity of their services, hold a key USP: your data are stored securely in Europe, under European laws and regulations, and thus less dependent on geopolitical unrest.’

## European Google Maps alternative chooses its own path

Amsterdam-based Magic Lane shows that digital sovereignty and commercial success can go hand in hand with their white-label Location Intelligence technology: technology that lets other platforms easily integrate navigation or shared-mobility solutions, from e-bikes to car sharing, with smart features such as offline navigation and live traffic data. CEO Raymond Alves: ‘And the technology guarantees privacy: unlike American providers, users are not tracked; all data remain anonymous.’ The figures also show that this approach pays off: in spring, the number of users of the consumer app rose by 250% in six weeks. ‘This shows that privacy and performance do not have to be mutually exclusive.’

## European sovereignty and privacy in practice

That creates opportunities for European entrepreneurs who deliver on these local values: privacy-friendly, transparent and sustainable. ‘Being compliant turns out to be quite innovative,’ Krauwer quotes from Saskia Lensink, product manager of GPT-NL. ‘Companies that can translate European values into technology with scale can really make a difference.’ Look at Deeploy, a Utrecht-based platform for AI monitoring, AI governance and AI compliance, which helps financial companies and large healthcare institutions safeguard the responsible use of AI in their organisations. Or Mistral AI, the French artificial intelligence platform, which has the potential to grow into a serious challenger to the large AI developers.’

> “We in the Netherlands and in Europe are great at R&D, but we find it daunting to declare that we are going to conquer the world.”

Julia Krauwer

Sector Banker Technology, Media & Telecom

## Culture and capital

There are plenty of promising initiatives, yet Europe is not there yet. Krauwer: ‘We in the Netherlands and in Europe are great at R&D, but we find it daunting to declare that we are going to conquer the world.’ That modesty does not work in our favour. Especially when it comes to raising enough capital, which is indispensable to let good ideas flourish. Tech companies in Europe find it significantly harder to secure large investments than their counterparts in America. According to the State of European Tech 2024 report, the cumulative gap has now grown to $375 billion. ‘In the US, start-ups raise more than $15 million twice as often,’ says Krauwer. ‘That is a huge difference. And in the end, capital determines whether you can scale.’ Fortunately, Krauwer also sees a cautious start to easing there. ‘Investors are teaming up more often,’ she notes. ‘In addition, Brussels is working on a so-called 28th regime to make investing and scaling across borders easier. With this, Europe is developing a single European framework for company law, insolvency law, labour law and tax law, to end the current fragmentation of 27 countries each with their own rules.’

## Cyber resilience as a maturity test

Alongside economic uncertainty, the security risk is growing. Tech companies are a prime target for hackers and espionage groups, especially since digital infrastructure is part of geopolitical power. ‘As a tech company you must assume you will be hacked at some point,’ says Krauwer. ‘The question is how you deal with it.’ In her view, cyber resilience is part of strategic maturity. ‘It is not only about measures to keep attackers out of your systems, but also about communication and recovery capability if you are unfortunately hacked anyway. Being resilient means you can get back up quickly. Companies that embed security and transparency in their brand win trust from customers and investors.’

## Fronts of growth

Krauwer sees several fronts on which European tech companies can grow, provided they show courage. ‘We should not only want to be less dependent on Big Tech, we should above all want to become stronger ourselves.’ The first growth layer is AI integration: businesses that weave artificial intelligence smartly into their products or processes. ‘You see software firms that integrate AI smartly into their products. And in doing so develop specialised and preferably highly intelligent, domain-specific applications that are preferably highly scalable, yet not easy for others to copy.’

A second growth layer is the technology infrastructure, from chips to data centres. Krauwer: ‘Demand for computing power is growing faster than the supply of data centres, while energy and grid capacity also set limits. That need has already led to great innovations. Such as Axelera AI, a Dutch chip start-up that develops energy-efficient and powerful AI processing units. Or Asperitas, which has developed a cooling system for data centres in which servers are submerged in a cooling liquid, making them more compact, more energy efficient and more sustainable.’

## Building blocks for developers

With Magic Lane’s modular architecture, developers can now build a solution even faster with AI support. ‘After all, we do not deliver a single app, but separate building blocks that developers can use. In addition, our software development kit can even be called by AI development tools. We are consciously positioning ourselves at the back end of an application,’ says Alves. ‘That is where the strength lies: hard-to-replicate technology, built on European values. We show that this kind of technology – like Signal and Protonmail, which offer an alternative to a messenger and email application respectively – is not idealism, but a realistic business model.’

> “We offer hard-to-replicate technology, built on European values.”

Raymond Alves

CEO Magic Lane

The third growth layer lies in the focus on European values. Providers of innovative products that are not only smart but also privacy-friendly, respect copyrights, are energy efficient, run on local servers and work without bias, win trust within and outside Europe. Krauwer cites Dutch company Ab Ovo as an example. ‘They have integrated AI into both the development process and their actual software, and are also focusing on developing software that works more flexibly and more sustainably. That is exactly the shift more companies need to make.’

## Talent and ecosystems

The sector’s success also stands or falls with people. ‘Talent gravitates to places where ambition is palpable,’ says Krauwer. ‘Universities, start-ups, investors: you need concentration. We must create more breeding grounds where science and business meet. Only then can you retain the talent that is now leaving for abroad.’ She points, among others, to TU Delft and TU Eindhoven, which now help dozens of spin-offs scale up. ‘Those are hopeful signs. But it must be faster, more ambitious and more international – with more guts and daring. In Silicon Valley, failure is mainly seen as a learning moment; here it still seems mainly a disgrace. If you dare to state big ambitions out loud, you are more likely to achieve them.’

> “In Silicon Valley, failure is mainly seen as a learning moment; in the Netherlands it still seems mainly a disgrace.”

Julia Krauwer

Sector Banker Technology, Media & Telecom

## More equal

All things considered, Krauwer expects Europe’s Big Tech and AI ecosystems to grow strongly in the coming years. Not with the primary aim of becoming completely detached from American tech, but to become more equal. ‘We are building plan B by strengthening our own alternatives. Those who dare to invest in that independence are building the next generation of European tech. Do not assume that what worked five years ago is good enough for the next five. The entrepreneurs who understand that are tomorrow’s winners. Those who dare to link their own values to innovation and scale can become a global player even now.’

# Management insufficiently involved in cyber security

[Report](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/rapporten/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

Cyber attacks can paralyse business operations and seriously disrupt relationships with customers. Cyber security is therefore a board-level issue, also because European legislation makes management responsible for it. Yet this awareness has not fully reached the boardroom, according to a survey by ABN AMRO and research agency MWM2 among 895 organisations.

The new European NIS2 directive places ultimate responsibility for cyber security at management level. If it fails to meet its obligations under this law, individual board members can even be held personally liable. Large companies still have work to do. Among these respondents, only 40 per cent already have the management team as the ultimate owner for cyber security. At almost as many companies (35 per cent), that role lies with an internal IT or security specialist. Half of large companies discuss cyber security “very regularly” at management level and more than a third (37 per cent) do so “regularly”.

## Gap between management and specialists

Compared with large companies, the management teams of small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) appear to have a slightly firmer grip on the wheel — possibly due to these companies’ smaller scale. Half of SMEs (49 per cent) see the management team as ultimately responsible for cyber security. The topic is discussed less often at management level than in large companies, though. A quarter do this “very regularly” and about half “regularly”.

According to Arwi van der Sluijs, Managing Director of cyber security service provider NFIR, there is still a gap to bridge between management on the one hand and IT and security experts on the other. Specialists do not always succeed in conveying the urgency of certain measures. “They should not only talk about the technical solution. It is better to speak in terms of concrete risks to the company. Otherwise, there is a risk that a good security initiative will be shot down purely on the basis of cost.” Conversely, many management teams labour under the false assumption that IT and security departments can be fully in control with a limited budget. “If, after a cyber attack, the IT staff are then blamed for dropping the ball, that is very painful.”

## Paper reality as a response

Although ownership at management level is essential for better-protected companies, the NIS2 obligation also brings a new kind of risk. “I foresee a situation in which companies will focus to an extreme degree on compliance, simply to prevent individual management and board members being held liable for cyber incidents,” says Daan Hoogendijk, Programme Director of Samen Digitaal Veilig. The platform, a partnership of major industry associations, helps companies with information and solutions in the area of digital security. “That would create a paper reality rather than people working in the real world to raise cyber resilience to a higher level. It is important that companies recognise that risk.”

All the more reason for companies to arm themselves properly against cyber attacks and have management take the lead. ABN AMRO says this can be done in three relatively simple steps. First, carry out a risk analysis to map out which matters are critical to keeping the company running and services up to standard. Second, take measures such as encouraging safe behaviour among employees, defining who owns certain data, and discussing cyber risks with partners and suppliers. Finally, it is essential to develop procedures to detect and handle cyber incidents.

# Sustainability is becoming a pressing necessity for software companies

[Analysis](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/analyses/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

As our society becomes ever more digital, the influence of software companies grows. They shape how we keep in touch with friends, what information we consume and how we travel. Because of that prominent role, software companies are facing closer scrutiny from both citizens and government.

Social media companies are being examined for how they can influence election results. Tech companies regularly make the news for privacy violations. And although software companies are generally—and rightly—praised for making things easier, there are many pitfalls: from algorithms that discriminate unjustly to energy-guzzling streaming services. Entrepreneurs in the digital world are therefore not in a boundless playground where only commercial opportunities lead the way. They constantly perform a challenging balancing act shaped by regulation and public opinion. They also have their own challenges, such as attracting staff in a tight labour market. That makes sustainability not only a noble aim for software companies, but also essential to safeguard their continuity and legitimacy. Data protection and AI ethics, diversity and energy efficiency are three key themes here.

## Data protection and AI ethics

In a digital society where large volumes of data are produced, processed and exchanged every day, privacy is unfortunately not a given. That is one reason why the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) was introduced in 2018. However, the GDPR does not offer a ready-made checklist for companies. It keeps evolving. New technological developments and expanding case law are shaping the GDPR further. For example, a recent [court case](https://fd.nl/ondernemen/1351533/baanbrekende-zaak-over-algoritmes-uber-waarschuwing-voor-andere-bedrijven) against taxi company Uber will clarify what ‘transparency’ means in the context of algorithmic decision-making. Software companies would therefore be wise to follow new cases on data processing closely.

Today, using data goes hand in hand with deploying Artificial Intelligence (AI), a set of technologies that mimic (elements of) human intelligence and use large volumes of data to do so. AI can unfortunately lead to unintended discrimination, for example when used in recruitment software. The European Commission therefore doubts whether current GDPR legislation suffices for effective enforcement and is exploring specific regulation on artificial intelligence. In 2021, the Commission will present a proposal, including specific attention to preventing discrimination. Until then, software companies can already assess themselves against the [Assessment List for Trustworthy Artificial Intelligence](https://futurium.ec.europa.eu/en/european-ai-alliance/pages/altai-assessment-list-trustworthy-artificial-intelligence).

Although the legal framework on data protection and AI ethics is still developing, software companies can already prepare for the relevant themes. That matters not only to avoid fines now and in future, but also because software users are making their voices heard. Citizens are assertive about their digital rights and do not shy away from pushing back. Recently, De Consumentenbond and the Data Privacy Stichting called on people to join a mass claim against Facebook. People in the Netherlands can also vote each year for the Big Brother Award from the Bits of Freedom foundation. The prize goes to the ‘privacy offender of the year’, which always draws media attention. Possible fines and public shaming are not the only incentives to invest in users’ privacy. In a survey by IT network company Cisco, as many as 74 per cent of companies reported a significant positive effect on [customer loyalty and trust](https://www.cisco.com/c/dam/en/us/products/collateral/security/2020-data-privacy-cybersecurity-series-jan-2020.pdf).

## Diversity

Where attention to privacy can lead to more loyal customers, a diverse workforce can lead to better processes and products. That is no different in software development. [Research](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/326079170_Diversity_in_software_engineering) shows that diverse teams of software developers collaborate more efficiently and show more creativity. Thinking from multiple perspectives can also help digital solutions fit the needs of a broader audience. Amazon even states that ‘[inclusive design](https://www.wherewomenwork.com/Career/1730/Amazon-solutions-architect-Suzie-Miller)’, which starts from the needs of people with disabilities, produces better products for everyone.

There is an even more pressing argument for diversity: the ICT sector has struggled for years with hard-to-fill vacancies. Even during corona times, the vacancy rate is high: about 43 vacancies per thousand jobs, compared with 24 for all sectors [on average](https://www.uwv.nl/overuwv/Images/uwv-duiding-arbeidsmarktontwikkelingen-oktober-2020.pdf). One solution to this shortage is to engage groups that are traditionally under-represented in ICT. The share of female software developers in the Netherlands is particularly low, at just 8.4 per cent of the total number of software developers ([source](https://insights.stackoverflow.com/survey/2020#demographics)).

Although this imbalance is partly due to the still modest number of women choosing a technical education, software companies also bear responsibility. A non-inclusive corporate culture is one of the main [reasons](https://www.accenture.com/_acnmedia/PDF-134/Accenture-A4-GWC-Report-Final1.pdf#zoom=50) why women leave tech companies. This masculine culture—also known as ‘brogrammer culture’—also makes it harder to hire women at all, because it seeps into recruitment and selection. For example, women may feel less addressed by a vacancy, or ultimately lose out to a male candidate. Software companies that actively attract and retain people beyond the usual pool improve their chances of success in a labour market plagued by shortages.

## Energy consumption

It is only a matter of time before software companies are drawn into the current debate about data centres and their energy use. For now, this mainly concerns the tech giants who are placing their ‘hyperscalers’ in the Dutch polder and the municipalities that grant permits. Software companies are also key players in this issue. Developers of digital services often buy the cloud services that run in these data centres. For instance, Netflix largely relies on Amazon’s cloud, and Zoom uses cloud services from Amazon, Microsoft and Oracle. Software companies can influence data centres’ energy consumption—indirectly—by choosing a cloud provider based on the extent to which the data centre uses renewable energy and on the facility’s energy efficiency.

Developers can also build their software to require less memory, data storage or data traffic. That gives them a direct influence on data centres’ energy use. For example, cleaning up and restructuring code (‘software refactoring’) can make a programme substantially more energy-efficient. Unfortunately, energy-efficient software is still largely uncharted territory; programmers’ knowledge in this area is [limited](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/308789636_What_do_programmers_know_about_the_energy_consumption_of_software), and measuring the energy consumption of different software components is complex. So it takes a clear intent during development to limit a product’s footprint.

This applies in particular to software that uses artificial intelligence. Training the underlying models uses [a lot of energy](https://medium.com/pcmag-access/ai-could-save-the-world-if-it-doesnt-ruin-the-environment-first-c8cd7e0de76c), and energy use rises exponentially for the last few percentage points of extra ‘accuracy’. Developers of facial recognition software could ask themselves whether those few extra correctly recognised faces outweigh the extra energy used to improve the model. The European Commission is exploring ways to encourage energy-efficient AI development. An ‘eco-label’ for software is one possible [avenue](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7225509/) being explored in academia.

Software companies operate in a challenging playing field. They face persistent labour market shortages, and both citizens and government are setting ever more demands on digital solutions. For these companies, themes such as data protection and AI ethics, diversity and energy efficiency are not empty phrases or attempts at ‘greenwashing’. They are a pressing necessity. It helps that investments here can lead to digital products that are better for people, nature and society.

# A cyberattack can have major consequences

[Report](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/rapporten/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

Business operations grinding to a halt, customers walking away; a cyberattack can severely damage a company. The costs Dutch companies incur as a result of such attacks are among the highest in the world.

DP World, a large Australian port operator, had to [close](https://www.volkskrant.nl/buitenland/australische-havens-weer-open-na-cyberaanval-wel-achterstand-van-30-000-zeecontainers~bb4dfe8f/) four ports after a cyberattack at the end of 2023. The systems were taken offline from the internet, so lorries could no longer drop off or pick up cargo at the ports. Thirty thousand containers were stranded on site. Operations were only back to their previous level after a week. Earlier this year, German battery manufacturer Varta was also [hit](https://www.security.nl/posting/830874/Productie+batterijfabrikant+Varta+ligt+nog+altijd+stil+wegens+cyberaanval#:~:text=De%20Duitse%20batterijfabrikant%20Varta%20is,de%20productie%20nog%20stil%20ligt) by a cyberattack. The company had to shut down five factories. Because the finance department was also affected, publication of the annual report was postponed. Varta’s share price fell by almost five per cent. Worldwide, a [quarter](https://www.paloaltonetworks.com/content/dam/pan/en_US/assets/pdf/reports/state-of-ot-security-report-2024.pdf) of industrial companies have had to halt operations at some point due to a cyberattack.

## Alertness ebbs away

Although ABN AMRO’s research shows that organisations are more aware of cyber risks after an attack, there is no guarantee that this alertness will be lasting after a successful attack, says Arwi van der Sluijs, Managing Director of cybersecurity service provider NFIR. “Half a year after an attack we get a call asking whether the measures ‘really have to be that strict’.” NFIR focuses specifically on the upper end of small and medium-sized enterprises, healthcare institutions, municipalities and provincial institutions.

In the Netherlands, median costs due to cyberattacks in 2023 were around 21,000 dollars per company, insurer [Hiscox](https://www.hiscoxgroup.com/sites/group/files/documents/2023-10/Hiscox-Cyber-Readiness-Report-2023.pdf) reports. This puts our country second worldwide in terms of highest financial damage from cybercrime, behind the United Kingdom and just ahead of the United States.

## Damage from cyberattacks takes many forms

Insurer Hiscox sheds light in its annual [Cyber Readiness Report](https://www.hiscoxgroup.com/sites/group/files/documents/2023-10/Hiscox-Cyber-Readiness-Report-2023.pdf) on the different negative consequences companies experienced due to a cyberattack. Most reported in 2023 were costs for notifying customers (31 per cent of the affected companies), followed by partner data leaks (26 per cent).

Strikingly, the latter figure increased by as much as four percentage points compared with the previous year — a confirmation that cyber risks are not limited to a company’s own operations. At 16 per cent of companies, such incidents even led to the loss of partners.

A quarter of organisations reported negative effects on the brand or corporate reputation; at around a fifth, customers walked away, or they experienced difficulties attracting new customers. Many companies also saw their performance worsen (24 per cent); at 21 per cent, there was even a threat to the organisation’s viability.

## Measures to reduce risks

Entrepreneurs should protect themselves against cyberattacks. You can do this in three relatively simple steps. First, carry out a risk analysis to map which matters are crucial for keeping the business running and maintaining service levels. Second, take measures such as encouraging safe behaviour among employees, recording who owns specific data and discussing cyber risks with partners and suppliers. Finally, develop procedures to detect and handle cyber incidents.

# Europe is still a long way from its own chip industry

[Analysis](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/analyses/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

Without semiconductors, the digital economy stops. This is exactly where Europe proves vulnerable. Production is largely outsourced to Asian foundries, and the design of advanced chips also mostly sits outside Europe. The European Chips Act was meant to turn the tide, but it has run into a complex reality.

In the chip sector, Europe relies heavily on foreign production capacity. Only six per cent of global chip production takes place in Europe and the Middle East. Asia accounts for more than 80 per cent of production. The United States supplies eight per cent of all chips, but it has the strongest grip on design, patents and software. Europe lacks that control. Most chips made here are relatively simple: for cars, household appliances and industrial applications. Advanced chips — needed for smartphones, AI or military technology — mainly come from Taiwan and South Korea.

## Large dependencies despite strong local players

ASML is Europe’s trump card. The Veldhoven-based company is the only one in the world that supplies machines for extreme ultraviolet lithography (EUV), the technology used to make the most advanced chips. But ASML supplies the world, not just Europe.

Germany has strong positions in power electronics with Infineon and Bosch; chips that switch and regulate electrical energy efficiently, for example in cars and solar panels. Players such as Dutch NXP and Franco‑Swiss STMicroelectronics focus on embedded chips. These are microchips built into devices or vehicles to perform one specific task, such as controlling a motor or reading a sensor. For the most advanced chips, Europe has no fabs and depends mainly on Taiwan and South Korea.

The Draghi report from 2024 calls the lack of production capacity a strategic risk. Taiwan, home to the world’s largest chipmaker TSMC, is considered by China to be a breakaway province and has lived for years under the threat of military annexation. In a crisis, chip supplies could stop abruptly. Even in segments where Europe is traditionally strong, such as car chips, vulnerability turns out to be high. During the coronavirus pandemic, car factories came to a standstill due to a shortage of simple chips. Large European chip companies are largely dependent on foundries in Asia. Car brands are relatively small customers there, which put them lower on the priority list when demand returned after the first lockdowns.

## Chips as a priority in Brussels

With the European Chips Act, Europe took a first step in 2023 to reduce its dependence on semiconductors. With € 43 billion in public and private investment, Europe aims to double its market share in chip production to 20 per cent by 2030. Europe is trying to catch up in advanced chips, while also building further on its existing strength in simpler chips.

There are plenty of initiatives in that second category. Local champion STMicroelectronics is expanding production, including by building a new factory in Italy. A factory by Taiwan’s TSMC is coming to Dresden in partnership with European players Bosch, Infineon and NXP. Production of advanced chips, by contrast, has yet to get off the ground. The planned megafab by GlobalFoundries and STMicroelectronics in France has been halted, and construction of Intel’s advanced factory in Germany has been pushed back to 2029.

## Chip Act 2.0

Because the Chips Act has not delivered enough overall, the European Commission is considering a ‘Chips Act 2.0’. The chip industry sees value in a programme that supports the entire chain, from design and materials to machines and fabs. The European Parliament is calling for investment in AI chips, but the industry itself argues for continued support for older chip technology as well, so that chips for cars, machinery and power grids can also come from Europe. The new programme will be presented before the summer.

The road to strategic autonomy in chips is tough. The supply chain branches worldwide and is highly capital‑intensive. New factories cost tens of billions and take years to become operational. The revision of the Chips Act signals the ambition for a serious change of course. Europe does not have to do everything itself — which is impossible anyway — but it does need enough capacity not to stand by helplessly when geopolitics cause shocks.

# Energy reduction is a matter for the entire cloud chain

[Column](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/column.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

It is not only data centres that should be concerned with reducing emissions and energy use. In fact, the entire chain should take responsibility for a greener digital landscape.

We consume digital services on our smartphones, laptops and TVs with great enthusiasm. A large share of those services runs from the cloud, or more concretely: on servers housed in mostly large-scale data centres. With their prominent place in the landscape and substantial electricity consumption, data centres underline what we already knew: the digital economy does not run on thin air.

As the unromantic embodiment of our digital economy, data centres are often a topic of debate. How many data centres can and does a small country like the Netherlands want to host? Will we meet our Climate Agreement targets if we also want to be a European hub for data traffic? At the same time, it is encouraging and motivating to see the steps the entire ICT chain is already taking to reduce the cloud’s ultimate energy consumption. Opportunities lie in the data centre itself, the servers housed there, and the digital services that run on those servers.

That at least some of those opportunities are already being seized is proven by the sharply increased efficiency of data centres. Global electricity consumption by data centres rose by only six per cent between 2010 and 2018, while data traffic to and from data centres increased tenfold and data storage capacity [rose by a factor of 25](https://www.gwern.net/docs/cs/2020-masanet.pdf). Thanks to greater scale and the use of innovative, less energy-intensive cooling techniques, data centres are also keeping their own energy use well under control. In theory, it is even possible to generate all electricity needed for cooling from the data centre’s own residual heat. Unfortunately, such a fully circular set-up is still one for the future, although residual heat is already regularly used externally, for example to heat a swimming pool or a block of flats.

The server owner also contributes to the relatively slow rise in energy use. This can be a hosting company that rents space in a data centre, or a party that also owns the data centre itself, such as large cloud service providers like Microsoft and Google, or companies with their own IT infrastructure. Using newer models often already delivers gains in terms of higher capacity and reduced energy use. In addition to replacing hardware, smart use of it can also reduce electricity consumption. For example, lightly used or idle servers consume a relatively large amount of power. In such cases, you can limit energy use by switching on ‘eco mode’. Using this (temporary) sleep mode delivered an [energy reduction](https://amsterdameconomicboard.com/nieuws/eco-mode-bespaart-extra-energie) of 7 per cent at KPN and had no noticeable impact on the performance of the servers concerned. Finally, it pays to reduce the number of servers and increase their utilisation. You can do this through virtualisation. One physical server then gets multiple virtual representations and can be used by multiple parties at the same time.

Data centres and their servers are not there for show. They host the websites we visit, give us access to software applications like Word and Netflix, and store our precious photos. We call on servers in the cloud every day, which drives up data centres’ electricity consumption. It is unlikely that users will stop using these cloud services for that reason, as they meet a need. But website and software developers can make changes to cut the amount of memory, data traffic or computing power their product requires. You can already easily view websites’ CO₂ footprint via the [Website Carbon Calculator](https://www.websitecarbon.com/), and several methods exist to reduce that footprint. Despite efforts in academia to develop a set of proven methods and guidelines, energy-aware programming is unfortunately still a [relatively unknown phenomenon](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/308789636_What_do_programmers_know_about_the_energy_consumption_of_software) for many software developers.

At first glance, websites, software and data have no other form than the pixels on our phone, laptop or TV screens. That makes the link between the digital services we use and the associated energy consumption less obvious. Yet there is another, more eye-catching manifestation that continues to stir emotions: the data centres rising in the polders. These complexes are proof that our digital activities do have a footprint, and their dominant presence should spur all parties in the ICT chain to do their part.

# Europe wants to join the AI race

[Analysis](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/analyses/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

Europe wants to get a grip on artificial intelligence, but is not yet a serious contender in the global AI race. Most computing power, investment and leading companies are in the United States or China. Yet initiatives are emerging in Europe to win back ground, with its own infrastructure, investment and AI start-ups that excel in niches.

European AI start-ups raise far less capital than their US competitors. In 2024, around 90 billion dollars went to US AI start-ups and scale-ups, according to Dealroom data. In Europe the amount stalled at just under 16 billion. According to Maarten Cleeren of Techleap, that is problematic. “In ten years’ time there will be no company that does not use AI. If we do not put money into the tech companies that support this, we have a problem.”

## Enormous investment

For now, European AI start-ups are fishing in a much smaller funding pool and are less likely to grow into heavyweights. The investments that such AI heavyweights in turn make are staggering. US company OpenAI, known for the smart chatbot ChatGPT, teamed up earlier this year with fellow American Oracle and Japan’s SoftBank under the name Stargate. In this joint venture, the technology companies will invest at least 500 billion dollars in AI infrastructure over the coming years.

China’s relatively low share of global AI investment is mainly because innovation there is largely driven by big technology companies such as Baidu, Tencent and Alibaba, which work closely with the government. Due to limited transparency in the capital market, many investments also remain out of sight.

The results of China’s efforts are, however, drawing more attention. For example, Chinese start-up Butterfly Effect recently launched the AI agent [Manus](https://www.businessinsider.com/manus-ai-china-agent-hype-deepseek-2025-3?international=true&r=US&IR=T#:~:text=,others%20are%20calling%20it%20overhyped), which performs complex tasks from start to finish with minimal human input. The AI model DeepSeek drew astonishment at the end of 2024 because it was developed with much less computing power, yet delivered results comparable to the well-known language models from, among others, OpenAI and Meta. This makes the AI race not only a contest between US tech giants, but rather a global race with the United States and China at its centre. Europe has not yet managed to secure a serious position in this race.

## Criticism of AI legislation

The AI Act adopted by the European Commission in March 2024 is the world’s first comprehensive legislative package for artificial intelligence. The regulation imposes strict requirements on so-called high-risk applications and meets resistance in parts of the tech sector. “Potentially disastrous,” is how industry association EU Tech Chamber described the AI Act; the rules would impose “stringent obligations” that [stifle](https://www.euronews.com/next/2023/12/15/potentially-disastrous-for-innovation-tech-sector-says-eu-ai-act-goes-too-far#:~:text=%27Potentially%20disastrous%27%3A%20Tech%20sector%20says,that%20underpin%20many%20downstream) innovation.

Some AI entrepreneurs fear high compliance costs and legal uncertainty. “Founders do not know exactly where the boundaries lie,” says Cleeren of Techleap. “Companies will be reluctant to bring certain AI features to the European market that are launched in the United States.”

## Investing in AI factories

Even so, Europe is now trying to win back ground. In February 2025, the European Commission launched the InvestAI initiative, intended to unlock 200 billion euros in investment in artificial intelligence. On 10 April the AI Continent Plan was presented, which includes thirteen ‘AI factories’ and even five ‘giga-factories’. These facilities will make high-grade infrastructure available for training large models.

There is also plenty of initiative from technology companies. Iliad Group, a French tech and telecoms conglomerate, announced in February an investment of 3 billion euros in sovereign AI infrastructure. It is investing in the large data centres of subsidiary OpCore and serves the market with AI compute through cloud company Scaleway. Mistral, a French company that develops advanced AI models itself, is one of Scaleway’s customers. France wants to become an important hub for artificial intelligence.

## Place in the value chain

An analysis by consultant McKinsey shows that Europe has secured an important place in [different links](https://www.mckinsey.com/~/media/mckinsey/business%20functions/quantumblack/our%20insights/time%20to%20place%20our%20bets%20europes%20ai%20opportunity/time-to-place-our-bets-europes-ai-opportunity.pdf) of the AI value chain. This applies, for example, to the machines used to make advanced chips, in which Eindhoven-based ASML has virtually a monopoly. IT service providers, including Capgemini, Ordina and Tietoevry, which help companies embed AI in their processes, also have a relatively large market share. Although Europe lags in developing so-called foundation models – the AI models trained on huge amounts of data that can generate new content on that basis – Europe’s market share is growing, according to the consultant. The same applies to the applications developed on the basis of these models.

In the Netherlands, Cleeren of Techleap also sees AI companies excelling in specific components and thus securing an important position within the value chain. “Weaviate uses AI to find and understand information based on what you mean, not just what you type,” he says. “And Deeploy is a platform that helps companies with a responsible implementation of AI.”

Europe is taking part in the development of foundation models, but the biggest players are in the US and China. Europe’s potential now lies mainly in specialised applications, responsible use of AI and a growing network of start-ups and infrastructure projects. Initiatives such as InvestAI and the rise of companies like Mistral lay the groundwork, although the road to a level playing field remains long.

# Europe aims for technological self-reliance

[Analysis](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/analyses/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

After years of lagging behind tech giants from the United States and China, the European Union is focusing on “digital sovereignty”. This means being less dependent on foreign technology by investing in a thriving domestic tech ecosystem. The road is still long, but there are hopeful signs.

At the end of March, thirteen European ministers met to discuss digitalisation and the tech sector. A key topic was Europe’s digital autonomy — or rather its lack, which has been seen as a greater vulnerability since President Trump took office. Calls for digital self-sufficiency are growing louder on four fronts in particular: the cloud, artificial intelligence, the business climate for young technology companies, and the chip industry.

During the summit, the ministers decided, among other things, to fast-track the revision of the European Chips Act — previously planned for 2026 — to accelerate investment in the semiconductor sector and reduce dependence on Asian suppliers. According to the ministers, strategic technologies such as chips, artificial intelligence (AI) and the cloud must also be addressed in conjunction. They also advocate larger public procurement to help local players scale up.

## Cloud market dominated by US players

A majority of European governments and companies run on US-origin cloud infrastructure: Amazon Web Services, Microsoft Azure and Google Cloud. Together these parties control around 70 to 80 per cent of the European [cloud market](https://sciencebusiness.net/news/ict/new-franco-german-cloud-computing-project-set-standards-and-challenge-us-market-leaders#:~:text=However%2C%20with%20almost%2070%20per,for%20Cyber%20Studies%20%26%20Research). The remainder is fragmented across dozens of smaller providers, including European players such as SAP, Deutsche Telekom, OVHcloud and Orange. No European player commands more than a few per cent market share. SAP and Deutsche Telekom top the European ranking with just [2 per cent of the market](https://www.srgresearch.com/articles/european-cloud-providers-continue-to-grow-but-still-lose-market-share#:~:text=been%20Amazon%2C%20Microsoft%20and%20Google,list%20of%20national%20and%20regional) each.

The Dutch government also appears to be heavily dependent on cloud services from the US tech giants. Almost half (44 per cent) of the cloud services the government uses fall into the “public cloud” category and therefore rely on the infrastructure of these parties. In many cases, a risk assessment has not been carried out, according to a recent report by the Algemene Rekenkamer with the ominous subtitle “Dark clouds are gathering”. According to Simon Besteman of industry association Dutch Cloud Community, the risks are substantial. “If your data are in a data centre of a cloud provider with a US UBO, then the US government can access them. It does not make a damn bit of difference if the data centre is in Europe.”

The industry association warns that technological dependence can put countries and companies in a tight spot, especially if geopolitical relations worsen. For example, Amsterdam Trade Bank (ATB), a subsidiary of Russia’s Alfa Bank, was cut off from IT services by Amazon and Microsoft after the invasion of Ukraine, causing major disruption: employees lost access to applications and email, and digital payments ground to a halt. It fuels fears of a scenario in which US tech companies stop providing services to Europe if political pressure mounts. “If the United States want to put pressure on us, they can do it via IT,” says Besteman.

US clouds have grown steadily in recent years, even faster than local providers. Synergy Research calculated that between 2017 and 2022 the European cloud market became five times larger, but the combined [market share](https://www.srgresearch.com/articles/european-cloud-providers-continue-to-grow-but-still-lose-market-share#:~:text=New%20data%20from%20Synergy%20Research,and%20Deutsche%20Telekom%20are%20the) of European providers fell from 27 per cent to 13 per cent. Amazon, Microsoft and Google invest billions every quarter in new European data centres.

European providers are therefore retreating to niches: they serve specific local needs or partner with the big three in hybrid arrangements. For example, French firm OVHcloud provides cloud services that meet strict European regulations. In some cases, organisations combine these services with those of Amazon Web Services or Microsoft Azure, so they can benefit from the scale and functionality of US platforms while keeping sensitive data within Europe and under European rules.

The dominance of US cloud providers also extends to the backbone of artificial intelligence: the advanced servers on which AI models are trained and run. The United States accounts for about [70 per cent](https://geopolitique.eu/en/2025/02/10/financing-infrastructure-for-a-competitive-european-ai/#:~:text=A%20risk%20of%20strategic%20dependence,higher%20than%20in%20the%20US.) of this global AI compute capacity, the vast majority — 80 per cent — of which is in the hands of the hyperscalers. Europe has to make do with just 4 per cent of global capacity.

## Europe as the tail-ender in the AI race

This uneven distribution is indicative of Europe’s position in the broader AI race. European AI startups raise far less capital than their US counterparts. In 2024, around 90 billion dollars went to US AI startups and scale-ups, according to Dealroom data. In Europe, that amount stalled at just under 16 billion. According to Maarten Cleeren of Techleap, that is problematic. “In ten years’ time there will be no company that does not use AI. If we do not put money into the tech companies that support this, we have a problem.”

For now, European AI startups are fishing in a much smaller financing pond and are less likely to grow into heavyweights. The investments that such AI heavyweights make, in turn, are enormous. US company OpenAI, known for the smart chatbot ChatGPT, teamed up earlier this year with compatriot Oracle and Japan’s SoftBank under the name Stargate. In this joint venture, the technology companies will invest at least 500 billion dollars in AI infrastructure over the coming years.

The fact that China’s share of global AI investment is relatively low is mainly because innovation there is largely driven by large technology companies such as Baidu, Tencent and Alibaba, which work closely with the government. Due to limited transparency in the capital market, many investments also remain out of sight.

The results of China’s efforts are, however, attracting increasing attention. For example, Chinese startup Butterfly Effect recently launched the AI agent [Manus](https://www.businessinsider.com/manus-ai-china-agent-hype-deepseek-2025-3?international=true&r=US&IR=T#:~:text=,others%20are%20calling%20it%20overhyped), which performs complex tasks from start to finish with minimal human input. The AI model DeepSeek caused amazement at the end of 2024 because it was developed with far less compute power, yet achieved results comparable to the well-known language models of, among others, OpenAI and Meta. This makes the AI race not only a contest between US tech giants, but rather a global race with the United States and China at its centre. Europe has so far failed to secure a serious position in this race.

The AI Act adopted by the European Commission in March 2024 is the world’s first comprehensive legislative package for artificial intelligence. The regulation imposes strict requirements on so-called high-risk applications and faces resistance in parts of the tech sector. “Potentially disastrous,” said industry association EU Tech Chamber of the AI Act; the rules would impose “stringent obligations” that [stifle](https://www.euronews.com/next/2023/12/15/potentially-disastrous-for-innovation-tech-sector-says-eu-ai-act-goes-too-far#:~:text=%27Potentially%20disastrous%27%3A%20Tech%20sector%20says,that%20underpin%20many%20downstream) innovation. Some AI entrepreneurs fear high compliance costs and legal uncertainty. “Founders do not know exactly where the boundaries lie,” says Cleeren of Techleap. “Companies will be reluctant to bring certain AI features to the European market that are launched in the United States.”

## European tech startups face many barriers

That uncertainty does not stand alone. Europe is known for its strong regulation, but for fast-growing technology companies it rarely works to their advantage. Startups that want to scale up face 27 national tax systems, labour laws and insolvency rules. Each country has its own interpretation of corporate tax, shareholder structures and employment protection — something that hinders rapid international growth. According to the [State of European Tech report](https://www.investeurope.eu/media/g3yobbcs/soet2024_report.pdf), 47 per cent of founders and investors see regulation and policymaking as a major barrier to growth.

Another frequently cited frustration is limited access to growth capital. Since 2015, European startups in the growth phase have raised around 300 billion dollars, but according to the authors of the report this could have been double if they could as easily close large rounds as in the US. European companies make it through the first funding rounds, but fall back at investments from 15 million dollars: only 4.1 per cent reach that threshold, compared to 8.3 per cent in the US. One reason is the lack of deep capital buffers. European pension funds, for example, invest only 0.01 per cent of their assets in global venture capital, more than three times less than their US counterparts. As a result, it remains difficult for European startups to scale up to the kind of tech companies that underpin digital autonomy.

## Europe relies heavily on foreign chip production

But without physical industry, capital is not enough. This is painfully clear in the chip sector, where Europe relies heavily on foreign production capacity. Only 6 per cent of global chip production takes place in Europe and the Middle East. Asia accounts for more than 80 per cent of production. The United States supplies 8 per cent of all chips, but has the greatest control over design, patents and software. Europe lacks that control. Most chips made here are relatively simple: for cars, household appliances and industrial applications. Advanced chips — needed for smartphones, AI or military technology — mainly come from Taiwan and South Korea.

ASML is Europe’s trump card. The Veldhoven-based company is the only one in the world that supplies machines for “extreme ultraviolet lithography” (EUV), the technology used to make the most advanced chips. But ASML supplies the world, not just Europe. Germany has strong positions in power electronics with Infineon and Bosch; chips that switch and control electrical energy efficiently, for example in cars and solar panels. Players such as the Dutch NXP and the Franco-Swiss STMicroelectronics focus on “embedded chips”. These are microchips built into devices or vehicles to perform a single specific task, such as controlling a motor or reading a sensor. For the most advanced chips, Europe has no factories and depends mainly on Taiwan and South Korea.

The 2024 Draghi report identifies the lack of production capacity as a strategic risk. Taiwan, home to the world’s largest chipmaker TSMC, is considered by China to be a renegade province and has lived for years under the threat of military annexation. In a crisis, the supply of chips can stop abruptly. Even in segments where Europe is traditionally strong, such as automotive chips, vulnerability is high. During the coronavirus pandemic, car factories came to a standstill due to a shortage of simple chips. Large European chip companies — such as NXP, Infineon and STMicroelectronics — design these, but have them largely produced at foundries in Asia. There, car brands are relatively small customers, which meant they were lower on the priority list when demand returned after the first lockdowns.

## The need for home-grown cloud solutions is felt

There is movement, however. On multiple fronts, there is a growing awareness in Europe that technological autonomy is only achievable with substantial investment, smart regulation and local alternatives. “Within the Dutch government, the question is no longer whether we should reduce dependence on US cloud companies, but how,” says Besteman of the Dutch Cloud Community. The House of Representatives recently adopted a motion calling on the government to tender for a “state cloud under full Dutch control”.

Besteman does not expect such a government cloud to appear overnight, but such an intention can set the local cloud market in motion. “If you want European cloud providers to invest further in their services, you first need a buying signal.” Similar discussions are now taking place in the business community, Besteman notes. “They are also considering how safe their data really are, and what a plan B could be — an exit strategy.”

Europe is not aiming for a single alternative cloud solution that can match Microsoft Azure or Amazon Web Services in scale, but for a network of providers between which data and workloads can be moved and distributed easily. ECOFED is an example of such a “federated cloud”. The Dutch project is developing a solution that decouples software from the physical cloud infrastructure, giving users more freedom in choosing and combining cloud services.

The Dutch company LeaseWeb is also making its voice heard. In March, the cloud provider presented the first results of its contribution to the IPCEI-CIS project — the same initiative that includes ECOFED. The company is building a cloud platform specifically designed to comply with European laws and regulations. The platform forms the heart of LeaseWeb’s so-called European Cloud Campus — a broader investment in sovereign cloud technology, local data centres and collaboration with knowledge institutions.

## Investing in AI factories

Europe is also trying to gain ground in artificial intelligence. In February 2025, the European Commission launched the InvestAI initiative, intended to unlock 200 billion euros in investment in artificial intelligence. On 10 April, the AI Continent Plan was presented, which includes thirteen “AI factories” and even five “giga factories”. These facilities will make high-quality infrastructure available for training large models.

Technology companies are also taking plenty of initiative. The Iliad Group, a French tech and telecoms conglomerate, announced in February an investment of 3 billion euros in sovereign AI infrastructure. It is investing in the large data centres of subsidiary OpCore and serves the market with AI compute via cloud company Scaleway. Mistral, a French company that develops advanced AI models itself, is one of Scaleway’s customers. France wants to become an important hub for artificial intelligence.

An analysis by consultant McKinsey shows that Europe has secured an important place within [different links](https://www.mckinsey.com/~/media/mckinsey/business%20functions/quantumblack/our%20insights/time%20to%20place%20our%20bets%20europes%20ai%20opportunity/time-to-place-our-bets-europes-ai-opportunity.pdf) of the AI value chain. This applies, for example, to the machines used to make advanced chips, in which Eindhoven-based ASML has a virtual monopoly. IT service providers, including Capgemini, Ordina and Tietoevry, which help companies embed AI in their processes, also have a relatively large market share. Although Europe lags in the development of so-called foundation models — the AI models trained on vast amounts of data and able to generate new content — the consultant says Europe’s market share is growing. The same applies to the applications developed on the basis of these models.

In the Netherlands, Cleeren of Techleap also sees AI companies excelling in specific areas and thus securing an important position within the value chain. “Weaviate uses AI to find and understand information based on what you mean, not just what you type,” he says. “And Deeploy is a platform that helps companies with a responsible implementation of AI.”

## Europe wants to be attractive to startups again

The narrative that Europe is unfriendly to tech entrepreneurs is giving way to a new story. In the State of European Tech 2024, a large majority of European founders said they would incorporate their company in Europe again if they could make that choice anew. The current geopolitical situation can even act as a flywheel, according to Cleeren: “America is turning inward, the market is destabilising. This creates space for companies in Europe.”

The European Commission, meanwhile, wants to put an end to the patchwork of laws and regulations with the “28th regime”. This allows a company to register under a set of rules that apply in all member states. The plans are part of the broader European Innovation Act. It should make it easier for startups to raise growth capital, including across borders. At present, investors run into different tax rules, supervisors and legal structures in each country. Europe wants to make that simpler, so that funds and investors can operate more smoothly in multiple countries.

Reforms should also make it more attractive for startups to reward staff with share options. Since 2019, eleven countries have relaxed their national regulations, including Germany, France, Spain and the Netherlands. The European Commission is now investigating whether an overarching system is possible, as part of broader efforts to improve the business climate.

At the same time, there is growing political pressure to get pension funds to play a more active role. They currently put an average of only 0.007 per cent of their assets into venture capital. In theory they can invest larger amounts, but strict rules and internal guidelines discourage this. European institutions such as the Commission, the Investment Bank and the Investment Fund see pension funds as a key link in increasing the amount of growth capital in Europe, and are exploring ways to stimulate this — for example through risk-sharing via EU funds or adjusted capital requirements.

## Chips as a priority in Brussels

With the European Chips Act, Europe took a first step in 2023 to reduce its dependence on semiconductors. With 43 billion euros in public and private investment, Europe wants to double its own market share in chip production to 20 per cent by 2030. On the one hand, Europe is trying to catch up in advanced chips; on the other, it wants to build further on its existing strength in simpler chips.

There are plenty of initiatives in the latter category. Local champion STMicroelectronics is expanding production, including by building a new factory in Italy. A factory by Taiwan’s TSMC is coming to Dresden in cooperation with European players Bosch, Infineon and NXP. Production of advanced chips, by contrast, has yet to get off the ground. The planned megafactory of GlobalFoundries and STMicroelectronics in France has been put on hold, and construction of Intel’s advanced factory in Germany has been pushed back to 2029.

Because the Chips Act has ultimately not delivered enough, the European Commission is considering a “Chips Act 2.0”. The chip industry sees merit in a programme that supports the entire chain, from design and materials to machines and factories. From the European Parliament come calls for investment in AI chips, but the industry itself argues for continued support for older chip technology too, so that chips for cars, machines and power grids can also come from Europe. The new programme will be presented before the summer.

## Europe opts for technological decisiveness

To seize all the opportunities, structural measures and substantial investment are necessary. According to the Draghi report, 800 billion euros in public-private spending is needed annually to make Europe more technologically independent and economically resilient. The investments must focus on high-tech sectors such as semiconductors and artificial intelligence, but also on decarbonisation and defence.

Europe has strong assets to build on: renowned knowledge institutions, a large internal market and niche champions such as ASML and Mistral. At the same time, the gap with the US and China is considerable, especially in cloud infrastructure, advanced chips and AI. The coming years will show whether Europe can narrow that gap.

For years, Europe was mainly a regulator: influential, but reactive. Now the realisation has sunk in that regulation alone is not enough and that Europe itself must invest in technology, infrastructure and production capacity. The contours of digital autonomy are taking shape, although Europe will have to act faster and sharper than before.

# Europe’s software market in search of scale and niches

[Analysis](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/analyses/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

Europe’s technology sector struggles to scale potential winners due to a lack of growth capital. Yet the continent has a broad range of innovative players. In the Netherlands, almost two-thirds of investment in young software companies goes to parties that apply artificial intelligence.

In 2024, young European technology companies collectively raised about 54 billion dollars in venture capital. Almost half of that, over 23 billion, flowed to Software-as-a-Service (SaaS). This makes SaaS the largest category, bigger than digital marketplaces or companies developing physical technology. For investors, the combination of stable subscriptions and scalability is particularly attractive.

During the coronavirus pandemic, abundant capital flowed into technology, helped by low interest rates and investors’ search for scalable business models. SaaS benefited above average. From 2022, however, inflation took hold and central banks raised interest rates, making capital more expensive and correcting valuations. That made investors more cautious, especially with megadeals and loss-making growth companies. Venture capital investment volumes now seem to be stabilising. SaaS remains the centre of gravity of European tech financing, although within this category capital is increasingly going to companies that have woven AI into their core.

## The scalability gap

Within Europe, the United Kingdom attracts the most SaaS investment with over 77 billion dollars in ten years — more than one-third of the European total. France and Germany follow with 35 billion dollars (16 per cent) and 33 billion (15 per cent) respectively. The Netherlands ranks a steady fourth with 9.9 billion, accounting for 4.5 per cent of all European SaaS investment between 2015 and 2025.

Unfortunately, Europe faces a persistent problem: the lack of capital for tech companies to grow into internationally competitive businesses. US start-ups are twice as likely to raise a round of more than 15 million dollars as their European counterparts, according to the 2024 [State of European Tech report](https://www.stateofeuropeantech.com/chapters/investment-levels). As a result, the share of young companies that manage to break through to the scale-up phase is much smaller in Europe. According to the researchers, the cumulative shortfall now amounts to 375 billion dollars — the total amount that young tech companies would have raised over the past ten years if access to larger investments had been as easy as in the United States (US). In the US, a large share of the money even goes to rounds above 100 million dollars, with a dominant role for megadeals above 250 million. Although Europe does not even close that many fewer SaaS deals — last year there were 4,616, versus 5,040 in the US — the centre of gravity remains with smaller rounds.

## Broadly applicable SaaS niches

Venture capital in Europe mainly flows to several recognisable SaaS niches. The largest category is enterprise software — solutions that make organisations’ internal processes more efficient, from HR and planning systems to workflow and ERP tools. For example, German unicorn Personio further strengthened its position in HR software, and compatriot N8N specialises in process automation.

There are also other solutions that apply in virtually every sector. Marketing and communications software remains popular, with marketing platform Brevo as an example. Skeepers helps brands weave customer reviews and other user-generated content smartly into campaigns. Cybersecurity also benefits from broad applicability, combined with a general sense of urgency as cyberattacks become more sophisticated. For instance, French firm Sekoia.io raised growth capital in 2024 for its AI platform that detects threats in real time.

## SaaS for specific sectors

SaaS companies also attract significant financing in the FinTech space. UK-based Volt.io, for example, offers a platform that enables companies to accept payments from international customers, and Qover helps integrate insurance into service offerings across sectors — from mobility to e-commerce. On the other side are players that operate more specifically at the core of financial services providers, such as Backbase, which offers a client platform for financial institutions as a replacement for their legacy IT systems.

This is an example of one of the many vertical SaaS services designed for a specific sector. In healthcare, Doctolib is entering the broader European market with a platform that facilitates appointment management, consultations and patient communication, and Unmind raised growth financing for a platform that helps company employees improve their mental health. In hospitality, Dutch company Mews raised new capital to roll out its platform internationally, and in construction and real estate PlanRadar did so earlier with its project management software.

## AI SaaS as a new engine

Within the broader landscape, AI-driven SaaS is taking up an ever-larger place. In Europe, one-third of software deals now fall into this category, accounting for almost half of investment volume. In the Netherlands, that is even two-thirds. The development is even faster in the US; there, AI SaaS absorbs almost 80 per cent of all SaaS funding.

Even so, Europe produces many innovative, AI-first SaaS companies that specialise in all sorts of niches. The Netherlands even has an above-average number of AI SaaS deals and funding. Cradle, for example, developed an AI model that can design new proteins and sells it as a SaaS service to laboratories. Fellow Dutch company Fourthline uses AI for identity fraud and compliance in the financial sector, and FRISS does the same for insurance risks. TestGorilla makes selection processes smarter and less sensitive to bias, and Sympower uses AI to keep electricity grids in balance.

Germany’s Accure offers a platform for AI analysis of battery data in sectors including mobility and industry, to improve battery and accumulator safety and longevity. UK-based Synthesia uses generative AI to automate the production of corporate films and training videos and raised 180 million dollars in early 2025 at a valuation of over 2 billion. And from Sweden, Lovable is experiencing enormous growth with a platform that uses AI to accelerate and democratise software development. The company was founded in 2023 and reached a valuation of almost 2 billion dollars in a few years.

## Major AI developers set the tone

A large share of investment is concentrated in a few major AI developers that set the tone in the market with megadeals. Their AI systems are offered in two ways: as a building block for software developers to embed AI in their own product, and in the form of chat apps such as ChatGPT (OpenAI) and Claude (Anthropic) for end users. Those who want access to the most powerful features pay a monthly subscription. This makes the revenue model essentially the same as SaaS: software offered via the cloud for a periodic fee.

In the US, the megadeals in question led to enormous capital injections. OpenAI raised 40 billion dollars in March of this year at a valuation of 300 billion, followed by an additional round in the summer. Anthropic secured a further 13 billion dollars in September 2025, and xAI closed a 6 billion round at the end of 2024. Europe has fewer such outliers, but AI SaaS is growing rapidly here too. French company Mistral AI raised 1.7 billion euros in 2025 in a round — 1.3 billion of which came from ASML — doubling its valuation to almost 12 billion. Germany’s Aleph Alpha received half a billion in earlier rounds.

## Success factors

Amid the growth power of these major players, SaaS companies need to position themselves sharply. A key trait of promising providers is that they focus on critical business processes — parts of an organisation that are simply crucial. Fourthline, for example, automates the mandatory identification of customers in the financial sector. Banks must perform this process to comply with the law, and switching to another provider carries risks. FRISS does the same for insurers, who have a strong interest in recognising fraudulent claims. Doctolib supports doctors and patients with appointments and communication; an essential process that is often suboptimally digitised.

There are also opportunities in niches where large but generic AI models fall short. Cradle, which focuses entirely on generative AI for protein design, is trained on a huge database of protein structures and properties, and combines deep knowledge of biology with technical capabilities. Sympower has developed an AI solution that balances electricity grids, and connects both major consumers and energy producers. Collaboration with parties in energy-intensive industries such as agriculture, cement production and data centres requires strong relationships and a great deal of domain knowledge that is not easy to copy.

Finally, it is striking that successful SaaS companies often form a connective layer between multiple parties. N8N’s automation platform, for example, lets different software packages ‘talk’ to each other — from CRM systems and email tools to databases and cloud services. This allows users to automate complex work processes across different applications. Qover provides a connective layer in the insurance world; via APIs, the company enables insurance products to be built directly into, for example, mobility apps or e-commerce platforms. In both cases, it is a software platform that enables others to enrich or optimise their own services.

## The road ahead

Europe’s SaaS market brims with innovation, but the road to global dominance is anything but straightforward. The continent suffers from a structural scalability gap and a more cautious investment culture. However, encouraged by a geopolitical need to become more self-sufficient technologically as well, it is taking several steps in the right direction. On 23 September 2025, Minister Karremans presented a list of [measures](https://www.rijksoverheid.nl/actueel/nieuws/2025/09/23/meer-groei-nederlandse-startups-en-scale-ups-door-meer-kapitaal-talent-en-verlenging-techleap) to support “the tech champions of tomorrow”, with an emphasis on research-intensive key technologies such as cybersecurity, quantum computing and artificial intelligence. The caretaker government is investing 200 million euros in a second fund within the European Tech Champions Initiative (ETCI), and Invest-NL is exploring ways to bring more pension capital to venture capital funds.

The current generation of SaaS companies at any rate shows that there are plenty of opportunities. A focus on business-critical processes, AI applications in specific niches, and building a connective layer are strategies that enable local players to position themselves strongly, even amid the growth power of American parties. The success of Mistral at any rate proves that even capital-intensive models can gain traction here, provided there is sufficient willingness to invest. The task now is to make these occasional successes structural.

# Generative AI enters the sustainability transition

Since 2018, investors have put 793 million dollars into young companies that apply “generative AI” for sustainability purposes. This form of artificial intelligence can create many things based on instructions, such as text, 3D designs and even new materials. However, the potential sustainability gains come with the high energy use of AI technology.

Developments in artificial intelligence, also called AI for short, accelerated after the ultra-smart chatbot ChatGPT was introduced at the end of 2022. Since then, the big technology companies have raced to introduce new AI models that generate ever better answers to increasingly complex questions and, based on written instructions, create impressive images and video material.

Although people often talk about the entertainment value of generative AI and potential productivity gains, there are also potential applications in sustainability. We cannot ignore this perspective now that the effects of global warming are painfully clear in many places. The new technology can complement legislation and regulation, financing and behavioural change.

## Twin Transition: where technology and sustainability meet

The rise of generative AI is a textbook example of the “twin transition”. This term refers to the digital transition and the sustainability transition, which take place at the same time and influence each other. Companies that are further ahead in digitalisation perform, for example, [better](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0040162522007934) on sustainability. Scientific literature also points to a positive contribution by AI to the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) defined by the United Nations. Specifically for the SDGs related to climate change, natural ecosystems and making sectors more sustainable, this applies to 50 of the 55 sub-targets. At the same time, AI also appears to have a dampening effect on 18 of these sub-targets — sometimes at the same time. This is partly due to the large amount of energy needed to develop and use AI models.

Investors see value in AI as a supporter of the sustainability transition. In 2021 — when the technology market showed huge growth after the initial “coronavirus shock” — investors worldwide put 25.2 billion dollars into sustainable AI start-ups, according to data from data collector Net Zero Insights. In 2022, this amount fell to 21.4 billion dollars due to rising interest rates and a generally less favourable investment climate, and 2023 has not been immune to these dynamics either. On 1 December, the counter stood at 18.5 billion.

## Sustainable applications of generative AI

This report explores various sustainable applications of generative AI based on investments in start-ups and scale-ups that are active in this area. These applications are already firmly embraced in the field of corporate reporting and analysis of climate risk and climate impact. Several software companies use AI’s language talent to convert large amounts of company data into clear reports and insights. Virtual assistants are also prominent. They answer questions about sustainability issues in a natural dialogue and can provide advice to the user based on all kinds of data. Together, these activities account for 258 million dollars in investments since 2018.

In addition, generative AI can be used to design new materials and proteins more quickly, with specific applications and benefits. Examples include green fuels, materials optimised for lifespan and recyclability, or enzymes that can break down plastic. Research into such new molecules is traditionally time-consuming with much trial and error. Generative AI can greatly shorten this. Investors have so far put 85 million dollars into young companies in this category.

A more nascent function of generative AI is that of designer or engineer. The technology can, for example, help to personalise clothing so that the pieces produced match market demand better, and the negative environmental impact of unsold — and therefore needlessly produced — clothing is reduced. There are also opportunities in the built environment, such as buildings that are optimised with AI for energy efficiency and material use. Current design methods are expected to be increasingly enhanced with generative AI.

## Developing AI costs energy

Developing generative AI also has a price. The technology works on the basis of large amounts of data, and the associated learning process — also called “training” — requires a lot of computing power. Training GPT-3.0 — one of the predecessors of the current models behind ChatGPT — was accompanied by 502 tonnes of CO2 emissions; the same as the annual emissions of 251 Dutch petrol cars. The constant stream of questions the model has to process also requires considerable computing power and energy. Although the applications outlined above are not used on the same scale as the immensely popular chatbot, it remains important to weigh both the positive and negative effects carefully when developing and applying generative AI.

# The end of the algorithm as a black box

[Column](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/column.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

The time when algorithms were accepted as a black box seems to be over for good. Government and society are calling ever more loudly for transparency. Algorithmic decision-making can in fact fuel inequality. The lawsuit that Uber drivers recently filed against the technology company illustrates this.

Companies no longer seem able to avoid showing how they use algorithms when making decisions.

Taxi drivers demand transparency about how the Uber app works. The underlying algorithms determine a lot: they allocate rides and set the fare. In the background, the app combines all kinds of data to create a profile of the driver. It is unclear exactly what is recorded about the driver and how those data are used in algorithmic decision-making. To remove that asymmetry, drivers are invoking the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) in the Amsterdam court. “Whatever the ruling,” [writes Het Financieele Dagblad](https://fd.nl/ondernemen/1351533/baanbrekende-zaak-over-algoritmes-uber-waarschuwing-voor-andere-bedrijven), “companies that work with algorithms and artificial intelligence will prepare better for new transparency requirements because of this case.”

Can the algorithmic decision-making behind the Uber app be seen as disguised employment? That is one of the drivers’ questions. If the app is indeed an employer and not purely a platform, that would give Uber drivers a different position regarding their appraisal and pay. However, the drivers currently lack a fair basis to enforce a more balanced relationship. For example, they also worry that customers may unconsciously discriminate when they rate their driver. This kind of input carries through into the decisions the algorithms make. With their case, the drivers put their finger on the sore spot: an algorithm works on the basis of data selected or generated by people, and cannot therefore be seen as an objective third party.

Just like people, algorithms make mistakes. In fact, if you are not careful from the start, it is precisely algorithms that make mistakes. Imagine the following: an image-recognition algorithm that is generally quite accurate describes a photo on a good day as “a woman with a surfboard on the beach”. The only problem is that the woman’s loving gaze is not directed at a surfboard at all, but at her white horse. This mistake underlines that it is simplistic to attribute a (super)human kind of intelligence to algorithms – which today form an important basis of artificial intelligence.

If we assume an algorithm that you train rather than program by hand – also called a ‘machine-learning algorithm’ – then the flaw in the example above can lie at roughly two levels. First, it is very likely that the training set contained a large number of photos of surfers on the beach, and fewer of horses. Or the training set may have contained photos of horses, but in a very different context, such as in the woods. In such cases, the chance is smaller that the horse will be recognised as a horse outside the ‘normal’ context for the algorithm (the beach). The other explanation is that the coders, who manually created the descriptions of the photos in the training set, were simply asleep at the wheel. In that case they themselves must structurally have mistaken white horses for surfboards.

Data determine the algorithm’s reality, and people play a leading role in selecting and labelling those data. This means that every algorithm by definition shows a certain degree of bias. Unfortunately, the consequences sometimes go much further than mistaking a horse for a surfboard. Facebook made negative headlines because comments by Black Americans were more likely than those by white users to be flagged as ‘hateful’ by an algorithm that automatically scans content. Amazon automatically selected applicants’ CVs, but went off the rails when it turned out that the underlying algorithm disadvantaged women. The Dutch government did not escape either: earlier this year it halted the SyRi fraud detection system, because it was unclear on what basis people were identified as potential fraudsters.

It is therefore not surprising that artificial intelligence as a theme is being approached ever more critically. [Research by the European Commission](https://www.computable.nl/artikel/nieuws/overheid/6962900/250449/kunstmatige-intelligentie-vraagt-om-nieuwe-regelgeving.html) among companies, governments, academics and developers confirms that an ethical approach to artificial intelligence is top of mind: 90 per cent of the 1,200 respondents are concerned about the possibility that artificial intelligence will violate citizens’ fundamental rights. And 87 per cent of them fear that the use of artificial intelligence will lead to bias and disadvantage certain groups. Most respondents are looking for guidance in the form of new regulations.

However, it will not come down to legislation and regulation. [Recent research](https://www.rijksoverheid.nl/documenten/rapporten/2020/06/30/ek-bijlage-2-juridische-aspecten-van-algoritmen-die-besluiten-nemen-een-verkennend-onderzoek) commissioned by the Ministry of Justice and Security shows that the current legal framework is largely sufficient to “avoid or mitigate the identified risks of algorithmic decision-making”. The GDPR, for example, requires companies to be able to provide insight into the logic behind automated decision-making if this has a potentially negative impact on individuals. Further elaboration in the GDPR itself is lacking, but the Uber lawsuit may form important case law in this light. Data-driven companies will therefore follow this case closely, because the ruling can offer a glimpse into how they will need to explain their algorithms in the future. On their own, they are generally not eager to divulge this information, especially if their algorithms are core to operations and mean a competitive advantage.

Yet the trend seems set, regardless of the ruling in the Uber case. More and more companies are proactively setting out their approach to transparency and ‘algorithmic bias’, whether or not under social pressure. Dutch insurers, for example, see opportunities in big data and artificial intelligence for customer acceptance and premium setting. To ensure responsible use of data, the Verbond van Verzekeraars published an ethical framework in June. Tech giants such as Google and IBM are already sharing their knowledge on this topic more widely. They provide extensive documentation on how they counter bias in algorithms and make tools for software developers available free of charge.

Given the potential injustices that can flow from algorithms and the increased social attention to them, this trend towards self-regulation will only strengthen. It is time for companies – large and small – to look more critically at how they use algorithms to make decisions and how they communicate about this with their customers. In the future, companies’ competitive advantage may lie increasingly in transparency about the algorithm, and less in the algorithm itself.

# The explosive growth of software-as-a-service

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

Steven Peters

Software-as-a-service (SaaS) is a relatively new business model for software companies, in which software is sold as an online subscription service. For several years now, ABN AMRO has successfully gained experience in financing SaaS companies.

## Download the full report

# How 5G can help raise labour productivity

[Analysis](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/analyses/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Sonny Duijn](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/sonny-duijn/index.html)

Kasper Buiting

The Netherlands faces an economic challenge. Our country is struggling with a shortage of labour while the growth of labour productivity — the added value per hour worked — is slowing. The introduction of 5G can help lift productivity further.

Companies will be able to let more devices communicate with each other faster and process large amounts of data. This offers opportunities in industry, transport and agriculture to scale up existing experiments. It is important to prepare now for the arrival of 5G.

## Staff shortages are a daily reality

Raising productivity matters because the other source of economic growth — increasing the number of hours worked — is difficult at present. Security guards, cleaners, industrial workers, IT professionals, drivers, nurses and many other types of workers are hard to find. In five years, the share of companies that experience staff shortages as an obstacle has risen from five to 25 per cent. Shortages are exacerbated by mismatches between the supply and demand for education and experience, and because people cannot or do not always want to travel to where the work is. Staff scarcity therefore seems set to remain for now.

Economic growth in recent years largely rested on an increase in hours worked. This is feasible as long as there is sufficient labour supply. Hospitality made an impression by growing by almost four per cent per year on average from 2014 through 2018. Hospitality businesses benefited as consumers ate, drank and stayed overnight out of home more often, and they met that demand by hiring staff at a similar pace. The number of hours worked rose almost as fast each year as the sector’s added value. When new employees are no longer available, however, the party is over for many hospitality businesses.

To keep meeting growing demand, firms must now focus more on raising productivity. In other words: deliver more value with the same number of employees. Unfortunately, growth in labour productivity in the Netherlands slowed sharply in the past decade. In the coming decades it will be even harder to increase productivity because the average employee is ageing. That means fewer workers can raise their productivity relatively quickly through training and education. Scientific research shows that this way of raising productivity pays [dividends](https://www.cbs.nl/nl-nl/nieuws/2019/51/lagere-productiviteit-hangt-samen-met-groei-zelfstandigen) mainly for young employees.

## Use of capital goods

Higher productivity will therefore have to come mainly from deploying capital goods such as machinery, software and robots. In the past, companies already made great strides here, which made them highly competitive exporters on the global market. This ranges from the use of harvesting machines and milking robots in agriculture to automation in factories. Labour productivity is therefore also crucial for competitiveness.

Companies are currently struggling to take further steps to keep growth up to par. Figures from Statistics Netherlands (CBS) show that in 2018 only one in a hundred companies invested in labour-substituting automation. The bar is rising to ratchet up productivity further. Publications on faltering labour productivity growth refer to the famous economist Robert Gordon, who argues that innovations further back in time created a more fundamental lead. Examples include the invention of the steam engine and electricity, and later the telephone and the car.

## Major business potential thanks to 5G

Even so, there are many ways to prevent a standstill in labour productivity growth. Many of these applications are now in an experimental phase. The new generation of mobile networks, 5G, makes it possible to take these applications to the next stage, provided it is used well.

With its major business potential, 5G differs from its predecessors, which mainly made consumers’ lives easier. With 4G, consumers already enjoy many conveniences, especially in the Netherlands, where coverage is relatively good. Watching a TV series on a mobile phone is no longer exceptional. Although heavy data users among consumers would certainly like an even faster subscription, 5G mainly offers opportunities for the business market.

This concerns the development of technologies that must process large volumes of data. Thanks to 5G, much more data can be processed with higher up- and download speeds. In addition, far more devices than now can exchange information simultaneously via the mobile internet, which supports internet-of-things applications. Another important feature is that communication between devices becomes much faster. This is known as low latency: once a signal is sent, the receiver can get it in a very short time. There is then minimal delay in the network. The network remains only a tool, though: real innovation comes from business.

The new applications that 5G offers unfortunately cannot yet be rolled out on a large scale in the Netherlands today. Frequency auctions still have to take place, standards must be finalised, and a denser network of masts must be built. There are also ongoing discussions on privacy and health. The current auction schedule in the Netherlands was initially delayed because one of the required frequencies (3.5 GHz) was used by Defence as a listening station. This frequency, with more economic potential than the frequencies now being auctioned (700 MHz, 2.1 GHz and 1.4 GHz), will be auctioned in the Netherlands in 2022.

It is nevertheless important that companies already prepare for new possibilities so they are ready once the infrastructure is in place. A quick start matters for maintaining competitiveness. Foreign companies are likely exploring this as well or even further ahead, because in many other countries the auction of 5G frequencies started earlier.

## Opportunities for industry and logistics

The opportunities of 5G lie mainly in the ability to improve existing processes. This applies, for example, to industry, where 5G is seen as an essential link for a future in which production machines and entire factories are connected online to their suppliers, logistics partners and customers. This is also known as Smart Industry.

Thanks to the 5G connection, devices and machines will soon be able to communicate with each other more easily on a continuous basis. This allows constant monitoring of how devices and machines are functioning; variations in vibrations can predict when maintenance is needed. These improvements mean fewer errors occur in the production process, devices and machines have shorter downtimes than now, and future machines are designed better. Labour productivity then goes up.

5G also creates conditions for higher labour productivity in the logistics sector. One example is [truck platooning](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/logistiek/innovatie-in-logistiek.html). In this case, lorries driving one behind another are digitally connected, and only the front one has a driver, who determines speed and route. The following trucks do exactly the same. When the driver brakes, the next lorry brakes almost simultaneously. Much more freight can then be transported per person. Truck platooning is still in the experimental phase, but in time 5G can enable larger-scale and broader application.

## Groningen already making strong progress with 5G

In Groningen, companies are already experimenting with possible applications for the logistics sector thanks to 5G. These are pilots with autonomous inland vessels and inventory management in a distribution centre using a drone that scans barcodes. Tests are also being carried out with navigation of vessels near the shore and the automatic, near real-time measurement of silt content, current and depth of waterways in Delfzijl and the Eemshaven.

These innovations are part of the ‘5Groningen’ initiative, through which the Economic Board Groningen has for several years been energetically exploring the possibilities of future 5G. Due to existing restrictions on the 3.5 GHz frequency, this has used carrier aggregation. By connecting simultaneously on multiple 4G frequencies, high up- and download speeds can be achieved. The initiators have linked connectivity and equipment providers to entrepreneurs from various sectors.

## Opportunities for agriculture and healthcare

Other experiments within this initiative show the potential to raise productivity in agriculture. One example is a drone that takes five photos per second of farmland and crops for analysis. This immediately shows where there is a possible lack of water or fertiliser, and where disease threatens. Another agricultural example is the smart potato, a ‘digital potato’ that sends information to the farmer on, for example, the temperature, humidity and potassium levels of and in the soil. With such applications, farmers can track potato growth more easily and deploy inspection more efficiently and effectively.

Groningen is also testing remote support from a medical specialist, in this case looking in on the patient during the ambulance ride. This could save more lives, which is of course a much higher goal than raising labour productivity.

## Great potential

5G has great potential for the business market. It is important that companies and regions already think about the applications that the new generation of mobile networks can offer. Groningen’s example deserves to be followed across the country. 5G will be an important tool to scale up technological applications that raise labour productivity. Raising productivity is the answer to staff shortages and keeps Dutch business competitive internationally. It is therefore important that business is ready in time to take this step.

# Internet not only faster, but also more energy-efficient

[Headlines & Insights](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/headlines-insights/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

Two-thirds of Dutch households can now access ultra-fast ‘gigabit internet’, reports industry association NLconnect. This means the central government’s target for 2023 has already been met.

The high coverage of this fast, fixed broadband network can be explained by two efforts from the telecoms sector: the ongoing roll-out of fibre to the home and higher internet speeds over existing TV cables.

## Insight

Both strategies not only give consumers the chance to enjoy ultra-fast internet, but also improve energy consumption. The energy efficiency of communications networks is topical because data traffic has been growing exponentially for years. To limit CO2 emissions, it is important that the energy used for data traffic does not rise at the same pace.

Most of the fixed broadband network in the Netherlands now consists of fibre. This is good news, because fibre is known as the most energy-efficient way to exchange data. In most cases, however, the last leg of internet traffic, between the local exchange or street cabinet and the home, still runs over the ‘old-fashioned’ connection. In KPN’s existing network this is the copper telephone line, and in VodafoneZiggo’s network it is the TV cable. These parties are powerful; together they currently account for eighty to ninety per cent of fixed internet connections for households.1

In terms of speed, the copper network has reached its limit. That is why KPN has worked this year to fibre up this last stretch for more and more households. Such a connection is called ‘Fibre to the Home’ (FttH). FttH delivers ultra-fast internet, which is not possible with the existing copper network. VodafoneZiggo has a different strategy. The internet provider continues to use the last piece of TV cable and performs an upgrade on it according to the ‘gigabit without digging’ principle: it restricts itself to upgrades in local exchanges and overhead cables. These upgrades can achieve download speeds comparable to full fibre.2

Both strategies not only mean faster internet, but also improve energy consumption. For KPN, the gain lies in the fact that a full fibre network requires less network equipment. According to recent research by cable manufacturer Prysmian, this translates into energy consumption about 7.5 times lower than the old copper network.3 The fibre network also uses this energy more efficiently; where one kilowatt hour over a copper telephone line can carry a maximum of 4 terabytes of data, a Fibre to the Home network can transfer up to 56 terabytes with the same amount of energy.

The upgrade of the TV cable network by VodafoneZiggo also offers a sustainability benefit. However, this benefit seems to sit primarily in the equipment in people’s homes4: the modem. The new modem installed at customers with gigabit internet can automatically enter sleep mode when there is no internet traffic. In standby, the device uses less energy. Assuming eight hours of internet use per day — a figure that is probably higher now — researchers at the University of Melbourne estimate that using sleep mode can result in 40 per cent lower energy consumption.5

At present, such settings are not yet possible for FttH equipment in the home. That is a pity, because this is the most energy-hungry component in the entire chain. This applies to all types of networks, but in the case of Fibre to the Home the imbalance is striking: the router and ‘converter’ (the device that converts the optical signal from the fibre-optic cable into an electrical signal) are responsible for about 95 per cent of total energy consumption by FttH.6 This percentage is high in part because the fibre network itself is so efficient — a positive sign. At the same time, it means the sustainability journey is not over even with full fibre networks. We should therefore focus on developing energy-efficient routers and additional network equipment.

The specific strategies of both KPN and VodafoneZiggo seem to stem from the (im)possibilities of the existing network. Within those constraints, both parties have chosen the most logical route to gigabit internet, a route that also reduces CO2 emissions. Let us hope energy efficiency stays high on the agenda as these ultra-fast networks continue to develop.

# More cyberattacks using stolen login details

[Report](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/rapporten/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

Cybercriminals are increasingly starting their attack by logging in to company systems with a username and password. This strategy is now used as often as phishing, which over half of companies faced in 2023, according to a survey by ABN AMRO and research agency MWM2 among 895 organisations.

This increase shows how easy it has become for cybercriminals to obtain combinations of usernames and passwords. In many cases, they no longer even need to set up a separate [phishing](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/cybersecurity/wat-is-phishing.html) campaign, where only a small share of recipients will take the ‘desired’ action. Malicious actors are therefore willing to pay top money for ready-to-use datasets. This is one reason why criminal groups that previously focused on [ransomware](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/cybersecurity/wat-is-ransomware.html) are shifting their hunting ground to creating malware designed specifically to steal information.

## Getting in unnoticed

Attackers can obtain those login details in different ways. Sometimes they get hold of ready-made lists of leaked user accounts, often offered via the ‘dark web’. They then try to log in to company systems with these details, taking advantage of the fact that people often use the same combinations of usernames and passwords for different accounts. In other cases, the leaked lists lack passwords and attackers guess them using enormous computing power. This latter method also marked the start of a Chinese cyber-espionage operation at the Dutch chip manufacturer NXP, which began in 2017 but went unnoticed until 2020. The hackers therefore had long-term access to the manufacturer’s intellectual property, such as chip designs.

## Exploiting vulnerabilities

Vulnerabilities in internet-connected programmes were also a popular starting point for cyberattacks in 2023. Such backdoors can be abused to break into systems before a fix or ‘patch’ is available. Abuse occurred via newly found [vulnerabilities](https://blog.google/technology/safety-security/a-review-of-zero-day-in-the-wild-exploits-in-2023/#:~:text=In%202023%2C%20Google%20observed%2097,of%202021) in the Windows, iOS and Android operating systems, and the Chrome and Safari web browsers. Cybercriminals are often quick off the mark; in 2023, attempts to exploit a [quarter](https://blog.qualys.com/vulnerabilities-threat-research/2023/12/19/2023-threat-landscape-year-in-review-part-one#:~:text=MITRE%20ATT%26CK%20tactics.-,2023%20Statistics,found%20than%20the%20year%20before.) of these ‘zero-days’ were made within a day.

## Combination of measures matters

This top three of ‘initial access vectors’ underlines the importance for companies of applying a combination of measures. Training employees to recognise phishing can reduce the number of successful attempts, and applying ‘multifactor authentication’ is important to prevent leaked or stolen login details being misused. With this authentication method, one or more extra checks take place, for example via a confirmation in a mobile app or a code sent by SMS. The abuse of vulnerabilities in programmes and devices highlights the importance of regular software updates and a solid check of IT suppliers’ cybersecurity policies.

# Need for alternative cooling in data centres is increasing

[Headlines & Insights](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/headlines-insights/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

Cooling installations at data centres in North Holland use so much water that this could locally lead to a drinking water shortage, De Telegraaf reported. Not every cooling system uses drinking water, though; there is an alternative, with many other benefits as well.

## Insight

Data centres’ drinking water use has its roots in so-called “open cooling towers”. Heat from IT equipment is absorbed by water, which is then sprayed in these cooling towers. Heat is thus automatically transferred to the outside air. Part of the water evaporates in this process and must be replenished. A portion of the remaining water also has to be discharged regularly.

These systems are now under fire because they could cause a drinking water shortage on hot days. The Dutch Data Center Association [plays down](https://fd.nl/futures/1377663/zorgen-om-onduidelijk-waterverbruik-datacenters-jvc1cawVIomh) in Het Financieele Dagblad the high water use that [De Telegraaf](https://www.telegraaf.nl/nieuws/1219494172/datacenters-slurpen-zo-veel-water-dat-er-mogelijk-te-weinig-voor-huishoudens-overblijft) mentions. The FD article also states that this form of cooling is needed only on warmer days, about five per cent of the time. Even so, caretaker minister Van Nieuwenhuizen has said she wants to find out “[what is going on](https://www.rtlnieuws.nl/tech/artikel/5220867/datacentra-water-watergebruik-noord-holland-nederland-hollands-kroon)”.

Whatever the case, to save drinking water data centres could consider a “closed cooling system”. Unlike open systems like those described above, in a closed system the coolant keeps circulating and does not need to be replenished. It gets even more interesting when such a closed system is combined with efficient heat capture right at the source, the server. This combination not only brings benefits in terms of water and energy use, but is also the most future-proof in view of the latest technological developments.

Server heat can be harvested via air or via liquid. In the first case, cool air is blown past the server, the “server rack” or simply through the entire room. The heat is in turn removed in a warm airstream. Air is not a particularly good medium for transporting heat; liquid is a much better conductor. Experts have therefore been predicting the breakthrough of liquid cooling for years, also known as “direct liquid cooling”. Small tubes carrying coolant are led along or through the servers, or the entire server is placed in a bath of synthetic oil. This latter, still new variant is also known as “immersion cooling”.

The data centre industry has not yet switched on a large scale, but the first tentative steps towards direct liquid cooling are now being taken. For example, Google cools part of its servers using small tubes directly on the [computer chips](https://datacenterfrontier.com/google-shifts-to-liquid-cooling-for-ai-data-crunching/). Microsoft has issued [encouraging reports](https://datacenterfrontier.com/microsoft-tests-immersion-cooling-tech-from-bitcoin-mining-for-its-cloud-servers/) on immersion cooling, and oil company [Shell](https://www.hpcwire.com/off-the-wire/asperitas-selected-by-shell-to-deliver-immersion-cooling-for-amsterdam-hpc-datacentre/) and French bank [Crédit Agricole](https://www.datacenterdynamics.com/en/news/cr%C3%A9dit-agricole-opts-for-immersion-cooling/) have also said they will use this technology in their own data centres. Several considerations are at play here.

First, direct liquid cooling means lower energy use. For immersion cooling this is even a [reduction](https://www.datacenterknowledge.com/power-and-cooling/liquid-cooling-can-lower-data-center-pue-s-not-its-main-draw) of 30 to 50 per cent. Because cooling takes place directly at the source, the liquid — oil, in this case — reaches temperatures of 50 to 60 degrees Celsius. And counter-intuitive as it may sound, waste heat at a higher temperature cools down more easily — a principle of physics. Heat removal that initially takes place via air later in the process yields water with residual heat temperatures of only 25 to 30 degrees, which conversely requires more intervention to cool. Think of fans that blow cool air along the warmed water. At higher outside temperatures it even involves “chillers”, cooling machines that use even more energy.

An even more important consideration for switching to liquid cooling is the large amount of heat generated by the latest generation of computer chips. These chips are specially developed for applications that require a lot of computing power, such as the development of artificial intelligence. Even the most advanced air cooling is, according to experts, unable to eliminate the heat released in the process. Only liquid cooling seems up to the task.

This form of cooling also takes up less space, an advantage given the expected increase in the number of data centres. With the roll-out of 5G, more data centres will also appear in urban environments close to the end user. These are smaller facilities that support all kinds of smart applications based on artificial intelligence. Liquid cooling is ideal in such cases, not only because of the advanced, heat-generating chips used, but also because this method can limit the number of square metres of data floor. Air cooling often requires high spaces, large installations and wide aisles on either side of the servers.

An alternative way of cooling also better meets the increasing societal demands placed on data centres. Last year Amsterdam stated that it would allow the construction of new data centres only if they [make their waste heat available free of charge](https://www.parool.nl/amsterdam/amsterdamse-datacenters-moeten-woningen-verwarmen-gratis~be0be8f4/) for heating homes in Greater Amsterdam. The high residual heat temperatures harvested via direct liquid cooling are widely applicable. Waste heat at a relatively low temperature is less easy to use, because it can heat only well-insulated new-build homes.

Of course, this does not mean data centres will switch cooling techniques overnight. Liquid-based cooling systems involve a paradigm shift: they require differently designed buildings, adjustments and standardisation of the servers themselves, and a change in operational processes. This entails significant investment. Yet the benefits do seem to be recognised by data centre owners; in recent research 60 per cent of respondents said they would consider liquid cooling. The percentage was even higher (70 per cent) when this question was asked specifically on the assumption that they would have to [build](https://www.delltechnologies.com/resources/en-us/asset/white-papers/products/ready-solutions/informa-liquid-cooling-survey-results.pdf) a new facility.

All in all, it seems the ground for liquid cooling in data centres is becoming ever more fertile. A growing focus on sustainability in society is a key driver, as are the demands that new digital applications will place on our digital infrastructure in the coming years. It is now up to all forward-looking entrepreneurs to decide when they will place this final piece of the puzzle.

# Entrepreneurs recognise cyber risk mainly only after damage

An overwhelming majority of companies have already faced a cyberattack. Yet entrepreneurs tend to underestimate the associated risks. Often only after an organisation has suffered damage, for example lost revenue or financial costs for system recovery, do they recognise the risks.

Last year, almost three-quarters of Dutch companies faced a cyberattack, according to a survey by ABN AMRO and research agency MWM2 among 895 organisations. Among large corporates, with annual turnover of at least 25 million, this share is highest at 86 per cent. In small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs), 71 per cent were confronted with an attack last year. More than half of the self-employed (55 per cent) were targeted. The study included 232 large companies, 524 SMEs and 139 self-employed professionals.

The likelihood that a company will at some point be the target of a cyberattack is now particularly high; 95 per cent of large corporates have already experienced this at least once, compared with 85 per cent of SMEs and 65 per cent of sole traders. Although this percentage logically increases each year for all company sizes, this is not the case across the board for risk perception. In both the SME and sole-trader segments, the share of businesses that see cybercrime as “a lot” or “a great deal of risk” for the organisation has even fallen slightly. Respectively, 38 and 13 per cent rate the risks as high this year. This applies to a much higher share of large corporates: 60 per cent, compared with 53 last year.

## Higher risk assessment after damage

Cyberattacks have now led to damage at 59 per cent of large corporates and 43 per cent of SMEs, such as financial costs for restoring systems, lost income due to business downtime or reputational damage from leaked customer data. This occurs significantly less in the sole-trader population, where only 6 per cent report ever having suffered damage from a cyberattack.

Further analysis shows that companies that have actually suffered damage from an attack on average assess the risks as higher. Of these, 63 per cent see cybercrime as (very) high risk for the organisation. Of the companies that have not yet suffered damage from an attack, only 26 per cent share this risk assessment. This is comparable to the risk assessment of companies that have never been attacked at all.

Even so, CEO Maarten Roerink of cybersecurity service provider MMOX also sees that experiences of similar companies can serve as a wake-up call. “Entrepreneurs who hear from their neighbour on the business park about an unpleasant cyberattack are more inclined to start looking at solutions. They do not walk away with a contract straight away, but they are at least more aware of the risks.”

## Measures to reduce risks

Entrepreneurs would do well to protect themselves against cyberattacks. You can do this in three relatively simple steps. The first is to carry out a risk analysis to map what is crucial to keep the business running and services in order. The second step is to take measures, such as promoting safe behaviour among employees, recording who owns certain data and discussing cyber risks with partners and suppliers. Finally, it is essential to develop procedures to detect and handle cyber incidents.

# Phishing proves a popular form of cybercrime

[Report](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/rapporten/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

Phishing is a popular method for cybercriminals to gain access to companies, and entrepreneurs fear it. Attackers send messages that prompt the recipient to take an action that later turns out to be harmful. Attacks are becoming more targeted and more sophisticated.

[Phishing](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/cybersecurity/wat-is-phishing.html) is the most reported type across all company sizes, according to a survey by ABN AMRO and research agency MWM2 among 895 organisations. In 2023, well over half of companies faced phishing emails or texts. These messages tempt the recipient to open a link that triggers an automatic download of malicious software, or to visit a fake login page where data are shared with malicious third parties.

## Careful research for a targeted attack

CEO fraud is a specific form of phishing, also known as “whaling”. Fraudsters pose as the CEO or a senior manager and approach employees in their name. The channels range from email to WhatsApp and even phone calls. These are usually urgent requests to transfer money, pay invoices or provide sensitive information. The attackers have often already done careful research. First to find the right contact details, and also to imitate the manager’s communication style. No fewer than 33 per cent of large-company respondents faced such an attack last year.

[Also read: Sharp increase in CEO fraud](/nl/zakelijk/insights/cybersecurity/phishing/sterke-toename-in-ceo-fraude.html)

## Deepfakes of colleagues

Artificial Intelligence (AI) further expands the attack options. By using deepfake technology, which creates images or audio that are indistinguishable from real, bad actors can make a victim believe they are dealing with someone else. In Hong Kong, a finance employee at a multinational transferred more than [25 million dollars](https://edition.cnn.com/2024/02/04/asia/deepfake-cfo-scam-hong-kong-intl-hnk/index.html) to fraudsters after joining a video meeting with a deepfake version of the CFO and other colleagues.

In October last year, there was a similar attempt at Dutch online bank Bunq, when an employee was invited to a video call with what later turned out to be an AI clone of chief executive Ali Niknam. In a [LinkedIn post](https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7114874320627085312/), Niknam calls the clone “convincing”.

[Also listen to the podcast: Generative AI: Telling the real from the fake](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/in-gesprek/podcast/de-innovatiegolf/generatieve-ai-het-verschil-tussen-echt-en-nep.html)

## Ready-made login details

Phishing is a popular way for bad actors to start a cyberattack, but it only causes damage if the recipient actually takes the bait. That is why attackers now often start their journey through the target’s IT landscape by simply logging in to one of the systems with a username and password. In 2023, almost one-third (30 per cent) of cyberattacks began this way, up from just 16 per cent in 2022, according to [IBM X-Force](https://www.ibm.com/reports/threat-intelligence). These login details can be bought ready-made, or guessed using brute computing power. It is an unobtrusive way to get in, which gives the intruder plenty of time to look for further vulnerabilities and information.

## Measures to reduce risks

Encouraging safe behaviour among employees is crucial to counter phishing. It is one of three steps entrepreneurs should take to protect themselves against cyberattacks. The first step is to carry out a risk analysis to map what is crucial to keep the company running and services operational. The second step is to take measures, such as promoting safe behaviour among employees, assigning ownership of specific data, and discussing cyber risks with partners and suppliers. Finally, it is essential to develop procedures to detect and handle cyber incidents.

# Record amount extorted with ransomware

[Report](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/rapporten/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

Where phishing and seemingly legitimate logins often mark the start of an attack, other tools come into play once the victim has been drawn in more deeply. Installing malware is a prominent strategy used by cybercriminals to achieve their ultimate goal.

This malicious software can be used to steal data, disrupt services, or spy. Nearly one quarter (23 per cent) of respondents faced this in 2023, according to a survey by ABN AMRO and research agency MWM2 among 895 organisations. Large companies were the most frequent victims at 32 per cent, followed by small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) and sole traders at 19 and 16 per cent respectively. The most common type of malware is ‘[ransomware](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/cybersecurity/wat-is-ransomware.html)’, which blocks systems and encrypts data until the affected party pays a ransom. It ranks in the top three most feared cyberattacks among both large companies and the sole-trader community. In SMEs, ransomware does not make the top three, but malware in a broader sense is cited.

## Record amount of ransom paid

This [ransomware](https://www.wired.com/story/ransomware-payments-2023-breaks-record/) is being deployed on a larger scale than ever; 4,399 ransomware attacks were recorded worldwide in 2023. Victims paid a record total of 1.1 billion dollars – almost double 2022, when this form of cybercrime briefly seemed to be in decline. The relatively modest 567 million dollars in 2022 now appears to have been an exception in an otherwise clear trend.

Although the rise in attacks and ransom collected is large, a smaller share of victims are actually paying. While between 70 and 80 per cent paid in 2019 and 2020, this fell to 29 per cent in the fourth quarter of 2023. The amount paid in 2023 can largely be explained by cybercriminals focusing on companies that cannot afford any disruption to their systems and that are also able to stump up the ransom. Three-quarters of the total amount came from payments of more than one million dollars. Arwi van der Sluijs, Managing Director of cybersecurity service provider NFIR: “Smaller companies are less willing to pay; they simply do not think it is worth it. They accept that this means losing certain data.”

At the end of last year, Tunstall Healthcare also fell victim to [ransomware](https://www.vpngids.nl/nieuws/tunstall-hackers-hadden-toegang-tot-data/). The company specialises in solutions that enable remote care and produces, among other things, wearable emergency buttons for older people. They use the system to get help quickly after, for example, a fall. But due to the hack, their alerts no longer reached the control room. With help from an external cybersecurity company, the system was restored within a few days. In the meantime, clients were advised to carry a mobile phone at all times.

## Measures to reduce risks

Entrepreneurs should protect themselves against cyberattacks. You can do this in three relatively simple steps. First, carry out a risk analysis to map out what is critical to keep the business running and maintain service quality. Second, take measures such as promoting safe behaviour among employees, defining who owns specific data, and discussing cyber risks with partners and suppliers. Finally, set up procedures to detect and handle cyber incidents.

# Act fast to promote working from home to help the climate

[Headlines & Insights](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/headlines-insights/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Sonny Duijn](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/sonny-duijn/index.html)

[Franka Rolvink Couzy](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/franka-rolvink-couzy.html)

The new government plans to promote working from home to protect the environment, according to the coalition agreement. Now that part of the Netherlands is already working from home en masse under pressure from the pandemic, this has a chance of success. The condition is that the plan is developed further, so we also find a solution for the loneliness and poor interaction caused by working from home. Otherwise, this measure could harm people’s well-being.

## Insight

It is not the most eye-catching and certainly not the most worked-out part of the coalition agreement. Even so, it is notable that the incoming cabinet says it will make agreements with business and government bodies on promoting working from home.

The reason is easy to guess, as the plan sits under the subheading Climate and Energy. When more people work from home there are fewer journeys, which reduces greenhouse gas emissions. That is welcome, as emissions must fall sharply to prevent the Earth from warming by more than 1.5 degrees this century, with all the consequences that entails. It is therefore worth employers’ while to promote working from home.

Working from home has limits. You cannot expect it from workers whose jobs are tied to a location, such as lorry drivers or waiting staff in hospitality. But for IT staff, civil servants, business services workers and many office roles, working from home is more feasible. In 2020, the share of employees who worked wholly or partly from home rose to 47 per cent, according to data from research organisation TNO. A year earlier it was 37 per cent.

Promoting working from home can prevent a lot of CO₂ emissions. If 12 per cent of the Dutch workforce were to work from home structurally one and a half days a week more than before the coronavirus pandemic, this would cut emissions by 353,600 tonnes a year of greenhouse gas equivalents, according to calculations by research agency CE Delft. Although this is only a few tenths of a per cent compared with total Dutch annual greenhouse gas emissions according to Statistics Netherlands (CBS), it is still a significant amount. The saving equals 150,000 return flights between Amsterdam and New York, [according to environmental organisation Natuur & Milieu](https://www.natuurenmilieu.nl/nieuwsberichten/15-dag-thuiswerken-scheelt-nederlanders-3-miljard-kilometers-per-jaar/).

Employers can kill two birds with one stone by promoting working from home. Besides cutting emissions, it can improve employees’ work-life balance. Research for ABN AMRO’s Sectoral Well-being Monitor shows that employees see work-life balance, together with health, as the most important aspect of a job. These matters are relatively close to employees’ daily lives, so they are more likely to motivate than the much more abstract “the organisation’s efforts on the climate”.

Even so, working from home has downsides. During the pandemic, the flip side included a lack of “real” interaction between employees, fewer chances to brainstorm and growing loneliness. We need structural solutions for this, so employers can shape working from home more effectively and without personal harm.

There are also several aspects of working from home that need further elaboration. These include the relationship between employers and home-working employees, for example around privacy, employment law and tax treatment, or subsidising innovation that enables more working from home. In addition, restricting or making work-related activities more expensive could further encourage working from home, such as the incoming cabinet’s proposed road pricing.

We should work with employers to find the right approach. Some employers will certainly want to help think this through. Recent research by Statistics Netherlands shows that about 43 per cent of entrepreneurs who see hybrid working as an option for their operations plan to promote hybrid working structurally.

It is important to use the current momentum to take these steps. Due to the coronavirus crisis, employees are now more used to working from home than before and, based on their experience, are probably able to do it more efficiently. That momentum can fade quickly, as the jam-packed morning rush hour in early October 2021 showed. The work-from-home guidance had barely been lifted when hundreds of kilometres of traffic jams filled the motorways.

# Software companies not yet ready for AI agents

[Report](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/rapporten/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

Although four out of five software companies expect AI agents to change the software landscape noticeably, almost one third of businesses are not yet technically ready to embed the technology. Half are in the experimentation phase, and one quarter are not doing anything with agentic AI yet.

A large majority of software companies expect the software landscape to change noticeably due to “AI agents” — smart programs that can perform complex tasks independently. Almost half of respondents even expect far-reaching to fundamental changes in the way software is designed, used or controlled, according to research by ABN AMRO and Panelwizard among 204 Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) companies. SaaS is software taken out on a subscription and offered as a cloud service.

## AI agents are transforming the software market

The development of artificial intelligence (AI) is moving from generating text, software code and images within an app to independently running complete work processes. Examples include AI agents that manage inventories, send quotations or prepare and execute a complete social media strategy. Through agentic AI, SaaS companies can make their product more attractive, for example by automating tasks or by putting an AI agent forward as the user’s point of contact.

## The AI agent is not a new concept

The term “intelligent agent” was introduced back in the 1980s to describe systems that can perceive, reason and act independently. Due to technological advances, AI agents have become much more powerful in recent years. Through Large Language Models (LLMs), the engine behind tools like ChatGPT, agents have the necessary knowledge about the world, which makes communication smooth. With the rise of so-called “reasoning models”, language models that can reason step by step, agents can also devise strategies themselves and determine the associated intermediate steps. By linking to external applications, AI agents can also actually perform actions, such as launching an online advertising campaign or contacting a customer or supplier.

Aside from the opportunities to improve existing SaaS or create new “AI-first” SaaS, the rise of agents could also influence the market at a deeper level. According to Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella, this means the end of software in its current form. He believes that before long people will no longer work with separate software packages, but with AI agents that independently draw on different data sources and, based on that, carry out the necessary actions. To automatically draw up a customer quotation, the AI agent retrieves information from the customer database, combines it with current product or price information, and, if necessary, adds financial conditions based on previous customer behaviour. After review — or fully autonomously — the quotation is sent automatically by email. Users no longer need to switch between systems themselves, because application-independent agents do the thinking and organising, in Nadella’s view.

AI agents can also create entirely new applications. Claude, the AI assistant from tech company Anthropic, for example, creates immediately usable apps based on a few user instructions. With specialised software such as Lovable, Windsurf and Cursor, people without programming knowledge can also develop software, apps and websites; the AI agent interprets the user’s request and turns it into code — in some tools the result is even visible straight away. Although building good, secure and reliable software is a specialism in its own right, this trend of “vibe coding” could mean that more and more companies choose to develop tools themselves rather than buying them in as SaaS.

## An AI agent as the point of contact

The influence of agents will extend across multiple levels. For instance, 75 per cent of respondents assume that the buttons and menus we now see on our screens will at least partly make way for an AI agent as the point of contact. It takes on tasks from the user, executes them in the background and reports back the outcome. Many people now know this form of interaction from smart AI assistants such as ChatGPT, Gemini and Claude.

Samir Selimi, founder of software company PurchAIse, has also put an AI agent at the forefront of his platform. The assistant, called PAI, can handle a wide range of tasks in supplier management, such as validating data, placing orders and renegotiating contracts. Selimi sees the chat with PAI as a smart addition to the traditional user interface, which he believes will remain valuable. “We are used to things being displayed visually when we interact with a computer,” he says. “You do not need forty screens or sixty tabs, but you do need some sort of cockpit where, as a user, you can keep an overview.”

The expectation is that communication in natural language — in other words, everyday spoken and written language as people use it themselves — will play an important role within software. “You see a shift in the way users interact with software,” says Jelle Visser of ValueBlue, a platform for enterprise architecture. ValueBlue makes software that allows companies to record, analyse and improve their IT landscape schematically. “If a business analyst wants to understand exactly what such an architecture diagram is telling them, it helps if they can ask that question on our platform in that same way.” At present, 38 per cent of software companies see opportunities for themselves to use an AI agent as the point of contact. Visser himself does not feel there is room to wait. “As a software company, you must reinvent your user interface. If we do not do it, our competitor will.”

## Communicating brand identity through agents

One fifth of the companies surveyed fear that their user experience will no longer be distinctive as a result. What now still runs via mouse clicks and menu structures can at least partly be replaced by typing or speaking a single sentence. This may put pressure on an important element of software companies’ distinctiveness — design, ease of use and visual branding.

One third of respondents, however, see the user experience as their main source of distinctiveness for the future. Even if interaction with software becomes more conversational, there are still plenty of opportunities to give the AI agent a clear brand identity. Since the early rise of chatbots on corporate websites, experts have been looking at ways to give these digital assistants a personality that matches the brand — for example with a fitting avatar, name and manner of communication.

## A different dashboard for every user

More than a quarter of SaaS companies see merit in ad hoc generated screens. “You then tell an agent: the window must be green, with corners and no rounding. And suddenly there is a screen, adapted to your preferences,” illustrates Franklin Evers of software company Usere casually. The company wants to keep its front end — the part the user sees — flexible anyway. Co-founder Pim Schipper: “Each employee benefits from a different kind of information. Agentic AI allows everyone to get a tailored dashboard.”

Visser of ValueBlue also recognises the tension. “The people who use our software all have different roles and different levels of maturity. But it is a lot of work to program a separate variant of the software for everyone.” In his view, agentic AI can offer a solution here. “By giving an agent the necessary context about a role, it can determine what kind of screens and functions an individual user needs.”

Dashboards generated on the spot, or even full applications and games that are spun up from scratch, are not a hypothetical scenario. For example, researchers developed an AI model with which the shooter game Doom can be generated ad hoc. The player encounters enemies and navigates through all kinds of spaces without any software code being needed; there is only an AI model trained on large amounts of visual material from the game. Google built a prototype in which its latest Gemini model creates a user interface, each time based on the events in the previous screen — no menu was fixed in advance.

## Software as a silent force in the background

Of the SaaS companies surveyed, 16 per cent fear that their software will fade into the background — a concern that is not entirely unfounded now that consumers and businesses are increasingly turning to independent AI assistants. ChatGPT and Claude now support so-called “actions” with which they independently perform tasks in other applications, such as sending emails or modifying CRM data. The established order in the tech world is also increasingly positioning itself as coordinating platform players. Microsoft allows companies to build their own AI agents with Copilot Studio, which can call on not only Microsoft apps but also external software. Salesforce developed a similar infrastructure with Agentforce.

One in five software companies are considering a link with such AI agent platforms. One quarter are not focusing on specific platforms, but want to make their own software “agent-ready” in a broader sense. These entrepreneurs make functions available modularly, ensure clear documentation, and set up their software so that instructions from external agents — whether or not in natural language — can easily be translated into concrete actions within the application. “As a software company, you must make sure that LLMs can talk to your back end,” summarises Dennis De Reus of Stellar. He is co-founder of Stellar, a platform that allows companies to develop customer service agents themselves.

Almost one third of companies are focusing more broadly on improving data access, integration options and standardised communication with other systems (“interoperability”). Among them is Last Mile Solutions, a software platform for service providers in electric transport — such as charge point operators — that provides technology for, among other things, payments, fraud detection, and preventing overloading of the power grid. A lot of data is needed to provide these services, such as information about the amount of green energy generated at a given time, and how full individual car batteries are.

“Those data can be used by other parties to develop AI models and specialised agents,” says Marketing Director Ben Ridgway. “So we provide a ‘data backbone’ that enables AI innovations, such as smart charging and predictive maintenance.” Ridgway of Last Mile Solutions, which acts as a central hub in the field of e-mobility software, fully agrees. “Our back end is extremely complex. There is so much data that we manage and must make available in the right way. For us, it is crucial to demonstrate time and again that we are a reliable partner.”

## Developing applications à la carte

AI agents do not necessarily operate within the boundaries of an application, but can also switch between separate databases and tools to carry out tasks independently. For example, an agent that retrieves historical sales data from system A, predicts future sales based on an algorithm from system B and displays the associated chart in a dashboard of system C. It is therefore less about the total package, and more about how individual pieces of software can be accessed by AI systems.

Even before the rise of AI agents, many software companies were working to break their systems into small, reusable modules and make them available via so-called APIs or SDKs. Many AI models are also offered this way, so that developers can, for example, embed a smart chatbot on their own website. “Building software is increasingly a matter of combining all kinds of components,” says Visser of ValueBlue. “As a result, entering the software market is easier than ever.”

This applies equally to AI agents, which can draw à la carte on data and functions from different providers. Only one quarter of SaaS companies, however, have the preconditions in order for this. They have the necessary digital connections in the form of APIs — short for application programming interfaces — or are sometimes even explicitly designed to work with AI agents and agent platforms.

If software companies make separate elements of their systems widely available, it can be quite difficult for external agents to determine each time which function they take from which party. Sebastiaan den Boer, Director of AI and Data Science at digital agency Youwe: “If you have to keep making an AI agent choose between twenty tools and fifteen databases, then you must also capture the knowledge needed for that in a prompt. That can soon become a very complex instruction.”

## Existing software under fire from AI agents

Some companies (14 per cent) see the risk that specific functions in their software will become redundant because externally developed AI agents perform tasks in their own way, without using existing software modules. One example is an agent that segments customers itself, instead of calling a built-in filter function in existing software. This concern is limited among companies with annual turnover of more than 25 million euros (7 per cent), while one quarter of smaller companies fear this.

Another 9 per cent fear that their carefully built logic or AI models will no longer be used, for example when agents developed elsewhere can make decisions based on their own reasoning abilities. For instance, a recruitment agent could assess applicants using its own scoring method, whereby the internal matching model of the software is completely ignored. This previously purchased HR software then serves only as a data source, or is even bypassed entirely.

De Reus of Stellar is less afraid of such a scenario. He can imagine that software users will interact more and more with AI agents, but is convinced that the underlying systems will often remain relevant. “For example, an agent cannot just store data at random. You need a system to ensure that this happens according to certain structures and rules. The same applies to customer identification; you need modules that allow you to establish someone’s identity with certainty, and then rules that determine which data a particular customer may or may not view.” According to Franklin Evers, co-founder of Usere, this is exactly where software companies distinguish themselves. “It is precisely in that smart middle layer that your intellectual property sits, and you must protect it,” says Evers, who, together with partner Pim Schipper, is developing an AI-driven marketing solution.

## Smart task automation

Companies with fundamental concerns about their business model are in the minority. Software companies smell opportunities to improve their own product with AI agents. Two thirds of respondents see merit in agents that perform tasks on behalf of the user, such as retrieving information, or running processes that previously happened based on strict steps and rules. Six in ten companies would like to deploy AI agents to support their own customers.

For example, PurchAIse uses agentic AI for supplier management, including retrieving and providing market insights. An automatically detected price drop of ingredients can be a reason to renegotiate a contract, for which AI agent PAI then also drafts an email to the supplier. Usere is initially focusing on marketing and is relieving SMEs by automating, among other things, the creation and placement of social media posts.

Among respondents there is concern about the potential unpredictability of AI agents. Almost half of software companies see a lack of transparency and control as a real risk. AI agents do not work according to fixed rules, but on the basis of probability; they choose the most likely next step and its content, but not necessarily the right one.

“It is hard to set up processes well and predictably with agentic AI,” says Rogier Hosman, co-founder of digital agency Youwe, which develops digital solutions for companies. Hosman knows from experience what is needed for a successful implementation. “Do I have sufficient control over the technology, or will half of my SaaS product collapse? What risk am I willing to take in that?” Although concrete rules can also be given here to steer the agent, it is a fundamentally different way of working from traditional software.

## Technically, there is still a lot to do

Although software companies see countless opportunities in the field of agentic AI, the question is whether they can take the necessary steps in the short term. Nearly 30 per cent of respondents doubt whether they are technically ready for AI agents; their software architecture or organisation is not yet sufficiently set up for the application of and integration with agents. It is striking that the smallest companies, with annual turnover of less than 2 million euros, see this risk much less — possibly because their IT landscape is less complex and therefore more flexible.

It is also striking that a large proportion of software companies (53 per cent) are still in the experimentation phase with AI agents. These companies are exploring the technology, but have not yet embedded it structurally in their product. More than one quarter do not yet have any concrete plans in the field of agentic AI at all. Maturity in the field of agentic AI appears to be highly dependent on company size; there is hardly any large company (turnover greater than 25 million euros) that is not yet doing anything with the technology, whereas within micro-companies (turnover less than 2 million euros) this applies to 40 per cent. Vertical software companies, which focus on specific sectors, are also on average less advanced than software companies with a broader orientation.

This limited maturity is worrying, because in the meantime a new generation of technology companies is storming the market. In 2024, according to data from Dealroom, around 3.2 billion dollars was invested worldwide in start-ups that have embedded agentic AI in their service. This is exponential growth compared to the previous years, which recorded investments of 727 million and 1.3 billion respectively.

Incidentally, the customers of SaaS companies are also far from all ready for large-scale application of AI agents, observes Selimi of PurchAIse. “In theory, technology can do everything, but in practice companies find it difficult to adapt. It is mostly people and processes where things break down.”

## Distinctiveness for the future

Amid this technological transformation, software companies cling to the relationship they have built with their customers. More than half — and within large companies even two thirds — expect to retain the most distinctiveness here in the long term, for example due to a deep understanding of the client’s context or collaboration on implementation and further development of the software. Schipper of Usere is on the same page and even thinks there is room for improvement. “You must invest in the relationship with your customer. For a long time, many software companies have failed to really listen to their customer.”

For the time being, AI often lacks context that is essential in specialist sectors. That is why a significant proportion of software companies also expect to be able to fall back on domain knowledge in the future, such as relevant laws and regulations or sector-specific processes. “In the end, you need domain expertise to really add value,” says Hosman of Youwe. Selimi of PurchAIse agrees. “One way to differentiate yourself as a software company is to know your end market and the associated processes inside out.”

This awareness is logically stronger among vertical software companies (47 per cent), which already focus on specific niches, than among horizontal players (32 per cent). Among large companies, too, a higher percentage (55 per cent) expect to remain distinctive through deep domain knowledge than among SMEs and micro-companies, where the percentage is around one third.

## Investing at the front or back end

The “soft” elements of customer relationship and domain knowledge are followed by concrete matters that are directly visible in the software: the functional coverage or feature set of the software, and the user experience — both cited by one third of respondents as points of distinctiveness in the long term.

Conversely, the technical matters in the background — data, integration capability and algorithms — rank lowest. According to Schipper and Evers of Usere, software companies would do well to keep investing under the bonnet. Evers: “You must keep the core of your software very robust. Other parties can then talk to it via an API. They must feel that they can build on your technology, can trust it.” Ridgway of Last Mile Solutions, which acts as a spider in the web in the field of e-mobility software, fully agrees. “Our back end is extremely complex. There is so much data that we manage and must make available in the right way. For us, it is essential to demonstrate time and again that we are a reliable partner.”

## Adaptability is essential

SaaS companies agree: the software market will change significantly in the coming years. They will be able to optimise their own software with AI agents, as a point of contact or in the background. Meanwhile, the well-known chat assistants are gaining an increasingly prominent position. As they become the daily starting point for more and more users and integrate further with all kinds of tools, the chance grows that existing software companies will, in one way or another, become less visible.

The rise of platforms that allow users to build agents or software themselves can also mean that activities previously supported by software packages are now handled by individually fabricated AI agents or self-built software. Some software companies see merit in an offensive strategy in which they themselves play a platform role, or rely on the unique added value of (elements of) their software.

A black-and-white scenario in which SaaS is completely wiped out by AI agents developed elsewhere is highly unlikely. However, the new reality requires considerable adaptability from software companies; links with external tools and parties are more important than ever, and making the necessary automation leap yourself is essential to remain relevant.

# Spectacular chatbot symbol of AI divide

[Column](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/column.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

OpenAI’s new chatbot shows an impressive ability to understand and produce language. Yet such technological developments do not reach everyone. Only eight per cent of smaller companies in the Netherlands use artificial intelligence (AI).

ChatGPT, the underlying AI model of the recently launched chatbot, can handle the most diverse requests. It can explain the theory of relativity in a way a 9-year-old understands, or draft an essay outline on the dangers of the algorithmic filter bubble. But the chatbot does not shy away from more creative tasks either, such as writing a song lyric or TV dialogue. For example, the request to write a “hysterically funny TV dialogue” for a couple who are completely fed up with porridge for breakfast produces a genuinely entertaining conversation.

Behind this impressive model is OpenAI, a research laboratory founded in 2015 with the aim — in the company’s own words — of developing “friendly AI technology”. Through safe and transparent solutions, the laboratory hopes to make a positive contribution to society. OpenAI is connected in several ways to the biggest names in the tech industry. The organisation was founded with seed capital of no less than 1 billion dollars, from, among others, Tesla boss and OpenAI co-founder Elon Musk, cloud giant Amazon, and venture capital icon Peter Thiel. Since 2019, Microsoft has also been on board, which is now reportedly considering an [additional investment](https://www.semafor.com/article/01/09/2023/microsoft-eyes-10-billion-bet-on-chatgpt) of ten billion dollars.

## Struggle to apply AI

Through its investors, the laboratory has been able to build enormous development capacity, both in terms of computing power and scarce AI skills. This stands in stark contrast to the struggle of the average company to embed such technology in their daily practice. In 2020, only 13 per cent of Dutch companies used one or more AI technologies, according to figures from [CBS](https://opendata.cbs.nl/#/CBS/nl/dataset/84830NED/barh?ts=1670493464021). Among the smallest companies this share is lower still, namely 8 per cent.

Although a divide is emerging here, the major AI developers and the rest of organisations do not live in completely separate worlds. Many state-of-the-art AI techniques are shared with the public. For example, OpenAI’s technology is used in all kinds of [innovative applications](https://medium.com/@theclickreader/openais-gpt-3-powered-applications-list-as-seen-on-twitter-4a3b09b4349) by third parties, such as an AI copywriter or a programme that enables a layperson to draw up annual accounts. The way technology companies make this knowledge available does differ. Sometimes it is a standalone service via an Application Programming Interface (API). Other times even the full source code of the model is made public. Such a truly open-source approach is still the exception, [critics](https://techcrunch.com/2022/05/19/when-big-ai-labs-refuse-to-open-source-their-models-the-community-steps-in/?guccounter=1) in academia note.

## AI-as-a-Service

But while the average scientist benefits from full insight into a model, most entrepreneurs are simply looking for a way to apply AI in an accessible way. AI-as-a-Service, of which ChatGPT is an example, can help. It is AI that is offered, to a greater or lesser extent, ready-made. Many of these services offer a very specific component from the AI toolkit, for example image recognition, translation, or sentiment analysis. Virtual assistants — programmes that can answer customer questions in a chat — are also available as a service. The provider of such assistants supplies the generic technology, after which it can be tailored to the client’s specific case. In addition, there are the so-called machine learning frameworks. These provide the building blocks to create AI models yourself relatively easily, including algorithms that can still be trained on client-owned data.

The fact that smaller companies have not yet started experimenting on a large scale is because AI is usually not just a matter of plugging in. First, technological expertise is essential to identify the possible application areas of AI in a company, and then actually integrate it into operations. In a time when IT staff are scarce, that creates challenges. In addition, many AI solutions must first be fed with data from the specific company. Those data are by no means always available.

## Large companies use more AI

Logically, smaller companies in particular suffer from a lack of IT expertise and data. This is also clear when looking more closely at the CBS figures; there is a clear link between company size and AI use. Of the largest companies, as many as 48 per cent applied AI in 2020 — a big difference from the 8 per cent of the smallest companies.

However, these figures do not reflect that AI is quietly making its way into all kinds of everyday applications. During a meeting in Microsoft Teams you can blur or change your background because a facial recognition algorithm ‘sees’ where the surroundings end and the person begins. LinkedIn suggests replies in private chats and on posts from people in your network because it uses a model that ‘understands’ and produces language. Newer cars are also packed with AI features, such as automatic parking aids and braking systems. To quote the Swedish philosopher Nick Bostrom: “Once something is sufficiently useful and normal, it is no longer called AI.”

## Persistent AI divide

OpenAI’s chatbot is of a very different order, and as such symbolises the AI development divide between the tech giants and the rest of the world. Yet the ubiquity of less spectacular AI — which we therefore do not recognise as such — teaches us that the futuristic technologies of today eventually find their way to the masses. The fact that large-scale experimentation with ChatGPT is now taking place and that people are reflecting on how such technology will change [education](https://www.nu.nl/algemeen/6247374/nieuwe-uitdaging-op-scholen-scholieren-laten-huiswerk-door-chatgpt-maken.html) also points in that direction. There will, however, always be a divide. As soon as one AI technology becomes embedded and is therefore no longer seen as AI, the next spectacular AI model rolls off big tech’s production line.

If the big tech companies choose to do this open source and thereby share more in-depth knowledge with academia and business, this would at least make the AI divide a little less deep. The chance that OpenAI will do this seems limited, however. From the ambition to keep AI “friendly”, the company considers the risk of misuse too great when the source code is available to the entire world.

# Tech entrepreneurs navigate between ambition and constraint

[Analysis](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/analyses/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

The European business climate for tech companies has been under debate for years. In their rapid growth, young technology companies often hit complex rules, fragmented markets and limited access to capital. Lawmakers do promise improvements, and entrepreneurs see the benefits of relatively stable Europe.

Europe is known for its strong regulation, but for fast-growing technology companies it rarely works to their advantage. Startups that want to scale up run into 27 national tax systems, labour laws and insolvency rules. Each country has its own interpretation of corporation tax, shareholder structures and dismissal law. That hinders rapid international growth. According to the [State of European Tech report](https://www.investeurope.eu/media/g3yobbcs/soet2024_report.pdf), 47 per cent of founders and investors see regulation and policymaking as a major barrier to growth.

## Limited access to growth capital

Another frequently cited frustration is the limited access to growth capital. Since 2015, European startups in the growth phase have raised about 300 billion dollars, but according to the report’s authors that could have been double if they could close large rounds as easily as in the US. European companies do pass the first funding rounds, but fall back at investments from 15 million dollars. Only 4.1 per cent pass that threshold, compared with 8.3 per cent in the US.

One cause is the lack of deep capital buffers. European pension funds, for example, invest only 0.01 per cent of their assets in global venture capital, more than three times less than their American counterparts. As a result, it remains difficult for European startups to scale up to the kind of tech companies that underpin digital autonomy.

## Europe is not so bad after all

Even so, the narrative that Europe is unfriendly to tech entrepreneurs is making way for a new story. In the State of European Tech 2024, a clear majority of European founders said they would choose to base their company in Europe again, if they could make that choice again. The current geopolitical situation can even act as a flywheel, according to Maarten Cleeren of Techleap: “America is turning inward, the market is destabilising. This creates room for companies in Europe.”

The European Commission, meanwhile, wants to end the patchwork of laws and regulations with the ‘28th regime’. This allows a company to register under a set of rules that are valid in all member states. The plans are part of the broader European Innovation Act. It should make it easier for startups to raise growth capital, including across borders. Investors now still run into different tax rules, supervisors and legal structures per country. Europe wants to make that simpler, so that funds and investors can operate more smoothly in multiple countries.

## Smart rewards, better financing

Reforms should also make it more attractive for startups to reward staff with share options. Since 2019, eleven countries have relaxed their national rules, including Germany, France, Spain and the Netherlands. The European Commission is now examining whether an overarching system is possible, as part of broader efforts to improve the entrepreneurial climate.

At the same time, political pressure is growing to have pension funds play a more active role. They now put an average of only 0.007 per cent of their assets into venture capital. In theory, they can invest larger amounts, but strict rules and internal guidelines discourage it. European institutions such as the Commission, the Investment Bank and the Investment Fund see pension funds as an important link to increase the amount of growth capital in Europe, and are exploring ways to spur that on — for example through risk-sharing via EU funds or adjusted capital requirements.

The conditions for a stronger ecosystem for startups and scale-ups are slowly falling into place. Brussels is moving, and entrepreneurs themselves also see the benefits of Europe. That is essential, because technological sovereignty starts with holding on to home-grown innovative strength.

# From 5G to nitrogen damage: ten themes for 2020

[Report](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/rapporten/index.html)

[Cross-sector](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/alle-sectoren/index.html)

[Sonny Duijn](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/sonny-duijn/index.html)

Legislation, consumer behaviour, sustainability. An entrepreneur has to keep many plates spinning at once. Having a good game strategy may be more important than ever in 2020. Our sector specialists already provide insight and background on ten themes that will affect companies and entire industries next year.

One thing is clear: an entrepreneur must be able to deal with rapid changes in the environment. And that is certainly not unique to our time. At the end of the nineteenth century, large cities reached the limits of their capacity. Horse-and-carriage transport led to chaos in cities such as New York and London due to extreme population growth: it caused many traffic deaths and nuisance from stench, and the required animal feed placed a heavy burden on the agricultural harvest.

Moreover, a total disruption of liveability loomed in the long term. Within fifty years London would be buried under almost three metres of manure, a journalist at The Times predicted in 1894. This gave the global capacity problem a name: [the Great Horse Manure Crisis](https://isgeschiedenis.nl/nieuws/grote-paardenmestcrisis-van-1894). The doomsday scenarios did not come to pass thanks to the rise of the car.

"Many capacity problems are the order of the day."

The manure crisis of the past has now given way to many new capacity problems. The pace of economic growth is limited by ecological constraints and other forms of scarcity. Emitting too much nitrogen harms nature, an ageing population can make our healthcare system unsustainable, and staff shortages limit companies’ ability to invest. And pushing the clock forward by fifty years produces doomsday scenarios such as a world that is unliveable due to global warming.

Companies also have to deal with their customers, whose behaviour is changing fast and is harder to read than before. A key cause is the rapid pace of technological development, which many entrepreneurs struggle to keep up with. The only constant companies can really hold on to is change. And change brings risks, but above all opportunities. Those who can handle it best will be the winners.

To give insight into all the plates on which the game must be played, we present here, in random order, the key themes for 2020. Without claiming to be complete, it is clear that scarcity underlies most challenges, while technological developments, as in the nineteenth century, can provide the necessary solutions.

## 1. Nitrogen

Many construction projects, such as building roads or homes, release nitrogen. An excess of nitrogen can damage nearby nature reserves. Previously, under the Integrated Approach to Nitrogen (PAS), it was possible to obtain permits for such construction projects, provided that measures would be taken in future to limit the negative effects on nature.

In May 2019, however, the PAS scheme was suspended because it was in conflict with the European Habitats Directive. For all projects that emit additional nitrogen compared to the current situation, it must now first be demonstrated that they have no direct significant effect on 118 of the more than 160 Natura 2000 areas that are sensitive to nitrogen.

The government has now taken various measures to reduce nitrogen emissions. For example, the maximum speed on motorways during the day is going to 100 kilometres per hour, livestock will receive a different type of feed and there will be voluntary buy-outs of pig farms. The measures are intended to give the construction sector some breathing space in 2020 and thus enable the construction of 75,000 new homes and get seven major infrastructure projects moving again. That does not mean this will succeed.

Suspending the PAS scheme will in any case have consequences for entrepreneurs, especially those in construction. “Without concrete solutions, the revenue loss for the construction sector could lead to 70,000 job losses in the coming years. Because of this nitrogen issue, some companies are choosing not to extend temporary contracts and to send self-employed workers and agency staff home,” says Madeline Buijs, sector economist Construction and Real Estate.

**Read more on this topic in this publication: [Nitrogen blows widely across sectors](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/bouw/stikstof-waait-breed-uit-over-sectoren.html)**

## 2. PFAS

PFAS is a collective name for about 6,000 substances with water-, fat- and dirt-repellent properties. Because of these useful properties, they are widely used in, among other things, raincoats, pizza boxes, firefighting foam, cosmetics and as a non-stick coating on Teflon pans. Various scientific studies in recent years have shown that at least some forms of PFAS can lead to different types of cancer, cardiovascular diseases and reduced fertility.

Because of the possible damage to public health, stricter standards now apply to, for example, moving soil in which PFAS is found. This directly affects sand and gravel extraction and dredging activities.

"Entrepreneurs fear that policy on nitrogen and PFAS will cost them 8 per cent of revenue in 2020."

That the measures on PFAS are often mentioned in the same breath as those on nitrogen is mainly due to the roughly similar moment when the measures took effect and the fact that more or less the same companies are affected. In both cases, it mainly concerns entrepreneurs in construction, the building materials industry, the building trade and the transport sector.

“Entrepreneurs in these sectors fear that policy on nitrogen and PFAS will cost them 8 per cent of revenue in 2020. That would amount to around 16 billion euros,” says Albert Jan Swart, sector economist Industry and Transport & Logistics.

**Read more on this topic in this publication: [Billions in damage expected due to nitrogen and PFAS](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/bouw/miljardenschade-verwacht-door-stikstof-en-pfas.html)**

## 3. New generation mobile networks: 5G

Mobile networks are considered essential, strategic infrastructure for the Netherlands. In 2020, frequencies will be auctioned in the Netherlands for the first time for the new generation of mobile networks, called 5G. Rolling out 5G will require a major effort. 5G needs a much denser network of masts than its predecessor 4G. The installation costs of the new 5G network will therefore be higher than for 4G at the time.

When it comes to auctioning 5G frequencies, a number of Asian countries and, among others, Germany, Finland and Italy have already gone before us. Planning in the Netherlands was initially delayed because one of the required frequencies was used as a Defence listening station. When the government announced at the end of 2018 that this station would move abroad, the auction could be scheduled. The auction of the first frequencies will take place in 2020.

“To maintain competitiveness despite the relatively late auction, it is important that companies can benefit fully from 5G. Thanks to much higher download speeds, the new network has far greater potential for the business market than its predecessors,” says Kasper Buiting, sector economist TMT and Business Services. He foresees that 5G can play a major role in, among other things, developing the self-driving car and deploying Virtual Reality services. “Such applications can be important for the Netherlands’ future earnings model.”

**Read more on this topic in this publication: IT must drive higher labour productivity**

## 4. Staff shortages

In the Netherlands in recent years it has become painfully clear that there is a mismatch in the labour market. For example, among entrepreneurs in construction and the IT sector, the shortage of staff is experienced as a strong constraint. Companies will face this in 2020 as well.

The challenge for companies is to find new ways to bind staff. This can be done by offering more flexible working hours or by giving extra pay to people who are willing to work more hours. Companies can also push harder on automation to replace labour and keep growth on track. Dutch companies appear relatively reluctant to choose this route for the time being.

“The mismatches vary by region; they differ by area. For example, North Brabant has a shortage of lorry drivers for domestic transport, while in Friesland there is more job search activity in this occupation than there are vacancies,” says Sandra Philippen, Chief Economist at the Economics Department.

**Read more on this topic in this publication: [The labour market is larger for men than for women](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/leisure/arbeidsmarkt-is-groter-voor-mannen-dan-voor-vrouwen.html)**

## 5. The ‘commodity trap’

Some companies get caught in a so-called ‘commodity trap’: the services or products they offer are then barely distinguishable from those of the competition, so price is the only deciding factor. The number of companies that can afford to structurally compete on the lowest price in the long term is limited.

A striking example is the challenges faced in recent years by some shop operators. They have faced strong competition from online players who sell their goods at generally lower prices, often shipping to the consumer cheaply or free of charge. Distinctiveness is important to avoid being in a constant price fight.

This threat of the ‘commodity trap’ will also be very present in a number of sectors in 2020. The transport sector is one of them. It turns out to be difficult for some parties to differentiate themselves. Clients play logistics service providers with a weak negotiating position off against each other to pay the lowest possible price. This can lead to a price war among those service providers. As a result, the profitability of logistics companies is under pressure, making it difficult to invest in innovative services.

“Threat of the ‘commodity trap’."

“It is important for logistics companies to offer distinctive services,” says Bart Banning, sector banker Transport & Logistics. “And to be able to do that, ‘leadership’ is a more important characteristic for entrepreneurs than ever. This helps to stay out of the commodity trap and to invest in innovative logistics concepts and sustainability. The transport sector must reduce CO₂ emissions and other forms of waste.”

**Read more on this topic in this publication: [Good leadership brings you new leaders](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/logistiek/goed-leiderschap-brengt-u-nieuwe-leiders.html)**

## 6. The changing employee and legislation

Employees are essential to a company. In earlier publications we concluded for both the retail and the [construction](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/bouw/soft-skills-sleutel-tot-structureel-succes-in-bouwsector.html) sectors that success is derived from company culture. This of course applies to other industries as well: for example, staff is crucial for [hospitality](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/leisure/verloop-personeel-kost-horeca-jaarlijks-1-4-miljard.html).

Employees’ wishes have changed. Employees nowadays look more than before for a job that fits their own values, standards and beliefs. For companies it has become more important to pursue a ‘purpose’, a higher social goal.

“Companies would do well to work on ‘strategic HR policy’. This can be done, for example, through upskilling and reskilling, by applying new methods of recruitment and selection, and by adjusting employment conditions to the wishes of the future,” says Han Mesters, sector banker Business Services.

Companies are also being forced to look more strategically at their staff due to new legislation being introduced from 2020. New self-employment (zzp) legislation is in the making and, as of 1 January, the [Balanced Labour Market Act](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/leisure/nieuwe-wetgeving-komt-horeca-duur-te-staan.html) takes effect.

The Balanced Labour Market Act aims to make permanent contracts more attractive. Employers who offer many flexible forms of work will face much higher unemployment insurance contributions. In addition, in future an on-call worker must be scheduled at least four days before the relevant working day. This imposes restrictions on companies that offer seasonal work, such as hospitality.

**Read more on this topic in this publication: [Getting a grip on the changing labour market](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/logistiek/grip-op-veranderende-arbeidsmarkt.html)**

## 7. Healthcare takes place more ‘remotely’

Dutch hospital care faces a difficult task. [Profit margins in 2020](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/headlines-insights/stijging-loonkosten-stelt-ziekenhuis-voor-problemen.html) will be wafer-thin, because the rise in wage costs is only partially reimbursed due to agreements already made with health insurers.

At the same time, due to ageing and the increasing number of people with a chronic condition, it is important to make investments that make care future-proof. With the rising number of patients and an acute shortage of care providers, delivering hospital care in its current form is not possible.

To prevent the quality of care from coming under pressure, care must therefore be organised differently. A logical route is for hospitals to ensure that care can be provided more ‘remotely’. This can be done by offering patients explainer videos, measuring health remotely, diagnosing remotely and deploying care robots. Digitisation of the entire care process is the key.

Another part of remote care is the ‘regionalisation’ of care, where a larger share of the required patient care is taken up by GPs and home care organisations. This is being put into practice by, for example, the Isala hospital in Zwolle, which expects to deliver 10 per cent of care outside the hospital within three years thanks to cooperation with care providers in the region.

“It will soon become clear that it is ultimately better for the patient if care takes place more remotely on the basis of information collected at home. It can prevent a large part of time-consuming and intensive hospital visits,” says Anja van Balen, sector banker Healthcare. “The complex circumstances in 2020 may prove to be a ‘blessing in disguise’ and the transition to remote care may take further shape.”

**Read more on this topic in this publication: [Will a patient no longer visit the doctor](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/healthcare/komt-een-patient-niet-meer-bij-de-dokter.html)**

## 8. The energy transition

To limit global warming as much as possible, the Netherlands has committed to a 49 per cent reduction in CO₂ emissions by 2030 compared to 1990. That target will only be met if the required energy transition gains more momentum and the planned projects are actually implemented.

In the climate agreement, Dutch policymakers and stakeholders have therefore made numerous arrangements. The number of offshore wind farms will be scaled up significantly, a large number of buildings must be disconnected from natural gas and the intention is to close all coal-fired power stations by 2030.

However, implementing these plans is not that simple. Many pieces of the puzzle have to fall into place to achieve all the ambitions. There are technical, legal, operational and financial bottlenecks that must be overcome for each project. A lot of cooperation is also required between companies and sectors that previously had little to do with each other.

"For now, the climate agreement is little more than a good start."

2020 could be the year in which various disciplines and sectors find each other better, so that the promises in the climate agreement can actually be fulfilled. “Many people from various disciplines and sectors have contributed to the climate agreement. Unfortunately, it is little more than a good start. The climate agreement only sketches a rough route towards the 2030 climate target. The real challenge lies in actually realising all the individual projects,” says Arnold Mulder, sector banker Energy.

Part of the energy transition will be enforced by the government. From 1 July 2020, the Nearly Energy Neutral Buildings (BENG) requirements will apply to new-build, and the NTA8800 requirements to all existing buildings. Due to these new requirements, energy consumption will become the leading factor in determining the energy label, instead of the presence of sustainable applications. For offices it has long applied that from 2023 they must have at least Label C and in 2030 Label A. From 2021, standards for other non-residential buildings will also be set.

Regulation provides certainty, making it more attractive for companies to invest in, for example, solar panels or in thermal energy storage.

**Read more on this topic in this publication: [Christmas puzzle](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/energie/kerstpuzzel.html)**

## 9. The future of food

The world’s population will grow to ten billion people by 2050. All these people must be fed. At the same time, obesity has become a bigger problem worldwide: between 1980 and 2015, the number of cases of obesity doubled in more than seventy countries and increased in others as well, according to [this study](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa1614362).

This is why 37 scientists have examined in depth, in the [EAT Lancet report](https://eatforum.org/eat-lancet-commission/), how ten billion people can be fed with healthy food. An important part of the answer is that diets must change and production must be adjusted accordingly. More vegetables, fruit and nuts, and less red meat and sugar, for example.

The recommendations from the report will gain more traction in 2020. The food industry is therefore focusing on healthier and more sustainable. This seems easier than it is, because for many consumers price still plays a dominant role when buying food and drink. We do want to eat more sustainably and healthily, but it must not cost too much. And it still has to taste good.

And that calls for creativity, says Nadia Menkveld, sector economist Agrifood. “Food producers are innovating strongly. Products with fewer or no animal proteins or sugar are gaining market share.” New ingredients are also being experimented with, such as insects or algae.

It is not only food products that are being renewed; the process is also being examined. An important part of producing more sustainably is reducing food waste. Currently, one third of all food is lost worldwide. Many countries, including the Netherlands, have set the goal of halving food waste by 2030.

“Producers are adapting their processes to this, but are also developing new products from food that would otherwise be destroyed. Think of bread made from brewers’ spent grain, a residual stream from the beer brewing process,” says Menkveld. Entrepreneurs can take various steps to reduce food waste. Restaurants can opt for less choice on the menu, smaller plates and less stock. Clear communication with guests ensures not only that it is understood, but also appreciated.

**Read more on this topic in this publication: [Halving food waste by 2030. How?](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/food/halvering-van-voedselverspilling-in-2030-hoe-dan.html)**

## 10. Brexit

Entrepreneurs also face political uncertainties. One of the most topical is Brexit. After three and a half years of wrangling, 2020 will finally be the year of Brexit. After the historic victory of the British Conservatives, the UK’s departure from the European Union (EU) seems inevitable. With their vote, the British electorate have sent a clear signal that the knot must be cut after all these years and that the deal concluded by Prime Minister Johnson with the European Union in October must be implemented.

Although ‘Bremain’ is no longer an option, it is reassuring that Brexit will very likely take place in an orderly fashion. A no-deal Brexit would have cost the Dutch economy 0.75 percentage points of growth in a year. The UK will formally leave the European Union on 1 February.

"Some caution around Brexit remains necessary."

Even so, some caution remains necessary. The Brexit book is not yet closed. The transition period during which the United Kingdom will continue to operate under EU trade rules lasts until 31 December 2020. Under the agreement (Withdrawal Agreement), the transition period can be extended by one or two years if the parties fail to reach a free trade agreement in time.

However, Johnson says he is sticking to the end of 2020 as a deadline. Given the complexity of the withdrawal process, this is ambitious. If the negotiations stall during the course of next year and Johnson stubbornly sticks to that deadline, the danger of a no-deal Brexit could resurface.

# Sustainable IT needs a push

[Report](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/rapporten/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

Ingrid Laane

Kamalika Patra

Show all authors

Despite rapidly growing attention to energy saving and climate change, IT sustainability is not yet embraced across the board. A minority of IT decision-makers (35 per cent) include sustainability as a purchasing criterion. This emerges from research by ABN AMRO in collaboration with PanelWizard among 588 IT decision-makers from companies across all sectors.

## Download the full report

(Can’t see a download or unable to download the report? Then upgrade your browser to the latest version.)

- [Sustainable IT needs a push (7 May 2021)

  2 MB](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/media/abn-amro-verduurzaming-it-heeft-duw-in-de-rug-nodig_tcm16-109386.pdf)

To give the market for green IT a boost, we need government intervention as well as more collaboration and knowledge sharing among IT companies across the entire chain.

## Traditional mindset hinders sustainable IT procurement

Other priorities, costs that are too high and insufficient knowledge are the main reasons for IT decision-makers1 not to opt for sustainable procurement. Interviews with IT companies that present themselves as sustainable — for example because they programme in an energy-efficient way or supply circular hardware — also show that many IT purchasers have a traditional mindset. Decisions are based on a narrow set of key performance indicators (KPIs) that emphasise, for example, speed and continuity, while sustainability does not yet play a role. Better one server too many than too little capacity later is still too often the thinking, according to Patrick de Ridder of Cobra Systems. “For the IT professional, safety and reliability come first. Building in overcapacity is therefore commonplace. ‘Do we need ten servers? Then make it twelve.’”

## Growing environmental burden from IT forces action

IT contributes greatly to our society and will only grow in the coming years. That means we can keep benefiting from what digitalisation offers us, from working from home to smart buildings and precision agriculture. But there is also a flipside: digitalisation places a heavy burden on the environment, in terms of both energy and raw material use. For example, the production of IT hardware depends on various critical raw materials, while only a small percentage of those materials is recovered after the device is discarded. Data centres also place an ever greater strain on our energy supply. Production and use of IT currently account for 2.1 to 3.9 per cent of global greenhouse gas emissions.2 Researchers estimate that this share will be between 6 and 14 per cent in 2040.3

## Expectations of IT parties unevenly distributed

These facts, combined with increasing pressure from politics and society, call for continued effort to limit the ecological footprint of digitalisation. As part of the Climate Agreement, the Dutch IT sector has therefore stated the ambition to be climate neutral by 2030. All parties in the IT chain have a responsibility here: from the companies that provide the physical IT infrastructure and equipment to the developers of digital services. However, IT decision-makers expect the most from data centres; almost half of respondents expect to a reasonable to strong degree that data centres take sustainability into account. For cloud and hosting providers this percentage is slightly lower (43.4), followed by suppliers of IT hardware (41.1). The lowest expectations on sustainability are for software companies. This is a missed opportunity, because energy-aware software design, among other things, can deliver major gains.

## Fragmentation of the chain hampers sustainability

A barrier for IT companies to (fully) make the switch to sustainable propositions is that demand is still simply insufficient. This is the familiar chicken-and-egg problem. In addition, fragmentation of the IT chain works against them. The owner of the problem is not always the solver. For example, grid operators carry the problem when power shortages arise, but IT companies must implement the solutions. This can involve, for instance, a commercial data centre that can organise its cooling more efficiently and a hosting company that must smartly configure its servers in that data centre. Many solutions also come from outside the chain, such as from academia or start-ups.

## Combination of government intervention and collaboration in the IT chain is crucial

A bright spot is that IT companies offering sustainable products and services do cautiously see demand for their products increasing, as they told ABN AMRO. This is all the more true for sustainable IT propositions that also deliver commercial benefits. To give the market for sustainable IT a nudge and secure a permanent place in the digital ecosystem, government intervention is desirable, such as subsidies and regulation. IT companies themselves can help the market by being transparent about their sustainability efforts and joining forces with other chain partners for experiments and knowledge sharing.

This publication explores the market for IT companies that are actively working to reduce their ecological footprint, or want to take the first steps in this area. The report contains a broad inventory of the key themes in ‘green IT’, provides an overview of the research results and identifies specific challenges for the market to mature.

# A bitter aftertaste looms from a blind 5G auction

[Headlines & Insights](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/headlines-insights/index.html)

[Technology, Media & Telecom](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/index.html)

Kamalika Patra

The government is considering partially ‘blind’ auctioning the 3.5 gigahertz 5G frequency next year. It hopes this will channel higher proceeds into the treasury. High costs for the frequencies, however, mean less room for necessary network investment. That could hinder the growth of the Dutch economy and the circular transition.

## Insight

Owners of mobile networks are [concerned](https://fd.nl/ondernemen/1373591/mobiele-operators-vrezen-te-hoge-kosten-nieuwe-5g-veiling-oqb1ca9Nlm1S) that the new auction model will result in sky‑high licence costs at the expense of network investment. In last year’s auction of lower 5G frequencies, all bids were transparent to participants and the three highest bidders ultimately each secured the same amount of bandwidth at the same price. In the coming blind auction, three bandwidths will be allocated without candidates having insight into each other’s bids. In theory, that would yield higher proceeds because bidders are prompted to submit their best offer straight away. Network owners therefore recently [argued](https://www.telecompaper.com/nieuws/mnos-pleiten-bij-tweede-kamer-tegen-plannen-35-ghz-veiling--1371854) in the House of Representatives for an investment‑friendly auction model. They argue that rising licence costs ultimately lead to higher economic and social costs.

Additional investment in network infrastructure is crucial to unlock the full potential of 5G. The increase in speed and data capacity when upgrading to the 5G network is achieved not only through higher frequencies and hardware adjustments, as with previous generation upgrades, but also by replacing old antennas with new ‘smart’ antennas1. These Multiple‑Input Multiple‑Output antennas can actively track devices and direct an isolated signal at devices that are processing data at that moment. This reduces energy consumption and streamlines data transmission for ultra‑fast mobile internet with versatile, intelligent applications in industry.

Experience shows that higher costs easily lead to higher prices for end users, which holds back their technological development. The sectors where those higher auction costs weigh most heavily are industry and trade, where 5G can deliver major system improvements.

For example, the development of smart factories in industry can benefit greatly from fast networks. Among other things, machine‑to‑machine communication makes it possible to collect and analyse data in real time to optimise production processes. In wholesale and retail, fault diagnostics could also be carried out continuously and intelligently, so less delay occurs in identifying causes of malfunctions in complex sorting processes2. Less delay, lower energy consumption, greater reliability and wider reach in deploying 5G are the indispensable [characteristics](https://www.mckinsey.com/~/media/mckinsey/industries/advanced%20electronics/our%20insights/the%205g%20era%20new%20horizons%20for%20advanced%20electronics%20and%20industrial%20companies/the-5g-era-new-horizons-for-advanced-electronics-and-industrial-companies.pdf) of the successful production processes of the future.

All these smart capabilities, however, cannot be fully utilised if network investment fails to materialise. That affects the Netherlands’ competitive position, especially as other countries are already [actively working with 5G](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/hulp-van-5g-bij-verhoging-arbeidsproductiviteit.html). An outdated technological infrastructure leaves traces in the long term; essential infrastructure forms the basis for further technological development. A substandard 5G network will readily suppress economic growth.

And industry and trade are precisely the sectors with the largest added value in the Dutch economy. Industrial activities account for as much as 22 per cent of all added value and trade accounts for 12 per cent3. A flawless 5G network is therefore decisive for the Netherlands’ future [earning capacity](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/5g-essentieel-voor-het-verdienvermogen-van-nederland.html).

Besides economic damage, substandard infrastructure also has social consequences. Internet‑of‑things applications lead the way here. For example, vehicles can be controlled autonomously to prevent traffic jams, cut CO2 emissions and reduce the number of traffic accidents. Internet of things can also create smart buildings that automatically adjust heating and lighting to save energy. In addition, this technology enables predictive maintenance of electrical devices, because they can indicate themselves when maintenance is needed; existing systems will then last longer.

The economic and sustainable potential of a fully functioning 5G network is huge. This new generation of mobile networks can process far more data, achieve higher speeds and allow more devices to exchange information simultaneously compared with 4G. It is therefore unwise for the auctions of these networks not to prioritise higher short‑term treasury proceeds. Let this not be a case of penny wise, pound foolish.

# Without the right figures, green IT remains an illusion

[Analysis](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/analyses/index.html)

[Cross-sector](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/alle-sectoren/index.html)

[Julia Krauwer](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/auteurs/julia-krauwer/index.html)

Companies that use computing power and data storage in the cloud and want to make their IT greener need to know where they can make gains. For now, however, cloud providers are not sharing all the figures on energy use and CO₂ emissions, and they each do so in a different way.

## Interview with Julia Krauwer on BNR Nieuwsradio

Listen to the segment with Sector Banker Julia Krauwer on the importance of good data for making IT more sustainable.

As a result, IT teams do not know where they can achieve the most sustainability gains, and they cannot measure the impact of optimisations. For the time being there are no legal requirements or binding standards that set out which information cloud companies must make available to their own customers.

## Limiting negative environmental effects

Around the world, companies are moving their IT activities to the cloud, and both the development and use of artificial intelligence (AI) are pushing up demand for data centre capacity. According to an [estimate](https://www.iea.org/reports/energy-and-ai/energy-supply-for-ai) by the International Energy Agency, global electricity consumption by data centres in 2024 was around 460 terawatt-hours (TWh), equal to the annual consumption of countries such as Sweden or Argentina. By 2030 the agency expects it to more than double to 1,000 TWh. About 40 per cent of that power will then come from renewable sources, but nearly half will still require coal- and gas-fired power stations. The carbon footprint of digitalisation will therefore remain substantial for the time being.

Data centres have become increasingly efficient in recent years. This is largely due to scaling up; a large data centre can spread the energy used to cool servers — a relatively large share of power consumption — across many customers. There has also been progress with new, more energy-efficient cooling techniques and software to manage the indoor climate. However, users of AI and cloud services, which determine demand for data centre capacity, in practice lack sufficient tools to reduce the negative environmental effects of their IT efforts.

## Measurement is knowledge

“Greening IT always starts with the right information,” says Wiebren van der Zee, a specialist in green IT at ABN AMRO. “Think of data on energy and water use, emissions, and use of raw materials. In our IT organisation we collect these data for our employees’ workstations — the laptops and monitors — but also for our own data centres and the cloud services we procure.”

## Tools for sustainable digitalisation

Together with various organisations from the Nationale Coalitie Duurzame Digitalisering (NCDD), ABN AMRO has developed the [Sustainable IT Impact Assessment](https://eur03.safelinks.protection.outlook.com/?url=https%3A%2F%2Fcoalitieduurzamedigitalisering.nl%2Fsustainable-it-impact-assessment-siia%2F&data=05%7C02%7Cjulia.krauwer%40nl.abnamro.com%7C9948116aa7d649b3c17508de1082ebd8%7C3a15904d3fd94256a753beb05cdf0c6d%7C0%7C0%7C638966351662129336%7CUnknown%7CTWFpbGZsb3d8eyJFbXB0eU1hcGkiOnRydWUsIlYiOiIwLjAuMDAwMCIsIlAiOiJXaW4zMiIsIkFOIjoiTWFpbCIsIldUIjoyfQ%3D%3D%7C0%7C%7C%7C&sdata=Yz6QXd%2FJKDj3vTjpdic9%2FGPln8C8x2QH6hBRajvDbgc%3D&reserved=0) (SIIA). This tool offers practical guidance and extensive background information to get started with making IT more sustainable. The advice is based, among other things, on the concrete experiences of businesses and public authorities.

Large cloud providers have been trying for some years to meet this information need through CO₂ dashboards that allow their customers to keep track of the emissions from their digital efforts. Willem Jan Groenewoud, CEO of software company Ab Ovo, welcomes this wave: “It is good that they are providing more transparency than a few years ago. Measurement is knowledge.” The company strongly focuses on making the software it develops for its customers’ mission-critical processes more sustainable. “We assume a scenario in which energy may be rationed, and therefore design software that uses as little energy as possible.”

The cloud world is far more transparent than a few years ago, but there is still much to criticise about the information provided. Not all the required data are supplied, and measurement methods and the level of detail differ greatly between providers.

## Paper reality

It starts with how cloud companies report their own, organisation-wide emissions. According to an analysis by the British newspaper The Guardian, the data centres owned by the largest tech companies emitted almost [eight times](https://www.theguardian.com/technology/2024/sep/15/data-center-gas-emissions-tech) more than reported in total. This analysis covered data from 2020 through 2022.

The difference can be explained by the measurement method. Often it is “market-based”. This means that providers calculate their CO₂ emissions on the basis of the number of certificates for green energy purchased or long-term power purchase agreements for energy from, for example, a wind farm. Such arrangements say nothing about the actual power mix at the data centre’s location. “It is a paper reality,” says Van der Zee of ABN AMRO. “Green energy is being shifted administratively all over the world.”

For example, a cloud provider can buy “guarantees of origin” for a hydroelectric plant in Norway and allocate them in the accounts to a server farm in Ireland, while that site partly runs on electricity from coal-fired power stations. On paper the footprint is then zero, but in reality there is definitely CO₂ emitted through the use of fossil energy.

## Average power mix

Under the European sustainability law CSRD, short for Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive, market-based reporting alone is no longer enough. On the one hand, organisations should be encouraged to purchase green energy and it is good that they provide insight into these efforts. On the other hand, the market-based measurement method paints too rosy a picture of the actual emissions from electricity generation. That is why the CSRD also requires companies to report “location-based”. Here the average power mix in the region where the company or data centre sources its electricity is the starting point.

Both measurement methods also appear in cloud companies’ emissions dashboards. However, the location-based figures are not always shown as prominently. At Amazon, the market-based emissions data are foregrounded, and users must click through to gain insight into the location-based figures. Microsoft even omits location-based emissions entirely from its dashboard.

## Location-based reporting

Location-based emissions are calculated by multiplying electricity consumption in kilowatt-hours (kWh) by the local “emission factor”. This shows how much CO₂-equivalent is released on average in producing 1 kWh of electricity. In regions with wind turbines or hydroelectric plants, the emission factor is lower than in regions where electricity is mainly generated in coal- or gas-fired power stations. The location-based method is an average and does not require insight into daily or hourly fluctuations in the energy mix.

## Suppliers’ willingness

In the world of emissions dashboards there are no binding standards, and customers of cloud services are in practice dependent on the willingness of their cloud providers to share information. This leads to great variation in the level of detail. Most offer monthly figures, but the update frequency differs. Some update continuously, others are months behind. There is also variation in granularity; sometimes emissions are shown only per organisation or region, at other times you can also monitor at the level of individual services or projects.

Not all dashboards also include scope 3 emissions in their calculations. Whereas scopes 1 and 2 in this case concern the use of fossil energy and fossil-generated electricity to keep data centres running, scope 3 concerns the more indirect emissions. These are caused, for example, by the production and disposal of IT equipment, and the construction of data centres and offices. The cloud providers that report at scope 3 level do not do so consistently either; one limits itself to hardware production, while another also includes, for example, operating the staff canteen.

Boavizta, an initiative of dozens of European IT companies and consultancies, has developed an unambiguous way to calculate the climate impact of digital infrastructure. At present every cloud provider calculates in its own way, creating an incomplete picture and making figures hard to compare between suppliers. Boavizta, for example, provides data on the environmental effects of producing and transporting servers, which companies can use for a more thorough calculation of the ecological footprint of their IT activities. Resilio also offers such data, combined with advice, but as a commercial service.

## Insight for development teams

Cloud dashboards are often primarily aimed at insight and reporting. It is positive that some providers also give concrete advice on how companies can reduce their emissions. Microsoft and Google, for example, suggest scaling down unused capacity.

However, for organisations with a rich IT landscape it is difficult to gain insight into emissions at application level. Software usually consists of several components that are spread across projects, regions or services. As a result, the direct effects of changes made by development teams — “lighter” code, or a more efficient architecture — easily remain out of sight. “This is why we have developed our own AI-driven tooling for this,” says Groenewoud of software company Ab Ovo.

## Using less energy

According to Max Schulze, founder of the Sustainable Digital Infrastructure Alliance (SDIA), the focus on emissions is actually insufficient. “We should optimise for lower energy use, for fewer data centres. We live on a continent with limited space and resources,” he says.

The other experts also recognise that green IT starts with insight into energy use. “A data centre’s emissions are not a pure measurement instrument,” says Groenewoud of Ab Ovo. “We first map the electricity used in kilowatt-hours at function level. Only then do we look at the location of the data centre and the energy mix at that site.”

None of the cloud providers, however, provides insight into the number of kWh their customers consume. Illogical, says Schulze of the SDIA, because you must always start with the efficiency of your product. “If you buy a car, you want to know how many kilometres you can drive on a litre of fuel to get a picture of the vehicle’s efficiency. Only after that do you translate it into the number of grams of CO₂-equivalent per kilometre.”

## Alternative solutions

Various parties are stepping into the gap created by the limited information provided by cloud providers. One example is Cloud Carbon Footprint (CCF), an open-source tool that reads the actual use of cloud services and converts it into both kilowatt-hours (kWh) and CO₂ emissions. These data are also made available hourly, allowing IT teams to see, for example, when applications are used most and which systems keep running unnecessarily at night.

Although it can be a good starting point for organisations seeking extra insight, with open-source services such as CCF there is no guarantee that they will be updated in a timely and regular manner. Commercial parties do offer that guarantee, and various paid services have been launched in recent years, including Greenpixie.

## Data from the source

All services, from the cloud providers’ own dashboards to externally developed solutions, are based on assumptions about the energy use of the servers and the digital services running on them. That is why project NADIKI, an initiative by SDIA together with the University of Stuttgart and data centre operator Kolo, goes right back to the source: the data centre. It collects live measurements via sensors and links them to the current carbon intensity of the power grid. “We have sensors everywhere, from the power supply of the entire data centre, down to rack and server level,” says Schulze. This information is used to capture energy use and emissions for specific workloads.

Schulze: “This is the first time that energy and emissions are measured end to end.” The project is still in the pilot phase, but it does show that improvement is possible compared with current methodologies.

## Keep communicating

When the emissions dashboards were first launched, they were greeted as a welcome and much-needed feature. Some of the cloud service providers have big ambitions around transparency and see it as a source of differentiation. Across the board, however, the pace differs greatly.

According to Van der Zee of ABN AMRO, organisations should make their wishes known to their IT suppliers. “They should keep communicating about the type of information they need.” As long as the information provision is not optimal, the gap will be filled by open-source tools and the services of specialised software companies.

There are also standards, such as those from Boavizta, and the Greenhouse Gas Protocol also has IT-specific guidelines. There are, however, no real obligations, which unnecessarily presents challenges for organisations that want to make their IT greener. Regulation can help and ensure that all players report in the same way.
