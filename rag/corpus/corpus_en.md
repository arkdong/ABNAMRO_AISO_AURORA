# Using AI Successfully Requires More Than Technology

[Analysis](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/analyses/index.html)

Companies worldwide are investing tens of billions in artificial intelligence, yet only a handful are seeing demonstrable returns. The organisations willing to redesign both their business model and operations with the help of AI are setting the tone for the years ahead.

The [World Economic Forum](https://www.weforum.org/publications/the-future-of-jobs-report-2025/) predicts that by 2030, 170 million jobs will be created worldwide and 92 million will disappear. Further advances in AI and information-processing technology are expected to create 11 million jobs while displacing 9 million others, more than any other technological wave so far. Mass unemployment is not expected immediately, but the nature of work will change dramatically. One important difference from earlier technological waves is that the impact of AI will be felt most strongly by highly educated employees in knowledge-intensive sectors and in roles requiring medium to higher levels of education. Administrative, financial and legal roles in particular, but also customer service, consultants, ICT professionals and marketing roles, will be affected. In the past this group was [barely affected](https://www.uwv.nl/nl/achtergrond/ai-verandert-niet-je-baan-maar-wel-je-takenpakket) through lower wages or declining employment, but recent and future AI developments can increasingly perform non-routine cognitive tasks as well.

[Accounting firm PwC](https://www.pwc.nl/nl/actueel-publicaties/assets/pdfs/gen-ai-at-work.pdf) calculates that 44 per cent of Dutch jobs are highly exposed to AI. That does not mean these jobs will disappear immediately, but it does mean a substantial part of current tasks can be taken over or supported by AI. In specialist business services, that exposure rises to as much as 75 per cent of jobs, according to PwC. By comparison, in construction or agriculture, where physical labour and human interaction are central, exposure remains below 25 per cent. Jobs that still remain largely out of reach are those involving fine manual work or direct human interaction. This effect of AI marks a clear reversal from earlier technological waves, which mainly affected physical work. [Labour market researcher Intelligence Group](https://intelligence-group.nl/nl/resources/een-ai-impact-analyse-op-beroepen-en-sectoren-in-nederland/) also sees this shift and likewise concludes in a recent report that the impact of AI on business services is relatively large. In financial services and accountancy, 43 per cent of the current task package could be automated by AI, compared with 40 per cent in consultancy and around 34 per cent in the legal sector. Despite these high percentages, only 15 per cent of employees worry that AI will largely take over their work within five years. The researchers conclude that "the short-term effect is overestimated, while the long-term effect is underestimated".

## Big promises, small results

There is no doubt that AI has enormous potential, but researchers at the [Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT)](https://www.forbes.com/sites/jasonsnyder/2025/08/26/mit-finds-95-of-genai-pilots-fail-because-companies-avoid-friction/) warn that its real-world application is still proving difficult. Employees are using chatbots such as ChatGPT on a massive scale for individual tasks such as writing emails, summarising reports and generating ideas, but once organisations try to integrate AI into their core processes, only 20 per cent of initiatives reach the pilot stage. The main reason is that employees show little willingness to embrace new internal AI solutions. The research shows that many employees already make heavy private use of generic tools such as ChatGPT, which in practice are often more powerful, more user-friendly and more reliable than the often more limited AI tools offered by their employer. As a result, a lasting preference emerges for these generic tools, and employees fall back on tools such as ChatGPT.

At the same time, the researchers warn that the current generation of AI applications often behaves like a "memoryless" assistant and therefore cannot be used in core processes. Although the underlying models are trained on enormous datasets, in practice they do not automatically adapt to a company's specific and changing context. They are trained on general data, but they do not anchor specific feedback or new business logic into their system. Because of this lack of adaptation to the organisation, results remain superficial and the expected transformation does not materialise.

For carrying out core tasks, such as long-running project work and client management, 90 per cent of users still prefer people, the MIT researchers conclude. With every new interaction, the user has to enter the full context all over again, a time-consuming process that wipes out the promised efficiency gains. The same study shows that many bespoke solutions fail because integration is difficult and alignment with business processes is poor. Without seamless links to core systems, AI ends up back in the drawer. Large organisations in particular run into this problem: implementations there often take nine months or longer, while medium-sized companies see results within a few months. Investment priorities are also skewed: around half of AI budgets go to front-office functions such as sales and marketing, where returns become visible quickly. Yet the biggest efficiency gains are in the back office. In legal departments, procurement and finance, AI can speed up contract analysis and reporting while reducing costs. Because these returns are harder to measure, investment in AI lags behind in those areas.

## The hourly billing model under pressure

With AI applications, repetitive and time-consuming tasks such as data analysis, reporting, code generation and customer service can be completed in a fraction of the original time. Because AI delivers such major time savings, value is shifting from time to outcomes, putting pressure on the foundations of the traditional hourly billing model. This development is forcing service providers to fundamentally reconsider their pricing model. According to an analysis by trade publication [The Information](https://www.theinformation.com/articles/new-ai-business-model-charging-customers-only-when-the-tech-works), more and more companies are switching to so-called outcome-based pricing (OBP). Under this model, clients are no longer billed for software or hours worked, but only for the successful completion of an assignment.

That shift does, however, bring significant risks. Companies moving to OBP often first experience a dip in revenue and profit because they take on the operational risk themselves: invoicing only happens once the result has actually been achieved. At the same time, the model acts as a driver of innovation, because every efficiency improvement translates directly into higher margins rather than a lower invoice. This forces service providers into a fundamental choice: stick with the shrinking hourly billing model, or move to a value-driven model in order to remain relevant.

## The canary in the coal mine: marketing and communications

The marketing and communications sector is acting as a preview of what lies ahead. Global advertising spend, measured as a percentage of advertisers' revenue, has fallen from 11% to 7% over the past two years. According to Victor Knaap, co-founder of digital marketing agency Monks, AI is the main reason. He describes the sector as "the canary in the coal mine" and sees clients viewing the traditional way of working as outdated because AI can now automate text, images and video almost completely. The speed with which AI is accelerating processes is forcing the entire sector to change course. This is also reflected in stock market valuations. Large advertising holdings such as British company WPP have seen their market value come under heavy pressure over the past year. The market is anticipating a structural shift: many of these firms are reporting declining profits while being forced to rethink their business model. Knaap observes that technologically progressive clients no longer want to pay for the traditional hourly billing model: "Revenue is falling, because the disruption is moving faster than the new revenue you generate."

## The factors holding it back

The shift towards billing based on outcomes is proving difficult in practice. The biggest bottleneck lies with large clients: legal and procurement departments act as gatekeepers and still work with contracts based on hours and pre-agreed results. That slows the implementation of new pricing models and delays the broader AI transition. According to Knaap, the change is moving faster and going deeper than many expected. He warns that everyone with an office job, especially people paid by the hour, will face challenges. Knaap notes that companies in Silicon Valley no longer hire people if an AI solution is expected to be available within two years. It may seem less visible in the Netherlands, but jobs are disappearing there too because of AI, first among freelancers and agency workers. For permanent employees the threshold is higher because of severance packages and reorganisation costs, which slows the transition but does not stop it.

Knaap is nevertheless optimistic about the future. He expects a great deal of new business to emerge in which organisations no longer need large numbers of people, but rather "senior-level sparring partners". In his view, AI becomes the professional's co-pilot. That shift is already visible in the way organisations are reshaping their teams. Large consultancies are responding by redefining the role of juniors. Where entry-level consultants traditionally produced analyses, reports and presentations themselves, companies now have them mainly checking and refining AI-generated work. The traditional pyramid structure, with many junior consultants at the base, is shifting towards a smaller, more differentiated team model with relatively more senior experts and AI support. This transformation requires a fundamentally different way of working. Work is moving away from routine cognitive tasks towards skills that distinguish humans from AI: critically assessing AI output, judging reliability, making ethical trade-offs, collaborating and adapting to new circumstances. Professionals are focusing on what AI still cannot do: taking strategic or physical end responsibility and maintaining human relationships.

Knaap predicts that the professional who can direct AI will replace the professional who cannot. But that transition will succeed only if organisations also adapt their structure and culture. For organisations, successful transformation starts at the top. Knaap stresses that acceptance within the leadership team is crucial: "The manager must be absolutely convinced that the work will be done wholly or partly by AI. This is the greatest technological revolution of our time, but we are being held back by human behaviour."

## Opportunities for the Netherlands

Although the Netherlands remains highly dependent on American Big Tech for the largest and most advanced AI models, there is still plenty of room for innovation. Europe may not be able to compete on the same scale as the parties developing these foundation models, but it does offer real opportunities for companies focused on specific sectors and applications. The fact that the US also has successful companies with this kind of sector-specific solution does not mean European or Dutch players cannot build clear specialisations of their own. According to Knaap, that is precisely where the big opportunity lies: "There are incredible opportunities for the Netherlands here. We have a highly educated workforce, strong infrastructure and, with the recent Wennink report underlining the urgent need for a national approach to technical and digital talent, a clear route to developing new opportunities and new business."

# Outdoor Advertising Flourishes in the Digital Age

Digital outdoor advertising is making a remarkable advance. New screens are appearing in shopping centres, bus shelters and railway stations. Analogue outdoor advertising also remains popular because of its mass reach. ABN AMRO expects outdoor advertising revenue to grow by 5 per cent in total in 2025 and 6 per cent in 2026. Up to 2030, outdoor advertising revenue is expected to grow by an average of 5 per cent a year and overtake radio as an advertising channel.

The Netherlands has 83,000 outdoor advertising assets, including bus shelters, motorway masts, transformer boxes, scaffold wraps, blank walls, billboards, information pillars and lampposts. Even buses, trams and ferries are used as advertising platforms. The number of assets is growing with the addition of new digital screens. At the same time, revenue per asset is rising through the digitalisation of existing assets.

The most common format is the abri format (1185 mm x 1750 mm, around 2m2), accounting for 65 per cent of all advertising displays and found in bus shelters and information pillars. The A0 format (841 mm x 1189 mm) accounts for 32 per cent and is found on triangular boards and electricity boxes. Larger assets are rare and vary in size; only 1,300 are larger than the abri format. These are located in high-reach locations, such as the LED facade of the Ziggodome in Amsterdam (23 metres high and 400 metres long) or the NEO screen at Hoog Catharijne in Utrecht (193 m2).

## Digital outdoor advertising is growing explosively and offers new opportunities

In three years, digital outdoor advertising revenue has more than doubled to EUR172 million in 2024, according to the Digital Ad Spend 2024 report by VIA/Deloitte. In 2025, revenue will grow by 4 per cent to around EUR179 million, ABN AMRO expects. That is 56 per cent of total outdoor advertising revenue in 2025, which is expected to reach EUR317 million. This is striking because only 17 per cent of all advertising assets are digital; the remaining 83 per cent are analogue. Digital screens generate more revenue than analogue ones because they are larger on average, located in the busiest places and attract more attention.

Digital screens attract more attention thanks to moving images and rotating adverts. They are also larger and positioned in busy places in major cities and along heavily travelled roads, where large numbers of passers-by create strong reach. Advertising assets in these locations were the first to be digitised in order to recoup the high investment costs. Analogue assets are digitised when their reach is large enough to justify the expense. The new BRO Next study is becoming the new standard for measuring reach.

Most of the new digital outdoor advertising screens are located indoors, in shop windows. As long as the screens are visible from the street or public space, they count towards outdoor advertising inventory. This phenomenon is part of the [rise of retail media](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/technologie/advertenties-lucratieve-bijverdienste-voor-winkelketens.html), in which supermarket chains and other retailers are turning to advertising as a lucrative side business. HEMA, for example, has nearly 330 digital screens running a range of campaigns that generate income in the evenings when the shops are closed. Global, a major outdoor advertising operator, handles ad sales for HEMA.

For advertisers, digital screens offer greater flexibility. Campaigns can be tailored to circumstances, such as adverts for ice cream on warm days. They also allow advertisers to reach specific target groups more effectively, such as young people seeing an advert for matcha tea at a railway station on their way to school. Around 30 per cent of digital advertising space is placed automatically using algorithms that take factors such as time, location and weather conditions into account. That percentage is increasing, pointing to a more efficient and more flexible use of digital advertising space.

## Digital advertising dominates the media landscape

Digital advertising, especially via web pages, social media, streaming services and the digital screens mentioned above, dominates the Dutch media landscape and accounted for EUR4.1 billion in 2024, nearly three quarters of all advertising spend. Large tech companies, with a market share of 80 per cent (EUR3.5 billion), dominate the digital advertising market. The media's digital transformation is not yet complete, and the share of digital advertising is therefore still rising.

While traditional mass media such as television, radio and newspapers are losing reach, outdoor advertising, also known as out of home, is becoming more important. Johan Smit, director of PMA, the trade association for media agencies, explains: "Out of home is effectively the only mass medium left and is therefore taking over the leading role from linear TV."

Digital outdoor advertising differs from other digital media, such as online adverts, because it is generally not experienced as intrusive. Online, ad blockers can be used and some people experience advertising fatigue. "Outdoor advertising is not intrusive on the one hand and impossible to miss on the other," says Sita Bakker, director of OUTREACH, the trade association for outdoor advertising operators. "It does not interrupt people during their activities, which contributes to a positive experience," Bakker adds.

## Analogue outdoor advertising also remains strong in the digital age

Analogue outdoor advertising remains relevant and is even growing, with revenue expected to rise by 6 per cent in 2025. That would bring analogue outdoor advertising revenue to around EUR138 million, according to ABN AMRO. Although digitalisation is slightly reducing the number of analogue assets, traditional formats such as paper posters and static billboards remain popular with advertisers because of their mass reach. New measurement methods from the BRO Next study are also playing a role in this revaluation, allowing advertisers to estimate their reach ever more accurately. As the "only mass medium left", outdoor advertising offers an alternative to advertising channels such as television, which are seen as more expensive.

Outdoor advertising remains untouched by the online competition affecting other media. When people are on the move, for instance travelling to school or work, they come into contact with outdoor advertising. Bus shelters, motorway masts, billboards and A0 frames reach 80 per cent of the Dutch population every week. By comparison, linear television reaches 80 per cent of the Dutch population in a month.

Around 5 per cent of all advertising spend goes to outdoor advertising. That share varies by advertiser: McDonald's allocates as much as 20 per cent of its budget to outdoor advertising, while WPP Media, the largest media agency, spends around 10 per cent on outdoor advertising on behalf of advertisers. These examples point to further growth potential for outdoor advertising, as experience in other countries also shows. In a positive scenario, where current trends continue strongly, outdoor advertising's share could rise to 7.5 per cent and revenue could reach EUR600 million by 2030. In the base scenario, however, outdoor advertising's share remains stable at around the current 5 per cent.

## Municipalities shape outdoor advertising policy

Outdoor advertising targets passers-by who are walking or cycling, driving or travelling by public transport. Municipalities apply strict rules on where advertising is allowed and how it may appear, meaning the permitted formats and, for example, the brightness of digital screens. Some municipalities levy a local advertising tax. Because municipalities are themselves responsible for the design of public space, the rules vary widely: the Netherlands has 342 municipalities, each with its own outdoor advertising policy. The municipality of The Hague, for instance, has banned "fossil advertising". Through a local by-law (APV), The Hague prohibits advertising for air travel, petrol cars and fossil energy contracts. In Haarlem, a tender is under way in which advertising for meat and fish is not allowed.

Through public tenders, municipalities award multi-year concessions for outdoor advertising. In return, they receive a financial payment, often combined with in-kind compensation such as street furniture or media space for their own campaigns and charitable causes. JCDecaux is one example of an operator that invests in bus shelters and maintains them in exchange for advertising rights. Notably, JCDecaux has more employees working in technical services than in advertising sales. A [PwC study](https://www.outsmart.org.uk/resources/ooh-supporting-uk-society) in the United Kingdom shows that 46 per cent of outdoor advertising revenue flows back to municipalities. Applied to the Dutch market, that amounts to around EUR146 million in 2025.

Outdoor advertising is heavily dependent on municipal policy and concessions, which vary widely across the Netherlands' 342 municipalities. While some municipalities impose specific restrictions, such as The Hague's ban on fossil advertising, there is no national guideline. This leads to variation in rules and uncertainty for operators. Central rules on tobacco, alcohol and gambling advertising show that a uniform approach can bring advantages. Without such guidelines, individual municipalities can impose stricter restrictions, creating a risk for the outdoor advertising sector.

The Dutch outdoor advertising market, with 52 operators, is relatively fragmented compared with neighbouring countries such as Belgium and Germany, where only two large players are active alongside a few specialists in their own niche. Bauer Media Group's acquisition of the Dutch arm of Clear Channel in April 2025 is an example of the consolidation that is expected to continue. Internationally, the Netherlands still trails countries such as the United States and China, especially in digital outdoor advertising and retail media. Based on international trends and the current market structure, ABN AMRO expects further consolidation and digital growth.

## The rise of the abri

In 1964, Jean Claude Decaux introduced his first bus shelters in Lyon. In exchange for financing, placing and maintaining them, his company, JCDecaux, received the advertising rights for these "abribus" shelters (French for bus shelter). This public-private partnership model spread worldwide, and the term "abri" became synonymous with a widely used advertising format: 1185 mm x 1750 mm, around 2m2. Advertising in the abri format represents 65 per cent of all outdoor advertising in the Netherlands and can be found in bus shelters across the country, stations, shopping centres and along busy streets.

# The Two Faces of Agentic AI: Weapon and Shield

Criminals are increasingly using artificial intelligence to launch lightning-fast attacks. At the same time, companies are deploying the same technology to defend themselves. The urgency to act is growing, but in this complex arms race many organisations do not know where to begin.

The world of cybersecurity is at a turning point. Agentic AI, artificial intelligence in which systems operate autonomously and make decisions without human intervention, is fundamentally changing how both defenders and attackers work. Unlike generative AI, which depends on predefined instructions, these "agents" can independently initiate, carry out and improve actions based on what they learn.

Recent research by Palo Alto Networks, an American cybersecurity company, shows just how profound this shift is. Researchers succeeded in simulating a full ransomware attack using Agentic AI in just 25 minutes, a hundred times faster than traditional methods. "The scenario they describe is disturbingly realistic," says Matthijs Mons, cybersecurity specialist at ABN AMRO. "Criminals will develop agents specifically tailored to targeted attacks."

## From science fiction to reality

The process described by Palo Alto Networks illustrates the power of Agentic AI. Agents begin by collecting public information about their targets via social media and company databases. Based on current data, they continuously adapt their strategy and attempt to gain access to networks through personalised phishing attacks, LinkedIn messages or fake invitations to video conferences. Once inside, they analyse patterns to identify the most valuable systems and choose attack techniques that match their target's vulnerabilities perfectly. "To many people, this sounds fictional, as if everything happens by itself," says Mons. "But with traditional AI there still had to be at least an initial action by a human. With Agentic AI, things now happen fully autonomously, without anyone being involved. And that applies to both sides."

The other side is the protective measures that organisations are implementing. Large financial institutions employ hundreds of cybersecurity specialists to protect vital infrastructure. Staff shortages in this sector are severe, and Agentic AI offers solutions here too. "When an incident happens, a security expert often has to pull information from several different systems," Mons explains. "That can take hours. An agent for security tasks can independently investigate data from system reports on the basis of a single instruction and build a complete picture, whereas an analyst has to work through an entire step-by-step process."

> "With Agentic AI, things now happen fully autonomously, without anyone being involved."

Matthijs Mons

ABN AMRO

## Simulating an attack

The most promising applications can be found in simulating attacks on an organisation's own environment, so-called red-team actions. Traditionally, security teams spend days or even weeks on this: mapping systems, finding access routes and testing different paths.

By learning from earlier simulated attacks, Agentic AI can infiltrate far more quickly and determine which attack sequence is most effective, exactly what criminals will do as well. There are already companies offering this as a service. They run simulations for their clients and learn from both attacking red teams and defending blue teams. This combined approach, also called purple AI, allows organisations to learn from both perspectives and continuously improve their security. It is important to note that Agentic AI builds on existing methods. It carries out attacks faster than ever, but it does not invent entirely new ones. So far, Agentic AI is above all a leap in efficiency. "The big cybersecurity companies are investing heavily in AI," says Mons. "But criminals are doing the same. The romantic image of hackers as lonely boys in basements is completely outdated. Cyberattacks are carried out by professional organisations with specialised roles focused on gaining access, building malware, installing malware, stealing data, negotiating and so on. They even have HR departments and call centres, supported by a solid business case built on the proceeds they generate from stolen data."

## The paradox of adoption

The use of Agentic AI inside organisations, both for security and for ordinary business processes, also creates new vulnerabilities. Hackers can, for example, try to manipulate AI agents that already have access to systems.

This leads to a striking paradox, revealed by a survey from SailPoint, an American technology company focused on identity and access management, among 353 cybersecurity professionals worldwide. Although 96 per cent of respondents regard agents as a growing security threat, no fewer than 98 per cent of organisations plan to expand their use of Agentic AI in the coming year. The survey shows that 82 per cent of companies are already using agents, with more than half reporting that these agents have daily access to sensitive data. Even so, only 44 per cent have introduced policies to protect these agents from external threats. Perhaps most alarming of all, nearly one in four companies reported that agents can be manipulated into disclosing access credentials.

"Using this technology is already a risk in itself," warn Hans van Haarlem and Steven Ottenhoff of MMOX, a Dutch cybersecurity consultancy. They point to a crucial issue that many entrepreneurs overlook. "In the past, attacking large companies took enormous amounts of time and resources. That threshold has now dropped dramatically. Criminals can work far more efficiently, which also makes medium-sized and smaller organisations attractive targets."

> "Run regular digital fire drills, so everyone is prepared and knows their role in a crisis."

Hans van Haarlem

MMOX

## Volume and quality are increasing

The lower barrier to attack is already visible in practice, as attackers widen their field of operations. "We receive piles of phishing emails to analyse," Van Haarlem says. "What stands out is that both the volume and the quality are increasing. Spearphishing, targeted attacks on specific individuals, is being carried out much better. We regularly see new employees receiving text messages almost immediately from 'our' CEO asking them to transfer money or get in touch. On their very first day at work. It is hard to imagine that this is not being automated in the background."

The number of cyberattacks has not yet risen explosively, and there is a reason for that. "Agentic AI systems are still incredibly expensive," explains ABN AMRO's cybersecurity specialist. "They require a lot of computing power and training models costs money. Criminals therefore continue to focus on relatively easy targets. Hacking a large bank can take years, but a small accountancy firm can be cracked within a week with a substantial payoff. Generative AI has caused the number of phishing emails to rise sharply. That is cheap and easy. Full Agentic AI attacks, where systems independently scan social media and conduct spearphishing, are certainly coming, but they are not yet widespread."

## Internal security risks

Besides external threats, agents used for security and for other business processes also bring specific internal security risks into organisations. "If such an agent is configured badly or not monitored properly, situations can arise that you really do not want," warns Mons. "Imagine an agent detects that you have clicked on a phishing email. The normal procedure is to isolate that computer from the network so it cannot cause damage. But if that agent accidentally isolates every computer in your network instead of just one, nobody can work any more."

There is an even more fundamental problem. Agentic AI can act on the basis of data and past experience without human control, but it can also hallucinate: draw incorrect conclusions or perform unexpected actions. "You do not then know exactly what it has done," Mons explains. "It learns by itself and acts by itself. If things go wrong, you have to work out what happened and what needs to be repaired. That is why it is crucial to build in strong control mechanisms and keep options open that allow people to intervene themselves."

The experts at MMOX add nuance to this picture. "Agentic AI does not suffer from stress or workload that makes someone careless. It can do the wrong thing with good intentions because the underlying model is inadequate, but the margin of error is often smaller than with people."

## What can companies do?

The practical course of action for organisations is clear, but wide-ranging. "Make sure you are better secured than your neighbour," says Mons. "On a street, the house with the weakest security gets burgled. The same basic principle applies in the digital world. It is not about absolute security, but about relative resilience." Concrete measures start with the basics: properly securing devices and using two-factor authentication. But even that is not watertight. "There are scenarios in which that second layer of control can still be bypassed. It is a constant cat-and-mouse game between security makers and malicious actors."

Van Haarlem therefore argues for a strategic approach that goes beyond technology alone. "AI changes nothing about the methodology of cybercriminals; it only changes the efficiency and therefore the scope," he says. "We are advocates of the NIST framework, a widely recognised set of guidelines that helps organisations manage and reduce cybersecurity risk. Invest in people as well as technology, in network segmentation as well as strong data analysis." Measures that mitigate impact are a crucial but underestimated element. "Instead of focusing only on keeping hackers out, you should ensure they cannot do damage once they are inside," Van Haarlem and Ottenhoff emphasise. "Segment your network so an attacker cannot move freely through all your systems. Try to build a high wall, but realise that if someone climbs over it, there still need to be more barriers on the inside."

> "The threshold for cyberattacks is lower than ever. Companies that do not act now face major risks."

Steven Ottenhoff

MMOX

## The human factor

The role of employees remains complex. While some see people as the weakest link, Mons at ABN AMRO takes a different view. "We see people as the last resort, the strongest link. Technology may have failed, but an alert employee can still stop an attack. That is why we promote safe behaviour across the entire organisation." Van Haarlem and Ottenhoff go deeper into this point. "Changing human behaviour is extremely difficult, especially when the digital skills of the current population lag behind and employees are not intrinsically motivated to work safely."

The experts at MMOX distinguish three essential factors. First, organisations need to establish clear guidelines for safe behaviour and provide user-friendly security tools. They warn: "Many tools die a beautiful death because adoption falls behind." Second, the urgency of cyber risk must be reinforced continuously. "This is not something for a single training session; it has to be institutionalised. Discuss it monthly or quarterly in meetings, repetition is essential." Third, organisations need to understand the working environment. "Map the systems you have. Listen carefully to your employees. If they download software or extensions themselves because something is missing from the official systems, you need to take that seriously. By understanding their needs and meeting them, you prevent them from seeking unsafe solutions. Make the topic discussable."

## Resilience instead of perfection

The central message from all experts is clear: perfect security does not exist. "The question is not if you will be hacked, but when," say the experts at MMOX. "The cost of attacks has become so low that every company is a potential target. Our advice to clients is therefore: be prepared. Build resilience." In concrete terms, that means making sure you have backups that actually work, developing an incident-response system, a structured step-by-step plan for responding quickly and effectively to a cyber incident, and practising scenarios regularly. "Simulate that you have been hacked," they advise. "What are you going to do? How will you communicate? What if your data ends up on the street? Run regular digital fire drills so everyone is prepared and knows their role in a crisis."

For small and medium-sized enterprises, the challenges are different from those faced by large organisations. "The blue-team agents and advanced systems available today require so much investment that they are not relevant for most entrepreneurs." Entrepreneurs often do not know where to begin. "That is why we focus on helping to lay a solid foundation: getting the basic measures right so you are better secured than your neighbour."

## Opportunities and threats

Despite all the risks, Agentic AI also offers substantial opportunities. ABN AMRO cybersecurity specialist Mons underlines this point. "Red-team actions that used to take weeks can now be done in days. Analyses that took hours now take minutes. With Agentic AI, we can test ourselves better, find vulnerabilities faster and respond more effectively to incidents," he says. But both parties also stress the need for caution. Ottenhoff adds: "Agentic AI is not necessarily transparent. Agentic systems can do the wrong thing with good intentions. Sometimes a simple, predictable system is a better choice than complex AI."

## The urgency is growing

Cyberattacks will become faster, larger in scale and more invisible in the future. That requires security systems that adapt continuously. Old systems that rely on fixed checks and slow updates are no longer sufficient. A new approach is needed, one that continuously monitors for vulnerabilities in the cloud and other digital systems, and where simulating realistic threats helps identify and remedy weak spots. Within organisations, all Agentic AI systems, whether used for security or for other business processes, should not be treated as ordinary system components but as separate entities with their own security needs. This requires a strategy that identifies, monitors and controls all agents. In this way, companies can not only comply with regulation but also strengthen their overall security position.

At the same time, agents deployed by malicious actors represent a growing external threat. The rise of Agentic AI does not introduce completely new attack methods, but it does make existing techniques faster and more effective. The speed and adaptability of these systems pose a major challenge. Companies therefore need to innovate proactively and anticipate agent-driven attacks.

"It is very difficult to move cybersecurity from 'important but not urgent' to 'urgent'," Ottenhoff observes. "But with the rise of Agentic AI, that urgency is now real. The industry behind cybercrime has matured, with solid business cases. The barrier to attack is lower than ever. Companies that do not act now face major risks."

The two faces of Agentic AI, as both weapon and shield, force organisations to rethink their approach. The technology offers unprecedented opportunities for efficient security and for scaling cybersecurity teams. But that same power is also in the hands of criminals who are operating ever more professionally. The challenge is clear: companies must invest now in foundational security, involve and prepare their employees, and recognise that absolute security does not exist. Resilience and preparedness are the new watchwords in an era in which the question is not if, but when, you will be attacked.

# Digital Sovereignty Offers Major Opportunities for Europe

Will Europe be crushed between the US and China when it comes to technology? Will we lose both our digital autonomy and our role in the tech economy? Far from it, predicts ABN AMRO sector specialist Julia Krauwer. In a Europe that is waking up digitally, she sees abundant opportunity. "A lot of great things are already happening, but the internal barriers have to go. And we need to speak our ambitions out loud."

When it came to technology, Europe long walked hand in hand with big brother America. Software, servers, cloud storage: all our data and systems felt familiar, but they were completely dependent. At first that was fine, until geopolitical relations were turned upside down and the rise of AI began to shake virtually every layer of our business community and society. "The rapid breakthrough of AI is transforming business models. It affects every company and certainly those in tech, media and telecom," says Julia Krauwer, ABN AMRO's sector specialist in this area. "The question is not whether you will change, but whether, as an entrepreneur, you can keep up with the pace."

## Dependence creates vulnerability

The European tech sector is posting strong growth figures, but it also faces major uncertainties. Behind digital progress lies an enormous dependence that remains largely invisible to end users. Virtually all the servers, chips and software on which companies, sectors and even societies run come from the US or Asia. "If that supply stops, our entire digital economy is affected. The war in Ukraine, recent developments involving Nexperia and the continuing tensions between China and Taiwan have accelerated that awareness."

That dependence is reflected in Synergy Research's figures. Between 2017 and 2022, the European cloud market grew fivefold, yet the combined market share of European providers still fell from 27 to just 13 per cent. The reason: Amazon, Microsoft and Google grew much faster. They too have invested billions in data centres on European soil in recent years. "So while we are growing ourselves, the outside world is growing much faster still," Krauwer says, summing up the urgency.

> "The future belongs to companies that provide technology that is hard to reproduce and that can be integrated intelligently into other people's ecosystems."

Julia Krauwer

Sector Banker Technology, Media & Telecom

## AI as a catalyst and a threat

According to Krauwer, artificial intelligence is both the most important driving force and the greatest disruptor of the moment. "AI changes everything, from the way we develop software to the way we advertise and sell products."

She describes a two-sided movement: on the one hand, AI is driving an explosion in productivity. IT service providers are helping clients with automation, forecasting and bespoke solutions, opening up new markets. On the other hand, artificial intelligence threatens to undermine parts of the software industry itself. "Software companies are in a strange bind: AI can make your product smarter, but it can also make it redundant. The winners have a strong core, unique data or deep domain knowledge." Companies whose value rests on unique algorithms or datasets are better protected against generative AI that can easily copy standard features.

According to Krauwer, a new distinction is emerging in which scale is no longer the only factor; what matters above all is whether you offer a very specific and ingenious solution. "Small tech companies can scale very quickly too; just look at Sweden's Lovable. But anyone who only delivers a nice interface runs the risk of an AI agent taking that over tomorrow. The future belongs to companies that provide technology that is hard to reproduce and that can be integrated intelligently into other people's ecosystems."

## From risk to opportunity

Where dependence on foreign players used to be seen mainly as a risk, Krauwer now also sees an opportunity. "Because of the changing relationship between America and Europe, we in Europe have become acutely aware of the importance of cloud sovereignty. As a European company, you now want more than ever to know where your data sits, who can access it and who controls the master switch. Companies that can demonstrably show they handle privacy and transparency, data sovereignty and therefore the continuity of their services with care have an important USP in their hands: your data is stored safely in Europe, under European law and regulation, and is therefore less dependent on geopolitical unrest."

## European Google Maps alternative charts its own course

The Amsterdam-based company Magic Lane shows that digital sovereignty and commercial success can go hand in hand with its white-label Location Intelligence Technology: technology that allows other platforms to integrate navigation or shared-mobility solutions easily, from electric bicycles to shared cars, and with smart features such as offline navigation and live traffic data. CEO Raymond Alves says: "And the technology safeguards privacy: unlike American providers, users are not tracked; all data remains anonymous." The figures also show that this approach is paying off: in the spring, the number of users of the consumer app rose by 250% in six weeks. "This shows that privacy and performance do not have to be mutually exclusive."

> "We are excellent at R&D in the Netherlands and in Europe, but we still find it daunting to say out loud that we are going to conquer the world."

Julia Krauwer

Sector Banker Technology, Media & Telecom

## Culture and capital

There is no shortage of promising initiatives, but Europe is still a long way from where it wants to be. Krauwer says: "We are excellent at R&D in the Netherlands and in Europe, but we still find it daunting to say that we are going to conquer the world." That modesty does not work in our favour, especially when it comes to raising sufficient capital, which is essential if good ideas are to flourish. Tech companies in Europe find it far harder to secure large investments than their counterparts in America. According to the State of European Tech 2024 report, the cumulative gap has now risen to USD375 billion. "In the US, start-ups raise more than USD15 million twice as often," says Krauwer. "That is a huge difference. And in the end, capital determines whether you can scale." Fortunately, Krauwer also sees the first signs of a broader opening there, albeit cautiously. "Investors are seeking one another out more often," she notes. "Brussels is also working on a so-called 28th regime to simplify investment and scaling across borders. With this, Europe is developing a single European framework for company law, insolvency law, employment law and tax law, to put an end to the current fragmentation of 27 countries each with their own rules."

## Cyber resilience as a test of maturity

Alongside economic uncertainty, the security risk is increasing. Tech companies are attractive targets for hackers and espionage groups, especially now that digital infrastructure has become part of geopolitical power. "As a tech company, you have to assume that you will be hacked at some point," says Krauwer. "The question is how you deal with it." In her view, cyber resilience is part of strategic maturity. "It is not only about measures to keep attackers out of your systems, but also about communication and recovery capacity if you are hacked despite all precautions. Being resilient means being able to get back on your feet quickly. Businesses that embed safety and transparency in their brand win trust from both customers and investors."

## Fronts of growth

Krauwer sees European tech companies able to grow on several fronts, provided they dare to act boldly. "We should not only want to be less dependent on Big Tech; above all, we should want to become stronger ourselves." The first growth layer lies in AI integration: companies that weave artificial intelligence intelligently into their products or processes. "You see software companies integrating AI cleverly into their products themselves, and in doing so developing specialist, and preferably highly intelligent, domain-specific applications that are ideally highly scalable but not easy for others to imitate."

The second growth layer is technical infrastructure, from chips to data centres. Krauwer says: "Demand for computing power is growing faster than the supply of data centres, while energy and grid capacity also impose limits. That pressure has already led to some great innovations. Take Axelera AI, a Dutch start-up in chips, which develops powerful and energy-efficient AI processing units. Or Asperitas, which has developed a cooling system for data centres in which servers are immersed in cooling liquid, making them more compact, more energy-efficient and more sustainable."

## Building blocks for developers

Thanks to Magic Lane's modular architecture, developers can now build solutions even faster with the support of AI. "We do not deliver one app, but separate building blocks that developers can use. In addition, our software development kit can even be called by AI development tools. We deliberately position ourselves at the back end of an application," Alves says. "That is where the strength lies: technology that is hard to replicate, built on European values. In this way we show that this kind of technology, like Signal and Protonmail, which offer alternatives to messenger and email applications respectively, is not idealism but a realistic business model."

> "We offer technology that is hard to replicate, built on European values."

Raymond Alves

CEO Magic Lane

The third growth layer lies in the focus on European values. Providers of innovative products that are not only smart but also privacy-friendly, respect copyrights, are energy-efficient, run on local servers and operate without bias, win trust within and beyond Europe. Krauwer cites Dutch company Ab Ovo as an example. "They have integrated AI into both the development process and their actual software, while also focusing on building software that works more flexibly and more sustainably. That is exactly the step more companies need to take."

## Talent and ecosystems

Ultimately, the success of the sector stands or falls with people. "Talent is drawn to places where ambition is tangible," says Krauwer. "Universities, start-ups, investors: you need concentration. We need to create more breeding grounds where science and business meet. Only then can you retain the talent that is currently leaving for abroad." She points, among other things, to TU Delft and TU Eindhoven, which are already helping dozens of spin-offs to scale. "Those are encouraging signals. But it all has to be faster, more ambitious and more international, with more boldness and courage. In Silicon Valley, failure is mainly seen as a learning moment; here it still seems more like a disgrace. If you dare to state your big ambitions out loud, you are more likely to achieve them."

> "In Silicon Valley, failure is mainly seen as a learning moment; in the Netherlands it still seems more like a disgrace."

Julia Krauwer

Sector Banker Technology, Media & Telecom

## More equal footing

All things considered, Krauwer expects the European Big Tech and AI ecosystems to grow strongly in the years ahead. Not with the primary aim of becoming completely detached from American tech, but of becoming more equal. "We are building a plan B by strengthening our own alternatives. Anyone who dares to invest in that independence is helping to build the next generation of European tech. At the same time, you should not assume that what worked five years ago is still good enough for the next five. The entrepreneurs who understand that are tomorrow's winners. Anyone who dares to link their own values to innovation and scale can become a global player even now."

# Europe's Software Market in Search of Scale and Niches

The European technology sector is struggling to scale up potential winners because of a lack of growth capital. Even so, the continent offers a broad palette of innovative players. In the Netherlands, for example, nearly two thirds of investment in young software companies goes to businesses that apply artificial intelligence.

In 2024, young European technology companies raised a combined total of around USD54 billion in venture capital. Almost half of that, more than USD23 billion, flowed into software-as-a-service (SaaS). That makes SaaS the biggest category, larger than digital marketplaces or companies developing physical technology. For investors, the combination of stable subscriptions and scalability is especially attractive.

During the coronavirus pandemic, capital flowed abundantly into technology, helped by low interest rates and investors' search for scalable business models. SaaS benefited more than most. From 2022 onwards, however, inflation took hold and central banks raised interest rates, making capital more expensive and forcing valuations lower. That made investors more cautious, especially around mega-deals and loss-making growth companies. Venture-capital volumes now seem to be stabilising. SaaS therefore remains the centre of gravity of European tech financing, although within this category capital is increasingly flowing to companies with AI woven into their core.

## The scalability gap

Within Europe, the United Kingdom attracts the most SaaS investment, with more than USD77 billion over ten years, more than a third of the European total. France and Germany follow with USD35 billion (16 per cent) and USD33 billion (15 per cent) respectively. The Netherlands is firmly in fourth place with USD9.9 billion, representing 4.5 per cent of all European SaaS investment between 2015 and 2025.

Unfortunately, Europe suffers from a stubborn problem: the lack of capital needed for tech companies to grow into internationally competitive businesses. According to the 2024 [State of European Tech report](https://www.stateofeuropeantech.com/chapters/investment-levels), American start-ups are twice as likely to raise rounds of more than USD15 million as their European counterparts. As a result, the share of young companies that manage to progress to the scale-up phase is much smaller in Europe. According to the researchers, the cumulative shortfall now amounts to USD375 billion, the total amount young tech companies would have raised over the past ten years if access to larger investments had been as easy as in the United States (US). In the US, a large share of the money even goes into rounds above USD100 million, with mega-deals above USD250 million playing a dominant role. Although Europe actually closes not that many fewer SaaS deals, 4,616 last year versus 5,040 in the US, the centre of gravity remains with smaller rounds.

## Broadly applicable SaaS niches

In Europe, venture capital flows mainly into several recognisable SaaS niches. The largest category is enterprise software, solutions that make organisations' internal processes more efficient, from HR and planning systems to workflow and ERP tools. German unicorn Personio, for example, has continued to strengthen its position in HR software, while its compatriot N8N specialises in process automation.

There are also other solutions that can be applied in virtually every sector. Marketing and communications software remains popular, with marketing platform Brevo as one example. Skeepers helps brands weave customer reviews and other user-generated content cleverly into campaigns. Cybersecurity is also benefiting from broad applicability, combined with a general sense of urgency as cyberattacks become increasingly sophisticated. French company Sekoia.io, for example, raised growth capital in 2024 for its AI platform that detects threats in real time.

## SaaS for specific sectors

SaaS companies also attract a great deal of financing in the FinTech corner. British company Volt.io, for example, offers a platform that allows businesses to accept payments from international customers as well, while Qover helps integrate insurance into service offerings across a range of sectors, from mobility to e-commerce. Set against these are players operating more specifically at the heart of financial service providers, such as Backbase, which offers a client platform for financial institutions as a replacement for their old IT systems.

It is an example of one of many vertical SaaS services designed for a specific sector. In healthcare, Doctolib is entering the wider European market with a platform that makes appointment management, consultations and patient communication easier, while Unmind has raised growth funding for a platform that helps employees improve their mental health. In hospitality, Dutch company Mews raised new capital to roll out its platform internationally, while in construction and property PlanRadar did the same earlier with its project-management software.

## AI SaaS as a new engine

Within the broader landscape, AI-driven SaaS is taking up an ever larger place. In Europe, a third of software deals now fall into this category, accounting for almost half of investment volume. In the Netherlands, it is even two thirds. In the US the trend is moving even faster; there, AI SaaS absorbs nearly 80 per cent of all SaaS funding.

Even so, Europe is producing a great many innovative AI-first SaaS companies specialising in all kinds of niches. The Netherlands even has an above-average number of AI SaaS deals and funding rounds. Cradle, for instance, developed an AI model that can design new proteins and sells it to laboratories as a SaaS service. Fellow Dutch company Fourthline focuses on identity fraud and compliance in the financial sector using AI, while FRISS does the same for insurance risk. TestGorilla makes selection processes smarter and less prone to bias, and Sympower uses AI to keep electricity grids in balance.

German company Accure offers a platform for AI analysis of battery data in sectors including mobility and industry, in order to improve the safety and lifespan of batteries. British company Synthesia automates the production of corporate films and training videos with generative AI and raised another USD180 million at the start of 2025 at a valuation of more than USD2 billion. And from Sweden, Lovable is growing rapidly with a platform that uses AI to speed up and democratise software development. The company was founded in 2023 and reached a valuation of almost USD2 billion within just a few years.

## Major AI developers are setting the tone

A large share of investment is concentrated among a handful of major AI developers, whose mega-deals are setting the tone in the market. Their AI systems are offered in two ways: as building blocks for software developers to embed AI in their own products, and as chat apps such as ChatGPT (OpenAI) and Claude (Anthropic) for end users. Anyone wanting access to the most powerful features pays a monthly subscription. In essence, that revenue model is identical to SaaS: software delivered through the cloud for a recurring fee.

In the US, these mega-deals have brought in enormous injections of capital. OpenAI raised USD40 billion in March this year at a valuation of USD300 billion, followed by an additional round in the summer. Anthropic raised another USD13 billion in September 2025, and xAI closed a USD6 billion round at the end of 2024. Europe has fewer outliers of this kind, but the AI SaaS segment is growing quickly here too. French company Mistral AI raised EUR1.7 billion in a 2025 round, EUR1.3 billion of which came from ASML, doubling its valuation to nearly EUR12 billion. German company Aleph Alpha received half a billion in earlier rounds.

## Success factors

Amid the growth surge of these big players, SaaS companies need to position themselves sharply. One important feature of promising providers is that they focus on critical business processes, parts of an organisation that are simply essential. Fourthline, for instance, automates mandatory client identification in the financial sector. Banks must carry out this process to comply with the law, and switching to another provider entails risk. FRISS does the same for insurers, who place great value on recognising fraudulent claims. Doctolib supports doctors and patients with appointments and communications, an essential process that is often still inadequately digitised.

There are also opportunities in niches that large but generic AI models cannot handle. Cradle, which focuses entirely on generative AI for protein design, is trained on an enormous database of protein structures and properties and combines deep biological knowledge with technical capabilities. Sympower has developed an AI solution that balances electricity grids and connects both large consumers and energy producers. Its work with parties in energy-intensive industries such as agriculture, cement production and data centres requires strong relationships and a great deal of domain knowledge that cannot easily be copied.

Lastly, it is striking that successful SaaS companies often form a connecting layer between multiple parties. N8N's automation platform, for example, lets different software packages talk to one another, from CRM systems and email tools to databases and cloud services. In this way, users can automate complex workflows across multiple applications. Qover offers a similar connecting layer in the insurance world; through APIs, the company makes it possible to embed insurance products directly into, for example, mobility apps or e-commerce platforms. In both cases, the software platform enables others to enrich or optimise their own services.

## The road ahead

Europe's SaaS market is full of innovation, but the road to global dominance is far from automatic. The continent faces a structural scalability gap and a more cautious investment culture. Even so, encouraged by the geopolitical necessity of becoming more technologically self-reliant, it is taking various steps in the right direction. On 23 September 2025, Minister Karremans presented a list of [measures](https://www.rijksoverheid.nl/actueel/nieuws/2025/09/23/meer-groei-nederlandse-startups-en-scale-ups-door-meer-kapitaal-talent-en-verlenging-techleap) to support "the tech champions of tomorrow", with an emphasis on research-intensive key technologies such as cybersecurity, quantum computing and artificial intelligence. The caretaker government is thus investing EUR200 million in a second fund within the European Tech Champions Initiative (ETCI), while Invest-NL is exploring ways to channel more pension capital into venture-capital funds.

The current generation of SaaS companies shows, in any case, that there are ample opportunities. Focusing on business-critical processes, AI applications in specific niches, and building a connecting layer are strategies with which local players can position themselves strongly, even amid the growth surge of American firms. Mistral's success proves that even capital-intensive models can gain traction here, provided there is sufficient willingness to invest. The task now is to turn these occasional successes into structural ones.

# Made by Marí Makes Local Craftsmanship Scalable

[Retail](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/retail/index.html)

Marie Marie Amsterdam is choosing local craftsmanship and connection. The crocheted bags, made by Dutch hobbyists, are finding their way to consumers at home and abroad. Together with the Made by Marí platform, nominated for the ABN AMRO Newcomer Retail Award 2025-2026, the founders are also making local craftsmanship scalable for other brands.

The story of [Marie Marie Amsterdam](https://marie-amsterdam.com/), founded in 2022, begins with a bag that Marthe De Groot bought from her grandmother's crochet club. "I got so many compliments on it at home in Amsterdam," she says. "Then I thought: what a shame that this kind of craftsmanship stays hidden. How can I bring these crocheters together with people who want to buy their work?"

## From hobby to community

During the coronavirus period, De Groot decides to take the idea seriously. She posts calls in Facebook groups and within no time dozens of crocheters sign up. Marie Marie Amsterdam now has a community of 200 crocheters, divided into hubs around the country. Each hub has a coordinator who distributes materials and patterns. "For many women, it is valuable to connect their creativity to something bigger," says De Groot. "It shows that their handiwork has impact far beyond the hobby. All our bags come with a handwritten card bearing the maker's name.

Not just me, but Joke, Corrie and Jolanda are incredibly proud of our bags now being stocked at De Bijenkorf."

The colourful crocheted bags from Marie Marie Amsterdam are a hit. Alongside the company's own webshop and shop in De Pijp, the bags are now stocked by various international clients such as The Iconic in Australia and Le Bon Marche in Paris. This spring, the brand launched a collaboration with Dutch label Fabienne Chapot. This year, it also ran a promotion at Lowlands.

> "The colourful crocheted bags from Marie Marie Amsterdam are a hit."

## Active in 60 WhatsApp groups

That popularity also brings challenges. "I was overwhelmed by the influx of crocheters and the demand from shops," De Groot reflects. "The hardest part was organising everything operationally and making it scalable. I was in 60 WhatsApp groups and together with my mother I was working full time on community management."

That is when Rinke Adema enters the picture. With her experience at tech companies and start-ups, she immediately sees an opportunity. "I met Marthe and thought: this needs to be digitised. With a platform that automates everything, from onboarding makers to project management and payments."

## Making craftsmanship scalable

The two quickly decide to think beyond crocheted bags alone with their platform. "It escalated a bit, really," Adema laughs. "We saw that many makers have several hobbies, not just crocheting, but also ceramics, furniture making or clothing. In addition, Marthe noticed in her contacts with the fashion industry that many brands do want to work with craftsmanship, but get stuck in their own models. That is why we launched Made by Marí in 2024: a platform that brings makers and brands together and makes craftsmanship scalable."

> "This needs to be digitised. With a platform that automates everything, from onboarding makers to project management and payments."

Rinke Adema

Founder Made by Marí

The founders also see their platform as an answer to the growing pressure from legislation and regulation. Adema says: "Government is asking for more and more sustainable solutions, and many brands are struggling with that. With our platform, we make it possible to produce on a small scale and locally, exactly where the sector needs to go."

## The point on the horizon

Over the past year, the entrepreneurs have been working on developing that platform. A first version is now in place. "With Marie Marie Amsterdam as our first client, we are now testing the system, ironing out the bugs and learning how to make this work properly in the long term. You start small, but you already need a vision for five to ten years ahead," says Adema.

That point on the horizon is clear. Adema says: "We want to be a partner for major international brands with an international community of makers. From Tokyo to Terneuzen. Imagine a brand like Uniqlo working with local embroiderers in every city. Or Nike keyrings made by local makers. I also believe in Made by Marí evenings with makers all over the world."

De Groot adds: "We believe this platform can shake up the fashion industry and inspire it to revise its production models. Back in time, but with a hint of the future. The fashion industry is currently large-scale, polluting and complicated. We want to bring it closer to people."

## A change in mentality is badly needed

According to the founders, shaking up the industry is badly needed. "Some people think Marie Marie's bags are expensive. But when you consider how many hours, love and care go into them, they are actually incredibly cheap. It really requires a change in consumer mentality. We believe in inspiring people, rather than imposing things on them." According to Adema, that change starts with the big brands. "If the very brands that made fast fashion so big now start including small-scale, local collections, that sets an enormous movement in motion."

> "We believe this platform can shake up the fashion industry and inspire it to revise production models."

Marthe de Groot

Founder Made by Marí

The nomination for the ABN AMRO Newcomer Retail Award 2025-2026 feels to De Groot like confirmation that they are on the right track with Marie Marie Amsterdam and the Made by Marí platform. "We have not been at it for very long, so it is special that we are being seen by people in the field." Adema adds: "It is also the perfect stage for getting in touch with other retailers who can use our platform."

## The most enjoyable and the toughest trial

What would they like to pass on to other start-up entrepreneurs? Adema says: "Do not give up. You always run into hassle and setbacks, but persistence wins. Keep believing in your vision. We are not making money from the platform yet, which is why I am still working full time alongside it."

De Groot concludes: "You have to do something you are passionate about, otherwise you will not keep going. Of course there are risks, but we are not afraid of them. There is always a way. This is the most enjoyable, but also the toughest thing we have ever done."

# Software Companies Are Not Yet Ready for AI Agents

Although four out of five software companies expect AI agents to change the software landscape noticeably, almost a third of businesses are not yet technically ready to embed the technology. Half are still in the experimentation phase, while a quarter are doing nothing at all with agentic AI.

A large majority of software companies expect the software landscape to change noticeably because of AI agents, smart programs that can independently carry out complex tasks. Almost half of respondents even anticipate major to fundamental changes in the way software is designed, used or controlled, according to research by ABN AMRO and Panelwizard among 204 software-as-a-service companies (SaaS). SaaS is software purchased on a subscription basis and offered as a cloud service.

## AI agents are transforming the software market

The development of artificial intelligence (AI) is moving from generating text, software code and images within an app to independently carrying out full workflows. Examples include AI agents that manage stock, send quotations or prepare and execute an entire social media strategy. Through agentic AI, SaaS companies can make their product more attractive, for example by automating tasks or by presenting an AI agent to the user as the main point of contact.

## The AI agent is not a new concept

The term intelligent agent was already introduced in the 1980s to describe systems that can independently perceive, reason and act. Technological progress has, however, made AI agents far more powerful in recent years. Through large language models (LLMs), the engine behind among others ChatGPT, agents possess the necessary knowledge of the world, allowing communication to run smoothly. With the rise of so-called reasoning models, language models that can reason step by step, agents can also devise strategies themselves and determine the intermediate steps that go with them. By connecting to external applications, AI agents can also carry out actions in practice, such as launching an online advertising campaign or contacting a client or supplier.

Apart from the opportunities to improve existing SaaS or create new AI-first SaaS, the rise of agents could also affect the market at a deeper level. According to Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella, for example, it means the end of software in its current form. In his view, people will before long no longer work with separate software packages, but with AI agents that independently draw on various data sources and use them to perform the required actions. To draft a client quotation automatically, the AI agent pulls information from the customer database, combines it with up-to-date product or pricing information, and, if necessary, adds financial conditions based on previous customer behaviour. After checking, or fully autonomously, the quotation is then sent automatically by email. The user no longer has to switch between systems, because the thinking and administrative work is handled by application-independent agents, as Nadella sees it.

AI agents can also create entirely new applications. Claude, the AI assistant from tech company Anthropic, for example, can build directly usable apps based on a few user instructions. With specific software such as Lovable, Windsurf and Cursor, even people without programming knowledge can develop software, apps and websites; the AI agent interprets the user's request and turns it into code, and in some tools the result is visible immediately. Although producing good, safe and reliable software remains a profession in its own right, this trend of vibe coding could mean that more and more companies choose to develop tools themselves rather than purchasing them as SaaS.

## An AI agent as the primary point of contact

The influence of agents will extend across several levels. Some 75 per cent of respondents assume that the buttons and menus we currently see on our screens will, at least in part, make way for an AI agent as the primary point of contact. It takes tasks from the user, performs them in the background and reports the outcome back. Many people now know this form of interaction from smart AI assistants such as ChatGPT, Gemini and Claude.

Samir Selimi, founder of software company PurchAIse, has also brought an AI agent to the forefront of his platform. The assistant, called PAI, can take on a wide range of tasks in supplier management, such as validating data, placing orders and renegotiating contracts. Selimi sees chatting with PAI as a smart addition to the traditional user interface, which he believes will remain valuable. "We are used to things being displayed visually when we interact with a computer," he says. "You do not need forty screens or sixty tabs, but you do need some sort of cockpit that helps you keep an overview as a user."

The expectation is that communication in natural language, ordinary spoken and written language as people use it themselves, will play an important role within software. "You can see a shift in the way users interact with software," says Jelle Visser of ValueBlue, a platform for enterprise architecture. ValueBlue develops software that allows companies to map, analyse and improve their IT landscape schematically. "If a business analyst wants to understand exactly what such an architectural diagram is telling him, it is helpful if he can ask that question on our platform in that same way." For themselves, 38 per cent of software companies currently see opportunities for an AI agent as the primary point of contact. Visser himself does not feel he has the luxury of waiting calmly. "As a software company, you have to reinvent your user interface. If we do not do it, our competitor will."

## Communicating brand identity through agents

One fifth of the companies surveyed fear that their user experience will become less distinctive as a result. What now happens through mouse clicks and menu structures may at least partly be replaced by typing or speaking a single sentence. That could put pressure on an important element of software companies' differentiation: design, ease of use and visual branding.

A third of respondents, however, see the user experience as their main source of differentiation in the future. Even if interaction with software becomes more conversational, there are still all kinds of ways to give the AI agent a clear brand identity. Ever since the early rise of chatbots on company websites, experts have been thinking about ways to give these digital assistants a personality that fits the brand, for example through a fitting avatar, name and tone of voice.

## A different dashboard for every user

More than a quarter of SaaS companies see value in ad hoc generated screens. "Then you say to an agent: the window must be green, with corners and no rounded edges. And suddenly a screen is there, adapted to your preference," illustrates Franklin Evers of software company Usere. The company wants to keep its front end, the part the user sees, flexible anyway. Co-founder Pim Schipper says: "Every employee benefits from a different kind of information. With agentic AI, everyone can get a tailored dashboard."

Visser of ValueBlue also recognises the tension. "The people who use our software all have different roles and different levels of maturity. But it is a huge amount of work to program a separate version of the software for everyone." According to him, agentic AI can offer a solution here. "By giving an agent the necessary context about a role, it can determine what kinds of screens and functions an individual user needs."

Dashboards generated on the spot, or even complete applications and games built from scratch, are not a hypothetical scenario. Researchers have developed an AI model, for instance, that can generate the shooter game Doom ad hoc. The player encounters enemies and navigates through all sorts of spaces without any software code being needed; there is only an AI model trained on large quantities of imagery from the game. Google created a prototype in which its latest Gemini model builds a user interface based on the events in the previous screen, so no menu is fixed in advance.

## Software as a silent force in the background

Of the SaaS companies surveyed, 16 per cent fear their software will disappear into the background, a concern that is not entirely unfounded now that consumers and companies are increasingly turning to independent AI assistants. ChatGPT and Claude now support so-called actions that allow them to perform tasks in other applications independently, such as sending emails or changing CRM data. The established order in the tech world is also positioning itself ever more explicitly as a coordinating platform player. With Copilot Studio, Microsoft allows companies to build their own AI agents that can call not only Microsoft apps but also external software. Salesforce has developed a similar infrastructure with Agentforce.

One in five software companies is considering integrating with such AI-agent platforms. A quarter are not targeting specific platforms, but want to make their own software agent-ready in a broader sense. These entrepreneurs are making functions available in modular form, ensuring their documentation is clear, and structuring their software so that instructions from external agents, whether in natural language or not, can easily be translated into concrete actions within the application. "As a software company, you need to make sure LLMs can talk to your back end," Dennis De Reus of Stellar sums up. He is co-founder of Stellar, a platform that allows companies to develop customer-service agents themselves.

Nearly a third of companies are focusing more broadly on improving data access, integration options and standardised communication with other systems, interoperability. Among them is Last Mile Solutions, a software platform for electric-mobility service providers, such as charging-point operators, that offers technology for payments, fraud detection and preventing overload on the electricity grid, among other things. To provide these services, a great deal of data is needed, such as information on the amount of green energy being generated at a particular moment and how full individual car batteries are.

"Other parties can use that data to develop AI models and specialised agents," says marketing director Ben Ridgway. "So we offer a data backbone that makes AI innovation possible, such as smart charging and predictive maintenance." Ridgway of Last Mile Solutions, which serves as a central hub in the field of e-mobility software, fully agrees. "Our back end is extremely complex. There is so much data we manage and have to make available in the right way. For us, it is crucial to show again and again that we are a reliable partner."

## Developing applications a la carte

AI agents do not necessarily operate within the boundaries of one application; they can also switch between separate databases and tools to carry out tasks independently. Think of an agent that retrieves historical sales data from system A, forecasts future sales figures using an algorithm from system B and shows the accompanying chart in a dashboard from system C. What matters, then, is not so much the total package as the way individual pieces of software can be accessed by AI systems.

Even before the rise of AI agents, many software companies were already busy breaking their systems down into small reusable modules and making them available through so-called APIs or SDKs. Many AI models are offered in this way too, allowing developers, for example, to embed a smart chatbot on their own website. "Creating software is increasingly a matter of combining all sorts of components," says Visser of ValueBlue. "That makes entering the software market easier than ever."

The same applies to AI agents, which can draw a la carte on data and functions from different suppliers. Yet only a quarter of SaaS companies appear to have the necessary preconditions in place. They have the required digital connections in the form of APIs, application programming interfaces, or in some cases were even explicitly designed to work with AI agents and agent platforms.

If software companies start making loose elements of their systems available on a large scale, it may in practice become difficult for external agents to keep deciding which function to source from which party. Sebastiaan den Boer, Director of AI and Data Science at digital agency Youwe, says: "If an AI agent constantly has to choose between twenty tools and fifteen databases, then the knowledge required for that also has to be captured in a prompt. That can quickly become a very complex instruction."

## Existing software under pressure from AI agents

Some companies, 14 per cent, see the risk that specific functions in their software will become redundant because externally developed AI agents carry out tasks in their own way without using existing software modules. One example is an agent that classifies clients into segments itself rather than calling an embedded filter function in existing software. This fear is only limited among companies with annual revenue of more than EUR25 million, 7 per cent, while a quarter of smaller companies fear it.

Another 9 per cent fear that their carefully developed logic or AI models will no longer be used, for example when agents developed elsewhere can make decisions based on their own reasoning ability. A recruitment agent, for example, could assess applicants using its own scoring method, completely ignoring the software's internal matching model. Previously purchased HR software would then function merely as a data source, or even be bypassed altogether.

De Reus of Stellar is less afraid of such a scenario. He can imagine software users increasingly interacting with AI agents, but he is convinced that the underlying systems will largely remain relevant. "An agent cannot simply store data at random. You need a system that ensures this happens according to certain structures and rules. The same applies to customer identification; you need modules that let you establish someone's identity with certainty, and then rules that determine what data a particular client may or may not view." According to Franklin Evers, co-founder of Usere, this is exactly where software companies differentiate themselves. "It is precisely in that clever intermediate layer that your intellectual property sits, and you need to protect it," says Evers, who with partner Pim Schipper is developing an AI-driven marketing solution.

## Automating tasks intelligently

The companies with fundamental concerns about their business model are in the minority. Software companies overwhelmingly see opportunities to improve their own product with AI agents. Two thirds of respondents favour agents that carry out tasks on behalf of the user, such as retrieving information or going through processes that previously relied on strict steps and rules. Six out of ten companies would like to use AI agents to support their own customers.

PurchAIse, for example, uses agentic AI for supplier management, including retrieving and providing market insights. An automatically detected fall in ingredient prices can, for instance, trigger a contract renegotiation, after which AI agent PAI also drafts an email to the supplier. Usere is initially focusing on marketing and relieves SMEs of work by automating the creation and posting of social media posts, among other things.

Among respondents, there is concern about the possible unpredictability of AI agents. Almost half of software companies see a lack of transparency and control as a real risk. AI agents do not work according to fixed rules, but on the basis of probability; they choose the most likely next step and its likely execution, but not necessarily the right one.

"It is difficult to set up processes properly and predictably with agentic AI," says Rogier Hosman, co-founder of digital agency Youwe, which develops digital solutions for companies. Hosman knows from experience what is needed for a successful implementation. "Do I have sufficient control over the technology, or will half my SaaS product collapse tomorrow? What risk do I want to take in that?" Although concrete rules can be provided here too in order to steer the agent, it remains a fundamentally different way of working from traditional software.

## Technically, a great deal still needs to happen

Although software companies therefore see countless opportunities in the field of agentic AI, the question is whether they will be able to take the necessary steps in the short term. Almost 30 per cent of respondents doubt whether they are technically ready for AI agents; their software architecture or organisation is not yet sufficiently configured for applying and integrating agents. Notably, the smallest companies, with annual revenue below EUR2 million, see this risk far less, possibly because their IT landscape is less complex and therefore more flexible.

It is also striking that a large share of software companies, 53 per cent, are still in the experimentation phase with AI agents. These companies are exploring the technology, but have not yet embedded it structurally in their product. More than a quarter have no concrete plans at all in the area of agentic AI. Incidentally, maturity in the field of agentic AI appears to depend strongly on company size; it is almost impossible to find a large company, revenue above EUR25 million, that is doing nothing with the technology, whereas among micro companies, revenue below EUR2 million, this is true for 40 per cent. Vertical software companies, which focus on specific sectors, are also on average less advanced than software companies with a broader orientation.

That limited maturity is worrying, because meanwhile a new generation of technology companies is storming the market. According to Dealroom data, around USD3.2 billion was invested worldwide in 2024 in emerging technology companies that have embedded agentic AI in their service. That is exponential growth compared with the years before, which recorded investments of USD727 million and USD1.3 billion respectively.

Not all clients of SaaS companies are anywhere near ready for large-scale use of AI agents either, PurchAIse's Selimi observes. "Technology can do everything in theory, but companies find it difficult to adapt in practice. More often than not, it breaks down on the people and the processes."

## The differentiating power of the future

Amid this technological transformation, software companies are holding fast to the relationships they have built with their clients. More than half, and among large companies even two thirds, expect this to remain their greatest differentiator in the long term, for example because of a deep understanding of the client's context or collaboration on implementation and further development of the software. Schipper of Usere takes a similar view and even thinks there is room for improvement. "You have to invest in the relationship with your client. For a long time, many software companies failed to really listen to their client."

For the time being, AI often lacks the context that is essential in specialist sectors. That is why a significant share of software companies also expect to be able to keep relying in the future on domain knowledge, such as relevant legislation and regulation or sector-specific processes. "In the end, you need domain expertise to add real value," says Hosman of Youwe. Selimi of PurchAIse agrees. "One of the ways to distinguish yourself as a software company is by knowing your end market and the associated processes inside out."

This awareness is naturally stronger among vertical software companies, 47 per cent, which already focus on specific niches, than among horizontal players, 32 per cent. Among large companies too, a higher percentage, 55 per cent, expects to remain distinctive through deep domain knowledge than among SMEs and micro companies, where the percentage is about a third.

## Investing in the front end or the back end

The soft elements of client relationships and domain knowledge are followed by concrete matters directly visible in the software: the software's functional coverage, or feature set, and the user experience, both cited by a third of respondents as points of differentiation in the long term.

By contrast, the technical aspects in the background, data, integration strength and algorithms, rank lowest. According to Schipper and Evers of Usere, software companies would do well to keep investing under the bonnet. Evers says: "You need to keep the core of your software very robust. Other parties can then talk to it through an API. They need to feel that they can build on your technology and rely on it." Ridgway of Last Mile Solutions, which acts like a spider in the web in e-mobility software, fully agrees. "Our back end is extremely complex. There is so much data we manage and need to make available properly. For us, the task is to keep proving that we are a reliable partner in this."

## Adaptability is essential

SaaS companies agree: the software market will change sharply in the years ahead. They will themselves be able to optimise their software using AI agents, both as the main point of contact and in the background. Meanwhile, the familiar chat assistants are acquiring an ever more prominent position. Now that they are becoming the daily starting point for more and more users and are integrating ever further with all kinds of tools, the likelihood also grows that existing software companies will become less visible in one way or another.

The rise of platforms that allow users to build agents or software themselves may also mean that activities previously supported by software packages will now be handled by individually produced AI agents or self-built software. Some software companies see promise in an offensive strategy in which they themselves take on a platform role, or rely on the unique added value of elements of their software.

A black-and-white scenario in which SaaS is completely wiped out by AI agents developed elsewhere is, in all probability, not what will happen. The new reality does, however, require a high degree of adaptability from software companies; integrations with external tools and parties are more important than ever, and making the necessary automation leap themselves is essential if they are to remain relevant.

# Strong Growth Returns to Technology, Media and Telecom

The TMT sector is growing strongly once again. Growth weakened in 2023 and 2024 because of rising interest rates, and employment in the sector even fell. In 2025, growth recovered to 4.5 per cent and ABN AMRO expects growth rates of 5 per cent and 4 per cent in 2026 and 2027 respectively. Digitalisation, cloud migrations and investment in artificial intelligence (AI) are key drivers of this growth.

The TMT sector consists of IT companies, media companies and telecom companies. The sector has a long history of growth, particularly because of the strong use of information technology (IT). IT companies therefore make an important contribution to productivity growth in the Dutch economy. The digital transformation is clearly visible in the TMT sector's growth figures: an average of 6 per cent a year over the past thirty years, while annual growth in gross domestic product (GDP) remained stuck at 2 per cent. In 2025, 400,000 people work in the TMT sector, which accounts for around 7 per cent of total Dutch output.

## Entrepreneurs are optimistic

Entrepreneurs in the TMT sector are optimistic, according to business confidence data from the Netherlands Business Survey (COEN) by Statistics Netherlands (CBS). For the "information and communication" sector, the business sentiment indicator is positive in the fourth quarter of 2025, while business confidence is negative in all other sectors. Optimism is also visible in the mergers and acquisitions market, which is picking up again after two years of decline. The international trade war is focused mainly on goods. Trade restrictions therefore have only limited and indirect effects on the TMT sector, which primarily exports services.

## Sharp rise in AI investment

The United States is experiencing an unprecedented investment wave, with roughly USD400 billion in AI investment in 2025. Tech companies have all announced record levels of investment, including standout examples such as Amazon (USD100 billion), Microsoft (USD80 billion) and Alphabet (USD75 billion). These investments are focused mainly on chips, data centres and power supply. The breakneck pace of AI development stands in sharp contrast to the rest of the US economy, which is moving at [two speeds](https://www.abnamro.com/research/nl/onze-research/visie-op-de-vs-2026-de-ai-haves-en-have-nots): while AI and related sectors are flourishing, most other sectors are stagnating or shrinking. AI investment is now responsible for half of GDP growth.

Investment in artificial intelligence is also growing strongly in the Netherlands. According to data from Dealroom, AI investment has increased fivefold in five years. The sector is home to many innovative and fast-growing companies, such as DataSnipper, an Amsterdam software company and unicorn that uses AI to support accountants in preparing annual accounts and auditors in performing audits. Even so, AI investment in the Netherlands still trails countries such as Sweden and Switzerland.

The promise of AI is immense. A large share of knowledge work may be transformed or even replaced, as is already visible in fields such as marketing, communications and translation. According to the World Economic Forum's Future of Jobs Report 2025, by 2030 only one third of all work will still be carried out mainly by people, a sharp fall from the current 47 per cent. Even so, there is uncertainty about the returns on these vast AI investments, and high valuations on stock markets are prompting questions about a possible "bubble".

## Business succession in the TMT sector

A survey of 519 entrepreneurs, carried out by Ipsos I&O on behalf of ABN AMRO, shows that entrepreneurs who want to step back often still have no succession plan in place. Entrepreneurs in the TMT sector are no exception. Our sample for "information and communication" contains only 32 entrepreneurs, which is too small to be representative or to support detailed conclusions.

The survey clearly shows that younger entrepreneurs are more likely to look to an external party as their intended successor. A takeover by an external party is often seen as an opportunity to use the released capital to start a new company. This so-called serial entrepreneurship is common in sectors with a high share of young entrepreneurs, such as e-commerce, software development and IT services.

CBS data shows that the share of entrepreneurs under the age of thirty rose from 9 per cent in 2010 to 16 per cent in 2024. That may be linked to the rise of IT services and software development, as entrepreneurs in these emerging fields are younger than average.

# Without the Right Numbers, Green IT Remains an Illusion

[Analysis](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/analyses/index.html)

[Cross-sector](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/zakelijk/insights/sectoren-en-trends/alle-sectoren/index.html)

Companies that use computing power and data storage in the cloud and want to make their IT more sustainable need to know where gains can be achieved. For the time being, however, cloud providers do not share all the figures on energy use and CO2 emissions, and they each do so in a different way.

## Interview with Julia Krauwer on BNR Nieuwsradio

Listen to the clip featuring Sector Banker Julia Krauwer on the importance of good data for making IT more sustainable.

As a result, IT teams do not know clearly where the greatest sustainability gains can be made, nor can they measure the impact of optimisations. For now, there is also a lack of legal obligations and binding standards determining what information cloud companies must make available to their own clients.

## Limiting negative environmental effects

Across the world, companies are moving their IT activities to the cloud, and both the development and use of artificial intelligence (AI) are driving demand for data-centre capacity. According to an [estimate](https://www.iea.org/reports/energy-and-ai/energy-supply-for-ai) by the International Energy Agency, global electricity consumption by data centres in 2024 amounted to around 460 terawatt hours (TWh), equivalent to the annual consumption of countries such as Sweden or Argentina. By 2030, the agency expects that to more than double to 1,000 TWh. Around 40 per cent of that power will then come from renewable sources, but coal- and gas-fired power stations will still be needed for nearly half of it. The CO <sub>2</sub> footprint of digitalisation therefore remains substantial for the time being.

Data centres have become increasingly efficient in recent years. That has much to do with economies of scale; a large data centre can spread the energy used to cool servers, a relatively large share of power consumption, across a large number of customers. Progress has also been made with new, more energy-efficient cooling techniques and software for managing the internal climate. In practice, however, users of AI and cloud services, who determine demand for data-centre capacity, do not have sufficient tools to reduce the harmful environmental effects of their IT efforts.

## Measuring is knowing

"Greening IT always starts with the right information," says Wiebren van der Zee, specialist in green IT at ABN AMRO. "Think of data on energy and water use, emissions and the use of raw materials. In our IT organisation, we collect these data for our employees' workplaces, so laptops and screens, but also for our own data centres and the cloud services we purchase."

## Guidance for sustainable digitalisation

Together with various organisations from the National Coalition for Sustainable Digitalisation (NCDD), ABN AMRO developed the [Sustainable IT Impact Assessment](https://eur03.safelinks.protection.outlook.com/?url=https%3A%2F%2Fcoalitieduurzamedigitalisering.nl%2Fsustainable-it-impact-assessment-siia%2F&data=05%7C02%7Cjulia.krauwer%40nl.abnamro.com%7C9948116aa7d649b3c17508de1082ebd8%7C3a15904d3fd94256a753beb05cdf0c6d%7C0%7C0%7C638966351662129336%7CUnknown%7CTWFpbGZsb3d8eyJFbXB0eU1hcGkiOnRydWUsIlYiOiIwLjAuMDAwMCIsIlAiOiJXaW4zMiIsIkFOIjoiTWFpbCIsIldUIjoyfQ%3D%3D%7C0%7C%7C%7C&sdata=Yz6QXd%2FJKDj3vTjpdic9%2FGPln8C8x2QH6hBRajvDbgc%3D&reserved=0) (SIIA). This tool offers practical guidance and extensive background information for getting started with greener IT. The advice is based in part on the concrete experiences of companies and public authorities.

For several years now, the major cloud providers have been trying to meet this need for information through CO2 dashboards that allow their customers to monitor the emissions from their digital activities. Willem Jan Groenewoud, CEO of software company Ab Ovo, welcomes this wave: "It is good that they are providing more transparency than a few years ago. Measuring is knowing." The company is strongly committed to making the software it develops for its clients' mission-critical processes more sustainable. "We assume a scenario in which energy may end up being rationed, so we design software that uses as little energy as possible."

The cloud world is far more transparent than it was a few years ago, but there is still a great deal to criticise in the information being offered. Not all the necessary data are provided, and providers differ greatly in their measurement methods and the level of detail they supply.

## A paper reality

The issue starts with the way cloud companies report their own organisation-wide emissions. According to an analysis by British newspaper The Guardian, the own data centres of the biggest tech companies emitted almost [eight times](https://www.theguardian.com/technology/2024/sep/15/data-center-gas-emissions-tech) more than was reported in total. That analysis covered data from 2020 through to 2022.

The difference can be explained by the measurement method. It is often market based. That means providers calculate their CO2 emissions on the basis of purchased green-energy certificates or long-term purchase contracts for energy from, for example, a wind farm. Such constructions, however, say nothing about the actual electricity mix at the location of the data centre. "It is a paper reality," says Van der Zee of ABN AMRO. "Green energy is administratively dragged all around the world."

This means a cloud provider can buy guarantees of origin for a hydropower station in Norway and allocate them on paper to a server park in Ireland, even though that park partly runs on electricity from coal-fired power stations. On paper, the footprint is then zero, but in reality CO2 emissions are definitely being produced through the use of fossil energy.

## Average power mix

Under the European sustainability law CSRD, the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive in full, market-based reporting alone is no longer enough. On the one hand, organisations must be encouraged to purchase green energy and it is right that they provide insight into these efforts; on the other hand, the market-based measurement method paints too rosy a picture of the actual emissions from electricity generation. That is why the CSRD also requires companies to report on a location-based basis. Here, the average power mix in the region where the company or data centre buys its electricity is the starting point.

Both methods also appear in the emissions dashboards of cloud companies. The location-based figures, however, are not presented equally prominently in every case. At Amazon, the market-based emissions data are put front and centre, and users have to click through to gain insight into the location-based figures. Microsoft does not show location-based emissions in its dashboard at all.

## Location-based reporting

Location-based emissions are calculated by multiplying electricity consumption in kilowatt hours (kWh) by the local emissions factor. This shows how much CO <sub>2</sub> equivalent is released on average when one kWh of electricity is produced. In regions with wind turbines or hydropower stations, the emissions factor is lower than in regions where electricity is generated mainly in coal- or gas-fired power stations. The location-based measurement method is an average and does not require insight into fluctuations in the energy mix per day or per hour.

## Suppliers' willingness

In the world of emissions dashboards, there are no binding standards, and in practice buyers of cloud services depend on the willingness of their cloud providers to open up information. As a result, the level of detail varies greatly. Most provide monthly figures, but the speed of updates differs. Some providers do this constantly, while others lag months behind. There is also variation in granularity; sometimes emissions are shown only per organisation or region, while in other cases they can also be monitored down to the level of individual services or projects.

Not all dashboards include scope 3 emissions in their calculations either. While scopes 1 and 2 here relate to the use of fossil energy and fossil-generated electricity to keep the data centres running, scope 3 concerns the more indirect emissions. These are caused, for example, by the production and disposal of IT equipment, and the construction of data centres and offices. Incidentally, the cloud providers that do report at scope 3 level do not do so consistently either; where one limits itself to hardware production, another, for example, also includes running the company canteen.

Boavizta, an initiative of dozens of European IT companies and consultancy organisations, has developed a uniform way of calculating the climate impact of digital infrastructure. At present, each cloud provider calculates in its own way, creating an incomplete picture and making figures hard to compare across suppliers. Boavizta, for example, makes data available on the environmental effects of producing and transporting servers, which companies can use for a more thorough calculation of the ecological footprint of their IT activities. Resilio also provides such data, combined with advice, but as a commercial service.

## Insight for development teams

The cloud dashboards are primarily aimed at insight and reporting. A positive point is that some providers also give concrete advice on how companies can reduce their emissions. Microsoft and Google, for example, suggest scaling back unused capacity.

Even so, it is difficult for organisations with a rich IT landscape to gain insight into emissions at application level. Software usually consists of several components spread across projects, regions or services. As a result, the direct effects of changes made by development teams, lighter code or a more efficient architecture, can easily remain out of sight. "That is also why we have developed our own AI-driven tooling for this," says Groenewoud of software company Ab Ovo.

## Using less energy

According to Max Schulze, founder of the Sustainable Digital Infrastructure Alliance (SDIA), focusing on emissions is actually not enough. "We should be optimising for lower energy use, for fewer data centres. We live on a continent with limited space and resources," he says.

The other experts also recognise that green IT starts with insight into energy use. "The emissions of a data centre are not a pure measurement instrument," says Groenewoud of Ab Ovo. "At the functional level, we first map the electricity used in kilowatt hours. Only then do we look at the location of the data centre and the energy mix at that place."

None of the cloud providers, however, gives insight into the number of kWh their customers consume. Schulze of the SDIA finds that illogical, because you should always start with the efficiency of your product. "If you buy a car, you want to know how many kilometres you can drive on a litre of fuel to get a picture of the vehicle's efficiency. Only then do you translate that into the number of grams of CO <sub>2</sub> equivalent per kilometre."

## Alternative solutions

Various parties are stepping into the gap created by cloud providers' limited information provision. One example is Cloud Carbon Footprint (CCF), an open-source tool that reads actual cloud-service usage and converts it into both kilowatt hours (kWh) and CO <sub>2</sub> emissions. These data are also made available by the hour, allowing IT teams, for example, to see when applications are used the most and which systems continue running unnecessarily at night.

Although it can be a good starting point for organisations seeking additional insight, open-source services such as CCF do not come with any guarantee that they will be updated in time and regularly. Commercial parties do provide that guarantee, and several paid services have therefore been launched in recent years, including Greenpixie.

## Data from the source

All services, from the cloud providers' own dashboards to externally developed solutions, are based on assumptions about the energy use of the servers and the digital services running on them. That is why project NADIKI, an initiative of SDIA together with the University of Stuttgart and data-centre operator Kolo, goes all the way back to the source: the data centre. It collects live measurement data via sensors and links it to the current CO <sub>2</sub> intensity of the power grid. "We have sensors everywhere, from the power supply of the full data centre down to rack and server level," Schulze explains. This information is used to collect energy-use and emissions data for specific workloads.

Schulze says: "This is the first time that energy and emissions are being measured end to end." The project is still in the pilot phase, but it already shows that improvement is possible compared with current methodologies.

## Keep communicating

When the emissions dashboards were launched for the first time, they were welcomed as a sympathetic and much-needed feature. Some of the cloud providers have major ambitions when it comes to transparency and see it as a source of differentiation. Across the board, however, the pace varies greatly.

According to Van der Zee of ABN AMRO, organisations need to make their wishes known to their IT suppliers. "They have to keep communicating about the kind of information they need." As long as information provision is not optimal, the gap is being filled by open-source tools and the services of specialised software companies.

Standards do exist, such as those of Boavizta, and the Greenhouse Gas Protocol also has IT-specific guidelines. Real obligations, however, do not exist, and that unnecessarily creates challenges for organisations wanting to green their IT. Regulation can offer relief and ensure that all players report in the same way.
